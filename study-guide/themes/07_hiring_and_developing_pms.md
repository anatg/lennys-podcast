# Chapter 7: Hiring & Developing PMs

## Overview

Hiring and developing product managers is among the most consequential work a product leader does—and among the least systematized. Unlike engineering, where a coding exercise can test a discrete skill, PM hiring requires assessing judgment, curiosity, communication, and raw thinking ability that resist easy quantification. Unlike design, where a portfolio makes the work visible, a PM's best contributions are often invisible: the disaster that didn't happen, the bad idea that died before anyone shipped it, the alignment that made the team move twice as fast.

The collective wisdom across these guests converges on a counterintuitive insight: the PM hiring and development problem is primarily a *clarity* problem. Organizations that struggle to hire great PMs typically haven't defined what a great PM looks like in their context. Managers who can't develop their PMs typically haven't thought clearly about what "better" means, or haven't made their expectations visible. The most practical advice across every guest—from Bill Carr's Bar Raiser program to Petra Wille's PMwheel to Christian Idiodi's insistence on demonstrating competence—points back to the same root: build a shared, specific definition of excellence before you try to hire for it, measure it, or coach toward it.

The second theme that runs through these conversations is that PM development is deeply personal and frequently neglected. Nikhyl Singhal's observation stings: "In a hundred years when the archeologists look back at tech in the early years, they'll say the biggest surprise was how much we thought it was okay to not train managers." Yet management training and PM coaching require investment of scarce time, and most organizations default to hoping good PMs develop themselves. The guests who've thought hardest about this—Petra Wille, Christian Idiodi, Ravi Mehta, Ken Norton—offer a corrective: development requires a compass (a definition of good), honest assessment, a shared vision, and consistent follow-up. All four, not just one or two.

## What Actually Makes a Great PM

The most debated question across these conversations is deceptively simple: what are you actually hiring for? The answers reveal productive tensions.

Several guests emphasize *judgment and taste* as the irreducible core. Ken Norton argues that hiring has become so systematized—candidates drill mock exercises the way students prep for the SAT—that the most important questions get crowded out: Can this person inspire a team? Do they have the right mindset for *this* particular job? Do we even agree on what the job is? Jeremy Henrickson of Rippling distills it even more sharply: "Leaders are right a lot" is the quality that matters most in product, because PM decisions reflect across entire organizations. The ability to make good calls with incomplete information—and have confidence even when others disagree—is "a very peculiar skill you can't really teach."

Shreyas Doshi adds a behavioral dimension to the judgment question: the quality he calls *high agency*. High-agency PMs find ways to achieve goals despite adverse conditions, without waiting for perfect circumstances. This trait, he argues, explains the gap between "looks great on paper" and actual impact more reliably than credentials or communication scores.

Other guests emphasize *competence and domain knowledge* as the foundation of PM effectiveness. Christian Idiodi is direct: "Most people don't like product managers often because they haven't experienced good product managers." When PMs don't deeply understand customers, data, and business better than anyone else in the room, organizations stop trusting them—and sales teams and executives fill the vacuum. Competence is the precondition for influence.

These views aren't necessarily in conflict. Jackie Bavaro's PEARL interview framework threads the needle: she looks for the *epiphany*—the insight the candidate had that others didn't. That's simultaneously a test of judgment (did they see something important?) and domain knowledge (did they understand the space well enough to see it?). Ravi Mehta's 12 PM competencies similarly span both: execution and customer insight represent craft, while product strategy and leadership represent judgment at scale.

One tension worth naming explicitly: several guests distinguish between *qualities you hire for* versus *qualities you can develop*. Petra Wille advocates hiring for curiosity and empathy—traits that are genuinely hard to develop in a corporate setting—and coaching the rest (user interviewing technique, prioritization frameworks, hypothesis-driven development). Jiaona Zhang has her own formulation: "Become known for something specific." The implication is that a PM's edge may be in execution excellence, technical depth, analytical rigor, or relationship-building—and hiring managers should look for which specific combination fits their context, not a universal archetype.

## Designing an Interview Process That Works

The guests who've managed the most PM hiring share a common frustration: standard behavioral interviews are too easy to game and too far removed from the actual work. They've compensated with structural innovations that deserve close attention.

**Amazon's Bar Raiser program** (Bill Carr) remains the gold standard for fighting urgency bias at scale. Every interview loop includes a Bar Raiser—someone who is not the hiring manager, does not report to the hiring manager, and is not from the same department. The Bar Raiser runs the debrief and has a rarely-used but real technical veto. The mechanism's genius is targeting the most common failure mode in PM hiring: the desperate hiring manager who fills a seat with whoever seems good enough this quarter. The Bar Raiser's explicit job is to keep the bar calibrated across the entire organization, not just this team's immediate need. The cost is real—up to 10 hours per week for active Bar Raisers—but Carr's argument is simple: the cost of a wrong hire far exceeds the cost of the process.

The **Interview Q Compilation** episode surfaces a different set of innovations: questions designed to break through rehearsed answers. Shishir Mehrotra's "teleportation device" question teaches Eigenquestion thinking—identifying the one or two questions that unlock most of the answer space—without any emotional stakes attached to real decisions. Nikhyl Singhal's "What does everyone believe that you think is hogwash?" test is explicitly designed to defeat prediction: "There's no way to answer that question without being genuinely opinionated. When I break that wall, I'm testing, is this person authentic?" Meltem Kuran Berkowitz goes further: she asks candidates "What would your siblings say about you?" Siblings won't give flattering answers; a candidate who can recount their siblings' honest critique demonstrates self-awareness that a polished self-assessment question can't reach.

Paul Adams of Intercom contributed the most underrated innovation in the compilation: asking references "What feedback will I be giving this person in their first performance review?" rather than "Can you describe their strengths?" This reframing is decisive. References cannot dodge it. There is always a real answer. And unlike "tell me about their weaknesses"—which invites strategic softening—the performance review framing is concrete and forward-looking.

JZ Zhang articulates what to actually listen for across all of these approaches: "Good answers are people who put structure and a way forward through the ambiguity. That's what you look for. You want your PM to not just be like, 'Oh no, we're swimming in ambiguity,' but actually put a path forward." This is the meta-skill the best interview questions probe: not any specific knowledge, but the candidate's instinct to create structure, name the key questions, and move forward with incomplete information.

## Reference Checks: The Step Everyone Skips

Almost every guest who discussed hiring mentioned reference checks as both critical and consistently underdone. The problem is structural: references provided by candidates are pre-selected to be positive, and referees are generally reluctant to say anything that could harm someone's career.

The solution is not to skip reference checks but to redesign the questions. Paul Adams' forward-looking "first performance review" question is one approach. Another is the technique described by Nikhyl Singhal as "the Jules Walter technique": repeat the feedback back to the referee better than they said it—"What I'm hearing you say is that this person struggles with X in situations Y"—until they confirm you've gotten it right. When referees feel truly heard, they go deeper.

The most effective PM leaders described above also emphasize *off-list* references: reaching out to former colleagues, skip-level reports, or peers who weren't on the candidate's official list. This requires more effort but bypasses the selection bias of references that candidates curate.

Ken Norton raises the deeper issue: companies have become so focused on structured processes that they've stopped asking the fundamental hiring questions—"Can this person inspire my team? Do they have the right mindset for this specific job?"—which can only be answered through direct, unstructured conversation, not a rubric. The best hiring decisions combine structured process (Bar Raiser-style consistency, behavioral interview frameworks, standardized reference questions) with genuinely exploratory conversation that probes for judgment and character.

## Onboarding: The Investment Nobody Makes

The episode compilation is largely silent on the topic of formal onboarding—which is itself a signal. Most PM organizations treat onboarding as "show up and figure it out." The guests who addressed it converged on the same corrective: the first 90 days define how a new PM is perceived for years.

Jiaona Zhang's framework is the most detailed: spend the first 90 days building context by talking to 40-50 people across functions and levels, not just leadership; identify strategic gaps; create a learning plan before stepping back; and communicate broadly—to the board, to leadership—about what you're seeing and who is being held accountable. The trap she fell into at Webflow: pushing too hard for change before building sufficient trust in the bank. "Trust is everything. You have to be thoughtful about your trust as a bank. You're putting money into your bank and then at some point you're going to take money out."

Molly Graham's insight from Facebook applies as a complementary principle: most new-hire problems are structural, not personal. Before assuming a new PM is struggling because of their individual capabilities, diagnose whether they understand their role and what success looks like. "Your only job as a manager is clear roles and clear expectations." The Waterline Model—diagnose from the surface down—instructs managers to check structure (goals, roles, expectations) before diving into interpersonal or intrapersonal issues. Most onboarding failures are failures of clarity, not capability.

## Coaching and Developing Existing PMs

The development section of these conversations is where the richest disagreements live—and where the most actionable frameworks concentrate.

**Petra Wille's Five Ingredients of Great PM Coaching** is the most complete framework: (1) a compass—a definition of what "good" looks like in your context; (2) current state assessment—where is this PM today?; (3) shared vision—aligned on their trajectory, thinking bigger than their current role; (4) a development plan the PM owns; and (5) consistent follow-up. The most important of these, per Wille, is the one most managers skip: follow-up. "Consistency beats intensity. Small, regular investments are far more effective than big-bang annual reviews or one-off training events."

**Ravi Mehta's "exponential feedback" concept** extends this: rather than addressing surface symptoms, identify the root-cause competency gap that's driving those symptoms. His 12 PM competencies provide the diagnostic vocabulary—rate each across "needs focus / on track / outperforming" and focus coaching conversations on the underlying capability, not the behavior that surfaced it. Feedback that targets root causes has compounding returns; feedback on symptoms doesn't.

**Ken Norton's inner work framework** challenges the assumption that PM development is primarily about skills. He argues that senior product leaders hit a wall not because they lack frameworks but because they haven't done the inner work to shift from reactive to creative leadership. Reactive leadership—responding from fear, wanting to be liked, needing to be right—correlates negatively with every success metric. Creative leadership—responding from curiosity, purpose, openness—correlates positively. The shift isn't achieved by adding more skills; it requires redesigning the internal operating system. Coaches and managers who focus only on tactics and skills are working on the wrong level for senior PMs.

Nikhyl Singhal offers a sharp diagnosis of why development often fails: managers are poor at identifying their direct reports' real development areas. "The listener has a development area, it's substantive. The manager is poor at identifying it, the individual refuses to hear it, or they hear it and just don't want to change it because 'This is who I am.'" His concept of **"shadows of superpowers"** is particularly useful: a PM's greatest strength creates a corresponding blindspot. The exceptional collaborator who always agrees struggles to be opinionated when it matters. The world-class storyteller misses critical details. The development need is hiding in the shadow of what the person already does well—which is why neither the manager nor the PM often sees it.

Christian Idiodi's approach addresses this from a different angle: **"practice before promotion."** Companies chronically promote people into incompetence by giving them titles before they've practiced the role. His prescription is to do VP-level work before you become a VP—take on strategic projects, practice coaching when you're still an IC, make mistakes in contexts where failure is recoverable. This requires managers to create those practice opportunities deliberately, not just wait for formal promotions to trigger development.

## Managing Underperformance

The hardest—and most avoided—dimension of PM management is the underperformance conversation. The collective wisdom here is unambiguous: avoidance does more damage than the conversation itself ever would.

**Kim Scott's "Bob story"** is the canonical example. Ten months of avoided hard conversations ended with a firing—and the fired employee's response: "Why didn't you tell me? I thought you all cared about me." The cost of ruinous empathy (caring personally without challenging directly) is not just organizational; it's a betrayal of the person being "protected." Scott's Radical Candor framework reframes the feedback question from "how do I avoid causing pain?" to "how do I care enough to be honest?"—with specific behavioral prescriptions: solicit feedback before giving it; praise in public and criticize in private; give feedback immediately rather than waiting for a "better moment"; gauge how it lands and adjust on the right axis (move up on "care personally" if someone seems hurt, never retreat on "challenge directly").

**Alisa Cohn's Pre-Firing Conversation** framework addresses the other end of the underperformance arc. Before firing anyone, she argues, you must have a crystal-clear "last chance" conversation: state the specific changes needed, give a concrete deadline (30 days), and name the consequence explicitly ("we'll have to part ways"). Most leaders skip this step. Cohn's observation is damning: "The leader who avoids hard conversations creates more problems than the conversation itself would have." The pre-firing conversation makes firing easier if necessary—no surprise—and often makes it unnecessary.

**Matt Mochary's humane firing process** extends this with tactical empathy: warn the person before delivering the news; allow emotional release; make them feel heard; then actively help them find their next role. The "Decision vs. Implementation" distinction (from the Wei Deng framework) is clarifying: the decision answers "what's right for the customer and business?" while implementation answers "how do we do this in a way that's humane for the person?" Conflating these leads to either cruel efficiency or indefinite delay.

**Molly Graham** adds an organizational dimension: "Your only job as a manager is clear roles and clear expectations. Most performance problems stem from people not knowing what their job is or what success looks like." Before launching into performance management, verify the person understands their role and how it ties to company goals. The Waterline Model instructs: snorkel before you scuba—diagnose structural issues (unclear expectations, misaligned goals) before concluding the problem is interpersonal or individual.

## Building PM Culture and Capability at Scale

Beyond individual coaching and hiring decisions, several guests addressed how to build PM culture and capability as an organizational property.

**Amazon's input metric philosophy** (Bill Carr) provides a model for measuring PM effectiveness that goes beyond ship dates. By identifying the 3-6 inputs to the customer experience that, if improved, drive outputs (revenue, retention), organizations can give PMs clear ownership of things they can actually control. This is a structural solution to a culture problem: PMs who are measured on output metrics spend their time on fire drills; PMs who own meaningful input metrics spend their time on the work that actually matters.

**Petra Wille's community of practice model** addresses the cost constraint in PM development: formal training programs and external coaches are expensive and time-limited; peer communities generate continuous, contextual learning at near-zero marginal cost. Internal PM communities—regular forums for sharing techniques, celebrating wins, and discussing hard problems—improve retention, reduce manager burden, and accelerate learning. Wille cites a consistent finding: people stay longer at companies where they're growing, and peer learning scales better than top-down programs.

**Brandon Chu's insight** about Shopify's culture cuts through debates about PM autonomy versus control: "Everyone at the company from engineers to support, to sales, to everyone's responsible for product thinking." When product thinking is democratized rather than hoarded, PMs become accountable for outcomes rather than gatekeepers of decisions. This changes what you hire for (judgment and facilitation, not domain monopoly) and what you develop for (raising the overall product IQ of the organization, not just the PM team).

**Molly Graham's principle** that high performers deserve more of a manager's time—not less—inverts the common practice. Most managers spend their energy on struggling employees while leaving high performers alone. The correct investment pattern is reversed: high performers unlock exponential impact when given bigger projects, less oversight, and more visibility. Low performers get clear expectations, specific feedback, and an honest timeline.

## Cross-Cutting Insights

- **Definition of good precedes everything.** You cannot hire for, measure, or coach toward a PM quality that you haven't defined. The organizations with the strongest PM cultures (Amazon, Stripe, Shopify) share a visible, specific definition of what great looks like—and interview and development processes that test for exactly that.

- **Urgency bias is the enemy of quality hiring.** The desire to fill a seat quickly produces mediocre hires reliably. Amazon's Bar Raiser program, Molly Graham's skepticism of rapid headcount growth, and Matt Mochary's argument for smaller teams all point the same direction: the cost of a wrong hire is much higher than the cost of a longer search.

- **Feedback must be specific, immediate, and observable.** Alisa Cohn's Observable Facts Framework, Kim Scott's CORE model (Context, Observation, Result, Next step), and Carole Robin's "behavior + feeling + because" formula all share the same structure. Vague feedback ("you need to be more strategic") is noise; specific, observable feedback ("in the last three reviews, your proposals haven't connected to the company's annual priorities—here's what that looks like") is signal.

- **Self-awareness is both the highest hiring signal and the hardest to assess.** Every guest in the Interview Q Compilation, and most guests in this chapter, identify self-awareness as the quality that most predicts PM success—and the quality most systematically missed by standard interview processes. The best questions design for authenticity: made-up scenarios, forward-looking reference questions, sibling perspectives.

- **Development requires following up, not just setting goals.** Petra Wille's repeated finding: the ingredient most missing in PM coaching is consistent follow-up. Development plans without accountability loops produce minimal change.

- **Career growth belongs to the PM, not the manager.** Ethan Evans' Magic Loop, Nikhyl Singhal's "Skip" framework, and Jackie Bavaro's "ask what to work on now" conversation all place career ownership on the individual. The manager's job is to create context and opportunity; growth happens through the PM's own initiative.

- **Storytelling is a PM career multiplier.** Petra Wille calls it a "career staller" when neglected; Ravi Mehta includes it under leadership competencies; the Interview Q Compilation features multiple guests testing for it independently. The ability to rally a team around a vision, make a boring-sounding problem feel existential, and represent multiple perspectives fairly is not a soft skill—it's a core PM deliverable.

- **The shadows of superpowers block the next promotion.** Nikhyl Singhal's framework applies across nearly all of the development stories in this chapter. The collaborative PM who can't say no; the execution-oriented PM who can't think strategically; the storytelling PM who skips the details—each is being held back by the shadow of their strength.

## Key Mental Models

**Bar Raiser Program** (Bill Carr / Amazon): A dedicated interviewer—not from the hiring team, with no direct stake in filling the seat—who runs the debrief and holds the calibration bar across all hires. Counters urgency bias, ensures consistent criteria, and makes the hiring manager's decision better rather than blocking it.

**Pre-Firing Conversation** (Alisa Cohn): Before any termination, have a crystal-clear "last chance" conversation with specific changes required, a concrete deadline, and an explicit consequence named. Makes firing fair (no surprise), often makes it unnecessary, and protects the manager from carrying ambiguity.

**Radical Candor 2x2** (Kim Scott): Four quadrants defined by two dimensions—Care Personally and Challenge Directly. The goal is the upper right: both caring and challenging. The most common failure mode is Ruinous Empathy: caring without challenging. The framework gives managers a compass, not a judgment; it identifies which axis to move on when feedback lands poorly.

**Shadows of Superpowers** (Nikhyl Singhal): Every PM strength creates a corresponding blindspot at the next level. The most powerful development insight is identifying what your superpower is hiding—in its shadow is the real development area blocking your next promotion.

**PMwheel / 12 PM Competencies** (Petra Wille / Ravi Mehta): Diagnostic frameworks that give development conversations specific vocabulary. Rather than "you need to be more strategic," these frameworks allow precise identification of which capability gap is creating which pattern of behavior—and enable targeted coaching rather than vague feedback.

**Waterline Model** (Molly Graham): When diagnosing team or individual problems, start at the surface (structural—goals, roles, expectations) before diving into dynamics, interpersonal, or intrapersonal issues. Most performance problems are structural; most managers diagnose them as personal.

**Five Ingredients of PM Coaching** (Petra Wille): Compass (definition of good) → Current state assessment → Shared vision → PM-owned development plan → Consistent follow-up. All five are required; most coaching efforts stop at two or three.

**Practice Before Promotion** (Christian Idiodi): The antidote to Peter Principle failures. Actively create opportunities for PMs to practice next-level work *before* the promotion, when mistakes are recoverable and feedback is available.

**Exponential Feedback** (Ravi Mehta): Target coaching at root-cause competencies, not surface symptoms. Surface feedback is forgotten; capability-level feedback compounds.

**Eigenquestion Method** (Shishir Mehrotra): In hiring and in development, identify the one or two questions that unlock most of the answer space. In interviewing, questions that force authentic thinking about novel problems reveal more than behavioral questions that can be rehearsed.

## Chapter Takeaways

- **Build your definition of "great PM" before you open a requisition.** Use a framework like Ravi Mehta's 12 competencies or Petra Wille's PMwheel as a starting point, then customize to your context. Interviewers cannot calibrate consistently—nor can managers develop effectively—without a shared, specific compass.

- **Redesign your interview process to defeat rehearsed answers.** Add at least one question that cannot be prepped for: a novel scenario (teleportation device), a perspective question (what would your siblings say?), or an opinion question (what conventional PM wisdom is wrong?). And make reference checks rigorous—ask the forward-looking performance review question and follow up with off-list references.

- **Treat the first 90 days as a structured investment, not a trial period.** Give new PMs a clear onboarding plan: context-building conversations with 40-50 people across levels and functions, explicit definition of their role and first-quarter success criteria, and a check-in cadence that detects structural misalignments before they become performance issues.

- **Shift your development time toward your best people.** Molly Graham's principle: investing in high performers yields 10x returns; investing in struggling performers yields diminishing ones. Give your best PMs bigger projects, less oversight, and more visibility—run experiments to test their ceiling rather than assuming it.

- **Build development plans that the PM owns, and follow up every week or month.** Petra Wille's most consistent finding: the missing ingredient is follow-up, not the plan itself. Identify 1-2 focus areas per quarter, make the PM co-author their development plan, and check in with small, consistent nudges rather than annual reviews.

- **Have the hard conversation early, with specifics.** Alisa Cohn's observable facts framework and Kim Scott's radical candor model converge: immediate, specific feedback based on observed behavior is kinder than avoidance. If you find yourself "hoping it works out," you haven't been clear enough. Name the expected change, the deadline, and the consequence.

- **Create conditions for PMs to practice leadership before promotion.** The most preventable PM failure mode is promoting into incompetence. Create visible opportunities for stretch projects, coaching moments, and next-level decision-making while the PM is still in a safe context—not after the title has changed and the stakes are real.
