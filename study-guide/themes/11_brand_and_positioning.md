# Chapter 11: Brand & Positioning

## Overview

Most product teams treat brand and positioning as downstream problems — things marketing will figure out after the product ships. The guests in this chapter argue, consistently and sometimes urgently, that this sequencing is backwards. Positioning determines which competitive frame customers use to evaluate you. Brand determines whether customers trust you enough to try you and stay. Naming shapes whether your product can be remembered, recommended, and searched for before a single feature is built. All three operate upstream of product development, not downstream of it.

The guests here come from very different perspectives: April Dunford is a positioning practitioner who has repositioned over sixteen companies. Christopher Lochhead argues that positioning is insufficient and category design is what actually creates market leaders. Geoffrey Moore's framework explains why even perfectly positioned products fail without a deliberate adoption strategy. Arielle Jackson breaks brand into three concrete, actionable components. David Placek has created over 4,000 brand names and has the cognitive science to explain what makes names work. Seth Godin brings a marketing philosopher's lens that asks whether your product creates genuine customer loyalty or just awareness.

What emerges from reading across these guests is not a single unified theory but a layered understanding. Positioning is the foundation — the choice about which competitive context makes your product's strengths most obvious. Brand is what gets built on that foundation over time — the promise you make and keep. Naming is the entry point — the first impression that either invites curiosity or creates confusion. Category design is the most ambitious move — redefining the problem so that your solution becomes the obvious answer before the evaluation begins. Each operates at a different time horizon and requires different work.

## What Positioning Actually Is (and Isn't)

April Dunford's contribution to this conversation is perhaps the most practically important: she has given the field of positioning a rigorous process where there was previously only intuition and judgment. Her definition is precise: positioning is how your product is the best in the world at delivering a specific kind of value to a well-defined set of customers. The "best in the world" is relative — relative to the competitive context you choose to compete in.

The competitive context is the pivotal lever. The same product can be positioned multiple ways, and the right answer depends on which frame makes your strengths most obvious and your weaknesses least relevant. Dunford's Help Scout example is canonical: Help Scout had customers who loved that the product felt like email, human and warm rather than robotic. When Help Scout was positioned against Zendesk, it looked like a toy — fewer features, less enterprise capability, lower price. When positioned against "chaotic shared email inboxes" — Gmail with four people sharing one account — every single Help Scout feature looked remarkable. Same product, opposite perception, same differentiated capabilities. Only the competitive frame changed.

This insight — that you are always positioned, and the question is whether you chose the position or let the market choose for you — is one of the most important in this chapter. When companies don't do explicit positioning work, customers position them using whatever frame of reference the product most resembles. That frame may be wildly disadvantageous, and the company will never know, because "why are you struggling in sales?" yields answers that point to execution rather than positioning.

Dunford identifies five inputs that define a complete positioning: the competitive alternative (what customers would do if you didn't exist, not just your direct competitors), your differentiated capabilities (what you genuinely do better), the value those capabilities create (business outcomes, not feature descriptions), the best-fit customers (who values those outcomes most), and the market category (the frame of reference that makes your value obvious). Weak positioning typically traces back to getting one of these inputs wrong — most often, treating direct competitors as the competitive alternative when the real comparison is to manual processes, Excel, or doing nothing.

## The Hierarchy: Positioning, Messaging, and Brand

One of the most useful clarifications in this chapter is Dunford's insistence on the distinction between positioning, messaging, and branding. They are not synonyms and they are not interchangeable, and confusing them produces costly mistakes.

Positioning is the strategic foundation: the choices about competitive context, differentiated capabilities, and best-fit customers. It is not customer-facing; it is the internal logic that drives everything external.

Messaging is the externalization of positioning: the specific language, claims, and narratives used to communicate with customers. A positioning that says "we win against manual processes because we automate in half the time" becomes many different messages for different audiences, different channels, and different stages of the buying journey.

Brand, in Arielle Jackson's framework, is the set of beliefs people hold about your company: what you stand for (Purpose), the space you occupy in their minds (Positioning), and how you show up in the world (Personality). Brand is not a logo or a color palette — those are outputs of brand strategy, not brand itself. As Jackson puts it: "Your brand is what people think you are. Developing a brand strategy is figuring out what you want people to think you are — and what you're going to do to help shape that perception."

The hierarchy matters because the failure mode of each layer is different. Weak positioning cannot be fixed with better messaging. Weak messaging cannot be fixed with a rebrand. Many companies spend enormous energy on logo redesigns and website copy when the actual problem is that no one has answered the positioning question: what competitive context makes our strengths most obvious?

Seth Godin's contribution is to push the brand question to its most fundamental form: would customers miss you if you were gone? Would they pay more to stay with you? If not, you have a logo, not a brand. The test is not awareness but emotional commitment — the willingness to choose you even when a comparably priced alternative exists. Nike has a brand; if Nike opened a hotel, we'd know what it would feel like. Hyatt does not have a brand in the same sense; if Hyatt made sneakers, we'd have no idea what to expect.

## Category Design: The Most Ambitious Play

Christopher Lochhead's category design framework is both the most ambitious and the most frequently misunderstood idea in this chapter. His central claim: legendary companies don't compete in existing markets, they create new ones. In most technology categories, one company earns approximately 76% of total market value; the only path to that position is to design a new category rather than fight for the remaining 24%.

The mechanism is linguistic before it is economic. When you introduce a new category name, you are introducing a new problem frame — a new way of understanding what the customer's actual challenge is. Starbucks didn't create better coffee; it created "the third place" between home and work. Salesforce didn't create a better CRM; it created "cloud software" and positioned installed software as the problem. OpenAI didn't create a better search engine; it invented "large language models" and positioned everything that came before as doing something fundamentally different.

Lochhead's "Frame, Name, Claim" process makes this concrete: first, reframe the problem (what is the old way of solving this, and what is inadequate about it?); second, give the new category a name (language is the mechanism through which new categories become legible); third, claim the category leadership position (become the evangelist, educator, and de facto standard for the new way). The category claim must happen through a "Point of View" document — not about your product, but about your customer's world: here's the problem, here's why old solutions fail, here's the new way.

The tension with April Dunford is instructive. Dunford is skeptical of category creation as a primary strategy for most B2B companies. Her critique: most companies cited as category creators didn't actually create the category first — they competed in an existing category, won a beachhead segment, and then redefined the category from a position of strength. Salesforce was a better CRM before it was the "cloud software" company. HubSpot was inbound marketing software before it was the "inbound" category leader. Category creation, in Dunford's view, is usually a post-hoc story — earned through winning, not declared at launch. Creating a category before you've won requires massive market education spending that most companies can't afford.

Both are right — about different companies at different stages. Lochhead's framework applies most powerfully to companies with genuine breakthrough technology that doesn't fit any existing category, or to companies with resources to invest in education. Dunford's bowling pin model applies to the vast majority: win a specific beachhead in an existing category, dominate it, and then potentially redefine the category once you have proof, case studies, and market presence.

## Naming: The Cognitive Science of First Impressions

David Placek has created over 4,000 brand names in forty years — including Powerbook, Pentium, Blackberry, Swiffer, Sonos, and Azure — and his insights about naming are backed by serious cognitive science. The central counterintuitive finding: great names create initial discomfort, and comfort means you don't have the name yet.

The reason is linguistic: comfort in naming means familiarity, and familiarity means the name sounds like everything else in the category. A name that makes a team immediately agree is probably descriptive or generic — it tells people exactly what you do, but it creates no cognitive anchor, no memorability, no "asymmetric advantage" that compounds with repetition.

Placek's concept of asymmetric and cumulative advantage captures how names work economically. An asymmetric advantage means you enter the market with an inherent edge over names that sound like "Cloud Pro" or "DataSync." A cumulative advantage means that edge compounds every time the name is used — each repetition strengthens the brand association. Descriptive names get neither; they start at parity and stay there. Distinctive names start with an edge and build.

The cognitive science behind this involves what Placek calls "processing fluency" — the brain prefers information that is easy to process, but it also pays more attention to information that is slightly unusual. The sweet spot is names built from familiar components arranged in new ways: Vercel combines "ver" (version) and "cel" (cell/excellence), both recognizable, combined in a form that's new. Sonos suggests sound and something more. Azure evokes the sky without being "Sky Platform."

Arielle Jackson's naming framework approaches the same territory from a brand strategist's angle. She identifies four types of names along a spectrum from descriptive to empty vessel: descriptive (tells you exactly what it does), suggestive (hints at meaning without spelling it out), evocative (has emotional resonance; the name makes sense once you know what the company does), and fanciful (no inherent meaning; requires marketing investment to build association). Her own preference is suggestive or evocative — names that do some of your marketing work without requiring a blank slate.

Both Placek and Jackson agree on one counterintuitive point: a bad name won't kill a good company, but a good name does real work. Disney, Volvo, and Nike were all "bad" names by conventional naming criteria when they launched. They succeeded because the companies behind them were remarkable enough to invest the names with meaning. But the right name creates a compounding advantage over time that a generic or confusing name never provides.

## The Sales Pitch and Overcoming Buyer Inertia

April Dunford's second episode focuses on a specific and underappreciated problem: in most B2B sales cycles, the biggest competitor is not another product — it is no decision. Between 40 and 60 percent of B2B pipeline ends with prospects choosing to stay with whatever they're doing rather than buying anything new.

This insight reframes what a pitch must accomplish. A pitch designed only to beat competitors misses the majority of deals lost. The pitch must also make the cost of staying with the status quo visible and, critically, reduce the perceived risk of change.

Dunford's two-part pitch structure does this deliberately. The Setup (insight, current alternatives and their trade-offs, "perfect world" statement) establishes the problem frame before the product is introduced. The Follow-through (product introduction, differentiated value mapped to alternative failures, proof, ask) delivers the solution within that frame. The strategic move in the Setup is getting the buyer to agree on the criteria for success before seeing the product — transforming "do you believe our claims?" into "does this product meet criteria you already agreed to?"

Matt Dixon's JOLT Effect research (which Dunford cites) identifies why the conventional response to buyer inertia — creating urgency through FOMO — makes the problem worse. FOMO reminds buyers that they might make the wrong choice, which increases paralysis rather than reducing it. The correct medicine for buyer inertia is JOLT: Judge the indecision (acknowledge they're stuck), Offer your recommendation (don't make them decide alone), Limit the exploration (help them stop researching), Take risk off the table (trial, phased rollout, return policy).

Andy Raskin's strategic narrative framework approaches the same problem from the top of the funnel. His "old game / new game" structure does what the Setup phase of Dunford's pitch does: it establishes a shift in the world that makes the status quo not just suboptimal but dangerous. The difference from Dunford's framework is emphasis: Raskin's approach is ideal for creating category-level demand (making prospects realize they have a problem they hadn't named), while Dunford's approach is better for converting prospects who are already aware of the problem but comparing multiple solutions. Both are correct for their respective moments.

## Brand Personality and the "We Are X, But Not Y" Test

One of the most practically useful tools in this chapter comes from Arielle Jackson's application of Jennifer Aker's five brand personality dimensions. Aker's research found that all major brands cluster around five dimensions: Sincerity (honest, down-to-earth), Excitement (spirited, daring), Competence (reliable, intelligent), Sophistication (charming, upper-class), and Ruggedness (outdoorsy, tough). Strong brands spike in exactly two of these dimensions. Most tech companies default to Sincerity + Competence. Apple adds Sophistication. Mountain Dew uses Ruggedness + Excitement.

The diagnostic power of this framework is in revealing personality drift: companies that try to be all things — helpful AND exciting AND competent AND sophisticated AND rugged — communicate nothing distinctive. The visual and copy expression of "we are great at everything" blurs into category noise.

Jackson's "We Are X, But Not Y" format is the implementation tool. Instead of listing five positive attributes that don't constrain anything ("helpful, reliable, smart, innovative, trusted"), brands define tension statements: "We are playful, but not silly." "We are confident, but not arrogant." "We are daring, but not stupid." The Y in each statement is where X goes wrong — taking the positive attribute to an extreme that most companies accidentally embody. These five statements then directly constrain every creative decision: copy tone, photography style, illustration direction, ad creative, and voice guidelines.

Seth Godin's framing of brand personality is more philosophical but arrives at a similar place. A brand is a promise — and a promise has to be specific enough to be kept or broken. Vague promises ("we're innovative") can never be broken and therefore never create trust. The most powerful brands make specific, testable promises that customers can verify in their actual experience. When those promises are kept consistently, trust accumulates. When they're broken, the brand erodes faster than any marketing investment can repair.

## Communication and Distribution: Getting the Word Out

Having a great positioning, compelling narrative, and distinctive brand name accomplishes nothing if the message doesn't reach the people it's meant for. Lulu Cheng Meservey, Emily Kramer, Camille Ricketts, and Jason Feifer each contribute a different angle on this distribution problem.

Meservey's "pressure = force / surface area" principle from physics is the most clarifying. With limited marketing resources (force), the only way to achieve impact (pressure) is to reduce the surface area — narrow the target audience. Trying to reach everyone simultaneously requires spreading force so thin that no one receives enough to move. The math demands focus. Notion's early community strategy exemplifies this: instead of mass marketing, they cultivated a small group of power users who became the loudest advocates for the product. The force concentrated on 100 ideal people created more pressure than the same effort spread across 100,000 marginal ones.

Meservey's "cultural erogenous zones" concept addresses message design. Rather than trying to change what your audience cares about — an enormously high-friction effort — find the bridge between what they already care about and what you need to say. Kamala Harris connected K-12 education to national defense via army enlistment requirements; it's much easier to reach people at their existing passions than to create new ones.

Jason Feifer's PR framework is built on one foundational insight: journalists don't care about you, they care about their readers. Every press pitch that leads with "our company does X" is wrong. The pitch that works leads with "here's what your readers will find fascinating, and our company happens to illustrate it." The context creation strategy he describes — creating surveys, reports, or data that journalists can reference — means you become a source even when your product isn't the story.

Emily Kramer's fuel vs. engine framework for marketing clarifies a common startup confusion. Fuel is everything you create (content, positioning, design, messaging). Engine is how you get fuel to the right people (distribution channels, SEO, paid, outbound). Many startups have fuel without engine (great content no one sees) or engine without fuel (distribution infrastructure but nothing worth distributing). Diagnosing which you're missing before hiring changes everything about what to hire for.

Camille Ricketts's community-led growth framework adds a dimension the other guests don't address: products that create "atomic units of sharing" — things users make that exhibit something about themselves (Notion templates, Figma designs, Canva creations) — have a structural advantage in distribution. The content users create markets the product every time it's shared. This is the difference between distribution you buy and distribution that accrues naturally from product use.

## The Timing of Brand Investment

A recurring question across these episodes is: when should an early-stage company invest in brand? The answers converge on a nuanced answer: positioning must come early (it saves downstream work), brand personality can come early and inexpensively (it's mostly a series of choices), naming should come early (it compounds), and heavy brand investment (advertising, campaigns) should wait until there's something to amplify.

Arielle Jackson's argument for early positioning is the most persuasive. Once positioning is clear, the website almost writes itself, every campaign decision has a test ("does this reinforce our positioning?"), and internal alignment dramatically improves. The hour invested in positioning saves weeks of copy rewrites, misaligned campaigns, and confused sales pitches. Positioning is not a scale-up problem; it is the foundation that makes everything at scale cheaper and faster.

April Dunford's "tuna net" advice for early-stage companies adds appropriate nuance: don't tighten positioning too early. In the earliest stages, before you have pattern recognition from actual customers about who buys and why they win, constraining your positioning kills discovery. Let the market teach you first; then tighten based on evidence, not assumptions.

Seth Godin's observation that most companies confuse brand investment with marketing investment is important here. Brand is built through product decisions (Volvo giving away the seatbelt patent to own "safety"), customer experience decisions (how you handle support, what you say in error messages, how you price), and the consistency of your behavior over time. None of these require budget. What builds brand is choosing what promise you'll make and keeping it consistently — a series of decisions, not a campaign.

## Cross-Cutting Insights

- **You are always positioned — the only question is whether you chose the position**. Customers use the frame of reference that your product most resembles. If you haven't done explicit positioning work, that frame might be costing you deals you don't know you're losing.
- **Positioning is relative, not absolute**. The same product can be the worst in category A and the best in category B. The frame of reference is the lever. Choosing the frame is the most important marketing decision most product teams never make.
- **Naming is not cosmetic**. A name that is distinctive, pronounceable, and memorable does real economic work — it creates asymmetric and cumulative advantage that compounds over the life of the product. A generic or confusing name requires perpetual marketing investment to overcome.
- **The biggest B2B competitor is no decision, not the competition**. Pitches that only address feature comparison miss the majority of lost deals. The most effective pitches also make the cost of the status quo visible and reduce the perceived risk of changing.
- **Brand is built through product decisions, not marketing spend**. What companies do — how they treat customers, what promises they make, whether they keep them — builds brand more durably than any campaign. Advertising amplifies brand; it doesn't create it.
- **Personality needs tension to be useful**. Five positive attributes that say the same thing communicate nothing. "We are X but not Y" — where Y represents the excess version of X — creates the creative constraints that make brand expressions distinctive and consistent.
- **Focus creates more pressure than breadth**. The physics metaphor is exact: narrowing your target audience allows the same marketing force to create more impact. The pressure that creates advocacy requires concentration.
- **Category creation is earned through winning, not declared at launch**. The companies remembered as category creators usually won a niche first. The category was named later, from a position of market presence, not before achieving it.

## Key Mental Models

**Five-Component Positioning** *(April Dunford)*: Competitive alternative → Differentiated capabilities → Value created by those capabilities → Best-fit customers → Market category that makes value obvious. Weak positioning traces back to getting one of these wrong.

**Brand = Purpose + Positioning + Personality** *(Arielle Jackson)*: Purpose (why you exist), Positioning (space you occupy in the customer's mind), Personality (how you show up in the world). All three feed into visual design and voice. Without them, a style guide is just "here are our colors."

**The Bar Test** *(Arielle Jackson)*: Test your positioning by saying it at a bar to someone in your target audience. If it uses words like "leverages" and "empowers" — nobody talks like that. If it can't survive spoken human conversation, it isn't finished.

**Frame, Name, Claim** *(Christopher Lochhead)*: Category creation requires reframing the problem (not just solving the existing one better), naming the new category, and claiming the leadership position through education and evangelism.

**Magic Triangle** *(Christopher Lochhead)*: Legendary companies align all three simultaneously — Product (what you build), Company (how you operate and position), and Category (the problem frame you define). Obsessing over product while neglecting category is how great products fail in markets.

**The Strategic Narrative** *(Andy Raskin)*: Name the shift (old game vs. new game) → Name the stakes (life-or-death framing) → Name the object of the new game (what winning looks like) → Name the obstacles (problems reframed as blockers to the goal) → Name your magic gifts (how you help). Product is a prop, not the protagonist.

**JOLT Effect** *(Matt Dixon, cited by Dunford)*: The cure for buyer indecision is Judge the indecision, Offer your recommendation, Limit the exploration, Take risk off the table. Not FOMO — which increases paralysis.

**The Sales Pitch Setup** *(April Dunford)*: Get agreement on what "perfect" looks like before introducing your product. The buyer who helped define the criteria will apply them honestly — and your product is designed to meet them.

**Aker's Five Brand Personality Dimensions** *(Jennifer Aker, applied by Jackson)*: Sincerity, Excitement, Competence, Sophistication, Ruggedness. Strong brands spike in exactly two. Most tech companies occupy Sincerity + Competence by default.

**Concentric Circles Communication** *(Lulu Meservey)*: Spread messages outward: yourself → co-founders → employees → investors → power users → broader audiences. Each assumes the inner circle knows better. You can't skip levels without losing credibility.

**Technology Adoption Lifecycle** *(Geoffrey Moore)*: Early Market → Bowling Alley/Chasm → Tornado → Main Street. The playbook for positioning and messaging in each phase is different; early market messaging (vision, belief) actively confuses pragmatist buyers.

## Chapter Takeaways

- **Do your positioning work before writing copy**: Answer the five questions (competitive alternative, differentiated capabilities, value, best-fit customers, market category) before touching the website, sales deck, or product narrative. The hour of positioning work will save weeks of confused messaging downstream.

- **Run the bar test on everything**: Say your positioning out loud to someone in your target audience. If it can't survive human conversation — if you'd never say it to a friend — it isn't finished. Jargon is positioning work that hasn't been completed.

- **Use "We are X, but not Y" to define brand personality**: Pick two of Aker's five personality dimensions. For each, write a tension statement that takes the positive attribute to its dangerous extreme. Five of these statements are more useful than any brand guidelines document.

- **Match your pitch to the actual source of lost deals**: If you're losing most deals to "no decision" rather than to a specific competitor, your pitch needs to address the cost of the status quo and reduce perceived change risk — not just demonstrate feature superiority.

- **Don't tighten positioning before you have customer pattern recognition**: Early-stage, fish with a net. Let the market show you which customers win and why before constraining your positioning to a specific segment. Tighten based on evidence, then dominate the beachhead before expanding.

- **Treat naming as a strategic investment, not a cosmetic decision**: The right name creates asymmetric and cumulative advantage that compounds over the life of the product. Look for polarization in your team's reaction (tension signals energy), prioritize distinctiveness over descriptiveness, and test for sound — names that are hard to say on a phone call create friction forever.

- **Build distribution into the product, not just the marketing plan**: Ask whether your product creates "atomic units of sharing" — things users make that they want to share because they reflect something about who they are. Products with this property accrue word-of-mouth; products without it must buy it.
