# Ethan Evans — Former Amazon VP on Career Growth, Leadership, and The Magic Loop

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Ethan Evans
- **Role/Company**: Former Vice President at Amazon; Executive Coach and Course Creator
- **Background**: Spent 15 years at Amazon where he helped invent and run Prime Video, Amazon Appstore, Prime Gaming, and Twitch Commerce (a billion-dollar business). Led global teams of over 800 people, helped draft one of Amazon's 14 core leadership principles (Ownership), and holds over 70 patents. ## TL;DR
Ethan Evans shares The Magic Loop—a five-step framework for career growth that works even with difficult managers: do your job well, ask how you can help, do what's asked, align your goals with your manager's needs, and repeat. He also reveals how he survived publicly failing Jeff Bezos on a launch, re-earned trust, and was promoted to VP two years later, emphasizing that ownership, rapid communication, and face-to-face accountability are crucial for recovering from failures.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Magic Loop (5-Step Career Growth Framework)**: 
  1. Do your current job well—ensure your manager sees you as a solid performer
  2. Ask your manager how you can help them
  3. Do whatever they ask, even if it's not exciting work
  4. Go back and ask: "How can I help you in a way that also helps me reach my goal?" (requires knowing your goal)
  5. Repeat—build trust and keep the cycle going
  
  This works because managers help those who help them, and it puts career growth in your control rather than waiting for a perfect manager.

- **Advanced Mode—Proactive Ownership**: As you build trust, evolve from asking "How can I help?" to suggesting "I see these things need doing, should I handle them?" to eventually just doing what needs to be done and keeping leadership informed. This progression demonstrates increasing leadership capability.

- **Fear the New York Times Headline**: When making decisions at scale, ask yourself if this action could generate negative press coverage. This mental model helps balance speed with appropriate risk management, especially for public-facing companies.

- **Systematic Invention**: Invention isn't magic—it requires (1) expertise in your domain, (2) dedicated thinking time (just 2 hours/month), and (3) combining two existing things in novel ways. Example: aircraft carrier + delivery trucks = drone-delivery trucks. One good idea can sustain years of work and reputation.

- **Leaders Are Right A Lot (with humility)**: Amazon's principle evolved to emphasize that great leaders "actively work to disconfirm their beliefs" and "seek diverse perspectives"—being right requires constantly questioning yourself and gathering input from different viewpoints.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Career growth is in your control**: Most managers have good intentions but are busy with their own work and career. The Magic Loop works because it doesn't require a perfect manager—you drive the partnership by offering help first, then aligning your goals with organizational needs.

2. **Express your goals explicitly**: Managers often assume you either want to keep doing what you're doing forever or that you want to become a manager like them. Clarifying your actual goals removes ambiguity and allows your manager to help you get there.

3. **The senior manager trap requires behavioral change**: Getting stuck at senior manager is common because (1) there's a structural bottleneck (fewer director roles) and (2) the job requires different skills—less execution focus, more influence, coordination, and strategic thinking. What got you to senior manager won't get you to director.

4. **Recovering from failure requires ownership and communication**: When Ethan failed Jeff Bezos on a public launch: (1) He owned the problem immediately, (2) He provided hourly updates with clear next steps, buying trust one hour at a time, (3) He met with Jeff in person where anger is harder to maintain, (4) He worked relentlessly to fix it, (5) He learned the lesson and changed his approach. Two years later, he was promoted to VP.

5. **Speed matters, but balance it with appropriate risk assessment**: Bias for action is crucial in competitive environments—being right is good, but being quick is necessary. However, at scale, combine speed with asking "could this generate a New York Times headline?" to avoid reckless gambling with reputation.

6. **Invention compounds over time**: You don't need many good ideas to be seen as tremendously inventive. Prime shipping, Kindle, Tesla—these single ideas have been optimized for decades. Most innovation is in the incremental optimization and expression of core ideas, not generating endless new concepts.

7. **An owner never says "that's not my job"**: Ethan helped write Amazon's Ownership principle with this phrase. Taking responsibility beyond your defined role, seeing problems and solving them proactively, and treating the company like you own it are hallmarks of executive-level leadership.

8. **Interview success is more about presence than content**: The top factors in interviews are appearance and enthusiasm. Show up looking professional, demonstrate energy and excitement, make eye contact through the camera, use body language, and be fully present. Then articulate impact, not just tasks—show how your work solved problems and changed outcomes.

9. **Be the person chosen out of eight**: When there are eight senior managers but only room for one director promotion, differentiate yourself by practicing next-level skills now—take on strategic projects, become more inventive, demonstrate broader thinking, and show you can already do aspects of the role.

10. **Remote work has more improvement potential than offices**: Offices have been optimized for 300 years since the first purpose-built office in 1726. Remote work has only been seriously practiced for a few years. The bigger opportunity for innovation and improvement lies in perfecting remote collaboration, not returning to offices.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- Lead with enthusiasm and appearance—these matter more than most candidates realize. Show up with energy, maintain eye contact through the camera, use gestures, and demonstrate you genuinely want the role and company.

- Articulate impact, not just activities—don't just describe what you did, explain why it mattered and how it changed outcomes. Leaders hire for problem-solving and business impact, not task completion.

- Prepare stories about disagreeing with management—senior roles require backbone. Have examples ready of when you had to stand up to leadership or fight for a position against people in power.

- Practice "tell me about a time you failed" deeply—be ready to show ownership, explain your recovery process, articulate lessons learned, and demonstrate how you changed behavior afterward.

- When asked about your goals, be specific—vague aspirations don't help interviewers see how to help you or where you fit. Know what you want (promotion, role change, new skills) and articulate it clearly.

- Show systematic thinking, not just intuition—whether discussing invention, problem-solving, or strategy, demonstrate you have processes and frameworks for approaching challenges, not just gut instinct.
