## TL;DR
SEO should be treated as a product discipline owned by product managers, not just a marketing tactic—success requires understanding the user's self-discovery journey and creating the right assets for each stage of their search intent. AI Overviews are fundamentally changing top-of-funnel SEO by absorbing generic informational queries, but mid- and bottom-funnel searches remain valuable opportunities if you focus on conversions rather than rankings.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **SEO as a Product**: Think of SEO as a product discipline, not a marketing tactic. Product managers should lead SEO strategy because it's fundamentally about building the right product for users in self-discovery mode.

- **Three-Step SEO Framework**:
  1. **Be the user**: Understand what problem they're searching for and their intent
  2. **Decide what asset to create**: Determine the right content or product page for that user need
  3. **Build the product**: Use proper resources (design, engineering, product management) to build for the SEO user

- **User Journey Stages**: Map SEO strategy to three stages—top-of-funnel (discovery), mid-funnel (narrowing options), and bottom-of-funnel (conversion). Each stage requires different content and optimization approaches.

- **Top-Down SEO Forecasting**: Instead of unreliable keyword tool estimates, forecast by starting with total addressable population, segment by demographics (gender, age), filter by online buyer percentage, estimate realistic market penetration goal, and multiply by average order value.

- **Link Building is Brand Building**: Building links through low-quality guest posts doesn't work. The right way to build links is to build a brand—mentions are becoming as valuable as HTML links now that Google can read and understand content context.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **AI Overviews change the game for top-of-funnel searches**: Generic informational content that used to rank first is now being absorbed by AI Overviews. Sites like WebMD providing general health information will be most impacted because Google can synthesize that information directly. However, mid-funnel and bottom-funnel queries (where users are narrowing options or ready to convert) remain valuable SEO opportunities.

2. **Most B2B SaaS companies should not invest heavily in SEO**: When the buyer journey requires sales involvement, committee decisions, and complex negotiations (like enterprise software purchases), there's no direct conversion path from search. Companies like Google Cloud shouldn't prioritize SEO because purchases require extensive sales processes, not self-service discovery.

3. **Traffic is a worthless metric—focus on conversions**: SEO is not about rankings or traffic volume; it should be about conversions that matter to your business model. The right metric depends on context: page views for media companies, MQLs for SaaS, transactions for e-commerce. Many companies use the wrong conversion metric, focusing on rankings instead of business outcomes.

4. **Understand what users are actually searching for**: Successful SEO requires deeply understanding the user's self-discovery journey. Zapier succeeded by creating pages for integrations between tools people already knew (like "Gmail Salesforce integration"), not by explaining what Zapier was. Tinder won by recognizing they solve a loneliness problem in local contexts, creating pages for "online dating in [city]."

5. **SEO is not free**: There are real costs in time, resources, and direct expenses that need to be weighed against other marketing channels. Ask whether investing a million dollars in SEO will return that investment faster than brand ads, influencer campaigns, or traditional paid marketing.

6. **Technical SEO importance scales with site size**: Technical SEO is less critical for small websites (100 pages) but becomes essential for large sites with tens of millions of pages like Zillow or Airbnb. Many technical SEO audits are misused to solve problems that actually stem from creating non-useful content.

7. **Programmatic SEO works only with scale and user use cases**: Creating data-driven pages at scale (like TripAdvisor's location pages or Zillow's property listings) works when there's genuine user demand for that content. Choose programmatic content over editorial content based on what users actually need, not just to generate pages.

8. **AI content isn't inherently problematic—unhelpful content is**: According to Google's documentation, using AI to generate content isn't the issue; it's whether the content is helpful and useful. Using AI to generate entire blog posts just for content's sake is wasteful, but using AI for product descriptions or genuinely helpful content is fine.

9. **Missing execution milestones is often why SEO fails**: The strategy may be sound, but failure to execute on specific milestones (like actually building the pages, getting engineering resources, or launching on time) is often the real reason SEO doesn't succeed.

10. **Google faces monetization and liability challenges with AI Overviews**: Google generates revenue from ads, but AI answers and ads can't coexist well on the same page. Additionally, Google has liability concerns—by providing direct answers through AI, they become the publisher rather than just pointing to other sources.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

Product managers need to rethink how users discover products in the AI era. Instead of users landing directly on your content from search, AI Overviews now provide initial direction, and users search deeper from there. The traditional journey of ranking for "best beach hotels in Miami" is changing—Google's AI answer starts users off, then they search more specifically based on that guidance.

Product managers should lead SEO strategy because it's fundamentally a product question: how do you position your product to users doing self-discovery through search? This requires product thinking about user needs, not just marketing tactics.

AI Overviews are particularly impacting top-of-funnel discovery queries. PMs should focus on mid-funnel (users narrowing options) and bottom-funnel (conversion-ready) opportunities where AI can't complete the entire user journey.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

**How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:**

- **Demonstrate user journey thinking**: When discussing growth or acquisition strategies, show you understand different stages of the user journey (discovery, consideration, conversion) and that different strategies work for each stage. Don't just talk about "getting users"—show you know where they are in their decision-making process.

- **Focus on business outcomes over vanity metrics**: If asked about success metrics for a growth initiative, avoid surface-level answers like "traffic" or "rankings." Connect metrics to actual business value: conversions, revenue, qualified leads. Show you understand that activities should drive business outcomes, not just activity metrics.

- **Apply product thinking to marketing problems**: If given a case about user acquisition or SEO, treat it as a product problem. Ask: What user problem are we solving? What's the user's intent? What asset should we build for them? This demonstrates cross-functional thinking and that you see growth as part of the product, not separate from it.

- **Question whether a strategy fits the business model**: When evaluating growth channels, demonstrate critical thinking about whether a channel actually makes sense for the business. For example, recognize that enterprise B2B with complex sales cycles shouldn't over-invest in self-service discovery channels. Show you can say "we shouldn't do X" with clear reasoning.

- **Use real examples from known companies**: Reference specific examples like Zapier's integration pages, Tinder's local dating pages, or Quora's HTML sitemaps to show you've studied how successful companies approach growth and product strategy. This demonstrates learning from the market and pattern recognition.
