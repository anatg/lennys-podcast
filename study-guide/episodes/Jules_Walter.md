## TL;DR
Jules Walter shares his systematic approach to developing PM skills by focusing on one skill at a time for 3-6 months, working backwards from concrete outcomes, and leveraging mentorship. He emphasizes that IQ skills (execution, product sense, strategy) unlock early career progression, while EQ skills (communication, leadership, self-awareness) become critical at senior levels—and the EQ skills take years of deliberate practice to develop.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models
- **IQ vs. EQ Skills Progression**: Early career success requires mastering "hard skills" (execution, product sense, strategy, interviewing). Senior-level success demands EQ skills (communication, leadership, management, self-awareness). The IQ skills can be learned in months; EQ skills require years of continuous practice.

- **Outcome-Driven Learning**: Set a concrete 3-6 month goal with measurable outcome (e.g., "ship experiments that improve activation by X%"). Work backwards to identify what you need to learn. Read minimally to understand the domain, then find experts to mentor you. Execute, measure results, return to mentors with learnings. Repeat.

- **Mentor Discovery via Smallest Ask**: Don't ask "can we meet?" Ask a highly specific question answerable in 2 minutes via email/tweet. When advice proves useful, circle back showing you applied it and results achieved. Over time, relationship deepens organically—you never explicitly ask someone to "be your mentor."

- **Strength Identification**: Your true strengths are things others consistently praise but you dismiss as "not a big deal" or "doesn't everyone do this?" Example: Jules dismissed his questioning skills as "basic questions" until mentors showed him it was a differentiator. The fish analogy: a fish doesn't realize swimming is special.

- **Strength-Weakness Duality**: Strengths and weaknesses aren't opposites—they're the same trait at different "dial settings." Jules's ability to simplify complex problems means he stays quiet in meetings while building mental models, which can appear as disengagement. Self-awareness lets you dial traits up/down contextually.

- **Deliberate Practice for Interviewing**: Most people do zero mock interviews before real ones. Jules recommends dozens of mocks, ideally with people skilled at interviewing. Interviewing is traumatic for underrepresented candidates (entering rooms where nobody looks like you while facing ambiguity and self-doubt), so over-practice until "your worst is good enough."

## Top Insights (Timeless)
1. **Joining world-class companies accelerates learning exponentially**: Being at Slack/Google surrounded Jules with exceptional artifacts (strategy docs, exec updates, presentations) he could reverse-engineer, plus peers he could shadow. The "logo on resume" also unlocks future opportunities—but you need strong interviewing skills to get in.

2. **Feedback is the unlock for EQ development**: EQ skills are harder to learn than IQ skills because people rarely give honest subjective feedback ("you come across as angry," "you sound junior when asking questions"). Build trust by enthusiastically receiving feedback externally (even if "heart is melting" internally), making people comfortable being brutally honest with you over time.

3. **Learning happens through iteration, not final products**: Don't just study finished strategy docs—ask to sit with a PM while they write the outline, see their drafts, understand what feedback they sought. Observing the "backstage" thought process teaches more than polished outputs.

4. **Focus on one skill at a time for 3-6 months**: Rather than dabbling, go all-in on a single skill—read one example/artifact per week for 20 weeks, practice it 1 hour/day toward your outcome goal. This depth gets you "over the hump" versus reading one article then returning to usual patterns.

5. **Mentorship scales through specificity**: Jules maintains relationships with multiple mentors (Bangaly Kaba for growth, Lawrence Ripsher for strengths/EQ, Aaron Teague for Google transition, Bradley Horowitz for leadership). Each serves a specific need. He always brings concrete problems ("I'm deciding between 3 job offers—can I walk through my thought process?") and follows up showing impact of advice.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
_How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:_
- **Do dozens of mock interviews, not zero**: Jules interviewed at Google many times over a decade before getting in. The difference-maker was deliberate practice with mocks. Find peers or ideally people who work at target companies to practice with—watching YouTube videos of questions isn't enough.
- **Treat interviewing as a learnable skill**: You won't get feedback after interviews, so you must proactively seek it through mocks and pattern-match what works. Interviewing is especially hard for underrepresented candidates facing added psychological stress, so over-prepare until you can perform under worst conditions.
- **Join communities for mock interview support**: Find 3-5 people also prepping and make practice fun/supportive (Jules mentioned Black PMs community does this). Repeated practice in a safe group builds confidence and surfaces blindspots before high-stakes real interviews.
- **Recognize that getting into top companies changes trajectory**: Access to world-class PMs, artifacts, and learning opportunities at Slack transformed Jules's career—but required passing rigorous interviews first. Investing heavily in interview skills unlocks compounding career benefits.
