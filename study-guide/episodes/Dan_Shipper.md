## TL;DR
Dan Shipper runs Every with just 15 people by being radically AI-first: engineers don't hand-write code anymore, they manage Claude Code agents; they have a dedicated "Head of AI Operations" who automates repetitive work across the company; and they've shipped four products, a daily newsletter, and a consulting business. The biggest predictor of whether a company successfully adopts AI is whether the CEO personally uses ChatGPT daily—leadership drives both excitement and realistic expectations.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Compounding Engineering**: For every unit of work, make the next unit easier. Example: Instead of writing PRDs repeatedly, create a prompt that converts rambling thoughts into proper PRDs. Each automation makes subsequent work faster, multiplying leverage over time (used by Every's two-person Cora engineering team managing 2,500+ active users).

- **The Allocation Economy Thesis**: As AI makes specialized knowledge cheaper, the economy shifts from rewarding knowledge workers to rewarding "allocators"—people with manager skills who can delegate to AI, evaluate output, set vision, judge quality, and decide when to dive into details. These skills (historically limited to ~8% of workforce) will become broadly distributed because managing AI is much cheaper than managing humans.

- **Don't Repeat Yourself Quarterly Goal**: Dan set a company-wide goal that no one should say the same thing twice in meetings. Forces codifying knowledge into prompts and automations that push taste/judgment to the edge of the organization, allowing writers/engineers to self-serve feedback without waiting for leadership bottlenecks.

- **The Leash Test for AGI**: Measure AI progress by how long you can leave it running autonomously before intervention. Started with tab-complete (seconds), then single responses (minutes), now agents work 20-30 minutes. AGI = when it's economically profitable to run agents indefinitely without supervision, like a teenager who can "live their life" productively while you focus elsewhere.

- **Sip Seed Fundraising**: Every raised $2M from Reid Hoffman and Starting Line VC, but structured as an on-demand safe (pull down capital as needed at set cap) rather than depositing everything upfront. Maintains psychological freedom to take risks, avoids pressure to burn, preserves optionality between venture scale and founder-friendly lifestyle business.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **CEO AI usage predicts company adoption**: The #1 predictor of whether a company successfully adopts AI is whether the CEO uses ChatGPT/Claude daily. If the CEO is "in it all the time," everyone follows; if CEO is ambivalent, no one leads the charge. CEOs who use it can both drive excitement and set realistic expectations. Tactic: Send an "AI-first company" memo noting "I wrote this with ChatGPT, and you should too."

2. **Hire a Head of AI Operations**: Have someone whose entire job is sitting with employees weekly, identifying repetitive tasks, and building prompts/automations so workers don't have to stop and build workflows themselves. Reduces friction of adoption. Example: Every's Katie Parrott, who built an editorial style prompt that Kate (editor-in-chief) can apply to any draft, and engineers can now run on code copy via Claude Code command.

3. **AI accelerates junior talent, not just senior**: Entry-level people with ChatGPT can progress a year's worth in two months by recording feedback as prompts and never making the same mistake twice. Example: Every's writer Alex Duffy went from "great ideas but not good writer yet" to shipping major projects because he captured all editing feedback in prompts. This suggests AI won't eliminate entry-level jobs—it will create supercharged juniors who learn manager + execution skills simultaneously.

4. **Context engineering is half the performance**: Getting the right context to the model at the right time is as important as model intelligence. Even frontier models perform poorly without proper context (Dan's CEO benchmark: predict what he'll say in meetings—models struggled despite being "smart"). This problem won't be solved by bigger context windows alone; it requires orchestration, memory systems, and knowing when to go "down a layer" into details.

5. **Software is becoming content**: We're entering an era where "software" won't just be conventional SaaS apps—it'll include lightweight AI skills (like Dia browser skills), custom GPTs, and agent-based tools that non-technical people can build and monetize. Similar to YouTube videos vs Hollywood movies: different formats, different economics, but both valuable. Every's incubation model: use ChatGPT/Claude for internal needs, unbundle what works into standalone products (Cora for email, Spiral for content).

6. **Generalists > Specialists in AI era**: Historically, civilization progressed through specialization and coordination (Adam Smith's pin factory). AI inverts this: you now have "10,000 PhDs in your pocket," so individuals can remain generalists and jump between domains (coding, writing, design, video) with AI handling specialized knowledge. This enables smaller orgs with higher-leverage generalists versus massive corps with narrow specialists.

7. **AI may reshore American jobs**: Cheap intelligence stimulates demand for expensive services (in-house counsel, customer service) that were previously only for big companies/rich people. It also allows small US teams to serve scale that previously required 100-person offshore call centers. Americans may be better at using AI tools + model companies are US-based, creating advantage. Contrarian take: AI could be a force for bringing jobs back to US rather than eliminating them.

8. **Management skills will become universal**: Today ~8% of workforce are managers; tomorrow everyone manages AI. Skills like delegation, evaluation, vision-setting, taste, knowing when to micromanage were historically expensive (required managing humans); now they're cheap (manage AI). This shifts what's valuable: not "can you do the task" but "can you allocate resources, judge quality, set direction."

9. **"GPT wrappers" are underrated and valuable**: Contrary to dismissive takes, wrapping frontier models with domain-specific UX, context, and workflows is extremely valuable. Every's products (Cora, Sparkle, Spiral) are essentially GPT wrappers that solve real needs by making general AI accessible for specific use cases. The "wrapper" is the entire product: UI, context engineering, integrations, trust.

10. **Find your unique company shape by following secret desires**: Dan tried to run Every like a "proper" startup (stopped writing to hire writers, raised little money to stay in control but felt constrained). When he returned to writing and leaned into weird desires (newsletter + apps + consulting bundle), business improved. The tax of doing what you "should" do versus what fits you is enormous. Finding examples of others who've done similar things (Joel Spolsky, Jason Fried, Sam Harris, Bill Simmons as writer-founders) gave permission to chart his own path.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Agents over interfaces**: Original AI apps pasted chat boxes into existing UIs (Copilot autocomplete, Cursor sidebar). Future is Claude Code-style: you never look at the "code," you delegate and it executes autonomously for 20-30 minutes. PMs will design for delegation, not for human-in-the-loop workflows.

- **Non-coders using terminal tools**: Claude Code (command-line interface with file system access, terminal commands, web browsing) is massively underrated for non-technical users. PMs can download meeting notes, customer interviews, or research and have Claude process everything in parallel over multiple turns, surfacing insights without stuffing into single context window.

- **Multiple agents, multiple model companies**: Teams use ensemble of agents (Claude Code, Friday, Charlie) because they have different "personalities" and strengths. Like hiring an Avengers team where each has unique skills. PMs will orchestrate multi-agent workflows rather than relying on single model.

- **Taste and judgment become core PM skills**: With AI writing code/copy, PM's job shifts to: Can you tell if output is good? Do you have vision for what "great" looks like? Can you give feedback that improves next iteration? These are manager skills now required of PMs managing AI rather than managing people.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate AI fluency with specifics**: Don't just say "I use AI." Share your tool stack (o3 for writing with memory, Claude Code for autonomous tasks, Granola for meeting notes), explain why you chose each, and describe workflows (e.g., "I use Claude Code to process all customer interview transcripts in parallel"). Interviewers want evidence you've spent real time at the frontier.

- **Show compounding mindset**: When discussing past work, highlight examples where you made future work easier—built templates, created reusable processes, automated repetitive tasks. This "compounding engineering" mindset (every unit of work makes next unit easier) signals you'll scale yourself and your team efficiently.

- **Emphasize allocation/management skills even if you're IC**: Talk about how you've delegated (to AI or people), evaluated quality, set vision, decided when to dive deep vs. stay high-level. These are the skills of the "allocation economy"—they matter more than pure execution speed.

- **Use "vibe check" framing for new tech**: When asked about emerging tech/tools, don't just recite benchmarks. Describe hands-on experience: "Here's how it *feels* to use this for real work." Shows you're builder/practitioner, not just reading headlines. Dan's insight: best writing about tech comes from people actually using and building with it.

- **Be a generalist who goes deep when needed**: Highlight breadth (can you code, write, design, analyze?) but also show you know when to "go down a layer in the stack" (understand how underlying systems work). Dan's point: knowing how to code remains important even as AI writes code, because you need to evaluate, debug, and understand when to intervene.

- **Tell your "why" story authentically**: Dan's vulnerable story about stopping writing, feeling miserable, then realizing he needed to be writer-first resonates because it's honest about finding fit. In interviews, share what truly motivates you—unique shapes are more compelling than cargo-culting what you think interviewers want.
