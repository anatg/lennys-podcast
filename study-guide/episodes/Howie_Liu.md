## TL;DR
Howie Liu shares how Airtable is fundamentally reinventing itself as an AI-native company after 13 years, restructuring into "fast thinking" (shipping AI weekly) and "slow thinking" (enterprise scale) teams. He advocates for CEOs becoming ICs again—getting hands-on with code, prototypes, and AI tools—while breaking down role silos so PMs, engineers, and designers become more full-stack and versatile.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Fast Thinking vs. Slow Thinking Groups**: Organizational structure borrowed from Kahneman's dual-process theory. Fast thinking group ships AI capabilities weekly with prototype-first execution. Slow thinking group handles deliberate, premeditated work like infrastructure and enterprise scalability. Both complement each other—fast creates top-of-funnel excitement, slow enables retention and expansion.

- **The "Refounding" Test**: Ask yourself: "If you were literally founding a new company from scratch with the same mission, how would you execute on that mission using a fully AI-native approach?" If your existing assets don't provide an advantage, sell the company and start fresh. If they do (like Airtable's no-code primitives as a DSL for AI agents), lean in aggressively.

- **Prototype Over Decks**: In the AI era, words in PRDs or decks can't capture the magic. Build functional, interactive prototypes that use real AI capabilities. You can't "vibe" the product from static designs—you need to play with it to understand what's possible.

- **Vibes Before Evals**: For novel product experiences, start with open-ended exploration and "throwing stuff at the wall" before defining structured evals. Evals are powerful for iterative improvement once you understand the use case cluster, but premature evals constrain discovery.

- **Chief Taste Maker Role**: To be the chief taste maker, you must participate in creating the soup, not just tasting it. With AI, you can't understand the solution space from screenshots—you need hands-on time with models, prompts, and tools to grasp what's possible.

- **The Upside-Down T**: Everyone needs depth in one discipline (the vertical bar) and baseline competency across adjacent roles (the horizontal bar). PMs need to be "dangerous" in design and engineering. Engineers need product thinking. Designers need technical fluency. The baselines are converging.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The IC CEO Trend**: In AI-native companies, CEOs are becoming individual contributors again—writing code, building prototypes, leading initiatives directly. This isn't micromanagement; it's understanding the details that matter and where tying details across groups creates non-incremental breakthroughs versus local optimization.

2. **Play as a Learning Mode**: There's a psychological difference between checking boxes versus exploring with curiosity. Liu encourages team members to literally block out days or weeks to "play" with AI products—cancel meetings, experiment freely, treat it as legitimate work. He leads by example, sharing links to his Replit prototypes and ChatGPT research threads.

3. **AI Value Economics Are Insane**: Liu proudly spends hundreds of dollars on inference costs running LLMs against sales call transcripts to extract insights. This is "trivial compared to the potential strategic value"—equivalent to hiring a brilliant chief of staff or paying consultants millions. People should "aggressively throw compute cycles at high-value problems."

4. **Founder Mode Is Not New Manager Mode**: As companies scale, conventional wisdom says to create executive fiefdoms with loose coupling—like a factory production model. This works for incremental execution but kills the "magical integrative value of holistic thinking." Product-centric companies need continuous product-market-fit refinding, which requires integrated thinking and big bets, not siloed swim lanes.

5. **The Meta-Learning on Advice**: Don't just follow recommendations from smart people. Inspect their "chain of thought"—the why behind their advice. Everyone is trained on different corpuses (the ServiceNow corpus vs. the Facebook corpus vs. the Airtable corpus). The reasoning is more valuable than the specific action, because context differs.

6. **Speed Is a Feature, Not a Bug**: AI moves at weekly cadence, not yearly. Companies must study competition constantly, experience products hands-on (not just read TechCrunch), and ask "is this maximally accelerated?" Liu wants Airtable to ship as fast as Cursor or Windsurf—AI-native companies that release major features weekly.

7. **Humility and Gratitude as Life Foundation**: Approaching each day from a place of humility and gratitude (even during tough moments like firing someone) becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. You're open-minded, you attract good people and opportunities, and it spills over into everything else.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **PMs Must Become Hybrid PM-Prototypers**: The role shifts from writing PRDs to building functional prototypes. Use AI coding tools (Cursor, Replit, etc.) to create interactive demos that exercise real AI capabilities, not static wireframes.

- **Technical Fluency Is No Longer Optional**: PMs need to understand how models work, tool calling, agentic workflows, context windows, prompting strategies. You don't need CS-level depth, but you need to be "dangerous enough" to prototype and design intelligently around technical constraints.

- **Cross-Functional Convergence**: The PM-engineer-designer triangle is collapsing. PMs need design sensibilities and prototyping ability. Engineers need product thinking. Designers need technical understanding. Everyone needs baseline competency in all three areas while maintaining depth in one.

- **Daily AI Usage Is the Signal**: Dan Shipper's test for company AI success: does the CEO use ChatGPT/Claude daily (or hourly)? Liu measures himself by inference cost—he's the #1 most expensive user of Airtable AI globally. This hands-on usage is how you understand what's possible.

- **Sales-Led vs. PLG for AI**: While companies like Palantir and Harvey succeed with sales-led AI adoption, the best way to show AI value is experientially through PLG. ChatGPT's 700M weekly users (10% of humans) in under 3 years proves the power of frictionless trial. Liu is shifting Airtable back toward its PLG roots.

- **Role Silos Must Break Down Everywhere**: Not just in EPD—marketing should have people who can do performance marketing + ad copy + content creation + demos. Sales AEs should be SE-fluent and demo products themselves. Everyone becomes more outcome-oriented by collapsing dependencies.

- **The "Infinite Fruit Trees" Problem**: AI creates almost infinite possibilities of what you could build. This isn't about doing the same work with fewer people—it's about having "massive watermelons on the ground" instead of hard-to-reach coconuts. Teams need to pick the biggest low-hanging fruit and move fast.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate Hands-On AI Fluency**: Be ready to discuss specific AI tools you use daily, weekend projects you've prototyped, and concrete experiments you've run. Don't just know about AI—show you've built with it.

- **Think Full-Stack for Your Role**: If you're interviewing for PM, show design sensibility and technical understanding. If you're engineering, demonstrate product thinking. Break out of traditional role boxes—the best candidates are becoming more versatile.

- **Tell "Refounding" Stories**: When discussing product strategy, show you can think from first principles: "If we started today, what would we build?" Then articulate your existing advantages. This demonstrates both bold thinking and pragmatic leverage.

- **Emphasize Speed and Experimentation**: Talk about prototypes over PRDs, weekly shipping cadences, and how you've accelerated execution. AI-era companies value bias toward action and learning through building.

- **Show You Inspect the "Chain of Thought"**: When discussing how you take advice or learn from others, demonstrate that you understand the reasoning and context, not just copy actions. This shows strategic thinking and adaptability.

- **Balance Depth and Breadth**: Highlight your deep specialty while proving baseline competency across adjacent areas. The "upside-down T" shape is increasingly valuable—especially in AI-native orgs.
