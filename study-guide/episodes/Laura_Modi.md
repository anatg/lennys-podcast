# Laura Modi — Founder & CEO, Bobbie (Infant Formula)

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Laura Modi
- **Role/Company**: Founder & CEO of Bobbie, the only female-founded and mom-led organic infant formula company in the US
- **Background**: Spent 5.5 years at Airbnb as Director of Hospitality, leading global host community and host operations. Prior experience at Google. Left Airbnb to found Bobbie after experiencing firsthand the lack of quality infant formula options. Mother of three children; husband is also a founder.

## TL;DR
Laura Modi left Airbnb to disrupt the infant formula industry by creating Bobbie, building a brand that removes guilt from formula feeding. Her journey demonstrates the power of naivety in disruption, the critical importance of slowing growth to serve existing customers ("slowth"), and how manufacturing momentum through arbitrary deadlines drives execution—all while building a culture that prioritizes personal connections and optimistic doers over specialists.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Commerce, Content, Community → Content, Community, Commerce**: Most D2C businesses prioritize commerce first. Bobbie flipped this to focus on content (SEO-driven education through Milk Drunk blog) and community before commerce. This drives sustainable acquisition rather than paid marketing dependency.

- **The Three Things Framework**: Identify the top three things keeping your customer up at night. If your brand isn't solving one of those three, you're being loud for no reason. All messaging and positioning should connect to those core pain points.

- **Manufactured Momentum**: Create artificial deadlines and launch dates to force action. "Why are we launching May 1st?" "Because we said it." Without manufactured milestones, momentum dies and things may never get done.

- **60/40 Product-Marketing Split**: 60% of growth comes from your product and the brand package around it. Only 40% comes from how you get the word out. Never let the 40% distract from making the 60% mediocre.

- **Personal + Professional Balance**: In moments of professional slowth, lean into personal growth. The give-and-take between personal and professional life creates sustainable energy over the long term.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Slowth is a valid growth strategy**: During the 2022 formula shortage, Bobbie turned off their website for six months to protect existing subscribers rather than chase new customers. This "slowth" built unshakeable brand loyalty—they became the only formula company that reliably served customers and never ran out.

2. **Naivety is your secret weapon for disruption**: An ounce of naivety enables creativity and innovation. Laura knew nothing about formula manufacturing or regulations, which allowed her to question the status quo. Hire "unlikely people" with naivety about what it takes to win—they drive the biggest impact.

3. **Brand internal processes to create momentum**: At Airbnb they called customer service deep dives "Air Dives." At Bobbie, legal compliance reviews became "Secret Shopping Program." Branding mundane workflows creates memory recall, keeps people motivated, and makes the work feel more meaningful.

4. **D2C isn't dead—paid marketing addiction is**: The D2C model works if you don't get hooked on the "drug" of paid performance marketing. Build sustainable growth through content (SEO thought leadership), community (word-of-mouth), and brand that people believe in—not by buying every customer.

5. **Supply is your product in marketplaces**: At Airbnb, they realized the hosts *were* the product, not the technology. This shift toward "product as supply" requires building culture where everyone understands that serving and equipping your supply side drives all growth—users beget users.

6. **Hire optimistic doers, not strategists**: Look for curiosity, ability to make decisions fast, and people who never say "I don't do that." Avoid "intellectual ejaculation"—people who pontificate strategy but don't execute. Generalists who roll up their sleeves beat specialists in startups.

7. **Personal connections are currency in remote culture**: Even in fully remote companies, build personal relationships through dedicated check-ins. People work better as a "second family" when they know each other's kids, lives, and personal challenges—not just professional updates.

8. **Infrastructure enables doing it all**: Having three kids and two founder-parent households requires extensive systems: EAs, nannies, shared calendars, weekly agenda meetings with your partner. You can't "do it all" without intentionally building the infrastructure first.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate manufactured momentum**: In interviews, show examples of how you created artificial deadlines or forcing functions to drive execution. PMs who wait for perfect timing never ship.

- **Show you understand supply/product relationship**: If interviewing for marketplace or platform roles, articulate how you think about supply as the product itself, not just the technology connecting supply and demand.

- **Prepare your "teach me something" answer**: Practice explaining something unrelated to work in a clear, compelling way. This reveals your communication ability and how you think about explaining complex topics.

- **Tell stories about choosing slowth over growth**: Demonstrate strategic thinking by sharing times you slowed down or paused to serve existing users better, even when it hurt short-term metrics.

- **Emphasize culture-building actions**: Share specific, tactical examples of how you've built culture (not just abstract values)—like branding internal programs, creating personal check-ins, or building storytelling into launches.

- **Show you're an optimistic doer**: Demonstrate bias for action over analysis paralysis. Share examples of rolling up sleeves to do work outside your "lane" to unblock the team.
