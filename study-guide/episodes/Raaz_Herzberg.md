## TL;DR
Raaz Herzberg shares how Wiz found explosive product-market fit after an early pivot (driven by her asking "I don't understand what we're building"), scaling from confused customer calls to 100M ARR in 18 months. She reveals why she moved from product to marketing, what CMOs get wrong, how to distinguish polite interest from real customer pull, and why embracing failure and saying "I don't understand" are superpowers.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Heat in the Kitchen**: Track where organizational urgency shifts as companies scale. Early: product (what to build) → engineering (build it) → sales (sell it) → marketing (create pipeline/awareness). Put your best people where the heat is. The heat reveals what's blocking growth at each stage.

- **Pull vs. Push Signals**: Don't mistake politeness for product-market fit. Real pull looks like: "How much does this cost?", "When can we start a POV?", "Let me connect you to my team." Polite interest sounds like: "Interesting, keep me updated." Create friction (long questionnaires, requirements) to test commitment—if they won't do the work, they don't really want it.

- **The Dummy Explanation Test**: If you live inside your product bubble, you assume knowledge customers don't have. Every piece of communication should be understandable by anyone outside your company. Ask: "Would someone who's never heard of us understand this?" The "Wiz Graph Database" means nothing to outsiders—say what it actually does instead.

- **The Bubble Principle**: You see your website, colors, and messaging every day—you're sick of it. Your customers just noticed it for the first time. When you're ready to change something because you're bored, they're just starting to understand it. Product teams live in their own bubble; customers don't.

- **Friction Is Good**: Where there's resistance or discomfort, that's where growth happens. If you're already good at something, that's not where to focus energy. Push into areas where you feel like an imposter. Applies to personal development, customer qualification, and strategic decisions.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Founder-led sales teaches you what actually works**: Wiz founders closed the first couple million in ARR themselves before hiring salespeople. This gave them confidence the product could sell and taught them the real customer conversation. Don't hire your way out of not knowing how to do something—do it yourself first, then hire to scale what works.

2. **10-15 customer calls per day is how you find PMF**: In Wiz's first weeks (March 2020, COVID lockdown), everyone was home and available. They did 10-15 customer calls daily, which helped them realize their original network security idea wasn't working. Volume of conversations reveals patterns you can't see in 2-3 calls per week.

3. **"I don't understand" is a superpower**: Raaz nearly quit six weeks in, thinking she was the only one who didn't understand what they were building. Admitting confusion opened a five-hour discussion that led to the pivot to cloud security. What feels like personal failure is often organizational clarity everyone needs but won't say.

4. **Marketing is the opposite of product**: Product requires deep consideration—every feature adds complexity and technical debt forever. Marketing has zero maintenance cost—post a video, if it flops, try something different tomorrow. This asymmetry means marketing should experiment constantly and take creative risks product teams can't afford.

5. **Being okay with failure unlocks velocity**: Raaz was "pretty sure" she'd fail as first PM, then as CMO. She did it anyway. Most people spend energy trying to build confidence; instead, accept you might fail and do it anyway. Let them find out you're an imposter—that's on them to reject you. This mindset enabled her to take the Wizard of Oz booth risk at RSA (5x foot traffic vs. previous year).

6. **Crystal-clear messaging is non-negotiable at scale**: From inside the product org, gray areas and caveats are fine ("not exactly, but it can do X"). When you cross to go-to-market, fuzziness gets lost in translation. Sellers, marketers, and customers need black-and-white clarity. Product teams must deliver messages that need zero interpretation.

7. **CMOs fail from lack of trust and domain knowledge**: Marketing is highly visible and touches what founders care about most (their company's identity). One bad ad can break trust. Coming from outside the founding team AND outside the domain creates two massive challenges. Success requires deep trust, deep product understanding, and deep market knowledge—hard to build from outside.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate customer obsession through volume**: Be ready to discuss how many customer conversations you drove, not just participated in. "10-15 calls per day" shows relentless focus on understanding the market. In interviews, specificity about conversation volume and patterns shows rigor.

- **Show vulnerability as strength**: Raaz's "I don't understand" moment led to Wiz's successful pivot. In interviews, demonstrate you're comfortable admitting confusion and asking clarifying questions rather than pretending to understand. This shows intellectual honesty and prevents wasted effort.

- **Quantify pull signals**: Don't just say "customers were interested." Describe behavioral evidence: did they fill out your questionnaire overnight? Did they ask about pricing, POV timing, or make team introductions? Interviewers want to know you distinguish genuine demand from politeness.

- **Explain cross-functional moves with strategic logic**: Raaz moved product → marketing because "the heat moved there." Show you understand where organizations get blocked at different scales and that you're willing to move toward problems, not titles.

- **Embrace the "try everything" mindset in low-risk domains**: Discuss how you've run experiments rapidly where failure is cheap (messaging, positioning, creative) versus carefully in high-cost areas (product features). Shows strategic thinking about where to move fast vs. slow.
