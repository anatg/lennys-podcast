## TL;DR
Ada shares a deeply practical toolkit for navigating your career intentionally: use Curiosity Loops to collect structured advice, clarify your values to filter opportunities against the "inner scorecard" rather than the "outer scorecard," and embrace the Explore/Exploit career framework to sequence learning in your early career. The overarching message is that the best career move is the one that aligns with who you actually are, not the one that looks best on a résumé.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Curiosity Loop**: A structured approach to gathering advice. Craft a specific, unbiased question, curate 5–10 people with subject-matter expertise or personal knowledge of you, make the ask lightweight (e.g., "pick your top 2 from this list and say why"), then close the loop by sharing outcomes and thanking respondents. Inspired by customer advisory councils applied to personal decisions.

- **Explore and Exploit (Career)**: Treat your early career as exploration with a thesis—sample different roles, company sizes, and functions to build signal. Once you've found high-signal territory, switch to exploitation mode: go deep to extract maximum learning from the specific domain (e.g., growth, subscriptions, product marketing). Intentionally choosing what to exploit—rather than just chasing the biggest title—is the real strategy.

- **Inner Scorecard vs. Outer Scorecard** (Warren Buffett): The outer scorecard is how the world evaluates you (status, title, wealth). The inner scorecard is what actually matters to you (values, relationships, energy, alignment). Most people optimize for the outer without realizing it.

- **Values Exercise**: A 10–15 minute process of selecting words that resonate from a curated list, grouping them, and stack-ranking to produce 3–5 sentences describing your personal values. Use this to evaluate decisions—does this choice move toward or away from what I actually care about?

- **Eating Your Vegetables**: Powering through the initial discomfort of a new skill before you can accurately evaluate whether you like it. You may dislike networking (or podcasting, or writing) because you're bad at it, not because it's genuinely wrong for you. Force 10–12 exposures before judging.

- **Don't Be the Frog**: Awareness of slow-boiling bad environments. If you would jump out of the pot if thrown in directly, ask yourself: is the temperature slowly rising in your current role? Monitor the direction of the trend, not just the current state.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Structure makes advice actionable.** "What should I do?" is a terrible question. "Here are three concrete options—which two would you choose and why?" gives you rich, comparable signal across respondents.

2. **Explore with a thesis.** Early-career exploration isn't random wandering—it's hypothesis testing. Every role should answer a specific question: Do I like big companies? Do I like marketing? Do I want to be a founder? Document what you learn and use it to sharpen your next hypothesis.

3. **The obvious next step is rarely optimal.** "Should I write a book?" often isn't really a question—social pressure has already answered it. Values clarification reveals that many "obvious" opportunities fail to serve your inner scorecard.

4. **Coaching is not the default solution.** Before hiring a coach, consider: a curiosity loop for specific advice, a course or community for skill-building, a trusted friend group for emotional support. Coaching makes most sense for founders in high-velocity, high-uncertainty phases, or for sensitive interpersonal issues where a neutral party adds value.

5. **Hard feedback is an act of generosity.** Withholding honest feedback (about appearance, communication habits, blind spots) to protect yourself is a form of selfishness. The safest way to give it: communicate deep care first, then challenge directly. The recipient may not hear it immediately, but it often lands later.

6. **Build a habit with a physical anchor.** Ada's networking rule: attend one event per week, hand out 10 business cards, touch the back wall of the venue, then leave. Physical constraints override abstract goals.

7. **Write for an audience of one.** For content creation, LinkedIn posts, or newsletters—write as if you're explaining something useful to a specific trusted friend. This beats optimizing for virality and produces more genuine content.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **Reference "Curiosity Loops"** when answering questions about how you make decisions or gather customer insight. Frame it as structured, asynchronous research—subject-matter experts + people who know you well + lightweight ask.
- **Use the Explore/Exploit framework** to tell your career story. "I spent my early career in exploration mode, testing hypotheses about company stage and function. Once I found high-signal territory in X, I deliberately exploited it to go deeper." This shows intentionality rather than opportunism.
- **Show inner/outer scorecard awareness** if asked about why you left a role or turned down an opportunity. Saying "it looked great on paper but didn't align with what I actually care about" demonstrates emotional intelligence and self-awareness.
- **Demonstrate Radical Candor literacy**: reference giving or receiving hard feedback as an act of genuine care, not just a process.
- **If asked "how do you grow?"** mention the "eating vegetables" approach—deliberately practicing things you're bad at, 10–12 reps before evaluating whether you actually dislike them.
