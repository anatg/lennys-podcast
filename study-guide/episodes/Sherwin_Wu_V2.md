## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Surgeon Model of Management**: From *The Mythical Man-Month*, treat top performers like surgeons in an operating room—the manager's job is to anticipate needs, remove blockers, and provide everything required before it's asked for. Spend 50%+ of time with top 10% performers.

- **The Sorcerer's Apprentice Paradigm**: From the 1980 textbook *SICP* (Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs), software engineers are wizards casting spells (incantations/code). With AI, this metaphor becomes literal—engineers now manage fleets of agents doing work, but must stay vigilant to prevent the "brooms going wild" scenario.

- **Build for Model Trajectory, Not Current State**: "Make sure you're building for where the models are going and not where they are today." Kevin Weil's line: "This is the worst the models will ever be." Products should target 80% capability today that will click into place as models improve.

- **The Bitter Lesson (Applied to AI Products)**: "The models will eat your scaffolding for breakfast." Vector stores, agent frameworks, and product scaffolding repeatedly become obsolete as models improve. Avoid over-architecting solutions that assume static model capabilities.

- **Top-Down + Bottoms-Up AI Adoption**: Successful AI deployments require both C-suite mandate AND grassroots champions. Create "tiger teams" of excited, technical-adjacent employees (not necessarily engineers) who explore capabilities, build best practices, and evangelize internally.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Engineer productivity gap is widening dramatically**: Engineers using Codex open 70% more PRs than those who don't, and the gap keeps growing as power users learn to leverage tools better. Top performers become exponentially more productive with AI.

2. **Most work isn't software engineering—it's business processes**: The biggest AI opportunity is in repeatable, procedural work (support, operations, standard processes) that dominates the real economy but gets little Silicon Valley attention. This market is massive and underrated.

3. **The one-person billion-dollar startup has profound second-order effects**: If one person can build a billion-dollar company, thousands can build $10M+ companies. This implies a golden age of B2B SaaS with hyper-vertical software and potentially reshapes venture capital.

4. **Context problems, not capability problems, block AI agents**: When coding agents fail, it's usually insufficient information/documentation, not model limitations. The 100% Codex-generated codebase team at OpenAI solves this by encoding tribal knowledge into .md files, Skills, and code structure.

5. **Listening to customers can lead you astray in AI**: The field changes so rapidly that customer feedback often reflects local maxima. Focus on where models are heading, not current pain points with scaffolding that will become obsolete.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Engineers becoming managers of agent fleets**: ICs now run 10-20 parallel Codex threads simultaneously, acting more as tech leads steering work rather than writing code themselves. The role is shifting from execution to orchestration.

- **Code review transformed**: 100% of OpenAI PRs are reviewed by Codex. Tasks that once took 10-15 minutes now take 2-3 minutes. Small PRs often don't need human review—Codex serves as the "second pair of eyes."

- **Management leverage increasing**: AI tools enable managers to handle larger teams than the traditional 6-8 direct reports. With ChatGPT connected to GitHub, Notion, and Slack, managers can research employee contributions, identify blockers, and manage 1:1s more efficiently.

- **The boring parts get automated first**: Teams immediately hand off the most tedious work (code reviews, linting, CI processes) to AI, making the job more enjoyable while dramatically increasing output.

- **Stress from agent management**: A new category of workplace stress emerges—"my agent isn't working and I'm wasting time." Teams without escape hatches (like the 100% Codex codebase team) must solve problems by improving context, not manual coding.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate AI fluency authentically**: Don't just claim to use AI tools—share specific workflows, limitations you've encountered, and how you've adapted. Sherwin's team solves agent failures by improving context/documentation, not giving up.

- **Show platform thinking**: For any product role, understand ecosystem effects. Sherwin emphasizes OpenAI's commitment to platform neutrality and "rising tide lifts all boats" philosophy. Discuss how you'd enable others, not just build features.

- **Focus on where technology is heading, not just where it is**: When discussing product strategy, demonstrate long-term thinking. Reference how you'd build for 12-18 month capability improvements, not just today's constraints.

- **Quantify impact on productivity**: Sherwin cites specific metrics (95% adoption, 70% more PRs, 100% code review coverage). In interviews, use concrete numbers to show how you've measured and improved team efficiency.

- **Address the boring work problem**: Every product has tedious tasks users hate. Show how you'd identify and automate these first—they're often the highest-impact, lowest-controversy improvements.
