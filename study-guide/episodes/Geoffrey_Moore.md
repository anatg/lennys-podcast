# Geoffrey Moore — Crossing the Chasm and Go-to-Market Strategy

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Geoffrey Moore
- **Role/Company**: Author of *Crossing the Chasm* and go-to-market strategy consultant
- **Background**: Former marketing executive at Regis McKenna (premier Silicon Valley marketing agency in the 1980s). Author of multiple influential books on technology adoption and strategy, including *Crossing the Chasm* (1991), *Inside the Tornado*, and *The Infinite Staircase*. English major turned tech strategy thought leader who has advised companies for over 30 years on navigating disruptive innovation.

## TL;DR
Geoffrey Moore explains why B2B startups must get narrow with their initial target audience—picking a segment small enough to dominate (big fish, small pond) where they can become the clear market leader through concentrated execution. The key insight: pragmatist buyers only purchase what their peers are buying, so you must create density of adoption in one segment before expanding, using different playbooks (early market, bowling alley, tornado, Main Street) at each stage of the technology adoption lifecycle.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Technology Adoption Lifecycle**: Markets evolve through four distinct phases, each requiring different strategies: (1) Early Market (visionaries and technology enthusiasts), (2) Bowling Alley/Crossing the Chasm (pragmatists with compelling problems), (3) Tornado (mass market adoption with established budgets), (4) Main Street (commoditized products, service-led innovation). Using the wrong playbook at the wrong stage actively undermines success.

- **The Chasm**: The gap between early adopters (visionaries who make independent buying decisions) and pragmatists (who only buy what their peers buy). Visionaries cannot serve as references for pragmatists because pragmatists view them as reckless risk-takers, not peers. This creates a "junior high school dance problem"—no one wants to go first.

- **Big Enough to Matter, Small Enough to Lead**: The formula for selecting your beachhead segment. Target a segment where you can achieve 30-50% market share within 2 years, causing ecosystem partners to organize around you. Segment definition: same geography, same industry, same profession, same compelling use case. If the top 20 customers all adopt your solution, the rest follow.

- **Bowling Pin Strategy**: After winning your beachhead, expand to adjacent segments only. Adjacent means either (1) same customer, different use case (leverage customer references) or (2) same use case, different customer base (leverage partner ecosystem). Non-adjacent expansion fails because you lose both reference and partner advantages.

- **Fish to Pond Ratio**: You must be a big fish in your chosen pond. A billion-dollar segment is too large for a startup to dominate. Better to own 40% of a $50M segment than 2% of a $5B segment, because ecosystems form around market leaders, and you can only be the leader in a small enough pond.

- **Trapped Value**: The economic justification for innovation. Where is $10B+ of value currently trapped that your technology can release? Markets typically give you ~10% of the value you unlock. Example: Uber unlocked trapped value in people's backseat/free time; Airbnb unlocked trapped value in unused bedrooms.

- **Compelling Reason to Buy vs. Compelling Reason to Sell**: Crossing the chasm requires customer-driven urgency, not product-driven pitching. The customer must fear their problem more than they fear your unproven solution. You need a problem severe enough that pragmatists will act despite risk. Don't lead with demos—lead with diagnostic questions about their pain.

- **The Bonfire Analogy**: Running a match back and forth under a log won't light it. You need to hold the match in one place (concentrated target segment) with kindling (adjacent early customers) until the fire catches. Trying to serve scattered customers across multiple segments prevents ignition.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The playbooks actively contradict each other**: Qualifying customers on budget is critical in tornado/Main Street phases but fatal in early market and bowling alley (where customers have no budget for you). Project models work for visionaries but won't scale past the chasm. Solution models work for pragmatists but over-commit resources for visionaries. Using yesterday's successful playbook in today's different market phase is the most common failure mode.

2. **Marquee customers and beachhead customers are different**: You need a famous visionary customer first (CIA using AWS) to get on the map and create a story, but you typically *cannot* use that customer's industry as your beachhead because (a) visionaries do weird things pragmatists won't replicate, and (b) visionaries want competitive advantage, not to help their industry peers.

3. **Crossing the chasm isn't expensive—it's focused**: You're reaching maybe 200 people in a specific geography/industry/profession/use case. No Super Bowl ads, no broad coverage. The constraint isn't money, it's discipline to stay narrow when you're desperate for revenue and want to take any deal.

4. **Pragmatists want therapy, not demos**: When selling to pragmatists with compelling problems, shut the laptop. Start with: "We've been working with people in your industry who have this problem—do you have it?" They'll talk about themselves for an hour. Your job is to collect problem domain knowledge, not to pitch product features. The gold is understanding their problem better than anyone else.

5. **You know you've crossed the chasm when you don't need more VC funding**: Not because you're profitable, but because you're cashflow positive with a loyal customer base, established ecosystem partners, and understood CAC/LTV. You're a "going concern"—a company expected to still exist in two years. Now you raise money on your terms and timeline, not from desperation.

6. **Product-led growth cannot cross the chasm**: PLG works when adoption risk is low (freemium, low-stakes decisions). The chasm exists precisely because of high-risk buying decisions requiring peer validation. You need a sales motion with domain expertise to cross the chasm. PLG excels at land-and-expand in the tornado and Main Street phases.

7. **Target the economic buyer, not the end user**: End users complain ("we're oppressed!") but can't sponsor budget. You need the executive with enough authority to redirect funds and drive organizational change. Look for the persona who says "I'm tired of the status quo and willing to jump off the escalator"—not the company person following process.

8. **Value pricing beats discounting before the chasm**: When customers fear their problem more than your price, they don't want discounts—they want guaranteed problem resolution. "Heart surgery $9.99 this Saturday only" doesn't reduce risk; it increases it. Commit to solving their problem completely, and charge accordingly.

9. **Adjacency is everything in the bowling alley**: If you light the fire here but the next log is in another room, the fire dies. Geographic, industry, or use-case distance kills momentum. Same customer/different use case works (customer references carry). Same use case/different customer works (partners carry). Everything else breaks the chain reaction.

10. **Venture funding milestones are about risk reduction**: Angel money proves you can build something. Series A takes "will this company exist?" off the table (crossing the chasm). Series B funds the journey from $10M to $100M (bowling alley). Later rounds capture tornado/category dominance. Each round should change your valuation state 2-3x by eliminating a specific existential risk.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI exists in all four lifecycle phases simultaneously**: From a customer perspective, Microsoft Copilot is Main Street (low-risk productivity add-on), Salesforce's sales co-pilot approaches tornado (broad deployment with established budget), Sal Khan's educational tutoring is bowling alley (specialized problem for post-pandemic grade-level dispersion), and new visual/legal/creative agencies using Gen AI are early market experiments.

- **Gen AI creates new service businesses from commoditized products**: The Main Street play—converting product ownership to service consumption (like Uber did for taxis). Many new agencies and service businesses will emerge using Gen AI to deliver human-in-the-loop outcomes at different price/speed/quality points than traditional methods.

- **AI startups still need the same discipline**: Despite the hype, AI companies must still identify trapped value, find compelling reasons to buy, dominate beachhead segments, and execute the appropriate playbook for their lifecycle phase. The technology being "AI" doesn't exempt anyone from go-to-market fundamentals.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate customer obsession beyond features**: When discussing product decisions, show you led with customer problem discovery rather than solution pitching. Describe how you conducted diagnostic conversations, collected problem domain knowledge, and understood the compelling reason to buy before building.

- **Show strategic focus over scattered execution**: Highlight times you resisted the temptation to pursue multiple customer segments simultaneously. Explain how you achieved concentration (same geography, industry, profession, use case) to become the clear leader in a small pond rather than spreading too thin.

- **Understand different playbooks for different stages**: Be prepared to discuss how you'd sell to visionaries differently than pragmatists, or how tornado-phase tactics (broad sales coverage, product standardization) would fail in the bowling alley phase (deep problem expertise, custom solutions). Show you diagnose the market phase before executing.

- **Use the language of risk reduction and trapped value**: Frame past wins in terms of how you reduced adoption risk for pragmatic buyers or how your innovation unlocked previously trapped economic value. Avoid pure technology/feature narratives in favor of customer outcome and market dynamic narratives.

- **Explain how you built ecosystems**: Share examples of working with partners to consolidate market position, or how achieving density in one segment attracted ecosystem support. Show you understand company power = your capabilities + ecosystem alignment.

- **Demonstrate discipline in positioning**: Describe how you positioned against both incumbent vendors (who understand the business but lack new technology) and technology competitors (who have similar tech but lack domain commitment). Show you know the "specialized leader in X" narrative.
