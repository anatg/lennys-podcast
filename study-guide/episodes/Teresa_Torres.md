## TL;DR
Teresa Torres teaches continuous discovery—building ongoing customer feedback loops into product work rather than project-based research. Her Opportunity Solution Tree framework helps teams move from outcomes to solutions by mapping customer needs (the opportunity space) before jumping to features, ensuring teams stay problem-focused and make better bets through weekly customer interviews and assumption testing.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Opportunity Solution Tree**: A visual framework starting with an outcome at the root, branching into opportunities (unmet needs, pain points, desires), then solutions, then assumption tests. Helps teams structure the messy problem of "how do I start from an outcome and figure out what to build?" The key is structuring opportunities using experience maps (3-7 steps in a customer journey) and keeping opportunities in the problem space, not solution space. As you move down the tree vertically, opportunities get smaller and more actionable.

- **Continuous Discovery vs. Project-Based Research**: Discovery is deciding what to build; delivery is building it. Continuous discovery means building ongoing customer feedback loops (as little as one interview per week) rather than stopping work to do large research projects. Discovery and delivery happen in parallel—you're always doing both. Discovery makes your bets better over time; it doesn't block shipping.

- **Trio Decision-Making**: The product manager, designer, and software engineer work from shared understanding and make decisions together. When they disagree, they find better options rather than negotiating power. This breaks down in dysfunctional cultures but is "magical" on well-functioning teams. The kindergartner marshmallow test shows that groups without power posturing outperform MBA teams focused on hierarchy.

- **Story-Based Interviewing**: Ask "Tell me about the last time you [did X]" rather than hypothetical or opinion questions ("What do you like?" "How do you decide?"). Follow the timeline: "What happened next?" Collect rich, specific stories grounded in actual behavior. Opportunities emerge from stories, not from direct questions. A good interview feels like "having a beer with a buddy"—conversational, not 50 rapid-fire questions.

- **Assumption Testing**: Break ideas into underlying assumptions, prioritize them, test the riskiest assumptions first with small, fast tests (half a dozen to a dozen per week across three ideas). This is different from big experiments testing entire ideas. Assumption testing and delivery are the same work—testing is the start of delivery.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **98% of people write opportunities as solutions**: An opportunity is an unmet need, pain point, or desire—not a feature. Example: "It's hard to enter my password selecting specific letters on screen with the Apple TV remote" (opportunity) vs. "We need a better keyboard interface" (solution). Staying in the problem space before jumping to solutions is the heart of good product work but opposite of how our brains are wired.

2. **Everything in your backlog is a bet—discovery makes it a better bet**: You don't need to "properly discover" everything before shipping. Some bets can be risky. If you've done zero discovery, start doing it in parallel with shipping, and your bets will improve over time. Don't stop shipping to do discovery; that kills appetite for it.

3. **You don't need organizational permission to talk to customers**: Even if you're told you can't talk to customers, you can find people like your users in your personal network. For individual contributors, don't try to force organizational change—just change how you individually work. You have more autonomy than you think.

4. **One interview per week is sustainable continuous discovery**: You can automate recruiting by embedding opt-ins in your product (like NPS surveys but asking "Do you have 20 minutes to talk?") or using internal teams (sales, support) who are already on the phone with customers. Use scheduling software so interviews appear on your calendar automatically.

5. **Small sample sizes are fine—you're changing behavior, not seeking new knowledge**: Product teams get held to standards no one else is held to. Everyone in business makes decisions with zero customer data. One interview is better than zero. You can make decisions on small data because you have continuous feedback loops—you'll get more reliable data as you ship and iterate.

6. **Interviewing well is a grossly underestimated skill**: Most teams ask direct questions out of context, which produces unreliable answers. Story-based interviewing surfaces needs customers aren't even aware of because they're so used to mediocrity. You can run an entire interview by asking one question and following up with "What happened next?"

7. **Work with three solution ideas at once to avoid overcommitting**: When you feel like you know "the obvious solution," that's when you need more options. Compare and contrast like you do when apartment hunting or job searching. This prevents premature commitment and surfaces better solutions.

8. **Business culture has taught us to work in silos—it's toxic**: The "product manager as CEO" mindset creates territorial office politics instead of collaboration. Kindergartners outperform MBA students at building marshmallow structures because they just do instead of posturing about power and strategy. Well-functioning teams work from shared understanding and find options where they don't disagree.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Demonstrate problem-space thinking**: When discussing past projects, frame opportunities as customer needs/pain points, not solutions. Show you can distinguish between "users struggle to find relevant content" (opportunity) vs. "we need better search filters" (solution).

- **Tell story-based discovery examples**: Describe how you gathered customer stories about specific instances ("Tell me about the last time you...") rather than asking hypothetical questions. Interviewers want to see you understand behavior-based research methods.

- **Show outcome-orientation with practical discovery**: Discuss how you balanced outcome-focused work with continuous learning (e.g., weekly customer interviews, automated recruiting). Avoid suggesting you'd "stop shipping to do research"—show discovery and delivery happen in parallel.

- **Describe collaborative decision-making**: Share examples of working with design and engineering as a true trio, making decisions from shared understanding rather than territorial power dynamics. Show humility about not being "the decider."

- **Frame assumptions and bets**: When describing product decisions, acknowledge they were bets and explain how you identified and tested the riskiest assumptions quickly. Show you understand risk assessment and incremental validation.

- **Use specific opportunity framing**: Practice describing a past product area using an experience map structure (3-7 journey steps with specific pain points beneath each). This demonstrates strategic thinking and customer-centricity.
