## TL;DR
Andy's core insight: the standard "problem → solution → why we're better" pitch (the "arrogant doctor") fails because it positions you as a vendor, not a movement. The strategic narrative reframes the conversation from "buy my product" to "the world has changed — here's how you win in the new game and we'll help you get there."

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Strategic Narrative (5-Part Structure)**:
  1. **Name the shift** — Define the old game vs. the new game. Name each concisely (transactions → subscriptions; software → cloud; opinions → reality). The naming is the hardest part — it oversimplifies by design, and that's okay. Examples: Salesforce ("software is over, the cloud is the new game"), Zuora ("transactions → subscription economy"), Gong ("opinions → reality").
  2. **Name the stakes** — Show that winners are already playing the new game. Use data or examples showing that companies not adapting are dying. Create life-or-death framing — the prospect must see the future as a binary between very bad outcomes and potentially very good ones. (Star Wars analogy: kill the uncle and aunt so Luke has to act.)
  3. **Name the object of the new game** — The rallying cry or buyer mission statement. What would it look like to win? Often framed as a question ("What would it take to turn every customer into a subscriber?") to make the prospect a co-adventurer in the story. Examples: "Turn customers into subscribers" (Zuora), "Belong anywhere" (Airbnb), "Live there" (later Airbnb).
  4. **Name the obstacles** — What makes winning hard? These look like problems (which is what the arrogant doctor leads with) but repackaged as obstacles to an already-defined, life-or-death goal. Now they carry emotional weight. The prospect understands *why* these obstacles matter — because they're blocking the new game.
  5. **Name your magic gifts** — How you help overcome the obstacles. Now — and only now — do you talk about your product. It becomes a prop in service of the story, not the subject of the story.

- **Arrogant Doctor vs. Strategic Narrative**: The arrogant doctor says: "You have a problem (pain). I have a solution (treatment). Here's why mine is better than the competition." This sets up comparison shopping on features and invites debate about who's better. The strategic narrative says: "The world has changed. Here's the new game. Here's how to win. We'll help you get there."

- **Movement vs. Product**: The strategic narrative isn't pitching product — it's defining a movement. Salesforce didn't pitch a better CRM; it pitched the end of installed software. Gong didn't pitch a call recorder; it pitched the end of opinions-based sales leadership. When you define the movement, your product becomes the vehicle that makes the movement possible.

- **Category vs. Narrative**: Category creation focuses on naming (e.g., "revenue intelligence"). Strategic narrative focuses on the story behind the category. The name matters less than most people think — Gong's CEO says in hindsight they could have called it "strawberry intelligence" and it wouldn't have mattered. The story matters. The three words are shorthand for the story.

- **Testing the Narrative**: The key test: when you explain the shift and stake, do prospects say "Yes, I'm seeing that" or "Am I crazy or are you seeing this?" and they respond by sharing their own version of the same problem? That qualitative resonance signals the narrative is working.

- **CEO Must Own It**: The narratives that become true north stars across organizations are always CEO-led, not just CEO-sanctioned. The product leader benefits enormously from having CEO air cover — otherwise the narrative is just a product story, not a company story.

- **Presentation Principle: Title = Takeaway**: On every slide, replace descriptive labels ("The Team", "The Problem") with the actual takeaway ("Our team has 15 years of enterprise security experience", "Most companies are leaving $2M on the table by missing X"). The audience should never have to work to understand what a slide means.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **People don't buy solutions — they join movements.** The purchase decision is made at the narrative level, not the feature level. Once someone believes they're in a new game and you understand the new game better than anyone, comparing features becomes irrelevant.

2. **Your obstacles are only powerful in the context of a defined movement.** The same problems that sound like vendor feature lists become emotionally compelling when they're blocking a life-or-death goal the prospect already cares about.

3. **The draft process IS the process.** Working teams come up with a hundred gold ideas. The good narrative throws out 98 of them. The second session is always painful (the team's ideas got cut). This is by design — the friction of the bad draft is what generates the good one.

4. **Most great insights come from solving your own problem.** Andy discovered narrative strategy trying to save his own startup pitch. The best founders build for needs they understand viscerally.

5. **Consistent writing is a career multiplier.** "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" was his ~30-40th post. The overnight success was 30+ pieces of quiet work. The platform explosion (Medium, LinkedIn) made distribution possible without gatekeepers.

6. **Strategic narrative scales the founder.** Once the CEO can't be in every sales call, the narrative transmits their thinking at scale — to salespeople, to product teams for prioritization, to recruiters for culture fit. It's how one person's judgment propagates through a company.

7. **The product roadmap filter.** If you have a strategic narrative, feature requests that don't serve the movement get deprioritized automatically. Gong uses opinions-to-reality as their bar: does this feature help people move away from opinions? If not, it's lower priority.

## AI & The Changing PM Role
*(This episode predates the current AI wave — focused on narrative strategy for B2B enterprise)*

The strategic narrative framework applies directly to AI products: the most successful AI companies (Salesforce in the cloud era, OpenAI in the AI era) have led with a shift narrative ("software is dead, models are the future") rather than feature comparisons. Any PM launching an AI feature should ask: what's the old game we're replacing, and what's the new game we're enabling?

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"How do you position a new product/feature?"**: Strategic narrative framework — name the shift, the stakes, the object of the new game, the obstacles, then your gifts. Don't lead with the product.
- **"How do you align sales, product, and marketing?"**: A single strategic narrative is the north star. Product prioritization, sales pitch, marketing content, and recruiting messaging all flow from the same story.
- **"How do you differentiate in a crowded market?"**: Define the movement, not the category. The movement reframes the competition as "playing the old game" — comparison shopping becomes irrelevant.
- **"How do you know if your pitch is working?"**: Qualitative resonance test — do prospects lean in and say "yes, we're seeing that"? Are they sharing their own version of the same shift? If they're asking "how are you different from X?" you haven't established the movement yet.
- **"How do you help product teams prioritize?"**: The narrative becomes the filter. Every feature request gets evaluated: does it help us get to the object of the new game? If not, it's lower priority.
