## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Fish Where the Fish Are (Munger)**: Don't go where competition is highest just because it feels exciting. The most attractive markets (restaurants, cafes, consumer apps, project management) attract the most competitors and compress margins. The highest-return businesses are often the boring, unloved ones that nobody romanticizes. Form-filling software for government assistance, pressure washing, DJ software — boring markets with pricing power and low competition.

- **Baby Weights Rule**: Don't try to deadlift 300 pounds on day one. First-time entrepreneurs who go straight to "the next great AI company" or a venture-funded competitive market skip the muscle-building phase. Start with a simple, immediately-feedback-positive business that works. The first win creates the narrative "I can do this" that carries you through future, harder attempts.

- **Idea Archaeology**: Follow your passion → study the industry deeply → find the profitable niche within it. Unfair advantage is the key variable. Andrew's path: loved design → started web agency. Loved movies → studied the industry → bought Letterboxd. Loved coffee → bought AeroPress. Loved DJing → bought Serato. The passion guides you to industries you understand; understanding reveals which niche has the moat.

- **Moat Taxonomy**: When buying or building, look for: (1) Brand/pricing power (Coca-Cola, AeroPress); (2) Network effects — each new user makes the network more valuable and hard to leave (Letterboxd, Dribbble, Twitter); (3) Switching costs — painful to rip out (Salesforce). Prefer network effects over switching costs (the latter is anti-user). Avoid businesses without moats, especially competitive ones.

- **"No Problems, Only People Problems"**: Any business challenge traces back to a people issue. Brilliant strategy fails with wrong people. When surrounded by excellent people, even disasters feel manageable. When one bad actor is present, everything unravels. Spend disproportionate time filtering people.

- **Hire Fully Formed, Not Potential**: Hire for who someone already is, not who you hope they'll become. Trying to change someone through mentorship or coaching almost never works. The "hammer" heuristic: listen to what people say they want to do — they will do that thing, not what you assigned them. If you ever catch yourself wondering whether to fire someone, fire them now.

- **Lazy Leadership / Teflon for Tasks**: The goal is to get away from tasks you hate as fast as humanly possible. Build systems and delegate ruthlessly so your time concentrates in the highest-leverage activities. Scale is the answer to "I don't want to do this thing forever" — at sufficient scale, you never touch the pressure washer.

- **Hold Forever (Berkshire Model)**: Buying businesses and leaving them alone outperforms the VC model for certain types of companies. When Tiny buys, nothing changes — especially management, culture, and operations. The acquisition value is in the business itself, not in Tiny's ability to transform it. No synergies, no integration theater.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Boring beats exciting.** The businesses nobody romanticizes have the fewest competitors, the most durable customers, and the most consistent margins. The more glamorous the sector, the more bloodthirsty the competition.

2. **Money doesn't move the anxiety loop.** From barista to former billionaire — the anxious internal monologue didn't change. The neighbor comparison always recalibrates upward. If you're hoping external success will quiet the inner voice, you're solving the wrong problem.

3. **The unfair advantage is the idea moat.** Coinbase happened because Brian Armstrong had cryptography + economics + CS in one person. Metalab happened because Andrew could code and sell. Your Venn diagram is your competitive advantage — build toward what only you can see.

4. **Founders are often undiagnosed.** ~30% of entrepreneurs have ADHD vs. ~5% of the general population. The ink-deep, mile-wide profile; the love of new things; the inability to focus on maintenance work; the self-description as "unemployable" — these are diagnostic signals. Getting checked opens both medication and self-understanding.

5. **Ask for the biggest thing first.** Asked for a Steve Jobs interview, got the Apple Store tour, met Steve Jobs. Ask for amazing and you'll get something great. The worst that happens is they say no and counter with something still remarkable.

6. **Easy choices, hard life. Hard choices, easy life.** (Jerzy Gregorek) Anytime you're taking the comfortable path — not firing the person, not shutting down the project, not saying no — your future self pays the cost. The hard call almost always makes the subsequent period easier.

7. **AI is the Palm Treo phase.** We're in the prototype era — powerful enough to see the vision, rough enough that only motivated tinkerers unlock it. The iPhone moment (AI that configures itself without technical skill) is probably 2-5 years away. Being early builds muscle; don't wait for the iPhone.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

**Andrew is one of the most practically advanced AI adopters in the podcast catalog.** His stack and use cases are unusually specific:

- **Lindy.ai** for workflow automation:
  - Email triage agent: Does Andrew need to see this? Archive threads where he's already chimed in. Label time-sensitive items in a "24-hour" folder. For simple decisions (accept/decline lunch), present multiple-choice options via text, then send the reply as Andrew.
  - Calendar geo-tracking agent: When Andrew emails someone, looks them up, determines their city, stores it. Two weeks before a trip, texts Andrew a list of nearby people he should grab coffee with.
  - Pre-meeting briefing agent: 30 minutes before every meeting, Perplexity research brief + email context + commonalities report delivered by text.
- **Replit** for building web apps he previously needed a team for — "I can just do entirely on my own."
- **Limitless** (AI life recording clip) for relationship debugging: query fights as an LLM, ask "What did each person need? Where did it go wrong?"
- **Gemini 2.5** for large context (all medical records); **Claude** for writing; **ChatGPT** for health queries with persistent memory of supplements + medications.

**The job displacement prediction**: Most knowledge work is in the "Palm Treo phase" — displaced for early adopters, not yet hit for most. If models reach Dario Amodei's "smarter than PhDs by 2027" prediction, the disruption will be large and fast. Advice: get deep with tools now, build wealth, diversify into compute/energy infrastructure.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"How do you think about identifying a good startup idea?"**: Fish where the fish are — boring, unloved markets. Unfair advantage from your Venn diagram. Start with baby weights. Idea archaeology: passion → study → find the profitable niche.
- **"Bootstrapping vs. VC?"**: Not a dichotomy. Bootstrapping can build hundreds of millions in revenue — the question is moat quality and BHAG size. VC is required only where burning money is itself the strategy (defensible monopoly capture).
- **"How do you hire and fire?"**: Hire fully formed, not potential. Listen to what they say they want to do — that's what they'll do. One "should I fire this person?" thought = fire now.
- **"What's your view on AI and future of knowledge work?"**: Palm Treo phase. Deep adopters already displaced assistants, researchers, admins. Knowledge work at scale hits when models are reliable enough for everyone (2-5 years). Advice: build AI muscle now.
- **"What's the most important thing for your personal effectiveness?"**: ADHD diagnosis + SSRI (for anxiety). Times Square → quiet library. Better to know and treat than to self-medicate via external achievement.
