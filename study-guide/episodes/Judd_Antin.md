## TL;DR
The user research discipline is going through a reckoning as companies realize they've been doing too much "middle-range" research (interesting but not pointed enough) instead of macro (strategic, business-focused) and micro (tactical usability) research. To survive and thrive, researchers must become more business-oriented, multi-method, and integrated into product processes from beginning to end—not as a service function called in at the last minute.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Macro, Middle Range, and Micro Research Framework**: Three types of research work. Macro = big picture, strategic, business-focused, forward-looking (competitive landscape, concept cars, TAM studies). Micro = tactical, pixel-perfect quality work (usability testing, AB test interpretation). Middle Range = blobular user understanding questions that are interesting but often not impactful enough—the danger zone where research gets sidelined. Companies should do more macro and micro, less middle range.

- **The Five Research Tools (Swiss Army Knife)**: Great researchers are multi-method and have five core capabilities: (1) Formative/generative research (ethnographic, innovation-focused), (2) Evaluative research (usability testing), (3) Rigorous survey design, (4) Applied statistics, (5) SQL/dashboarding/prompt engineering (technical data skills).

- **User-Centered Performance**: Customer obsession or user-centered practice that is symbolic rather than focused on learning—work done to signal how customer-obsessed we are, not to make different decisions. Examples: "Can you just validate our assumptions" studies at the end, executive listening sessions/focus groups for show, any check-the-box research. The antidote: "We don't validate, we falsify. We are looking to be wrong."

- **The Vicious Cycle of Research Marginalization**: Companies hire researchers → don't integrate them properly (service function, reactive) → researchers do less impactful middle-range work → executives conclude research isn't valuable → researchers get sidelined/laid off → cycle continues. Breaking it requires constant engagement and direct relationships from project start to finish.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Research as a service discipline fails**: When researchers are only called in at the end or not included in decision-making from the beginning, they can't frame the right questions that drive maximum business impact. The solution is consistent relationships where researchers are embedded in product processes from start to finish.

2. **Researchers must speak the language of business**: Read quarterly reports, listen to shareholder calls, understand OKRs, know the conversion funnel backward and forward. The best research lives in the overlap between user needs and business/profit goals—not purely user advocacy.

3. **PMs often ask for research to justify decisions they're reluctant to make on their own**: This creates a broken cycle where research becomes performative rather than genuinely exploratory. Strong intuition has value, but intuition is also where all biases and blind spots live—researchers expose these when properly integrated.

4. **The "multimillion dollar button" principle**: Micro-level research can drive massive business value incredibly fast (48 hours or less). At Airbnb, changing seven characters on a button CTA (because users felt scared to proceed) added ~1% conversion—millions of dollars from one quick insight.

5. **You are nothing like your users**: Dog-fooding and product walkthroughs are valuable for identifying potential issues, but terrible for prioritizing which problems matter most and for whom. Context of use, priorities, and constraints that real users face can't be imagined purely from your own usage.

6. **AB tests tell you what happened, not why**: Watching PMs and data scientists speculate endlessly about why an AB test result occurred is painful when a researcher could answer "why" with evidence in days. You need both: causal claims from experiments and the hows/whys from research.

7. **N=1 can burst bubbles**: A single well-chosen user interview can shatter false assumptions when the right people are watching (e.g., the "super hider" on Facebook who was just trying to achieve inbox zero). Statistical significance isn't required for massive insights when you're exposing blind spots.

8. **Great research teams work on three things max**: A full plate for a researcher is probably two big projects and one small project. More than that and quality suffers. Companies should create pain by protecting researcher time rather than spreading them too thin—that pain drives demand for more research headcount.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

Minimal discussion of AI specifically, but Judd notes that the fifth tool in the researcher's Swiss army knife may now be prompt engineering rather than pure SQL skills, as so much data querying has become dashboarded. Generative AI appears positioned to handle some technical data access, potentially freeing researchers for higher-level synthesis.

## Best Quotes

> "User-centered performance refers to customer obsession or user-centered practice that is symbolic rather than focused on learning. It's hugely common, I would argue. It's work we do to signal to each other how customer obsessed we are, not because we want to make a different decision."

> "Every time a PM comes to a researcher at the end of a product process and says, 'Can you just run a quick user study just to validate our assumptions,' that's user-centered performance. It's too late to matter. We got to ship it. What they want is to check the box."

> "One of my big mantras was, 'We don't validate, we falsify. We are looking to be wrong.' Many PMs, many designers are not in that place. They do not want to be wrong. They're looking to validate, and that's user-centered performance."

> "My metric for success is when they won't have that meeting without you. If they cannot have that decision making meeting without the researcher there, that means you've developed influence, strong, trusting relationships, you're an active participant in the process."

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **When discussing user research experience**: Frame your work using the macro/middle/micro framework. Show you understand strategic research (market analysis, concept development) and tactical research (usability, rapid testing), not just exploratory user interviews. Demonstrate business impact with specific metrics.

- **Show multi-method capabilities**: If you've worked with researchers, describe how you've leveraged different research methods for different questions—don't just default to "we did some user interviews." Mention surveys, usability testing, data analysis, ethnographic observation.

- **Demonstrate you avoid user-centered performance**: Give examples of times research changed your mind or falsified assumptions, not just validated them. Show you integrated research early in the process, not as a check-the-box exercise at the end.

- **Speak the language of business**: When discussing research findings, connect them to business metrics (conversion, retention, LTV) and strategic goals (OKRs, competitive positioning). Show you understand the overlap between user needs and business success.

- **Discuss researcher relationships**: If asked about cross-functional collaboration, emphasize consistent partnerships where researchers were embedded from day one, not called in reactively. Show you protected researcher time and prioritized ruthlessly so they could do fewer things better.

- **Challenge "obvious" conclusions**: When discussing insights from research, anticipate post-hoc bias ("we knew that already") by explaining what specifically was uncertain beforehand and how the research reduced risk or changed the approach.
