## TL;DR
Nickey Skarstad shares how she went from Etsy forums moderator to product leader across multiple top-tier consumer companies, emphasizing the importance of setting clear vision and strategy while involving teams early, maintaining obsessive product quality through balanced metrics (like Airbnb Experiences' review rate), and using second-order thinking to make decisions that scale. She advocates for cross-functional product reviews, empathy-driven decision-making, and being ruthlessly clear about what gives you energy as a PM.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Vision → Mission → Strategy → Objectives Pyramid**: Start with 10-year vision at the top, then mission (one level of abstraction down), strategy (how to execute), and finally quarterly OKRs/objectives. Walk down the pyramid to make abstract strategy actionable. Use cross-functional brainstorming (in Miro/FigJam) to involve the team in vision work, then synthesize and draft.

- **Quality Balancing Metrics**: Use quality metrics (e.g., Airbnb Experiences' review rate, Etsy's "first sale in 7 days") alongside growth metrics to prevent shipping high volume but low quality. Quality metrics often predict long-term success and create the right team incentives.

- **Second-Order Thinking (Systems Thinking)**: Before making decisions, map how today's choices cascade through your ecosystem tomorrow. Ask: "If we change this, what second-level decisions does it affect?" Read *Thinking in Systems* by Donella Meadows to build this muscle. Document first principles to force teams to align on foundations before building.

- **One-Way vs. Two-Way Door Decisions**: One-way doors are hard to reverse (platform standards, marketplace policies, core data models)—spend time debating these with cross-functional input. Two-way doors can be changed later—give teams autonomy to ship fast on these. This distinction helps you move quickly without creating technical debt or strategic lock-in.

- **Red/Yellow/Green Energy Calendar Audit**: Review past weeks of meetings and color-code them by energy level (red = draining/stressful, yellow = neutral, green = energizing). If most meetings are red/yellow, the role isn't a fit. Optimize your career for work that gives you green energy.

- **Three Check-In Product Review Process**: (1) First principles review—align on what you're solving and why. (2) Approach/architecture review—align on how you'll solve it technically and in design. (3) Ready-to-ship review—final quality gate. This prevents late-stage "you're solving the wrong problem" feedback and keeps leadership informed without blocking autonomy.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Bring the team along on strategy development**: Don't craft vision/strategy in a vacuum and present it. If people aren't involved in creating it, they won't buy in. Use collaborative brainstorming (with prompts like "what travel moments brought you joy?") to get input, then synthesize as the leader. Good product work isn't democratic, but it must be collaborative.

2. **Quality metrics prevent long-term failure**: Etsy added friction to seller onboarding (slowing growth) to improve "first sale in 7 days," which predicted long-term seller success. Airbnb Experiences optimized for review rate over pure booking growth. Balancing quality with growth creates sustainable marketplaces—pure growth focus creates unsustainable churn.

3. **Dogfooding reveals quality gaps immediately**: Airbnb Experiences team took experiences constantly and knew instantly if something was five-star or broken. When you experience your own product as a user, you get visceral motivation to fix issues. Make this a team habit, especially for consumer products.

4. **Platform/infrastructure roles require different energy than consumer-facing PM work**: Nickey realized quickly at Shopify that platform product work drained her energy, while zero-to-one consumer work energized her. Be self-aware about what type of PM work you love (marketplace balancing, early-stage PMF, platform systems, etc.) and optimize your career for it.

5. **Functional vs. GM org structures serve different needs**: Functional orgs (PM reports to CPO, design to CDO, etc.) work well for skill development and shared process at scale. GM structures (all functions report to a business leader) work when a new business unit needs autonomy to find PMF with its own resources and processes. Choose based on organizational complexity and strategic goals, not dogma.

6. **Empathy and listening beat command-and-control in decision-making**: Read *Never Split the Difference* by Chris Voss. FBI negotiation tactics are about empathy and making people feel heard. When someone disagrees with your strategy, understand why before pushing back—you might be wrong, or you'll know how to address concerns. This is especially critical for getting cross-functional and leadership buy-in.

7. **Communicate decisions quickly and asynchronously**: After leadership reviews, record a 5-minute Loom recap of feedback and changes, post it in Slack immediately. Don't wait for next week's meeting. Fast, visible communication keeps teams excited and aligned. Use async tools (Slack, Loom, Miro) to reduce meeting burden in remote work.

8. **Second-order thinking saves time and money at scale**: Every change to core data (e.g., Airbnb listing title length) ripples through the system—design, host experience, search results. Map these cascades before building. Early-stage founders skip this for speed, then face expensive rebuilds at scale. Build the muscle early by documenting potential downstream impacts in specs.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate collaborative strategy development**: In interviews, describe how you involved cross-functional partners in vision/strategy creation, not just execution. Show you bring people along rather than dictating from above.

- **Articulate quality trade-offs**: Be ready to discuss times you slowed growth or added friction to improve quality metrics. Explain how you balanced short-term and long-term success through metric selection.

- **Show second-order thinking**: When discussing past product decisions, map the downstream impacts you considered—on other teams, technical architecture, user behavior. This shows strategic maturity.

- **Explain your PM energy profile**: Be clear about what type of PM work energizes you (zero-to-one, growth, platform, etc.) and why. This helps interviewers assess fit and shows self-awareness.

- **Describe your review process**: Outline how you run product reviews—who attends, what gates exist, how you balance autonomy with alignment. This reveals process thinking and leadership style.

- **Use the one-way/two-way door framework in behavioral questions**: When asked about decision-making, categorize past decisions this way to show you know when to move fast vs. slow down.

- **Reference remote collaboration techniques**: In today's world, discuss how you use async tools (Loom, Miro, Slack) to keep distributed teams aligned without meeting overload.
