## TL;DR
Nilan Peiris explains how Wise achieved 70% word-of-mouth growth by systematically measuring NPS, identifying that users who rate 9-10 refer twice as many people as 7-8s (who refer twice as many as lower scores), then obsessively focusing on three product pillars—price, speed, and ease of use—to create experiences "10x better" than alternatives. The key insight: you can't experiment your way to word of mouth; you must build conviction around solving hard problems that blow users' socks off with experiences they didn't know were possible.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The 10x Better Product Standard**: Don't just improve incrementally—ask "what is the theoretical maximum?" (fastest speed, lowest cost possible). Word of mouth only happens when you deliver experiences users didn't know were previously possible. Incremental improvements (even 2x better) get usage but not advocacy.

- **The NPS-to-Virality Correlation**: Measure NPS ("would you recommend this?") and overlay with actual referral behavior. At Wise, each score jump doubled referrals: 6-and-below had minimal referrals, 7-8 doubled, 8-9 doubled again, 9-10 doubled again. This showed NPS wasn't just a metric—it was a viral coefficient multiplier. Moving NPS from 30% to 50% compounds growth geometrically.

- **The Three Pillars of Advocacy**: Through NPS comments and customer interviews, identify your product's 2-4 core dimensions that drive recommendation. For Wise: price, speed, ease of use. These became the entire strategic focus—not brand, not trust (solved through word of mouth), not features, but these three dimensions relentlessly.

- **Product Marketing Within Product**: Close the gap between value delivered and value perceived. Users believed Wise saved them money but didn't believe the amount. A graph showing bank fees vs. Wise fees (shared after transactions) 3x'd referral rates. Perception of value drives recommendation as much as actual value.

- **Cost-to-Customer Pricing Model**: Allocate every company cost back to the specific transaction/customer that generated it, add margin, and that's your price. This revealed 20% of customers generated 80% of costs—raise their prices, lower everyone else's, systematically reduce costs, expand market. Became profitable on every transaction while dropping prices from 0.5% to 0.35%.

- **Conviction Over Experimentation**: You can't A/B test your way to word of mouth. Build conviction through qualitative research and data analysis, then make bold bets on hard problems that take years to solve. Split testing is for optimization; transformation requires conviction that certain levers (like price) will compound over time.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Word of mouth requires remarkable experiences, not just good ones**: 70% of Wise's million quarterly new users come through word of mouth because the product is 8-10x cheaper than alternatives. When Wise entered markets at only 2x better pricing (5.9% vs. 6%), customers used it but didn't talk about it. Advocacy requires delivering something nobody thought was possible—instant transfers, accounts in 10 currencies with three clicks, prices near zero.

2. **NPS comments are more valuable than NPS scores**: Email all NPS comments company-wide weekly (Wise did this for 3-4 years). Customers explicitly told them "make it faster, cheaper, easier"—which became their entire strategy. The qualitative feedback reveals what dimensions matter; the scores show the viral multiplier effect of improving them.

3. **Do unscalable things to unlock 10x improvements**: To get Singapore's first EKYC license, Wise opened a physical office and met customers face-to-face for a year (as regulators required), while lobbying to change the law. This unscalable work led to the world's first selfie-based verification system in Singapore—a 10x better experience that drove massive word of mouth.

4. **Mission-driven companies can mobilize customers as allies**: When Wise shared their mission ("move the world's money instantly for almost nothing") via email, customers forwarded it more than any marketing campaign—despite having no call-to-action or signup button. Authenticity around solving a real problem (expensive international transfers) turned customers into advocates who helped lobby governments and spread the word.

5. **Global products require weak ownership and strong vision**: Wise has one codebase serving all countries (unlike banks with separate tech per country), but local teams can commit code directly. Global teams own the vision and KPIs; regional teams own local conversion and costs. This "weak product ownership" allows the domain model to emerge from real-world constraints rather than trying to design it top-down from one market's assumptions.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Demonstrate systems thinking about growth**: Don't just talk about features that drove growth—explain the system you'd build to measure and optimize word of mouth (e.g., "I'd implement NPS surveys integrated into the flow, overlay referral data, analyze comments weekly to identify product pillars, then set 2-3 year goals around making those pillars 10x better").

- **Show you can identify leverage points**: When discussing past work, explain how you chose what to prioritize based on compounding effects vs. one-time wins. Example: "Improving conversion 10% gives a one-time boost, but improving NPS from 30 to 50 increases viral coefficient—every customer tells more people—which compounds geometrically."

- **Balance conviction with data**: Wise's approach shows you can be both data-informed and conviction-driven. In interviews, demonstrate you use data to build conviction, not just to run endless experiments: "I analyzed NPS comments, talked to 20 customers, and built conviction that speed was the unlock—so we committed to a 2-year infrastructure project rather than iterating incrementally."

- **Think in flywheels**: Describe work as systems with reinforcing loops. Wise's flywheel: 10x better product → word of mouth → more customers → more lobbying power/scale → lower costs → even better product → more word of mouth. Frame your impact as contributions to flywheels.

- **Ask your interviewer about their word-of-mouth metrics**: "How do you measure word of mouth here? What's your NPS, and have you correlated it to actual referral behavior?" Shows you think like Nilan about systematic approaches to viral growth.
