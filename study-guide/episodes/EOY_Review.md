## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Five-Step Positioning (April Dunford)**: (1) Define competitive alternatives (including status quo); (2) List differentiated capabilities vs. alternatives; (3) Map capabilities to value themes; (4) Identify characteristics of customers who care most about that value; (5) Choose market category that makes your value obvious to those customers. Don't start with category—work backwards from differentiated value.

- **Three Bs of Behavioral Change (Kristen Berman)**: (1) **Behavior** - Get uncomfortably specific about the action you want users to take (not just "log in"); (2) **Barriers** - Reduce logistical friction (form fields, wait times) and cognitive barriers (uncertainty aversion, status quo bias); (3) **Benefits** - Increase immediate benefits, not just long-term value, because humans are present-biased.

- **Eigenquestions (Shishir Mehrotra)**: Instead of gathering endless data points, identify the one or two questions that, if answered, would unlock the most critical decisions. Example: For a teleportation device, ask "Is it safe enough for humans?" and "Is it more expensive CapEx or OpEx?" These two answers create decision quadrants for the entire go-to-market strategy.

- **PSHE Career Framework (Shishir Mehrotra)**: Problem, Solution, How, Execution. Junior PMs are handed P, S, and H—they just execute. Mid-level PMs are handed P and S—they figure out the how. Senior PMs are handed P—they create solutions. Staff+ PMs identify the problems. The "trough of disillusionment" occurs mid-career when growth shifts from scope to PSHE depth.

- **LNO Framework (Shreyas Doshi)**: Categorize all tasks as **Leverage** (10-100x return on effort), **Neutral** (1-1.1x return), or **Overhead** (less than 1x return). Spend maximum energy on L tasks when you're most productive. The same activity (filing a bug, taking notes) can be L, N, or O depending on context. Perfectionists default to treating all tasks equally—this is the trap.

- **Product Manager Core Competencies (Marty Cagan)**: Four essential areas: (1) Deep knowledge of users and customers; (2) Expertise in product data and analytics; (3) Understanding of all business functions (sales, marketing, finance, legal, compliance); (4) Knowledge of competitive landscape and industry trends. These are what you bring to an empowered team that designers and engineers don't have.

- **B2B Growth Prioritization (Elena Verna)**: Always start with product-led retention (activation + engagement/habit loops) before attempting product-led acquisition. Product-led acquisition only works if users habitually use your product, creating opportunities for virals, referrals, or content creation. If no one-to-many relationships exist in your product, rely on marketing-led and sales-led models instead.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Most analytics efforts fail because they gather measurements, not insights** (Crystal Widjaja). A measurement is "power users do 4x more bookings." An insight is "power users convert better with free shipping on high GMV baskets vs. discount codes, while non-power users don't." Insights require the why, provide context, and change your behavior—otherwise you're consuming entertainment, not news.

2. **Imposter syndrome correlates with growth periods** (Julie Zhou). Feeling unprepared and uncomfortable means you're in a situation you haven't encountered before, which is exactly when you learn fastest. After 7-8 years at Facebook, Julie realized discomfort and intense growth are "two sides of the same coin." Embrace it with curiosity rather than fear.

3. **Companies under-resource SEO and over-resource ads** (Ethan Smith). If you spend tens of millions on ads, why spend only $50k on SEO when the traffic potential is comparable? Signs SEO will work: addressable market is large, you have authority (1,000+ daily non-SEO visits, 1,000+ referring domains), and you can target personas even if direct search volume is low.

4. **~40% of B2B deals are lost to "no decision" (status quo)** (April Dunford). You're not just competing against alternatives on a shortlist—you're competing against spreadsheets, pen and paper, and interns. If you don't position well against the status quo, customers won't make any change at all.

5. **Small teams outperform large teams on an absolute scale** (Matt Mochary). After COVID layoffs (5-40% across portfolio companies), CEOs universally reported that within 60 days, companies were shipping more features, producing more code, and had higher NPS with fewer people. Coordination overhead and morale friction grow geometrically with headcount. WhatsApp, Instagram, Linear, and Notion prove small teams win.

6. **Freemium features should promote growth, not sacrifice monetization** (Elena Verna). Make something free if it: drives indirect monetization (virality/network effects), is commoditized anyway, enables your aha moment, or creates habit loops. Gate features that create friction for your growth model. Your freemium strategy must align with your strategic value of free.

7. **Product becomes less important as companies scale unless actively fought** (Marty Cagan, referencing Steve Jobs). As companies stop innovating, they celebrate sales, marketing, and finance people who become leaders. Good product people then leave for companies that value product, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. This theory from 1995 remains prescient today.

8. **Warning someone before difficult conversations prevents amygdala hijacking** (Matt Mochary). Start with: "This is going to be a difficult conversation. Take a few seconds to prepare yourself." Surprise triggers the amygdala violently. A few seconds of mental preparation lets people observe their emotions (fear, anger, sadness) rather than be consumed by them. Then actively listen to help them release emotions.

9. **Same-type activities can be L, N, or O tasks depending on context** (Shreyas Doshi). Filing a bug report for a critical issue affecting revenue is an L task (detailed report, annotations). Filing a bug for a minor UI glitch is an O task (one screenshot, hit submit). Notes from a contentious product review with the CEO are L (perfect the language). Notes from a standard meeting are O (list action items, send immediately).

10. **The best interview questions test judgment through eigenquestions** (Shishir Mehrotra). The teleportation device question reveals whether candidates can simplify complexity. Kids naturally find eigenquestions—it's a skill we unlearn. Great PMs quickly identify the one or two questions that unlock entire decision trees rather than gathering endless low-value information.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Practice eigenquestions thinking**: When given a case study or problem, resist gathering endless data. Instead, identify the 1-2 questions that would unlock the most important decisions. This demonstrates strategic thinking and judgment over data collection.

- **Use LNO framework to discuss prioritization**: When asked "how do you prioritize," explain that not all tasks deserve equal effort. Share examples of treating the same activity type (bug reports, notes, research) differently based on leverage, showing nuanced judgment.

- **Show growth mindset through discomfort stories**: When discussing career growth or challenges, frame periods of imposter syndrome or being "in over your head" as periods of maximum learning. This reframes weakness as growth orientation.

- **Demonstrate PSHE progression**: Structure your career narrative around the PSHE framework. Show how you went from executing others' plans to defining problems yourself. Use this language to describe your growth trajectory.

- **Lead with retention over acquisition**: For growth questions, demonstrate you understand retention (activation + engagement) must come before acquisition. Explain why product-led acquisition fails without habit formation.

- **Apply the Three Bs to product problems**: When discussing product improvements, structure answers around specific Behaviors (not vague goals), Barriers (both logistical and cognitive), and immediate Benefits (not just long-term value). This shows behavioral science sophistication.

- **Start positioning with competitive alternatives**: Don't jump straight to "market category" or features. Show you'd first identify what you're competing against (including status quo), then work backward to differentiated value.
