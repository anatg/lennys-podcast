# Bob Baxley — Design Executive & Advisor (Apple, Pinterest, ThoughtSpot)

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Bob Baxley
- **Role/Company**: Designer, executive, and advisor; built and led design teams at Apple (Apple Online Store, App Store design), Pinterest (Head of Design — one year), Yahoo (Yahoo Answers), and ThoughtSpot (VP Design); currently mentoring individuals and advising organizations on digital product design
- **Background**: 30+ year career at the intersection of design craft, product strategy, and culture. Studied film, worked in graphic design and music, found his medium in software. Deep believer that software is a medium like film or music, with corresponding emotional and moral obligations for its creators. Unusual perspectives on design org structure, the relationship between design and engineering, and what it means to build "great" software.

## TL;DR
Bob is the most philosophical voice on design in the catalog — but his philosophy is grounded in operational specifics. His core contributions: software is a medium with emotional obligations, design is a mindset not a function, great design requires waiting as long as possible to draw a picture, tenets (not principles) are what drive consistent decisions at scale, and design-led companies can only be design-led from the founding — you can never graft it on later.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Software as a Medium**: The foundational insight that shapes Bob's worldview. Software was invented in Northern California in the late 1960s partly because a small group at Stanford saw it as a new form of media on par with movies, music, and books. This meant it carries an emotional component: every piece of software produces an emotional response — confusion or empowerment, world-bigger or world-smaller. It is not a neutral tool like a hammer.
  
  Implication for builders: you can leave the emotional experience to chance (what most teams do), or you can be conscious of it — deciding intentionally what emotion you want users to have and designing toward that. "Design is not just the visual expression of an idea. It's the holistic mindset of imagining the future you want to live in and taking steps to make it real."

- **Moral Obligation to Build Great Software**: Almost every person in a modern economy now has hundreds of interactions with a phone or computer daily. Many of those interactions are confusing and frustrating. Each one takes a little energy and adds a little frustration. Builders are anonymous — nobody knows who designed the frustrating experience. This anonymity creates a blindspot: because you never see users, it's easy to lose sight of the scale of impact.
  
  Bob's practice: tell teams they're "at dinner" with millions of specific people tonight (the grandmother at a restaurant trying to pay with Toast's POS). You are either making her look like a superhero or a fool in front of her grandsons. This reframing collapses the distance between builder and user.
  
  The scale problem: "My team at Apple worked on products used by hundreds of millions of people. Nobody can really feel that from inside a team. You have to find ways to feel it."

- **What Design Actually Is**: Design is "clear thinking made visible" (Tufte). It is NOT aesthetics — that's the above-the-waterline iceberg. The real heavy lifting of design is building organizational alignment around:
  - **Vision**: What are we trying to achieve philosophically? Why do we exist?
  - **Mission**: How do we intend to achieve it?
  - **Design strategy**: How does the vision manifest in product decisions?
  - **Execution**: The specific interfaces and interactions
  
  Without this ladder, you have "bricks scattered across the backyard." With it, you have "an impenetrable wall." Apple ran a 30-country online store with 6 designers (any other company would have 60) because of extraordinary design clarity. Small teams can do more when there's less ambiguity about what they're trying to create.

- **Design-Led ≠ Designer-Led**: "A company is design-led does not mean it is designer-led." Anyone in any role can have a design mindset. Designers need the product mindset too. When Bob talks about companies being design-centric (Apple, Airbnb, Lego, Porsche, Patagonia), he means that everything they do ladders up into a coherent whole — the organization thinks in a design way. Google and Amazon are enormously successful but don't compete on design in the same way; they compete on other dimensions.

- **Design Must Be In the DNA at Founding — Can Never Be Grafted On**: Bob has never seen a company successfully graft design onto its culture after the fact. Design-centric companies have it in the root DNA from the beginning. When interviewing for a role, Bob looks for: can the CEO tell a credible story that traces design belief back to the founding? Not a recent initiative — a founding story.
  
  Practical application: before joining a company, ask yourself whether the thing you do is fundamentally valued there. "Why would you want to work in a place that doesn't value the thing that you do?"

- **Tenets vs. Principles**: Design principles are Applehood and motherpie — simple, clear, beautiful, fast. Nobody argues the opposite. They're platitudes, not decision-making tools.
  
  Tenets are decision-making tools. They resolve recurring debates once and for all. Keynote's founding tenets (from Steve Jobs): (1) Make it difficult to create ugly presentations; (2) Focus on cinematic quality transitions; (3) Optimize for innovation over PowerPoint compatibility. That third tenet alone settled a debate that would have consumed the team for years.
  
  Bob's ThoughtSpot tenets:
  1. **Documentation is a failure state**: Business users don't want to learn your software. If they need to consult a manual, you failed.
  2. **Start simple, let users opt into complexity**: Tableau started with complexity (for professional analysts). ThoughtSpot was designed for mere mortals; complexity available on request.
  3. **The entire product should look and feel like it came from a single mind**: Counter-force against enterprise fragmentation where each team optimizes their piece with no view of the whole.
  
  How to identify your tenets: look for recurring debates where teams dig in and bifurcate into camps. Have the debate once. Make an opinionated call. Put it behind you.

- **Wait as Long as Possible to Draw a Picture**: The most counterintuitive design advice Bob gives. The moment you draw something that looks remotely real — especially with generative AI tools that produce high-fidelity UIs instantly — everyone in the room gravitates toward that image and treats it as "the thing." The possibility space collapses from broad to narrow in a single meeting.
  
  The "primal mark" concept from art: the first mark on the canvas sets the baseline. Everything after is a response to that mark. If the first mark is wrong, everything compounds on the wrong foundation.
  
  Better approach: stay in words, whiteboards, conceptual conversation through second, third, fourth ideas before committing to any visual expression. Block frames (even more simplified than wireframes — just big rectangles and labels) are what Bob used at ThoughtSpot. Because they're so low-fidelity, people can't comment on what it looks like — they're forced to talk about what it is conceptually.
  
  Result at ThoughtSpot: when they finally moved from block frames to final comps, an agency could produce the high-res comps in a single day — because all the hard conceptual decisions had been made. The high-res work was fast because the hard work was done in the ambiguous stage.
  
  Script → storyboard → production metaphor from film: if you try to fix script problems during production, you waste enormous money on set. Similarly, you can't use gen AI prototyping as a substitute for conceptual thinking.

- **Design Reporting to Engineering (Controversial Take)**: At Apple under Steve Jobs, design always reported to engineering. Bob's argument: design is most effective at impacting what ships when it's treated as phase zero of the engineering process, not as an adjunct to product.
  
  When design reports to product, product and design can cook up things that don't make sense technically, leave engineering out, and bring them in at the end — engineering is less enthusiastic, doesn't understand the context, can't extrapolate well from specs.
  
  Alternative to full org restructuring: identify "creative technologists" — engineers who can sit comfortably with design ambiguity, who don't immediately start coding, who can think about what a thing could be before thinking about how to build it. Include a small group from all functions (PM, engineer, designer) that can sit in the ambiguous early phase. Don't bring something "fully baked" to engineering — bring them in at inception so they fall in love with it.

- **The Beatles Rule for Team Size**: 20 people worked on the original Mac. 24 are on the iPhone patent. "You get The Beatles with four people, you don't get The Beatles with eight people and you certainly don't get it with 24." Scenius (Brian Eno's term: the collective genius of a small group that transcends what any individual could do) is impossible at large scale.
  
  The important nuance: this applies to figuring out what to build — the discovery and direction phase. Once you know what Disneyland is, you can scale the production team. Vision with a lot of people is almost impossible. Execution with a lot of people is fine if vision is clear.

- **Vision Statements as a Practical Tool**: Companies fail at design because they don't know who they are. If you don't have a vision, designers face enormous ambiguity — they have to explore a wide design space because there's no guidance about which direction is "us."
  
  Examples of useful visions: Google = "organizing all the world's information" (never achievable, always over the horizon, guides acquisitions). Amazon = "Earth's most customer-centric company." Disneyland = "the happiest place on earth" (signals to an employee everything from how to pick up trash to how to greet guests).
  
  Yahoo: never had a clear vision. Stumbled into directory, added things around the edges, never made coherent sense. Designers had to explore enormous option spaces.
  
  Apple: no explicit vision statement, but "personal computing can have a transformative effect on the lives of individuals" functionally describes how they think about everything.
  
  If you find design taking too long, the most likely root cause is not a design problem — it's that the brief is too ambiguous. More context and specificity upstream makes design faster, not slower.

- **Apple Culture as Both Asset and Liability (Transplanting)**: Apple has an extraordinarily strong culture. The "Apple car wash" takes new employees through a recalibration to Apple's way. There's a matching car wash needed when leaving Apple. Bob's mistake at Pinterest: left on a Friday, started Monday, behaved exactly the way he did at Apple — direct, intense, fighting hard. Pinterest had a poster in every conference room saying "say the hard thing" — which Apple never needed to say because the culture did it automatically.
  
  Lesson for anyone switching from one strong culture to another: hold onto the **values** (attention to detail, excellence, user obsession) but recalibrate the **behaviors** (how those values are expressed in the new context). The new company likely hired you because of the values you embody, not the specific behaviors you use to express them.

- **Metrics as Consequence, Not Driver**: Bob has never seen a product be successful that used metrics as the primary driver of what to build. Successful companies use metrics as consequences — feedback to evaluate whether decisions were right — and then use them to triangulate and improve future decisions. "I believe in my heart that if we prioritize the user, wonderful things will happen for all the metrics including the money metrics."

- **AI as a Life Coach (Unusual Use Case)**: Bob uses AI (ChatGPT) explicitly as a life coach — not as a design tool. He gave it the prompt "what is an outdated mindset I'm holding onto that's not serving me?" and got back useful self-reflection about his attachment to control (valuable at Apple, less valuable in his current advisory/writing phase). 
  
  Key insight: by feeding ChatGPT context over time, the AI statistically reflected patterns from his "undermind" (term from Guy Claxton's *Hare Brain, Tortoise Mind* — the pre-linguistic processing layer of the brain that generates intuitions before they're put into language). The AI isn't pushing you toward new directions, but it is reflecting back patterns that were already there — making the implicit explicit. It's not a replacement for human coaching, but it's an unusual and useful mirror.

- **John Houbolt and the Moon Landing**: Bob's favorite story about ideas needing champions. When JFK committed America to the moon, nobody knew how to get there. Three options were debated: (1) direct ascent (big rocket, land the whole thing); (2) Earth Orbit Rendezvous (assemble in orbit); (3) Lunar Orbit Rendezvous (bring a small separate lander spacecraft, leave it in orbit, only land the small craft). Houbolt, a relatively junior engineer, championed LOR based on a paper from Yuri Kondrachev (Ukraine, 1918). LOR was the only mass-feasible option. Houbolt went around the NASA hierarchy and sent a famous 9-page memo that risked his career. He was right. Even von Braun eventually came around.
  
  Lessons: great ideas have long lives — they wait for their time. Ideas need champions willing to put themselves on the line. Advocates for ideas are not self-promoters — they're doing it for the idea, not for themselves.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Software is a medium with emotional consequences.** Every user interaction either empowers or frustrates. At scale, this is a moral obligation. Find ways to see users using software in the wild — not in labs, not through dashboards — actually using your product and other people's products.

2. **Design is not aesthetics — it's how you ladder from vision to execution so everything is coherent.** The companies with the smallest design teams are often the ones with the clearest design vision. Ambiguity of vision generates design overhead.

3. **Wait as long as possible to draw a picture.** The first image sets the baseline. Stay in words and concepts through your second, third, fourth ideas. The high-resolution work is fast once the conceptual decisions are made. Using gen AI prototyping before you know what you're building gives you first-order spaghetti faster.

4. **Tenets, not principles.** "Simple, clear, beautiful" are not decision-making tools — nobody argues the opposite. Tenets resolve recurring debates: "optimize for innovation over PowerPoint compatibility" is a tenet. Pick the debates that keep coming back and make an opinionated call once.

5. **Design-led culture can only be founded, not installed.** You cannot graft design onto a company after the fact. It either exists in the root DNA from the beginning or it doesn't. When evaluating where to work, find a credible story tracing design belief back to the founders.

6. **Good ideas have long lives and need champions.** Houbolt's LOR memo existed because Kondrachev's 1918 paper was still alive. Your best ideas may not land immediately. Push through proper channels, then push harder through improper ones if you genuinely believe it.

7. **Going fast and going together are in tension — choose wisely.** "If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together." Gen AI tools can help you go very fast on spaghetti. But making something meaningful, sustainable, something you're proud of — that takes the village. Design can't raise the baby alone.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- The "primal mark" problem is massively amplified by gen AI prototyping tools: a PM can get a working UI from a prompt in minutes, but that first-order output collapses the design possibility space before any real thinking has happened. The antidote is more upstream conceptual work, not less
- AI raises the stakes for vision clarity: when the cost of building drops toward zero, the ability to build becomes table stakes. What matters is knowing what to build — which requires the vision → tenets → strategy → execution ladder that Bob describes
- Bob's AI-as-life-coach use case is genuinely novel: using AI to surface patterns from your "undermind" (pre-linguistic intuition) into language, making implicit knowledge explicit. It reflects rather than directs
- The moral obligation argument intensifies with AI: AI-generated content and experiences will interact with billions of people at once. The anonymity problem gets worse as AI-generated software is even further removed from any human creator

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"What is design and why does it matter strategically?"**: Design is a mindset, not a function or an aesthetic. It's how you create coherence from vision through execution so everything ladders together. Companies with clear design vision can run enormous products with tiny teams (Apple's 6 designers for a 30-country store). Ambiguity generates design overhead.
- **"How do you build design tenets for your product?"**: Identify recurring debates where teams bifurcate — where the same question keeps coming up with different answers. Have the debate once, make an opinionated call, and encode it as a tenet. Tenets must be decision-making tools — something people would actually argue both sides of.
- **"How should design, product, and engineering work together?"**: Everyone is a maker. Bring all functions into the conceptual phase before anyone draws a picture. Design is phase zero of engineering, not a handoff from product. Respect functional boundaries — PMs own the roadmap, designers own the UI problem-solving space, engineers own implementation. Creative tension is good; functional disrespect is not.
- **"What makes a product worth being proud of?"**: At minimum, every 6-12 months, ask yourself: am I proud of this? Is this representative of my best work given the constraints? Did I think about the user on the other side of the glass? At most, ask it constantly. The companies that set out to delight users consistently discover that the business metrics follow.
- **"What counterintuitive advice do you have about building product fast?"**: Wait to draw a picture. Counterintuitively, staying in concepts and conversation longer makes the visual production faster — not slower. The high-res work is fast when conceptual decisions are made. Jumping to a prototype before the conceptual foundation is set means you'll churn through production rather than exploration.
