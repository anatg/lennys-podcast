## TL;DR
Kristen Berman shares how behavioral science and design can drive meaningful product changes by understanding the predictable ways humans behave irrationally. Using the "Three Bs" framework (Behavior, Barriers, Benefits), her team has helped companies like TikTok reduce misinformation shares by 24%, One Medical increase appointment bookings by 20%, and Credit Karma increase recurring deposits by 18%.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Three Bs Framework**: The foundational model for behavior change:
  1. **Behavior**: Define the uncomfortably specific action you want users to take (not just "log in" but what they do after). Get so specific that your team argues about it. Example: "Within seven days of starting the app, users complete two 10-minute workouts with two different instructors."
  2. **Barriers**: Remove obstacles (logistical and cognitive) that prevent the behavior. Logistical = form fields, wait times. Cognitive = uncertainty aversion, status quo bias, information aversion, optimism bias.
  3. **Benefits**: Increase the *immediate* benefits of taking action. Humans are present-biased—we prioritize our present self over future self. Build in completion bias, social desirability, or other immediate psychological rewards.

- **Behavioral Diagnosis**: Map every single step (200-300 screenshots) required to complete a behavior, then overlay the psychologies affecting each step. This reveals why behavior change is difficult before you try to fix it.

- **Right for Wrong**: Help people do the right thing for the wrong reason. Motivate desired behavior with immediate, tangible benefits even if they're not the "real" reason. Examples: giving donuts for vaccines, pizza at polling stations, Peloton instructor shoutouts to maintain workout streaks.

- **Hot vs. Cold State**: In "hot" states (high emotion, fast-paced), people make quick decisions. To reduce undesired behavior, create friction to cool them down. To increase desired behavior, reduce friction.

- **Rules of Thumb (Heuristics)**: Replace complex decision-making with simple rules. Instead of deciding "Should I take a Lyft?" each time, create a rule: "I don't take Lyft on weekdays, only weekends." Makes behavior change cognitively easier.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Behavior change requires defining specific behaviors, not just outcomes**: Teams are good at defining outcomes (revenue, engagement) but fail to specify the exact action. Without an uncomfortably specific behavior, you cannot identify the psychologies driving it or design effective interventions.

2. **What users say they want ≠ what actually changes behavior**: A FinTech app's most requested feature was budgeting. After building and testing with 10,000 users, it had zero impact on spending. Budgeting puts too much work on users. Better approach: defaults and simple rules of thumb.

3. **Adding friction can increase conversion when it makes people think about benefits**: Asking questions during signup increases friction but can boost conversion by 133% (Trunk Club example). Questions insert ideas into users' heads and engage them with benefits. Replace carousels with interactive questions (multiple choice, not open-ended).

4. **Status quo bias is one of the strongest forces**: People do what they did yesterday. To change behavior, you must either increase motivation significantly or make the new behavior dramatically easier. Most startups are fighting against users' existing habits.

5. **Incentives determine whether behavioral science is used ethically or manipulatively**: Kristen became a "predatory lender" for LendingClub when paid only if she hit a 5-point conversion bump. Set incentives on customer-aligned behaviors over shorter timeframes (annual vs. quarterly) to avoid dark patterns.

6. **Present bias means people need reasons to act *today*, not tomorrow**: Even if your product provides huge long-term value, users won't engage without immediate benefits. Build in completion bias, streaks, social proof, or other near-term psychological rewards.

7. **Interviews don't predict job performance**: Interviews are good for assessing culture fit and affinity, not performance. Use skill assessments and work trials to predict actual job performance.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Speak in behaviors, not outcomes**: When discussing product strategy, don't just say "increase engagement" or "drive retention." Describe the specific user action (e.g., "get users to book a second appointment within 30 days"). This shows you understand that outcomes are driven by specific behaviors.

- **Demonstrate psychological awareness**: Reference common biases like present bias, status quo effect, completion bias, or uncertainty aversion when explaining product decisions. Show you understand *why* users behave the way they do, not just *what* they do.

- **Emphasize removing barriers over adding features**: Talk about experiments where you removed friction (logistical or cognitive) rather than building new functionality. Shows product judgment and restraint.

- **Use the "Right for Wrong" framing**: Demonstrate sophistication by explaining how you motivated desired behaviors through immediate benefits even if they're not the "ideal" motivation. Shows pragmatism about human psychology.

- **Always mention testing and measurement**: Kristen tested 5 popup variations with 1,000+ users before building into TikTok. Always tie behavioral hypotheses to experimentation and quantitative validation.

- **Show awareness of ethical considerations**: Discuss how you've thought about incentive structures and avoided dark patterns. Reference customer outcomes and long-term value, not just conversion metrics.

- **Map user journeys at high resolution**: Describe how you've done detailed flow analysis (not just high-level journey maps) to identify specific friction points. Shows thoroughness and user empathy.
