## TL;DR
Janna Bastow challenges traditional timeline-based roadmapping, arguing that Gantt chart roadmaps create false certainty and set teams up for failure. Her "Now, Next, Later" framework treats roadmaps as strategy prototypes rather than delivery commitments, acknowledging the cone of uncertainty and allowing teams to add dates only where truly necessary (regulatory deadlines, seasonal launches) while maintaining flexibility everywhere else.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Now, Next, Later Roadmapping**: Replace timeline-based roadmaps with three columns (Now/Next/Later) that acknowledge uncertainty increases over time. This removes the implicit promise of delivery dates for everything while still providing clarity on priorities and sequencing. Dates can be added to specific items when genuinely needed (GDPR compliance, holiday shopping season), but most items remain flexible.

- **Roadmap as Strategy Prototype**: The value isn't in the roadmap artifact itself—it's in the roadmapping *process*. Just as you prototype a feature with mockups to test assumptions before building, you prototype your strategy with a roadmap to validate problem sequencing and priorities with stakeholders, customers, and team members. Expect to throw away and revise constantly.

- **Soft Launch vs. Hard Launch Separation**: Decouple engineering delivery from marketing launches. Let developers ship when ready (soft launch), then give marketing a working product to test, gather testimonials, create materials, and plan a proper launch weeks or months later (hard launch). This eliminates the stress of coordinating two fundamentally different project types with different timelines.

- **Product Management as Sales Experimentation**: Product teams should ask for the same leeway as sales teams—nobody expects sales to predict *which* specific deals will close, only that their process (pipeline, calls, experiments) will yield results. Similarly, PMs should be accountable for running experiments and moving metrics, not for predicting which specific features will succeed before testing them.

- **Product Vision Template** (adapted from Geoffrey Moore): "For [target customer], who [statement of need/opportunity], the [product name] is a [product category] that [key benefit/reason to buy]. Unlike [alternative], our product [statement of differentiation]." This elevator pitch structure forces clarity on positioning and vision.

- **Culture as Calcification**: Changing large company culture is like chipping away at limestone buildup—you can't fix it all at once. Find a small pocket (a "startup lab" within the business) with a strong leader, let them work differently, then spread the practices team by team. Requires buy-in from above but doesn't demand whole-company transformation immediately.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Timeline roadmaps create false certainty and hidden failure**: The format itself (time on X-axis, features on Y-axis) assigns implicit due dates to everything, forcing teams into commitments before they have information. Every early ProdPad customer requested the ability to "move everything by a month" because *no one* was actually delivering on their promised timelines—the problem wasn't individual execution, it was the format forcing unrealistic promises.

2. **Psychological safety unlocks everything else**: The best product teams consistently demonstrate one trait—the ability to question each other, speak up about problems, and challenge decisions at any level. This safety enables retrospectives, which enable continuous improvement, which naturally leads to better practices like discovery, experimentation, and flexible roadmapping. Without safety, teams calcify around bad processes.

3. **Consistency matters more than perfection in community building**: Mind the Product grew to hundreds of cities not through flawless execution but through showing up every month, every year, regardless of circumstances. ProductTank meetups happened consistently; conferences happened annually. Surrounding yourself with people who help maintain that consistency (finding speakers, curating content, expanding networks) compounds over time.

4. **Public speaking improves through recording and replay**: The most uncomfortable but effective practice is recording yourself and watching the playback. Most people hate hearing their own voice, but it reveals exactly what needs improvement. Combine with professional coaching (which can rewrite jokes, improve pacing, fix delivery) and deliberate practice (being able to deliver the talk flawlessly while doing groceries or showering).

5. **Conference economics are brutal and non-lean**: Mistakes at conference scale happen in thousands of dollars, not hundreds—missed speaker flights, printing errors, venue bankruptcies, food shortages. You can't iterate during the event; you must wait a year. The business requires years of community goodwill beforehand and carries massive risk (underselling tickets by even a small margin can break the entire event).

6. **The audience wants you to succeed**: When Janna blanked in front of 200 people early in her career, she simply said "I'm really sorry, I'm going to start again"—and everyone was fine with it. Speakers fear worst-case scenarios that rarely happen, and when mistakes occur, audiences are humanized by them and more forgiving than feared. People are rooting for you, not hoping you fail.

7. **Enterprise transformation starts with advocates, not top-down mandates**: Large organizations can't change all at once—culture is calcified. Find one division or department with a strong advocate who gets the new approach, let them succeed, then have multiple pockets band together before approaching VP-level leadership. Multi-year transformations work by proving value in small experiments first.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Frame your PM work as experimentation, not delivery promises**: When discussing past roadmaps in interviews, emphasize the discovery process, hypothesis testing, and learning cycles rather than "we committed to ship X by Y date." Show you understand the difference between output and outcomes.

- **Demonstrate psychological safety practices**: Highlight examples where you ran retrospectives, changed course based on team feedback, or questioned leadership decisions constructively. This signals you can build high-functioning teams, not just execute tasks.

- **Prepare a clear product vision using a template**: Practice articulating past products using the "For [customer], who [need], the [product] is a [category] that [benefit]. Unlike [alternative], our product [differentiation]" structure. This shows strategic thinking and communication clarity.

- **Show how you handled uncertainty and scope changes**: Timeline roadmaps are still common in many orgs. Prepare stories about navigating changing priorities, managing stakeholder expectations when dates slipped, or negotiating flexibility. Don't claim you always hit every deadline—show how you adapted.

- **Practice your "presentation" in behavioral interviews**: Record yourself answering common questions, then watch the replay (uncomfortable but effective). Notice verbal tics, clarity of examples, and storytelling flow. The discomfort of self-review is a forcing function for improvement.
