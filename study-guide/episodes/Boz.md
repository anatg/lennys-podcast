# Andrew "Boz" Bosworth — CTO of Meta, Creator of Facebook News Feed

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Andrew "Boz" Bosworth
- **Role/Company**: Chief Technology Officer at Meta (formerly Facebook)
- **Background**: Joined Facebook on January 9th, 2006 as one of the first engineers (approximately 10th-16th depending on counting). Over his 18+ year tenure, he created Facebook News Feed (the first algorithmically ranked content feed), built the original mobile ads platform that saved Facebook's IPO, ran ads for ~4 years, helped build messaging, profile, timeline, groups, and the internal engineering boot camp. As VP of ads and business platform, he led engineering, product, research, analytics, and design. Created Meta's AR/VR organization (Reality Labs) in 2017 and currently leads all AR, VR, mixed reality efforts and consumer hardware including Quest and Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses. Was Mark Zuckerberg's TA in an AI class at Harvard. National collegiate Taekwondo champion, recruited to Harvard for football, went to 14 proms in high school, has "Veritas" tattoo in monotype font.

## TL;DR
Boz shares lessons from 18+ years at Meta including creating News Feed and building Reality Labs, emphasizing that communication is fundamentally the job of leadership and that most people drastically underutilize their managers—nobody wants you to succeed more than your leader because your success makes their life easier. He reveals the unglamorous reality of startup life (waking every 4 hours for 2 years to check for attacks), the importance of extreme transparency in empowering talented people, and how Meta's failure to communicate their long-term AI and Reality Labs investments led to massive stock volatility before the recent turnaround.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **HPMs (Highlight, People, Me)**: Weekly update format used at Facebook 2008-2014 for structured communication across the organization, focusing on highlights first, then people updates, then personal notes.

- **Communication is the Job**: Boz's foundational framework that all leadership impact comes exclusively through communication—either through creating artifacts or verbalizations that affect other humans. Corollary: "Listening is the Job."

- **You Cannot Not Communicate**: Silence itself is communication; you're always communicating something with your face, clothes, body language, and presence. Understanding this helps leaders be more intentional.

- **Extreme Ownership**: Concept from US Marines and SEALs applied to leadership—taking full responsibility for outcomes enables you to identify what you can control and change.

- **Eye of Sauron**: Metaphor for founder/CEO attention on the most important projects. When the Eye turns toward your work, you get extraordinary resources and focus, but it won't stay there forever.

- **Top-down vs. Bottoms-up as a Spectrum**: Culture isn't binary; it exists on a spectrum. Meta is extremely bottoms-up but Mark Zuckerberg can still make top-down calls when needed.

- **Strong Opinions, Loosely Held**: Mark Zuckerberg's approach—takes strong positions but triangulates feedback from multiple people throughout the day, processes input, and adjusts over time.

- **Org Charts as Communication Devices**: Org structures aren't just physical arrangements but tools to communicate priorities, relationships, and how the organization thinks about itself.

- **Repetition Never Spoiled the Prayer**: Using multiple modalities and repetition to communicate important messages—people need to hear things many times in different ways.

- **Identity Threat Recognition**: Your worst behavior emerges when your core self-conception is questioned. Recognizing this moment and choosing curiosity over defensiveness is crucial.

- **Gell-Mann Amnesia**: The phenomenon of reading expert articles as gospel when you know other articles in the same publication are wrong—applies to how markets and media cover companies you're inside.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Leverage Your Leaders More Directly**: The most frequent advice Boz gives is that people underutilize their managers. Your job is to get things done, not necessarily do them yourself without help. Make it easy for your manager to help you—draft emails, frame specific questions, provide context. As Patrick Stewart said about auditions: "Nobody wants you to succeed more than the person you are auditioning for, because they want you to be awesome. Because as soon as you are awesome, they're done."

2. **Early Career Compound Learning**: Jumping between roles and projects early in your career builds compound knowledge that pays off exponentially later. Boz rotated through News Feed, site speed, infrastructure, Bootcamp, messaging, groups, and ads—each stint informed the next. Optimize for learning early even if it means slower initial promotion trajectory. Mark told him "Boz, that's not a real job" when he was jumping fires every 6 months, but that foundation proved invaluable.

3. **The Unglamorous Reality of Startups**: Early startup days involve tremendous sacrifice—120 hour weeks, no hobbies, constant vigilance. Boz didn't sleep more than four hours at a time for ~2 years, waking every four hours to check if anyone was attacking the site. Engineers bought components at Fry's Electronics, built servers themselves, drove to the colo to rack them. The romanticism only comes from successes; many sacrifice equally without the payoff. "They don't tell you about that stuff in the movies."

4. **Transparency Empowers Talent**: Information transparency is core to Meta's culture—anyone can ask Zuck questions at Q&As, data is shared publicly internally. This empowers talented people to make better decisions but requires systems to manage information overload and clear guardrails on where people have freedom versus constraints.

5. **Communication Failed During Meta's Downturn**: Meta had the largest single day stock drop in history (followed 18 months later by the largest single day gain). Stock went from ~$80 to $487. Boz identifies Meta's core failure as not effectively communicating with the market about their long-term AI and Reality Labs investments. "Communication is the job. We really did not communicate effectively with the market around our future investments." Companies that kill future growth when times are tough are committing to dying later.

6. **Being Honest Doesn't License Being Unkind**: Delivery matters as much as truth. Boz's biggest professional regrets were moments of unkindness. People don't remember what you said—they remember how you made them feel. You can be radically honest while still being kind in how you deliver difficult messages.

7. **Trust Yourself Includes Trusting You Can Handle Failure**: Boz's family motto is "Trust yourself"—which includes taking risks because you trust you can handle when they don't pay out. Most failures that matter are personal failures affecting people, not technical or product failures.

8. **Give Things Time Before Assuming You Won't Like Them**: Getting passionate about something requires giving it time. Don't assume you won't like something—give it at least 6 months. Boz wasn't initially excited about ads but it became one of his most impactful areas.

9. **You Know More Than The Critics**: When you're inside a company, you know more than the analysts, media, and critics do. Apply Gell-Mann Amnesia in reverse—trust your inside knowledge while staying curious about what you don't know. Lou Holtz quote: "You're never as good as they say you are when you're winning, and you're never as bad as they say you are when you're losing."

10. **Embrace Curiosity in Disagreement**: When you feel a strong internal clinch or defensiveness, that's often identity threat. Your worst behavior emerges in those moments. Instead, choose profound curiosity: "Wow, we do not see this thing the same way. That is fascinating." Ami Vora (legendary PM at Meta) modeled this perfectly in a conflict with Boz.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- The first AI code written at Facebook was for News Feed ranking—Boz built what was "probably the first consumer AI that was in a website of that kind around content."
- Boz built anti-spam/anti-scraping defense with "pretty rudimentary" AI in Facebook's early days.
- News Feed created "the most efficient monetizing surface in history outside of Search" through AI-powered ranking.
- FAIR (Meta's AI research lab) is the second most cited research lab in AI behind Google—Meta has been investing in AI for 10+ years alongside Reality Labs.
- Ray-Ban Meta glasses now have multimodal AI capabilities—you can use the camera to ask Meta AI assistant about things in your view. Example: AI gave Boz directions in a ski village by analyzing the visual scene through the glasses camera.
- Internal AR glasses prototypes allow controlling slides with gestures and displaying notes during speeches—AI integration with AR/VR hardware is creating new interaction paradigms.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate how you leverage leadership**: Don't try to be the hero who solves everything alone. Show examples where you strategically asked for help, made it easy for leaders to assist you by providing context and drafts, and accelerated outcomes. Frame this as strategic resource utilization, not weakness.

- **Show compound learning across domains**: Highlight how you've built knowledge across different areas (product, engineering, design, business) and how each experience informed the next. Early career breadth creates later career depth and unique insights.

- **Communicate your communication strategy**: Meta your communication—explain not just what you did but how you communicated it to different stakeholders using different modalities. Show awareness that execution includes communication, not just building.

- **Demonstrate ownership with curiosity**: When discussing disagreements or failures, show extreme ownership while also demonstrating curiosity about other perspectives. Avoid defensiveness. The "identity threat" framework helps explain moments of conflict.

- **Balance transparency with judgment**: Meta's culture of extreme transparency is powerful but requires judgment about what to share when. Show you understand the tradeoffs—transparency empowers but can create noise and leaks.

- **Know when to go deep vs. delegate**: Discuss how you've thought about when founder/exec attention (the "Eye of Sauron") should focus on your area versus when you should operate autonomously. Show strategic thinking about organizational attention as a resource.

- **Frame long-term bets**: Be able to articulate why long-term investments (like Meta's Reality Labs or AI) matter even when short-term metrics don't look good. Show you can balance quarterly execution with multi-year vision.
