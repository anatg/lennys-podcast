## TL;DR
The traditional design process is dead—engineering velocity driven by AI coding tools has fundamentally changed how designers work, shifting from 60-70% mocking/prototyping to 30-40%, with the rest spent pairing with engineers and implementing code directly. Design work now stratifies into two types: supporting rapid execution (not blocking engineers who can ship with AI assistants) and creating 3-6 month visions (not 2-5 years) that point teams in the right direction.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Legibility Framework (Evan Tana)**: A 2x2 matrix evaluating founders and ideas as "legible" (clear/understood) or "illegible" (frontier/unclear). The sweet spot is illegible ideas with energy around them—ideas that don't make sense yet but attract attention. Designers should act like VCs internally, spotting illegible prototypes and transforming them into legible products through UX and storytelling. Claude Studio (an internal prototype) was illegible but had energy; it eventually influenced Cowork's skills framework and UI.

- **Two Types of Design Work in the AI Era**:
  1. **Execution Support**: Working directly with engineers as they ship rapidly with AI tools, doing last-mile polish, pairing on implementation, consulting rather than gate-keeping
  2. **Vision/Direction Setting**: Creating 3-6 month (not multi-year) visions, often as prototypes rather than decks, to align teams when anyone can build anything

- **Three Designer Archetypes for Hiring**:
  1. **Strong Generalists (Block-shaped)**: 80th percentile in multiple core skills (not just T-shaped but approaching block-shaped with multiple deep competencies)
  2. **Deep Specialists (Deep T)**: Top 10% industry specialists who go exceptionally deep in one area (e.g., engineering-level technical depth, or world-class visual design)
  3. **Crack New Grads**: Early-career people who are wise beyond their years, humble, eager to learn, unburdened by old processes

- **Low-Leverage Time Paradox**: Tasks that appear "low leverage" (testing products exhaustively, fixing bugs yourself, hand-making anniversary cards) often become high leverage when done by senior leaders because they demonstrate care, build product intimacy, and create cultural impact that only you can create.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The design process taught as gospel is dying**: The traditional discover-diverge-converge process doesn't work when engineers can spin up seven Claude instances and ship features faster than designers can mock them up. Designers must let go of trying to preserve the old process and adapt to engineering's new velocity.

2. **Build trust through speed, not polish**: Launching "research previews" with known flaws is acceptable if you publicly commit to rapid iteration based on feedback. The brand damage comes from launching early and then never improving, not from launching imperfect products. Anthropic maintains trust by visibly responding to feedback and shipping fixes quickly.

3. **Engineering tools changed first, design is forced to follow**: It's not that design decided to change—engineering's ability to build so quickly with AI has forced design to adapt. Designers now spend less time on mocks and more time pairing with engineers, implementing polish directly in code, and providing real-time direction.

4. **Chat interfaces won't go away**: Despite expectations that chatbots were temporary, talking/chatting scales across all levels of intelligence (from low to 200+ IQ) and provides infinite flexibility that baked UIs cannot match. The future is likely hybrid: chat/terminal for flexibility plus generated/interactive widgets for specific tasks.

5. **Non-deterministic AI demands different design processes**: You can't mock up all states of an AI interaction; you can't predict use cases in advance. You must ship with real models, watch real users, and discover use cases through usage. This fundamentally breaks traditional design's reliance on comprehensive prototyping before shipping.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Designers now implement code directly**: With tools like Claude Code, designers polish features in production code, making last-mile improvements rather than relying on engineers. Jenny spends part of each day "in code" implementing polish.

- **The abstraction layer keeps rising**: While current designers benefit from learning some coding, AI tools will likely continue moving up abstraction layers—eventually designers may not need to understand individual lines of code, just how to direct AI to implement their vision.

- **AI will improve at taste and judgment**: Designers cling to the idea that AI will never match human taste/judgment in design, but Jenny believes AI will get better at this. However, someone still needs to be accountable for decisions—similar to how engineers remain accountable even when Claude writes all the code.

- **Humans remain valuable for decision accountability**: Even as AI generates ideas (as Boris noted, Claude Code now helps decide what to build), someone human must still be accountable for what actually gets built and shipped. The hardest parts of building software aren't coding—they're resolving disagreements about what should exist.

- **Keep up or fall behind**: At Anthropic, it's not just designers struggling to keep up with engineers—engineers themselves ask "how do we keep up with ourselves?" given their seven AI agents constantly running. The entire industry is in this acceleration together.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate adaptability and tool fluency**: In interviews, show you're actively using AI coding tools (Claude Code, v0, etc.) and adapting your process, not clinging to traditional methods. Share examples of how you've shifted your workflow in the past year.

- **Explain your thinking, not just your decisions**: When giving design feedback, articulate the principles behind your choices so others can extract patterns and make better decisions without you. This is especially important when working with engineers who are implementing directly.

- **Show you can spot illegible ideas**: Reference the legibility framework—demonstrate you can identify promising prototypes or ideas that have energy but aren't clear yet, and that you know how to transform them into legible products through design and storytelling.

- **Prepare for "design velocity" questions**: Be ready to discuss how you balance craft with speed, how you decide what's worth detailed mocking versus letting engineers prototype directly, and how you maintain quality when shipping constantly.

- **Emphasize accountability over control**: Frame yourself as someone who takes accountability for decisions rather than someone who gates or blocks. Show you understand the designer's role is evolving from "approver" to "direction-setter and execution partner."
