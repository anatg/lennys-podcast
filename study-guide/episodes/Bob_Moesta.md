## TL;DR
Bob Moesta explains that Jobs to Be Done is about understanding the context and outcomes that drive customer behavior, not just pain points. The key is studying "struggling moments" through detailed customer interviews to uncover the real competitive landscape and design solutions that help customers make progress.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models
- **Four Forces Model**: F1 (Push from current situation) and F2 (Pull toward new outcome) must be greater than F3 (Anxiety of new solution) and F4 (Habits of present) for customers to switch
- **Jobs to Be Done Timeline**: First thought → Passive looking → Active looking → Deciding → First use → Ongoing use - meet customers where they are in their buying journey
- **Context + Outcome = Job**: Focus on the situational context and desired progress, not just features or pain points
- **Hire and Fire Criteria**: Understanding what customers will stop using when they adopt your product reveals true competition

## Top Insights (Timeless)
1. **People hire products, they don't buy them** - Products are hired to make progress in specific contexts, which reveals unexpected competitive landscapes (Snickers vs. protein drinks, not Milky Way)
2. **Struggling moments drive all innovation** - Demand exists before products; study the moments when people feel compelled to change behavior, not just general pain points
3. **Context makes the irrational rational** - When customer behavior seems illogical, you're missing context. The "anomalies" often reveal the real job to be done
4. **Supply and demand are less connected than we think** - Most products fail because they make trade-offs customers don't agree with, not because of poor execution
5. **Interview people who recently acted, not who intend to act** - "Bitchin' ain't switchin'" - study people who actually purchased or switched, not those who say they will

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **Practice customer interview techniques**: Learn to ask follow-up questions like "Tell me more about that" and "Give me an example" rather than leading with "why"
- **Focus on behavioral evidence**: In interviews, emphasize recent actions customers took rather than future intentions or general preferences
- **Understand competitive landscapes**: Study what customers currently use and what they'd stop using, not just direct product competitors
- **Frame problems as progress**: Instead of talking about features, discuss the progress customers are trying to make in their specific context
