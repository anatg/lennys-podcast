## TL;DR
Scott Belsky shares hard-won lessons from founding Behance, leading product at Adobe, and angel investing: develop product sense through empathy over passion for solutions, obsess over the "first mile" (onboarding) because customers are "lazy, vain, and selfish" in their first 30 seconds, and in resource-constrained times, resourcefulness (muscle) beats resources (carbs). He advocates doing half the features you think you need, measuring conviction to decide when to quit, and embracing AI as a tool that expands possibility surface area rather than replaces human ingenuity.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **First Mile Experience (Lazy/Vain/Selfish Rule)**: In the first 30 seconds of using any product, users are lazy (want it done quickly), vain (want to look good to colleagues/friends), and selfish (want immediate success without learning curves). Most teams spend the final mile of development time considering the first mile of customer experience—reverse this. The first mile includes onboarding, orientation, and defaults.

- **Conviction Test for Persistence vs. Pivoting**: Ask founders/teams: "Knowing all you know now, do you have more or less conviction in the problem and solution than when you started?" If more conviction despite struggles, stay in the messy middle. If less conviction ("if I knew then what I know now, I wouldn't have done this"), it's time to quit or pivot. Conviction is the fuel for entrepreneurship.

- **Half-Half Principle**: Do half the features you want to build, offer half the options, focus on half the market. Optimize for the problems you *want* to have (customers asking for more features) rather than preventing every possible issue. When Behance killed features (Tip Exchange, Groups, portfolio color controls), core metrics (project publishing) increased each time.

- **Three Questions for Product Clarity**: Every screen/experience should answer: (1) How did I get here? (2) What do I do now? (3) What do I do next? If users can't answer these, the "object model" (mental model of how the product works) is unclear.

- **Resources vs. Resourcefulness**: Resources are like carbs (temporary fuel). Resourcefulness is like muscle (stays with you, makes you stronger). In constrained environments, refactor systems and leverage new technology rather than throwing headcount/money at problems. Best companies are built when forced to be resourceful.

- **Workflow to Flow**: AI's promise is removing mundane repetitive labor (the "work" in workflow) so humans can stay in creative flow state. Human ingenuity has always been held back by physics and friction—AI removes friction.

- **Golden Gut**: Experienced product intuition developed by being embedded in customer context. Comes from shoulder-to-shoulder time with customers, understanding not just product usage but the full context of their day (other meetings, products, life demands). Makes micro-decisions like "reduce cognitive load even if 10% get confused to move 90% faster."

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Empathy beats solution passion**: The biggest mistake teams make is becoming passionate about a solution rather than developing deep empathy for the customer's problem. Empathy gives you the solution; misplaced passion leaves you 30 degrees off target.

2. **Expand possibility surface area**: Great designers/PMs preserve time to explore many possibilities, cull to fewer, get feedback, refine, then present 2-3 options. AI's superpower is dramatically expanding this surface area—enabling exploration of 10X more options in the same time. (Hollywood director example: ChatGPT generates 5 scenarios he wouldn't use, but they reveal what NOT to do—valuable data.)

3. **People talk about the unexpected, not the expected**: For product-led growth, optimize for surprise and delight (things users didn't expect) rather than just meeting expectations. Tesla example: people don't talk about a great drive, they talk about dashboard Easter eggs. Create "magic tricks" (per Spotify's Gustav).

4. **Kill features to amplify core metrics**: When Behance launched with too many features (portfolio, groups, tips, snapshots), usage was diluted. Each time they killed a feature, the core metric (projects published) increased. Adding features should be accompanied by removing/replacing features.

5. **Customer cohorts change, requiring onboarding evolution**: Even at Adobe's scale, massive opportunity exists in first-mile optimization because each new customer cohort is different. Early adopters are forgiving; later pragmatists are skeptical and less tolerant of friction. Photoshop went from $100s to $10/month, fundamentally changing the funnel and required onboarding.

6. **Progress narrative prevents team stir-crazy**: Leading teams through the messy middle is like driving cross-country with blacked-out windows—if the team doesn't hear the narrative of progress (traffic clearing, state lines crossed), they'll go stir-crazy. Merchandise progress relentlessly. Progress is a primary source of motivation.

7. **Optimistic about future, pessimistic about present**: Best founders are optimistic about market size and ultimate potential but pragmatic/pessimistic about current state. Red flag: founders who sugarcoat problems, gloss over hard parts, name-drop excessively. Green flag: founders who share what keeps them up at night, obstacles they're facing, unknowns they're wrestling with.

8. **Collapse the organizational stack**: Bring design earlier into product process—have designers in the room for customer research, value prop debates, traditionally PM-only discussions. This gives designers "golden gut" when they sit down to create interfaces. Extends to all functions—AI will enable people to answer own questions (data) rather than needing intermediaries, making teams flatter and faster.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Human ingenuity scales, not job elimination**: As engineers became more productive, companies didn't want fewer engineers—they wanted more engineers doing more. If AI increases human ingenuity per person, companies may build 5-10X more products with same-sized teams rather than shrinking teams.

- **Surface area expansion for exploration**: PMs will explore far more possibilities in far less time. ChatGPT as "intern" that generates options you won't use but that inform decisions. Generative AI augments real-time design work (Photoshop out-painting example).

- **Apps meet us where we are**: AI will personalize onboarding and experiences based on user type, ending the era of generalized one-size-fits-all onboarding. Products will adapt to individual users' sophistication and needs.

- **ChatGPT mines the center, humans find edges**: ChatGPT gives you "what it would look like if"—regurgitating the center of existing knowledge. Humans bring ingenuity to find edges that become the center. Both valuable, different purposes.

- **Collapse functional boundaries**: PMs will do more design, engineering, data work. Everyone becomes mini cross-functional team. May shift from hierarchical promotion to meritocracy based on creativity/ingenuity rather than reports managed or bugs fixed.

- **Advice for PMs on AI**: Play. Try everything. Write poems for friends. Use auto-draft email. Try all generative AI tools. Risk of experience is getting stuck in your ways. Force yourself to understand implications (Belsky started Implications newsletter as forcing function).

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate empathy over solution advocacy**: In PM interviews, show you start with understanding the customer problem deeply (shoulder-to-shoulder observation, context) before jumping to solutions. Explain how you develop empathy systematically.

- **Show first-mile thinking**: When discussing product work, emphasize onboarding/activation optimization. Share specific examples of how you reduced friction in first 30 seconds, simplified defaults, or improved orientation. Quantify impact on activation metrics.

- **Practice the conviction question on yourself**: Be ready to discuss times you stuck with something despite obstacles (more conviction) vs. times you pivoted/quit (less conviction). Shows self-awareness and judgment.

- **Demonstrate reductionist discipline**: Share examples of features you killed, options you removed, or markets you narrowed focus on—and the positive impact. Shows you can say no strategically.

- **Answer the three product questions**: When presenting any product you've built, proactively answer: How did users get here? What did they do? What did they do next? Shows clear thinking about user mental models.

- **Show resourcefulness over resource requests**: In scenario questions, demonstrate solving problems through creative refactoring, new technology, or process innovation rather than immediately asking for more headcount/budget.

- **Express optimism about future, pragmatism about present**: When discussing vision, be expansive about potential but honest about current obstacles and unknowns. Avoid over-sugarcoating or name-dropping.

- **Play with AI tools**: Be conversant with ChatGPT, Midjourney, Firefly, and other AI tools. Have examples of how you've used them to explore possibilities or accelerate work. Shows adaptability and curiosity.
