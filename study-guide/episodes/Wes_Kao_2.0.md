## TL;DR
Wes Kao shares tactical frameworks for executive communication, emphasizing that poor communication is the bottleneck to getting what you want at work. The biggest unlock: spend a little more time upfront on preparation, anticipate objections (MOO), lead with "sales then logistics," and practice like it's game day every time—not just with executives.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Sales, Then Logistics**: Always sell the "why" before diving into the "how." People overestimate buy-in and jump straight to process details. Spend 30-60 seconds framing why something matters before sharing logistics—even in brief Slack messages.

- **MOO (Most Obvious Objection)**: Before any meeting or message, spend 2 minutes asking: "What's the most obvious objection?" Anticipating pushback lets you volunteer context upfront and avoid being blindsided.

- **CEDAF (Delegation Framework)**: 
  - **C**omprehension: Does this person have everything needed?
  - **E**xcitement: Have I explained the why?
  - **D**e-risk: What obvious risks can I prevent?
  - **A**lign: Are we actually on the same page?
  - **F**eedback: How can I shorten the feedback loop?

- **Strategy, Not Self-Expression (Feedback)**: When giving feedback, trim 90% of the venting. Focus only on what will motivate behavior change. The goal is not justice—it's getting the outcome you want.

- **Signposting**: Use power words like "for example," "because," "as a next step," "first, second, third" to guide readers/listeners through your logic without relying on heavy formatting.

- **Question Behind the Question**: When asked something you don't know, answer a related question in the direction you think they're asking, then validate: "Are you wondering if we're investing appropriately in mobile?" This keeps the conversation moving.

- **Practice Like It's Game Day**: Don't save your best communication for executives only. Treat every stakeholder as important. You won't get enough reps to be good if you only present to execs quarterly.

- **Single-Minded Martyr**: Avoid being so attached to your recommendation that you ignore company context. Ask: "Is this the right priority *right now* given our goals?" Senior people fit proposals into broader strategy.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Concise ≠ Brief**: Concision is about economy of words and insight density, not absolute word count. A 1,000-word memo can be tighter than a 300-word meandering one. The bottleneck to being concise is clarity of thinking—if you can't cut to the chase, you don't know what the chase is.

2. **The Blast Radius of Poor Writing**: A confusing Slack message to 15 people creates back-and-forth for all 15. Investing 30 seconds upfront to clarify saves exponential time downstream. Don't underestimate the leverage of small improvements.

3. **Speak Accurately About Conviction**: State hypotheses as hypotheses, not facts. Use "this could increase likelihood" instead of "this will happen." Over-confidence wastes resources; under-confidence ("ignore everything I said") is equally irresponsible. Match your language to your evidence.

4. **Preparation Is the Only Solution to Conciseness**: You can tell a favorite story concisely because you've practiced it. At work, we process ideas in real-time while speaking. Even 30 seconds of prep before a meeting—clarifying your main point—makes you dramatically more concise.

5. **Managing Up Never Stops**: Junior people think they'll outgrow managing up. The opposite is true: the more senior you get, the more critical it becomes. Senior roles have more ambiguity and less well-defined tasks, requiring proactive framing and point-of-view sharing.

6. **Share Your Point of View**: Don't just ask "What should we do?" Say: "Here's what I think we should do and why. Where do you see gaps?" This reduces cognitive load on your manager and shows strategic thinking—even junior people can do this.

7. **Everything Takes Longer Than You Think**: Add buffers everywhere—customer support calls, career building, product launches. Assuming things take longer reduces stress and sets realistic expectations.

8. **Excessive Formatting Negates Itself**: If 30% of your memo is bolded, nothing is highlighted. Use formatting sparingly. Avoid over-relying on bullets and sentence fragments—they can be a crutch that masks unclear thinking. If you can't turn a fragment into a full sentence quickly, your idea isn't fully formed.

9. **The "Yes, Yes, Yes, Next, Next, Next" Mindset**: Executives are constantly thinking "got it, move on." Don't be attached to your 15-slide deck if they agree by slide 4. Take the win and move forward—you might talk them out of it.

10. **Compound the Small Things**: No single email or Slack feels worth extra effort, but "all the small things" *is* all your work. Everyone does the "big things"—there's no alpha there. The juice is in squeezing more from the basics others claim diminishing returns on too early.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

Wes uses Claude as a thought partner for 3-4 hours some days. Key applications:
- **Drafting difficult responses**: Share context and your dilemma ("I need to say no respectfully after previously saying yes…") and Claude provides strong starting points. Then edit to your voice and iterate.
- **CEDAF applies to AI delegation**: The delegation framework (Comprehension, Excitement, De-risk, Align, Feedback) is directly applicable to prompting AI agents. Sharing your point of view in prompts dramatically improves output quality.
- **"This is very important to my job"**: A quirky prompt engineering trick—telling AI something is critical to your career actually improves output. Some people use extreme versions ("someone will die if you don't get this right").
- **Shortened feedback loops**: AI tools like Deep Research ask clarifying questions before executing, embodying the "align" and "feedback" steps of CEDAF.
- **Future of work**: As agents proliferate, the frameworks for delegating well to humans will become frameworks for delegating well to AI.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Use MOO before any interview answer**: Anticipate the most obvious objection to your story or recommendation. If pitching a feature, anticipate: "Why not focus on retention instead?" If describing a failure, anticipate: "Why didn't you see that coming?" Volunteering context preemptively shows strategic thinking.

- **Lead with sales, then logistics**: When describing a project, start with *why* it mattered to the business before diving into what you built. "We noticed churn spiking in SMBs, so I led an initiative to..." beats "I built a feature that..."

- **Signpost your narratives**: Use "First... second... third..." or "The most surprising part was..." or "Because of this, we decided..." Interviewers appreciate structure and clarity—it shows you think in frameworks.

- **Share your point of view confidently**: Don't hedge with "I don't know if this is right, but..." State your recommendation clearly, then caveat appropriately: "My initial hypothesis was X based on Y data." Speak accurately about conviction level.

- **Prepare like it's game day**: Spend 5 minutes before every interview clarifying: What's my main point in each story? What objections might they have? What question behind the question might they ask? This prevents rambling.

- **The question behind the question**: If asked something you're unprepared for, answer in the direction you think they're probing, then validate: "Are you wondering how I prioritize trade-offs?" This keeps dialogue flowing vs. shutting down with "I don't know."

- **Practice concision through preparation**: The only way to be concise is to know your argument cold. Rehearse stories out loud. If you can't tell it in 90 seconds, you don't know it well enough.
