## TL;DR
This special compilation episode gathers 17 favorite interview questions from top product leaders, covering everything from Shishir Mehrotra's "teleportation device" Eigenquestion framework to behavioral questions testing self-awareness, humility, and handling ambiguity. The questions reveal what elite product leaders look for: introspection, storytelling ability, curiosity, authenticity, and how candidates think through problems rather than memorized answers.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models
- **Eigenquestion Framework (Shishir Mehrotra)**: When faced with complex problems, identify the one or two questions that unlock most of the answer space. Example: for a teleportation device, asking "Is it safe for humans?" and "Is it more expensive CapEx or OpEx?" creates quadrants that drive entirely different business strategies. This tests ability to simplify complexity and identify core decision drivers.

- **Self-Awareness Testing Through Third-Party Perspective (Meltem Kuran Berkowitz)**: Ask "What would your siblings say about you?" rather than direct self-assessment questions. This reveals genuine self-awareness and humility—siblings won't give flattering answers, and candidates who acknowledge that show authenticity.

- **Controversy Navigation Framework (Yuhki Yamashita)**: Understanding how someone describes controversial product decisions reveals their ability to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously, communicate conflict fairly, and navigate tension without taking sides prematurely.

- **Ambiguity Navigation Structure (JZ Zhang)**: Strong PMs don't just acknowledge ambiguity—they create structure and path forward. Look for: 1) How they seek inputs, 2) How they chart a path with reasoning, 3) What milestones they set to validate/invalidate direction.

- **Unconventional Wisdom Test (Nikhyl Singhal)**: Asking "What does everyone believe that you think is hogwash?" forces authentic opinion and breaks the "predict what you want to hear" interview pattern. No way to answer without genuine conviction.

## Top Insights (Timeless)
1. **The best interview questions test introspection, not preparation.** Questions about what feedback someone will receive, what they attribute success to, or limitations they've discovered about themselves reveal self-awareness that can't be rehearsed.

2. **Authenticity emerges when candidates can't predict the "right" answer.** Made-up scenarios (teleportation device), unconventional framings (what's hogwash), and third-party perspective questions break standard interview patterns and reveal genuine thinking.

3. **How someone describes what they're proud of reveals taste, judgment, and values.** Multiple leaders (Katie Dill, Karri Saarinen, Camille Hearst) independently ask about work candidates are most proud of—it shows what they consider "good," what motivates them, and where their gravity pulls them.

4. **Storytelling ability predicts PM success.** If candidates can make you compelled to work on a boring-sounding problem, or can represent both sides of a conflict fairly, they demonstrate the communication skills essential for rallying teams.

5. **Failure questions reveal more than success questions.** How people talk about things going wrong (Paige Costello), what didn't work as expected (Ayo Omojola), or hardest challenges (Geoff Charles) shows mindset, accountability, and learning capacity.

6. **Reference checks should ask forward-looking questions.** Paul Adams' question "What feedback will I be giving this person in their first performance review?" is unskippable—referees can't dodge it and it reveals genuine development areas.

7. **Admitting luck is a sign of security and privilege awareness.** Scott Belsky's "Do you consider yourself lucky?" tests whether someone is comfortable acknowledging advantages or insecure about how they got where they are.

8. **Low-stakes, made-up scenarios reveal methodology better than real examples.** Shishir's teleportation question teaches Eigenquestion thinking more effectively than asking about actual products because candidates aren't defensive about past decisions.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
_How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:_

- **For interviewers**: Replace generic "tell me about a time" questions with specific frameworks that test core PM skills: Eigenquestion thinking, ambiguity navigation, storytelling ability, and authentic introspection. Use made-up scenarios or unconventional framings to break rehearsed patterns.

- **Prepare stories that show self-awareness, not just success**: Have ready examples of what you attribute success to (beyond luck), limitations you've discovered about yourself, failures where you learned something, and decisions that worked but not for expected reasons.

- **Practice explaining "why you're proud" of work**: Be able to articulate not just what you shipped but why it mattered, what was hard about it, what you learned, and what it reveals about your values and taste.

- **Develop your Eigenquestion skills**: Practice taking complex problems and identifying the 1-2 questions that unlock most of the solution space. Show your work on how those questions create decision frameworks.

- **Be authentic about conventional wisdom you disagree with**: Prepare genuine opinions on things "everyone believes" in your domain that you think are wrong. This shows independent thinking and domain expertise.

- **Have answers ready for unconventional framings**: What would siblings say about you? What's a good decision that didn't work? What unfair secrets have you learned? These can't be faked—prepare honest answers.

- **On reference calls, expect**: "What feedback will this person get in their first review?" Brief your references that this may come up and encourage honest, developmental feedback rather than pure praise.
