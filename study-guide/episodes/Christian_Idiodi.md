## TL;DR
Christian Idiodi shares why product managers are often disliked (lack of competency) and how to become indispensable by demonstrating deep knowledge of customers, data, and business. His favorite discovery technique—working with 6-8 reference customers in B2B (15-25 in B2C) who will evangelize your product—is his secret to never having a product failure across 205+ products built.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Four Product Risks Framework**: Every product must address four risks: (1) **Value** – will people buy/choose/use it? (2) **Usability** – can they use it? (3) **Feasibility** – can we build it? (4) **Viability** – does it work for our business? PM owns value and viability; designer owns usability; engineer owns feasibility.

- **Reference Customer Technique**: Find people with the problem, work with them intensely until you solve it, and don't leave until they're willing to put their reputation on the line by telling others. For B2B, need 6-8 reference customers; for B2C, need 15-25. This is the holy grail—indicates true product-market fit because people will only recommend what they genuinely love.

- **Certificate of Appreciation**: The essence of product work is "you wake up on behalf of someone else to solve a problem for them, and you have to do it well enough that they give you something back in return"—revenue, engagement, loyalty, or references. This "certificate" validates you've created real value.

- **Trust Acceleration via "Teach Me"**: To build trust rapidly with executives or influential people, ask them to teach you or let you shadow them. This (1) extends their trust to you, (2) makes them accountable for your growth, (3) gives you access to their network, and (4) ensures they prep you for success (because your failure reflects on their teaching).

- **Pressure-Cooked Discovery**: Get out of your building, find someone with the problem, and don't leave until you solve it. Immerse yourself in the environment of the problem (like COVID vaccine researchers surrounded by the problem). Discover who has the problem AND deliver a solution simultaneously—this generates the fastest path to product-market fit.

- **Practice Before Promotion**: Companies promote people into incompetence by giving them titles before they've practiced the role. Solution: Do VP things before you're a VP. Practice leadership/management skills when you're NOT in the role, where mistakes are safe and feedback is welcomed.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Product managers are disliked when they lack competency, not because the role is flawed.** The real issue is when PMs don't deeply understand customers, data, business, and product better than anyone else. Organizations trust competence—if you can't demonstrate it, influence erodes and sales/executives fill the vacuum.

2. **Value risk is the most overlooked and most important risk.** Teams often assume value because "the boss told us to build it." But just because people CAN use something doesn't mean they WILL buy, choose, or actually use it. What people say differs from what they do—your job is to validate actual behavior.

3. **The fastest way to build trust is to ask influential people to teach you.** This makes them accountable for your success (bad reflection on them if you fail), extends their credibility to you, and forces relationship-building. Walk with them, learn what drives their influence (usually competence), then continue learning so you surpass their knowledge.

4. **Most product failures happen because teams never found enough people with the problem.** If you can't recruit 25 B2C users or 6-8 B2B customers willing to work with you to solve their problem, it's probably not a real problem worth solving. The recruiting itself is a massive signal.

5. **Companies don't care about people; people care about people.** All problems are people problems. Corporate structures fail when they don't create space for practice and coaching. Managers think their job is to solve problems directly—it's actually to make their team better at solving problems.

6. **"If it's not fun, you're probably not doing it right. If it's not hard, you're probably also not doing it right."** Product management should be both challenging (because you're solving real problems) and enjoyable (because you're creating value on behalf of others who appreciate it).

7. **Do things that don't scale, then do things that do scale.** Christian manually recruited McDonald's employees, called candidates to remind them of interviews, drove to Chinatown to find Chinese-speaking workers—all before building any technology. The learning from doing it manually informed what to automate.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate competence above all else.** In interviews, show deep knowledge of customers, data, and business context. Competence is the foundation of trust—without it, everything else (communication, care, vision) matters less in corporate environments.

- **Tell stories of immersive problem-solving.** Share examples where you got out of the building, worked directly with customers to solve their problems, and iterated based on real behavior (not just feedback). Show you understand the difference between what people say and what they do.

- **Practice collaborative problem-solving.** Get reps in environments where you work with others to solve problems—volunteer at nonprofits, community events, or take on side projects. This builds the muscle memory you need in interviews and on the job.

- **Ask to be taught.** In interviews and when starting a new role, explicitly ask influential people to teach you. This builds relationships fast, shows humility, and demonstrates you value learning over ego.

- **Describe your discovery process with specificity.** When asked "how would you figure out what to build," talk about finding reference customers, working with them until they'll advocate for your solution, and using that as validation of product-market fit.

- **Prepare a problem-solving example for behavioral questions.** Practice the deaf alarm clock problem or similar: demonstrate intellectual curiosity, humility about what you don't know, recognition that you'd need a team, and customer empathy.
