# Ivan Zhao — Co-founder & CEO of Notion

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Ivan Zhao
- **Role/Company**: Co-founder and CEO of Notion
- **Background**: Grew up in Urumqi, China (northwest desert region), moved to Beijing where he won second place in a programming competition to gain entry to a good school. Moved to Canada at 16, studied art and science (not computer science) in college. Built websites for artist friends, which led him to discover Douglas Engelbart's "Augmenting Human Intellect" paper and the early computing pioneers' vision. Founded Notion in 2013 with co-founder Simon after being inspired by the idea of democratizing software creation through a "Lego for software" approach.

## TL;DR
Ivan Zhao shares how Notion spent 3-4 "lost years" before finding product-market fit by learning to hide their developer-tool vision inside familiar productivity software—what they call "sugar-coated broccoli." He emphasizes the craft of building horizontal products through better abstractions, staying lean by optimizing for talent density over headcount, and balancing building for your values versus building for the market.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Sugar-Coated Broccoli**: Hide your ambitious vision (the broccoli) inside a familiar form factor people already use and understand (the sugar). Notion wanted to build a developer tool for everyone but realized most people don't care about creating software—they care about getting work done. So they hid the no-code power inside productivity software.

- **Building for Value vs. Building for Business**: Two different energies that require balance. Building for business = optimizing for users and revenue (the scorecard). Building for value = creating something authentic to your taste and what you want the world to have. Too much business focus creates commodities; too much personal focus creates irrelevant projects.

- **Abstractions Compound**: Don't fear resetting your codebase or approach. Better abstractions allow you to rebuild progress faster than linear work. As Ivan says: "You can create progress through better abstractions. That thing compounds faster, can catch up to all the things you build much quicker than you ever thought."

- **Lego Bricks vs. Lego Boxes**: For horizontal products, hardcore fans care about the primitives (Lego bricks), but most customers—especially enterprise—want pre-packaged solutions (Lego boxes). You must build bricks underneath while presenting boxes to different segments.

- **Talent Density Over Headcount**: Track revenue per employee, not just headcount. Better systems and abstractions scale better than brute-force hiring. Keep the team small like a "small bus" that can turn corners quickly.

- **Bundling/Unbundling Cycles**: Markets oscillate between bundling and unbundling. Quote from Romance of Three Kingdoms: "Empires long united must divide, long divided must unite." Recognize which phase the market is in—SaaS unbundled Microsoft Office, now the market is re-bundling due to tool fatigue and AI capabilities.

- **Tools Shape Us**: "We shape our tools. Then after, our tools shape us" (Marshall McLuhan). Consider what part of human nature you're amplifying—creativity and beauty, or something more zero-sum?

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Product-market fit takes longer than you think for ambitious visions**: Notion spent 3-4 "lost years" trying different approaches before finding their angle. First tried building a pure developer tool—nobody cared. Took 2+ years to realize they needed to hide the vision inside productivity software people already use daily.

2. **The world is not like you**: As a founder, maturation means seeing beyond your own perspective. Early Notion was "more about what I wanted than what people wanted." Most people wake up with a report due—they don't care about optimizing their software creation workflow.

3. **Don't be afraid to reset and rebuild**: Notion threw away their code multiple times, including when they realized they'd built on the wrong technical foundation (Web Components vs. React). Better abstractions let you catch up faster than you'd expect—humans think linearly but abstractions compound exponentially.

4. **Staying lean is about systems over people**: Internal communication overhead is the real cost. If you can solve problems through better tools and systems rather than hiring, you create a positive flywheel of higher talent density, which attracts more talented people who want to work with other exceptional individuals.

5. **Enterprise needs "solutions" not just "Legos"**: For the longest time, Notion stayed in the "Lego brick" mindset. Moving upmarket requires thinking in "Lego boxes"—packaged solutions that fit somewhere on a P&L and reduce risk. But you still need to build modular bricks underneath to avoid pigeonholing yourself.

6. **Sleep resets everything**: When things feel low, just sleep. Next day you reset mentally. "High is never too high, low is never too low" kept Ivan and Simon going through dark years. A daily physical reset parallels resetting code or mental models.

7. **AI is a gift for horizontal products**: Language models are "really good with bundled offerings" because they excel at searching and reasoning across connected information. AI also solves one of Lego's biggest weaknesses—assembling the pieces. Coding agents can now piece together Notion's building blocks to create custom software for any vertical use case.

8. **Craft means making clever trade-offs**: Building products is about applying your values and technical know-how to make better trade-offs. Like a wood cabinet builder, you balance aesthetic direction with technical constraints to create something beautiful and useful.

9. **Build something unique to you that's useful to others**: If you create something authentic to yourself and it happens to be useful for others, things will follow. This creates more durable energy than purely optimizing for market scorecards.

10. **Look beyond tech to steal good ideas**: Most of tech is focused on the now—what happened six months ago. Humanity has figured out patterns in other domains and throughout history. Read books outside your industry, look sideways, study complex systems. Great artists steal.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI as a new material**: Language models are "like a new type of wood" or "like aluminum"—a fundamentally new material that enables new trade-offs and possibilities, similar to how cheap aluminum enabled mass air travel.

- **Three phases of Notion's AI integration**:
  1. **AI Writer**: Slicing language model writing capabilities into daily surfaces where people already work (writing, managing tasks)
  2. **AI Q&A/Connectors**: Using AI's reasoning and search capabilities to find information across bundled data—AI is "really good with horizontal tools"
  3. **AI Assembly Agents**: The most fascinating phase—AI coding agents can now assemble Lego blocks to create custom knowledge work software and vertical agents, solving Legos' biggest weakness (difficulty of assembly)

- **Horizontal products advantage**: AI needs data to be effective. Notion's bundling strategy means all information lives in one place, making AI much more powerful for search, reasoning, and creating solutions.

- **Shift in competitive dynamics**: "AI is so good at writing code. Coding is just assembling things together." This fundamentally changes what's possible with modular building blocks—users no longer need to be builders themselves.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Show patience with ambitious visions**: When describing your product work, don't hide the struggles or "lost years." Top companies often take 3-4+ years to find product-market fit, especially with horizontal or platform approaches. Discuss how you iterated based on user feedback.

- **Demonstrate market understanding vs. personal vision**: Be ready to discuss how you balanced building what users actually need versus your product vision. Show you learned to segment ("sugar-coat the broccoli") while maintaining long-term principles.

- **Emphasize systems thinking over resource scaling**: When discussing how you'd solve problems, focus on abstractions, frameworks, and systems that scale rather than defaulting to "hire more people." Show you value efficiency and clever trade-offs.

- **Use unexpected mental models**: Ivan uses Legos, wood cabinets, small buses, Chinese literature, complex systems, bundling/unbundling cycles. In interviews, drawing non-obvious parallels shows depth of thinking.

- **Be philosophical about craft and values**: Don't just optimize for metrics. Discuss what you want to create in the world, what human behaviors you want to amplify, and how you balance business needs with personal values.

- **Show comfort with reset/rebuilding**: Describe times you threw away work to pursue a better abstraction or approach. This demonstrates courage, long-term thinking, and comfort with short-term pain for better outcomes.
