## TL;DR
Eeke de Milliano shares insights from her time as one of Stripe's first PMs and current role leading product at Retool, focusing on fostering innovation while managing process at scale. She emphasizes that process reduces variance but can suppress top performers, advocates for building talent portfolios with complementary skills, and explains how companies can maintain innovation through normalizing failure, giving teams permission to think big, and treating new products like internally-funded startups with small, independent teams.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **70/20/10 Product Investment Portfolio**: Allocate 70% of resources to core product (including maintenance and tech debt), 20% to strategic initiatives that aren't core but are strategically necessary, and 10% to experimental bets. This ensures balance between stability and innovation.

- **Minimum Viable Process (MVP)**: When introducing templates or process, explicitly give people escape hatches. Write at the top: "If this doesn't work for what you're trying to explain, don't use it. This is the minimum viable thing—we're setting the bar here, but go higher if you can." Process by definition reduces variance, bringing high performers down to average while lifting low performers up.

- **Build for Your Best User, Not Your Worst User**: Focus product development on users who will immediately understand and adopt your product, not on edge cases, abuse scenarios, or users who are poorly suited for it. Build onboarding for the user who "gets it" immediately. Address worst-case scenarios only when they become meaningful problems.

- **Build the Scooter, Not the Axle**: When building MVPs, don't create incomplete pieces of the final vision (like just wheels and an axle). Instead, build a complete but simpler product that delivers end-to-end value (the scooter), then iterate to the bicycle, motorcycle, and eventually the car.

- **Charter → Goals → Roadmap Framework**: Every level of organization (company, function, team) needs three documents: (1) Charter (mission, vision, strategy), (2) Goals (what you're measuring), and (3) Roadmap (what you're shipping). Work top-down from charter to roadmap, not bottom-up. Time horizons shift with maturity: 3 months pre-PMF, up to a decade for mature companies.

- **Product Talent Portfolio**: Build teams with complementary skillsets rather than hiring in your own image. Balance homegrown PMs (deep product knowledge, culture carriers) with experienced external hires (bring PM rigor, outside perspective). Balance execution machines with visionaries. Balance strengths across different product pillars.

- **Trapdoor vs. Two-Way Door Decisions**: Rigorously assess which decisions are reversible (two-way doors—like pricing, which can be grandfathered) versus irreversible (trapdoor decisions—like titles, which can't be taken back). Move quickly on two-way door decisions; invest time in trapdoor decisions.

- **Think Bigger Framework**: In team planning documents, include a "Think Bigger" section asking: "With 20% more time, what would you do that isn't on this list?" or "If you doubled the team today, what would you do?" This breaks people out of planning toward existing constraints rather than what should be done.

- **Crazy Ideas Document**: Annually, create a blank doc with the prompt: "Crazy ideas are ideas that we shouldn't obviously do. There's a 90% chance they make no sense. But in the 10% chance that they do, they will make a 10x to 100x difference for the business." Consistently, 3–8 ideas from this list get implemented each year.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Sales experience is invaluable for PMs**: Eeke's first role at Stripe was as an account executive, spending all day talking to customers, understanding their pain points, and figuring out how products could solve their problems—essentially doing PM work. This customer intimacy became the foundation for products like Stripe Connect.

2. **Innovation is blocked by three organizational factors**: (a) Fear of failure—leaders want innovation's upside but won't accept its costs; (b) Teams drowning in urgent work (tech debt, bugs, incidents) can't focus on creative thinking; (c) Thinking big is genuinely hard and requires dedicated time. Address these by normalizing failure through retrospectives (not postmortems), clearing urgent work, and creating explicit "permission to think" moments in company processes.

3. **Keep new products separate to maintain velocity**: When launching new products (like Retool Mobile, Workflows, and Database), run them as separate teams independent of core product processes. This allows faster movement, prevents getting bogged down in bugs/maintenance of established products, and enables different thinking about target customers. The cost is later integration work, but the speed and independence are worth it.

4. **Writing culture drives clear thinking**: Stripe's emphasis on written communication (business reviews, strategy memos, product reviews) wasn't arbitrary—you can't be a good writer without being a clear thinker. Writing forces you to work through logical gaps. If you couldn't write well at Stripe, it was hard to be successful, especially in early days.

5. **Process is variance-reducing by design**: When you introduce process, you're explicitly accepting a trade-off: bringing low performers up to average while simultaneously bringing high performers down to average. The people most hurt by process are your most creative, highest performers who don't need it. Combat this with escape hatches and strong managers who can identify top performers and provide air cover, even if it means "breaking the org" for exceptional people.

6. **First principles thinking must be operationalized top-down**: Stripe's culture of rigorous, first-principles thinking came from founders consistently questioning assumptions (like "why does merchant account setup take weeks?") and hiring people who thought that way. In every meeting, saying something was "a best practice" would trigger "well, why?"—forcing deeper thinking. This trickled down through the organization.

7. **Treat internal product launches like VC-funded startups**: Start with 1–2 people (engineer + designer/PM) for first six months. They spend time with customers, prototyping, proving there's something there. Only when validated do they get more resources. Teams must prove ROI through engagement or revenue. Retool acts as the VC providing resources and customer base access.

8. **Balance timing of big bets carefully**: Retool launched three major products in one year, all maturing simultaneously. In hindsight, sequencing them would have been better for organizational headspace and satisfaction—even though the launches succeeded. Avoid having multiple teams all waiting to launch at once.

9. **Small teams with tight customer feedback loops reduce fear of failure**: Keep resource allocation small for new initiatives and get customer feedback quickly. Don't let anything take a year to ship without feedback. Lower stakes through smaller teams and faster cycles—this makes failure less devastating and more like learning.

10. **PM-sales alignment is critical in enterprise**: Whether PLG or sales-led, tight interaction mechanisms with sales/success/support are essential. This flows both directions: getting frontline customer feedback from sales, and ensuring sales has everything needed to accurately represent product/roadmap. Experimented with "science fair" format where each product team has a booth for go-to-market to visit rather than one-size-fits-all presentations.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

**On humans becoming more formulaic as AI becomes more human-like:**
Eeke references Ian Leslie's work on what it means to be human in the age of AI: "We're all really impressed when we see ChatGPT-3 spit out writing that feels very human-like. But what we're forgetting is that over the last 10, 20, 30 years, we're actually asking humans to be a lot more robot-like. We're really asking everyone to standardize in a lot of ways. We're making people a lot more formulaic." This applies to how we teach writing (five-paragraph format), introduce process, and create templates—all of which reduce creativity. The more formulaic and mass-produced you make something, the more you quench creativity and move away from the "really, really high highs."

**AI + human interaction in product (Stripe Radar example):**
Building Stripe Radar (fraud detection ML models) raised complex questions about human-AI interaction that feel prescient: "How do you explain a model to humans? How do you let them interact with it?" The product needed to help customers understand why payments were blocked and write their own rules on top of ML predictions—a pattern many AI products face today.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

**Demonstrating first-principles thinking:**
- When asked about past decisions, don't cite "best practices"—explain the reasoning from fundamentals
- Show you've questioned assumptions rather than following industry norms
- Practice explaining "why" for several levels deep on any product decision
- Prepare examples where you challenged the status quo or conventional wisdom

**Showing comfort with failure and learning:**
- Have 1–2 stories ready about projects that didn't work out, framed as learning opportunities
- Emphasize what you discovered and how it informed future work
- Demonstrate you can shine light on failures rather than hide them
- Show you understand the cost of innovation (occasional failure) and embrace it

**Communicating balance between speed and rigor:**
- Prepare examples of trapdoor vs. two-way door decisions you've made
- Show you can move fast on reversible decisions while being thoughtful on irreversible ones
- Demonstrate understanding of when to prototype quickly vs. when to plan deeply
- Have stories about MVPs that delivered end-to-end value, not just components

**Customer-centricity:**
- Prepare stories about direct customer interaction—especially sales/support experience if you have it
- Show how customer insights shaped product decisions
- Demonstrate technical depth (if relevant) that allows direct customer engagement
- Have examples of how you stayed close to users despite scaling

**Self-awareness and growth mindset:**
- For "to what do you attribute your success?" (excluding luck), have thoughtful self-reflection ready
- Show awareness of your strengths and how you've built complementary teams
- Demonstrate curiosity about your own development
- Be ready to discuss how you think about skill gaps in yourself and your teams

**Portfolio thinking:**
- Show you understand resource allocation trade-offs (core/strategic/bets)
- Prepare examples of balancing innovation with maintenance
- Demonstrate you think about team composition, not just hiring for skill X
- Show you can identify gaps in team capabilities and hire to fill them
