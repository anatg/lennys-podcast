# Maya Prohovnik — Head of Product, Spotify Podcasting

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Maya Prohovnik
- **Role/Company**: Head of Product for Podcasting at Spotify (formerly employee #1 at Anchor)
- **Background**: Started in customer support before transitioning to product. Joined Anchor as the first employee, which was acquired by Spotify five years ago. ## TL;DR
Maya Prohovnik shares how dogfooding—creating her own four podcasts—helps her build better products for creators, and reveals how Anchor scaled by having college interns manually submit hundreds of thousands of podcasts to Apple before automation was possible. She emphasizes balancing gut instinct with data, maintaining startup speed within a large organization, and the unexpected depression many founders face post-acquisition.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models
- **The 80/20 Rule for Product**: Don't build for the vocal 20% of users; focus on the silent 80% majority to achieve true democratization and scale. The loudest users can trap you in a niche.
- **Radical Candor (Kim Scott)**: Care personally AND challenge directly. Feedback is a gift. When someone underperforms, it's almost always a role-fit issue, not a character flaw—approach it with care to help them find the right path.
- **Eisenhower Matrix (Four Ds)**: Organize all tasks into Do, Defer, Delegate, or Delete. Review your to-do list daily and categorize everything to clear mental space for deep thinking.
- **Build Things That Don't Scale**: At startups, manually do unscalable work to create magical user experiences. Anchor had interns manually submit podcasts to Apple to eliminate friction, appearing automated to users.
- **Mission Over Method**: Stay unwavering on your mission but remain flexible on tactics. Anchor pivoted twice (1.0→2.0→3.0) while maintaining the core goal of democratizing audio creation.

## Top Insights (Timeless)
1. **Dogfooding as a product philosophy**: Maya runs four podcasts herself and requires her team to create their own. She believes you cannot build effective creator tools without experiencing the emotional journey, friction points, and motivational challenges creators face. Insights from her podcasts directly inform onboarding education and feature prioritization.

2. **Kill your darlings when data looks good**: Anchor 1.0 had highly engaged users and consistent growth, but Maya and the CEO recognized it would never reach their democratization mission. They rebuilt the entire app (2.0) despite vocal user opposition, which unlocked exponential growth. Sometimes the data says "keep going" but your gut knows it's a dead end.

3. **Friction reduction through illusion**: Anchor hired college interns to manually create Apple IDs and submit hundreds of thousands of podcasts, making distribution feel instant and magical to users. This "unscalable" approach drove massive market share growth by solving a critical user pain point before proper infrastructure existed.

4. **Post-acquisition depression is real**: Maya reveals that most acquired founders go through deep existential crises after acquisition, even when it's objectively successful. The loss of day-to-day survival mode and ownership creates identity questions. She wishes there was more support and discussion around this transition.

5. **Gut as a data type**: Present your intuition as one valid data source among many, backed by experience, user stories, and analogies. Never say "just trust me." In large organizations, purely gut-driven decisions without supporting narrative immediately doom projects because people won't follow.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*
- **Demonstrate user empathy through personal experience**: Share examples of using competitor products, dogfooding your own features, or creating content in the space you're building for. Show you understand user pain viscerally, not just analytically.
- **Tell stories of choosing mission over metrics**: Describe times you made bold pivots despite good short-term data because you saw long-term strategic limitations. Explain how you brought stakeholders along.
- **Discuss unscalable wins**: Share creative "hacks" where you manually solved user problems before automation, showing scrappiness and prioritization of user experience over perfect infrastructure.
- **Balance frameworks with intuition**: Reference specific frameworks (Radical Candor, Eisenhower Matrix) but also share gut calls backed by experience and user stories. Show you're neither purely data-driven nor purely instinctive.
- **Ask meaningful interview questions**: Use Maya's question "If you could make a podcast about anything, what would it be?" as inspiration—ask candidates about passion projects to assess intrinsic motivation and curiosity.
