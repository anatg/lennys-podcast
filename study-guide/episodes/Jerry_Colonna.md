# Jerry Colonna — Executive Coach, CEO of Reboot, Former VC at Flatiron Partners

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Jerry Colonna
- **Role/Company**: Co-founder and CEO of Reboot (executive and leadership development firm); Author of *Reboot* and *Reunion*
- **Background**: Former co-founder of Flatiron Partners with Fred Wilson (one of the most successful early-stage investment funds); former partner at JPMorgan Chase. After achieving outward success as a VC in his 30s, experienced severe depression and suicidal ideation, leading him to coaching and radical self-inquiry. Now one of the most respected executive coaches in the world, working with leaders to become "better humans" and build resilience.

## TL;DR
Jerry Colonna teaches that great leadership comes not from traditional skills alone, but from radical self-inquiry—examining the unconscious patterns and childhood baggage that drive our behavior. His equation (practical skills + radical self-inquiry + shared experiences = enhanced leadership + greater resilience) emphasizes that leaders must do their own inner work to avoid creating dysfunctional teams that mirror their unresolved issues.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Reboot Equation**: Practical Skills + Radical Self-Inquiry + Shared Experiences = Enhanced Leadership + Greater Resilience. The goal isn't just better leadership—it's greater resilience so you don't "kill yourself in the process" of being successful.

- **The Complicity Question**: "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?" Note: complicit ≠ responsible. You're "driving the getaway car, not sticking up the bank teller." This question evokes agency and cuts through self-delusion (e.g., "I say I don't want to feel busy, but I feel unnerved if my agenda isn't jam-packed").

- **Four Key Self-Inquiry Questions**:
  1. What am I not saying that I need to say?
  2. What am I saying that's not being heard?
  3. What's being said that I'm not hearing?
  4. How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?

- **Attachment vs. Purpose**: Buddhism teaches that attachment to outcomes fuels suffering. When we acquire things (house, status, growth metrics) to push away the suffering of "Am I good enough?", we increase suffering. The alternative: enjoy the puzzle, hold goals loosely, focus on purpose over outcome.

- **The Big Lie of Success**: We're socialized to believe that reaching a certain level of success, income, or status will make us happy. It won't. The real work is consciousness—understanding *why* we pursue what we pursue.

- **Unsorted Baggage**: Per Bruce Springsteen (who spent 25 years in psychoanalysis): "We all have unsorted baggage from childhood, and at some point we're going to pay the price of not sorting that baggage... and the price more often than not is in tears."

- **Teams as Family Systems**: Teams unconsciously recreate patterns from members' families of origin. Example: A team that deflects painful topics with jokes mirrors a CEO's childhood family dynamics. Carl Jung: "Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate."

- **The Growth Mindset Trap**: Having a growth mindset is helpful, but becoming *fixed* on having a growth mindset creates attachment and suffering. Hold mindsets loosely. Peter Senge: "It is virtually impossible to challenge the assumptions that made you rich in the first place."

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Self-inquiry is the actual leadership work**: "If you want to create a high-functioning team, do your work, and it starts with the person who has the most power." Teams become manifestations of the leader's unresolved childhood dysfunction if the leader refuses to examine their patterns.

2. **Busyness as avoidance**: Constant busyness often serves an unconscious need. We say we don't want to be busy, but feel "unnerved and disconcerted" if our agenda isn't jam-packed. This is complicity—we're creating the condition we claim to avoid.

3. **Success without attachment**: The healthiest path is to enjoy the work itself (solving puzzles, creating art) without attaching self-worth to outcomes. When success comes, stay present to your "why" rather than letting fear of losing it drive you.

4. **The socialization trap**: "We're socialized to bullshit not only ourselves, but everybody else, especially in the entrepreneurial community. All our companies are moving up into the right. Every product is working... and that's just a lie."

5. **The ancestor question**: "What kind of ancestor to my descendants would I like to be?" shifts focus from ego-driven legacy to purposeful contribution. This question helps orient toward meaning over status.

6. **Radical questions create growth**: "You know you're in the radical self-inquiry zone when the questions take your breath away." Questions that startle or frighten us reveal where the real work needs to happen.

7. **Unconditional self-worth enables performance**: When you internalize that you're "somebody regardless of what you do," performance anxiety decreases and you can actually perform better. The people who love you will love you even if the podcast/startup/project fails.

8. **Scaling requires tolerating mistakes**: "You want to build a scaled leadership team, you have to be willing to have them make boneheaded decisions." Leaders who can't tolerate disagreement hire people who can't make decisions without permission.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI as "glasses"**: Jerry uses AI (ChatGPT) as "glasses that are really, really sharp and helpful"—a thinking and writing partner that helps him ask better questions and explore concepts, but doesn't replace human connection.

- **AI for self-inquiry**: Colleagues upload journal entries to Claude/ChatGPT to ask questions like "What am I not saying?" The AI can surface patterns and suggest more questions, making coaches *better* rather than replacing them.

- **The optimistic view**: AI may burn away tasks that don't matter, elevating what *does* matter—presence, human connection, strategic thinking, conceptualization. "We spend more time on that which matters, and less time on that which doesn't matter."

- **The unsettling reality**: AI is "disturbing and unsettling" because it challenges fundamental questions about human purpose. But mediated experiences (like this podcast) still create heart-to-heart connection, suggesting human presence remains essential.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Practice radical self-inquiry before behavioral interviews**: Use Jerry's four questions to examine your own career choices and leadership moments. Interviewers value self-awareness and the ability to learn from mistakes over perfect execution stories.

- **Demonstrate conscious leadership**: When discussing team challenges, show you've examined your own complicity rather than just blaming others. Example: "I realized I was bottlenecking decisions because I couldn't tolerate disagreement."

- **Articulate your "why"**: Be prepared to answer not just *what* you've built, but *why* you chose product management, what drives you beyond external validation, and what kind of leader/ancestor you want to be.

- **Show vulnerability appropriately**: Jerry's equation includes "shared experiences"—the ability to be real with people. In interviews, appropriately sharing challenges (with self-awareness about your role) demonstrates maturity and trustworthiness.

- **Question growth for growth's sake**: If asked about growth strategy, demonstrate consciousness about *why* growth matters (user value, mission) vs. growth as ego validation. This shows mature strategic thinking.

- **Prepare for "tell me about a failure" with self-inquiry**: Don't just describe what went wrong—show the internal work you did to understand your patterns and what you learned about yourself, not just tactics.
