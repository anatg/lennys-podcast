## TL;DR
PR isn't just about direct signups—the real value for B2B companies is third-party validation for sales, recruiting, and credibility. Success comes from being direct and concise in pitches, positioning against known incumbents rather than creating new categories, and understanding that different publications serve different purposes (TechCrunch for funding, Fast Company for future of work, Business Insider for entrepreneurial journeys).

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Second-Order Effects of Press**: Press rarely drives direct B2B signups, but creates validation for sales teams (sharing articles in outreach), recruiting (credibility when reaching out to candidates), and sponsor decisions (demonstrating legitimacy). The logo on your website and shareable third-party validation matter more than traffic spikes.

- **Publication Targeting Matrix**: Each publication has a specific sweet spot—TechCrunch for funding announcements and some product news (but not MarTech/HealthTech), Axios for deals/FinTech/healthcare (never product launches), Business Insider for pitch deck stories and entrepreneurial journeys with metrics, VentureBeat for AI news, Fast Company for future of work/design, Forbes for awards and contributor network for earlier-stage companies.

- **Incumbent Positioning vs. Category Creation**: For PR purposes, position as "taking on [known company] with [specific approach]" rather than creating a new category. Reporters need frames of reference. "Ramp takes on Bill.com" gets coverage; "first seamless finance offering" doesn't. Category creation may work in sales/marketing but fails in PR pitches.

- **The Three-Sentence Pitch**: Most successful pitches are three sentences maximum. Structure: (1) What you're offering (funding exclusive, story idea), (2) Why it matters (amount raised, specific trend/gap in coverage), (3) Call to action. Longer pitches = weaker pitches.

- **Controversy as Hook**: When you don't have hard news, having a contrarian or controversial stance makes you quotable. If nine AI companies have one stance and you have the opposite, reporters need you for balance. Example: Clockwise's op-ed critiquing Shopify's meeting cancellation approach got Fast Company coverage.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Warm introductions to reporters matter less than you think**: Relationships only guarantee an open and a reply, not coverage. Good reporters make editorial decisions based on story merit, not friendships. Cold outreach done well (direct, concise, relevant) is just as effective.

2. **Start with the smallest possible pitch**: On LinkedIn or Twitter, don't lead with the full pitch. Try: "Hey, reaching out with a story idea. Are you open to hearing it?" This saves time, gets responses, and creates a more human connection. If they reply, they'll feel obligated to give you an answer.

3. **The funding announcement formula**: For funding stories, be extremely direct: "Reaching out with [Company] raising [$X] from [Investors]. Offering this as an exclusive." Don't bury the lead in problem statements or trend stories. TechCrunch covers ~9 funding stories per week out of ~50 that happen—being clear is how you become one of the nine.

4. **Press releases are mostly obsolete**: Reporters don't check news wires anymore. A well-written blog post on your own site is more shareable, flexible in style, helps with website traffic, and signals a modern company better than a formulaic press release. Save press releases for late-stage companies or regulatory requirements.

5. **The six-week buffer rule**: Start working with a PR agency or begin outreach six weeks before an announcement. This allows time for blog drafting, media training, reporter outreach, coordination with investors, and—crucially—flexibility to announce when the right reporter can cover it, not on your arbitrary internal date.

6. **Identify the most interesting thread and pull it**: Most companies have multiple storylines. Column Tax had many customers, but focusing PR exclusively on their partnership with Propel (serving low-income families, 40% hadn't filed taxes the previous year) connected to the macro TurboTax controversy and IRS free filing story—getting Fast Company coverage instead of a generic partnership announcement.

7. **Corporate social channels underperform; executive accounts win**: Following a brand is different from following a person. Executive social presence offers personality, flexibility in tone, and controversial opinions. Corporate channels struggle unless you're Duolingo-level creative. If investing in social, prioritize CEO/exec accounts over brand accounts.

8. **The Morning Brew coworking series hack**: Morning Brew (Emerging Tech Brew, HR Brew, CFO Brew) has a "coworking series" with a Google form at the top/bottom. Fill out ~10 questions about a day in the life of someone in a specific role (not necessarily CEO). Keep answers interesting and not self-promotional. 50% success rate for features with 10 minutes of work.

9. **Metrics over percentages for early companies**: Don't say "700% growth since exiting stealth" (could be 1 to 7 customers). Instead: "Revenue hit six figures end of last year, growing 30% per quarter, with 40% adoption from enterprise." Specific color is more credible than manipulated percentages.

10. **Test social before committing**: If posting feels like it takes enormous mental bandwidth, weigh the cost. But if you try posting once a month on LinkedIn and get client/candidate inbounds each time (like Emilie does), push yourself even if it doesn't come naturally. Social is easy to test—try for 1-2 months and measure value.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Tell your product story with clear comparisons**: When explaining your product in interviews, reference known incumbents rather than claiming category creation. "We're like Figma but for X" is clearer than "We're creating a new category of collaborative Y tools."

- **Practice extreme concision**: If you can't explain what your product does, who it's for, and why it matters in three sentences, keep refining. This discipline helps in pitch meetings, stakeholder updates, and interviews.

- **Quantify impact with specific color, not percentages**: Instead of "300% growth in adoption," say "Went from 5 enterprise customers to 40 in 6 months, with 35% coming from healthcare vertical." Specificity signals credibility.

- **Mine for the most interesting thread**: When discussing past work, identify the single most compelling narrative (like Column Tax focusing on the low-income tax filing story) rather than trying to cover everything. Interviewers remember focused stories.

- **Demonstrate you can find gaps and opportunities**: Emilie's "what gaps in coverage do you see?" approach applies to PM work—showing you can identify what's not being addressed (customer segment, use case, market) demonstrates strategic thinking.

- **Be direct about what you want**: Whether asking for resources, pitching a feature, or negotiating offer terms, Emilie's advice to be clear about intentions ("offering this as an exclusive," "reaching out for a funding story") shows confidence and respect for others' time.
