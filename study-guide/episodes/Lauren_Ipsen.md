## TL;DR
Lauren Ipsen shares battle-tested advice on hiring product leaders, emphasizing that founders should avoid chasing "shiny object" big-name executives who may be too far from the work. Instead, look for leaders with a chip on their shoulder who can still execute tactically. Both candidates and hiring managers should always keep a pulse on the market, build relationships long before they need them, and prioritize genuine human connection over transactional interactions.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Hire for the Role Today, Not the Best Person Ever**: Don't ask "who is the best talent in the world?" Ask "who is best for this specific role at this specific time?" Big-name executives from Google/YouTube may be far from the work and need to hire teams themselves—often not ideal for early-stage companies needing hands-on builders.

- **The 90-Day Plan Test**: Before hiring any product leader, get crystal clear on what success looks like in 90 days, 12 months, 18 months, and through potential IPO. If you can't articulate this, the search is "doomed from inception."

- **Three Product Leader Archetypes**: (1) Platform/infrastructure-focused, (2) Core/consumer product-focused, (3) Specialists in growth/monetization/design. Understand which bucket you need before starting the search—don't hire a "unicorn" who does everything.

- **Always Be Networking (ABN)**: Whether hiring or job-seeking, always keep a pulse on who's thriving, which companies are killing it, and maintain warm relationships. Plant seeds 6-12 months before you need them. Lauren placed a VP of Engineering after a 7-month courtship involving advisory work before formal hire.

- **Back-Channel > Provided References**: Provided references are like "Instagram Reels"—curated. Back-channel references are like "photos you're tagged in"—the real story. Always do both, but trust back-channels more.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Look for leaders one level below the big names**: The best hires often aren't the CPO of YouTube but rather their strong lieutenant who's still close to the work, hungry to prove themselves, and can operate tactically while building leadership capacity.

2. **Breadth beats specialization for leadership tracks**: PMs aiming for head of product roles should touch platform, core product, growth, and design—not just specialize in one area. Variety of experience across company stages (startup + established) also creates better leaders.

3. **Impact you can point to beats tenure**: Don't stay somewhere just for loyalty if the company is failing. Leave when you've made meaningful impact that cross-functional leaders can speak to. If 10 back-channel calls yield no one who can describe your impact, that's a red flag.

4. **Title inflation is a trap**: Don't chase "CPO" titles at struggling companies over "Head of Product" at thriving ones. Ask "CPO of what?" A head of product role at a rocket ship beats a fancy title at a sinking ship.

5. **Recruiting is relationship-first, transaction-never**: The transactional recruiter call is what makes great candidates want to hide. Listen, remember details (like vest dates), find genuine common ground, and play the 3-5 year game. Once you lose candidate trust, it's impossible to rebuild.

6. **Test recruiters in real-time**: Don't just review their deck. Give them your requirements, ask them to recite it back, then ask for candidate ideas on the spot without prep time. This tests listening skills and actual market knowledge versus recycled presentations.

7. **Start with "Head of Product" at early stage**: Keep titles simple and avoid over-promising with C-level titles you may need to layer later. Early-stage companies should be "almost allergic to C titles" until Series C/D.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

**For PM Candidates:**
- Practice telling your story before you need to—know how to articulate impact and why you were missed when you left
- Never speak down about previous teammates or blame others for missed deadlines (huge red flag)
- Be honest about weaknesses—recruiters immediately see through "I work too hard" answers
- Don't hide short stints from your resume; handle them with grace and context instead
- Build warm relationships with great recruiters even when not looking—they're your long-term career partners

**For Hiring Managers:**
- Ask recruiters in real-time (no prep) for candidate ideas to test their actual knowledge
- Do extensive back-channel references beyond provided ones—ask "Why would I NOT hire this person?"
- Look for patterns: Does anyone know what this person accomplished? Can cross-functional leaders speak to their impact?
- Test for reading people: Any pause after "Would you hire them again?" is meaningful

**Universal:**
- Keep a pulse on the market regardless of hiring/looking status
- Build relationships 6-12 months before you need them
- Treat every interaction as relationship-first, never transactional
