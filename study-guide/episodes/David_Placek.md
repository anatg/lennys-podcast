## TL;DR
David Placek shares the science behind creating iconic brand names, revealing that great names create discomfort initially and require boldness over descriptiveness. He outlines Lexicon's three-phase process (Identify, Invent, Implement) including the counterintuitive practice of briefing creative teams on fake assignments to unlock creativity, plus deep linguistic research on sound symbolism showing each letter evokes specific experiences.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Win Diamond**: Draw a diamond shape with four points: (top) "Win" - define what winning means; (right) "What do you have to win?" - existing strengths; (bottom) "What do you need to win?" - gaps and resources needed; (left) "What do you need to say to win?" - messaging focused on behavior and experience, not positioning statements.

- **Three-Phase Naming Process (Identify-Invent-Implement)**: 
  - **Identify**: Understand current vs. desired behavior (bidirectional: how you behave toward market and market toward you), map competitive landscape, create creative framework (a "window" not narrow objectives)
  - **Invent**: Use 2-3 small creative teams with different briefs (one knows real project, others think it's disguised/different category), generate 2-3,000 ideas, apply linguistic screening and trademark clearance
  - **Implement**: Build prototypes, create rationales for stakeholders, conduct customer research focused on imagination not comfort

- **Sound Symbolism**: Each letter of the alphabet sends specific signals: V is the most alive/vibrant (Corvette, Viagra, Vercel); B conveys reliability (Blackberry); Z/S create noise and energy (Sonos, Azure); X signals speed and innovation. Use this to engineer emotional responses.

- **Processing Fluency**: The brain prefers easy-to-process information. Build names from familiar components arranged newly (Vercel = ver + cel, both recognizable roots) to balance distinctiveness with cognitive ease.

- **Asymmetric + Cumulative Advantage**: Start with an advantage before launch (asymmetric) that compounds over time as the name is used repeatedly (cumulative). Distinctiveness creates this compounding effect; descriptiveness doesn't.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **You won't know a great name when you see it** - Most clients believe with "full confidence" they'll recognize the right name immediately, but this almost never happens across 4,000 projects. The best names require processing time and create initial discomfort because they're bold and unfamiliar.

2. **Polarization signals strength** - Look for names that split your team, creating tension and strong debate. As Andy Grove taught Placek with Pentium: "I see the polarization here amongst people... That tells me there's energy for Pentium." Comfort means you don't have the name yet.

3. **Suspend evaluation, speculate instead** - Don't ask "What do I think of this name?" Ask "What could we do with this name?" The execution determines success, not the word itself. Build prototypes (mock ads, products with the name) to help people imagine the future.

4. **Small teams outperform brainstorms** - Large brainstorming sessions don't work. Use 2-3 person teams, with most teams working on disguised/different briefs. Teams who think they're naming a bicycle (when it's actually software) make bold mistakes that become breakthrough names because they're free from constraints.

5. **Names create experiences, not statements** - Descriptive names make statements about what you are (Cloud Pro, Infoseek). Imaginative names start stories and create experiences (Azure, Google, Sonos). The right name predisposes people to think "they're not like the other guys" before knowing anything else.

6. **Generate volume, not precision** - 200 names isn't enough; create 1,000-1,500 directions and ideas. Most names come from the "second or third team" working on fake briefs because freedom from the actual constraint unlocks creativity.

7. **Force synchronicity** - Connect unrelated dots. If naming a sailboat company, look through hunting or aviation magazines and extract words you like. The collision of distant contexts produces names you'd never reach through direct thinking.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- AI companies present unique naming challenges: engineers want sophisticated names (Codium, Anduril, Anthropic) reflecting their technical vision, while consumers need tangible, natural names they can grasp (Windsurf)
- Lexicon invested $20,000 in research across US, Europe, and South Korea to understand developer vs. consumer perceptions of AI - developers are positive and future-focused; consumers are skeptical and job-worried
- The fastest-moving category Placek has experienced in 40 years, faster than the early internet/World Wide Web era
- SEO becoming less important with AI search, making .com domain less critical - "URL address has become an area code"
- Lexicon has developed proprietary software to help generate naming ideas (not yet producing solutions, but accelerating creative direction and sound symbolism application)

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate strategic thinking about identity**: When discussing product naming or positioning, show you understand names create asymmetric and cumulative advantage, not just labels. Talk about behavior and experience, not features.

- **Use the Win Diamond framework**: In strategy discussions, structure your thinking around what winning means, what you have, what you need, and what you need to say. This shows systematic yet creative thinking.

- **Show comfort with discomfort**: Great PMs, like great names, push boundaries. Demonstrate you seek polarization and healthy debate on your team as a sign of strength, not consensus for comfort.

- **Ask "what could we do with this?" not "what do I think?"**: In product discussions, shift from evaluation to speculation. Show you can suspend judgment and explore possibilities before deciding.

- **Prove you understand psychology and human behavior**: Reference how humans process information, seek comfort, and respond to distinctiveness. Connect product decisions to cognitive science concepts like processing fluency.

- **Tell stories about forcing synchronicity**: Share examples of how you've connected unrelated contexts to solve problems (like the bicycle-to-software team approach), showing creative problem-solving.

- **Emphasize experience over features**: When describing products, focus on the bidirectional behavior and experience you're creating, not technical specifications or positioning statements.
