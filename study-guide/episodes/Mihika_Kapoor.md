## TL;DR
Mihika Kapoor shares how she became one of Figma's most beloved PMs by mastering the art of building compelling visions through cross-functional collaboration, getting to strong conviction quickly through constant user conversations, and keeping the "flame" of zero-to-one ideas alive through relentless hype-building and momentum creation. Her approach emphasizes strong opinions weakly held, radical directness paired with psychological safety, and treating culture-building as essential infrastructure for high-performing teams.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Vision as Pain-Solution-Proof Point Loop**: When presenting vision, structure as repeating cycles of "pain point → solution → proof point (testimonial/data)" rather than linear narrative. Makes vision emotionally resonant and credible.

- **The Flame/Keeper of the Hearth Metaphor**: Zero-to-one ideas are flames destined to die unless constantly stoked. The PM is like Hestia (Greek goddess of the hearth)—responsible for keeping the flame burning during lulls and helping it spread like wildfire when ready. Three requirements: (1) right idea (empathy), (2) secure buy-in (vision), (3) make it spread (hype).

- **One-Way vs. Two-Way Doors (Jeff Bezos)**: Most decisions in software are two-way doors you can walk back through. Having strong conviction and moving fast on two-way doors creates momentum; being wrong is fine because you can course-correct.

- **Assumptions-Based Disagreement (Julie Zhu)**: When two people disagree on product strategy, it's because they have different assumptions. Surface assumptions explicitly to resolve disagreements faster and build shared conviction.

- **Vision = User Empathy + Cross-Functional Artifacts**: Don't vision in a vacuum. Constantly cross-pollinate research insights, designs/prototypes, and engineering feasibility checks. Create a single unified artifact (deck in Figma) the entire team builds together, not siloed deliverables.

- **See to Believe/Feel**: At Figma, "the future is visual communication." Words only get you so far—prototypes and visual demos help people feel the emotional pull of an idea. Design and prototype before formal project green-light.

- **Feedback as a Gift (Meta value)**: Directness only works when two-way. Always ask "Do you have feedback for me?" before giving feedback to others. Creates reciprocal culture of constant improvement.

- **Democratic Meetings Vision (FigJam example)**: Brainstorms work because they're democratizing—ideas come from everyone, not just loudest voices. How can we make all meetings (kickoffs, all-hands) more generative and participatory? Led to templates, voting, music features.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Your scope is the world, not your current project**: Nothing should be perceived as out of bounds. Don't limit yourself to what's "in flight"—understand your company's vision and users' full tool ecosystem to find opportunities that might exist inside or outside your company.

2. **Conviction creates momentum; perfect information never comes**: Getting to strong conviction extremely quickly (even if it's 60% real, 40% performative) rallies teams through messy zero-to-one journeys. Put out an opinionated idea to get reactions; people respond better to something than to nothing. Always communicate confidence level explicitly.

3. **Build your intuition repository through obsessive user conversations**: Product sense = good intuition. Intuition comes from building a mental library of user conversations. Talk to users at dinners, sales calls, everywhere. Over time, anecdotes ABC + DEF + GHI form a repository you draw from when making decisions.

4. **Culture is trust infrastructure**: Culture-building (hot seat game, Figgies awards, Kudos Boards) isn't fluffy—it establishes trust between people. When roadmaps change and times get tough, emotional connection to teammates makes you rally together rather than jump ship. Passion = vision you believe in + people you love working with.

5. **Zero-to-one success requires optimism bordering on delusion**: You need reality distortion field. Don't hear "no," translate it to "not yet." Keep pitching until it sticks (Mihika pitched her new product idea three times before it took hold at Maker Week hackathon). Push your product beyond what current stage merits to get visibility and signal.

6. **Put things on staging early to create ownership**: Figma's multi-month dogfooding process isn't just for feedback—it makes people invested. When colleagues give feedback and see it implemented, they feel "I shaped this product." Opens doors to product development, elevates quality, spreads hype.

7. **Non-users teach you more than users**: Ask people why they're NOT using your product. Reveals marketing/perception gaps, not just product gaps. Led FigJam team to build placeholder experience for non-designers after learning they felt it "wasn't built for them."

8. **Leverage company-wide forums to create momentum waves**: Sales Kickoff, Maker Week, CONFIG—use these moments strategically even if product isn't ready. A demo that "wasn't meant to be a demo" at SKO drove hype for Mihika's new product and gave invaluable user signal.

9. **Understand individual motivations, don't manage to the average**: Some engineers want solutions handed to them (they love finding technical solutions); others can't feel passionate about things they didn't contribute to. Directly ask: "How much do you like being involved in product decisions?" Tailor accordingly.

10. **Scrappiness beats perfection in zero-to-one**: Swapping a FigJam icon to a new product icon (2-line code change) made the hackathon demo feel real. Know where to take quality hits and where to push for believability. Mihika assembled the Figgies on Amazon in an hour—don't let lack of resources stop you.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI is lowering the floor to building**: Tools like Devin (Cognition's AI coding agent) and Replit make it easier for non-technical PMs to prototype. Figma aims to be your "designer co-founder," Replit your "technical co-founder." A year ago this wasn't possible; now scrappy PMs can build more independently.

- **Black-boxification is AI's biggest shortcoming**: Most AI products are optimized for the demo/tweet—you enter prompt, get output, but can't interact with it. If output is slightly off, you're stuck. Pika (video generation) is exciting because it lets you manipulate output, not just generate once. More products should focus on interactive refinement, not just generation.

- **FigJam example—Jambot AI plugin**: Came out of an AI hackathon. Shows Figma's bottoms-up AI innovation culture.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Show multi-disciplinary thinking**: Mihika's CS/visual arts background made her a design-engineering-PM hybrid. Interviewers love PMs who bridge functions. Share examples of where you straddled domains.

- **Demonstrate user obsession through storytelling**: Don't just say "I talked to users." Share the dinner conversation that led to an insight, the sales call that changed your roadmap. Make the repository of conversations tangible.

- **Communicate conviction levels explicitly**: In interviews, when sharing decisions, explicitly state "I felt 90% confident on this" or "medium confidence—I deferred to the designer." Shows self-awareness and collaborative leadership.

- **Bring prototypes/visuals to interviews**: If doing a product critique or case study, don't just talk—show mocks or flows. Figma-style "see to believe" applies to interviews too.

- **Highlight bottoms-up initiatives**: Mihika founded Design Nation, started hot seat tradition, pushed her new product through hackathons. Show entrepreneurial agency, not just executing handed-down roadmaps.

- **Discuss culture-building tangibly**: Don't just say "I built culture." Share rituals like hot seat, Figgies, Kudos Boards—interviewers remember specifics. Shows leadership beyond shipping features.

- **Frame pivots as learning, not failure**: When discussing projects that changed direction, use Mihika's framing: "We learned X, which helped us get to Y faster." Vision anchors make pivots feel like progress.

- **Ask about team motivations in behavioral questions**: "I always ask engineers how much they want to be involved in product decisions" shows thoughtful people management.
