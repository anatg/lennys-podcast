## TL;DR
Sam Lessin teaches founders and PMs proper business etiquette—not as superficial polish, but as a crucial trust-building skill in an era where software is commoditized and human relationships matter more. The core principle: etiquette is the skill of showing up in any room with a low heart rate, projecting calm confidence and abundance rather than desperate scarcity.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Low Heart Rate Protocol**: The goal of all etiquette is to show up in high-stakes situations (investor meetings, networking events, important dinners) with a calm, measured presence rather than anxious intensity. When you're racing into a Kleiner Perkins party thinking "this is my one shot," you project scarcity and desperation. Mastering etiquette allows you to signal competence, trustworthiness, and abundance even when stakes are high.

- **Abundance vs. Scarcity Mindset**: Even if a meeting *feels* like your only opportunity, you must project the confidence that other opportunities will come. This isn't arrogance—it's recognizing that desperate, transactional behavior ("I need your email and phone number right now") actively repels the opportunities you seek. Etiquette signals you're playing a long game.

- **The Ping Pong Conversation Model**: Every interaction should feel like a game of ping pong—hit the ball back and forth, not ten serves in a row. Ask questions but don't interrogate. Tell stories but don't monologue. Give information, then receive it. The person who monopolizes the conversation (either talking or only asking questions) has lost the rhythm.

- **Matching Without Mimicking**: Observe the room's vocabulary level, dress code, formality, and energy—then match it within one degree. Don't show up in a tuxedo to business casual. Don't order the most expensive item when others order moderately. Mirror enough to signal "I understand this context" without being so perfectly matched it's obvious you're performing.

- **Nothing Should Be Memorable (Except Ideas)**: Your dress, your fragrance, your tip, your napkin placement—none of it should stand out. The person should remember your conversation and ideas, not that you wore an odd cologne or tipped 100% or had your knife blade pointing the wrong direction. Etiquette gets out of the way so substance can shine.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Etiquette matters more as software commoditizes**: When technical differentiation is harder to achieve and AI democratizes coding, the human elements—trust, rapport, cultural fit, professionalism—become the differentiators in landing deals, hiring talent, and building partnerships. Founders who dismiss etiquette as unimportant are adding unnecessary friction to already-difficult paths.

2. **Early arrival = low heart rate**: Arrive 10-15 minutes early (not an hour—that's awkward). This buffer ensures you're never racing in flustered. When they keep you waiting a few minutes, your heart rate stays low. You're centered before the meeting even starts. This single habit projects more competence than most founders realize.

3. **The great to see you rule**: Never say "nice to meet you" when you're uncertain if you've met someone before (which happens constantly). Always use "great to see you"—it works whether you've met or not. Small linguistic tricks like this prevent embarrassing moments and signal social awareness.

4. **Leave them wanting more**: The best interactions end with the other person thinking "that was interesting, I'd like to continue this" rather than "I just heard this person's entire life story." Know when conversations are winding down. Exit gracefully. Don't overstay. The goal is a second meeting, not exhausting the relationship in one encounter.

5. **Famous people are just people (but respect the dynamic)**: When meeting well-known founders or investors, don't be sycophantic ("you're the most important person I've ever met") or falsely casual (pretending you don't know who Mark Zuckerberg is). Acknowledge their stature with respect, but treat them as humans. The gesture of offering to pay for dinner (even knowing they'll decline) matters more than the money.

6. **Ask questions, but not exclusively**: Questions show interest and engagement, but six questions in a row without giving anything back feels like an interrogation or extraction session. Balance inquiry with contribution. The best networkers give ideas, stories, and insights *while* asking good questions.

7. **Respect gatekeepers and support staff**: How you treat EAs, PAs, receptionists, servers, and office managers signals your character more than how you treat the CEO. Making eye contact, saying thank you, asking where to put your coffee cup—these small gestures reveal whether you see people as obstacles or as humans. Gatekeepers also control access, and they remember who was kind.

8. **Small talk is the TCP/IP handshake**: Those first moments of "how was your weekend?" or "this weather, right?" feel artificial, but they serve a function: syncing wavelengths before real communication begins. It's the social modem connecting. Skip it at your peril, especially when the other person isn't ready to dive straight into business.

9. **Offer to pay (and be prepared if they accept)**: Even if you're the junior person meeting a billionaire VC, offer to pick up the check. They'll almost certainly decline, but the gesture matters—it signals you view the relationship as one of peers, not charity. Caveat: if they ordered a $10,000 bottle of wine, you don't have to offer. But for normal dinners, the offer is expected.

10. **Humor is high-risk, high-reward**: A well-placed joke at the edge of appropriateness signals supreme confidence and social calibration. A joke that lands badly is catastrophic. Make fun of yourself, not others (unless you're extremely confident in your rapport). Keep a mental file of ranked jokes from safe to edgy, and deploy them with care.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI levels the playing field on technical skills, elevates soft skills**: As AI makes coding more accessible and product features easier to ship, differentiation increasingly comes from human-to-human interactions—building trust with customers, negotiating partnerships, managing teams, fundraising. Etiquette and interpersonal skills, once considered "nice to have," become core competencies.

- **AI for personal knowledge management**: Sam uses AI to build Letter Meme, a personal news aggregator that converts newsletters he doesn't have time to read into daily cartoon summaries. This is exemplary personal AI use—taking high-value inputs (trusted newsletters) and distilling them into actionable, digestible formats.

- **Contrarian AI investment thesis**: Sam argues seed investors will lose money on "AI companies" because even successful ones (like OpenAI) are so capital-intensive that seed returns are mediocre. Better to invest in companies *using* AI as infrastructure (like security firms handling AI-generated threats) than companies *selling* AI. The distinction: AI as tool vs. AI as product.

- **Cultural implications matter more than the tech**: The businesses worth building around AI are those addressing its *cultural* and *societal* impacts—e.g., Voight-Kampff test problems (distinguishing humans from bots), trust verification, data security—rather than foundation models or generic AI wrappers.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Control your entrance energy**: Arrive early to your PM interviews (10-15 minutes). Use that buffer to center yourself, review your notes, and ensure your heart rate is low when you walk into the room. First impressions are made in the lobby, not the conference room.

- **Master the "great to see you" line**: In networking settings at PM conferences or company events, never risk the "nice to meet you" / "we've met before" awkwardness. "Great to see you" always works.

- **Practice the ping pong conversation**: In behavioral interviews, balance answering questions thoroughly with asking thoughtful questions back. Don't turn "tell me about yourself" into a 10-minute monologue. Give the interviewer space to engage.

- **Show respect for everyone**: How you treat the recruiter coordinator who scheduled your interview, the receptionist who checks you in, and the junior PM who gives you the office tour matters as much as how you treat the hiring manager. Everyone reports back.

- **Don't overstay**: When you sense an interview is winding down naturally, help it wind down gracefully. "I know we're at time—thank you so much for this conversation" signals awareness and respect for their schedule.

- **Email follow-ups matter**: Send a brief, proofread thank-you note within 24 hours. Not long, not emoji-filled, just: "Thank you for your time. I appreciated learning about [specific thing]. Looking forward to next steps."

- **Prepare 3-5 good questions, not 20**: Asking questions shows engagement, but turning the interview into an interrogation feels extractive. Quality over quantity—ask questions that couldn't be Googled.

- **Dress one level up**: Research the company culture (check their career page photos, Glassdoor). If it's hoodies and jeans, wear a nice button-down and jeans. If it's business casual, wear business casual on the formal end. One notch up, never two.
