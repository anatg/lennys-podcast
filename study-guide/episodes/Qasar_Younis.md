# Qasar Younis — CEO & Co-founder, Applied Intuition

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Qasar Younis
- **Role/Company**: Co-founder & CEO, Applied Intuition ($15B valuation, 1,000+ employees)
- **Background**: Born on a farm in Pakistan, raised in Detroit. Started career as engineer at GM and Bosch, worked at Google, lived in Japan. Former COO of Y Combinator. MBA from Harvard. Third-time founder building software tools that add AI to physical vehicles (cars, tractors, planes, submarines, mining equipment). Customers include 18 of top 20 automakers plus major construction, mining, trucking companies and Department of Defense.

## TL;DR
Applied Intuition is the most important under-the-radar AI company in the world, adding intelligence to physical machines across farming, mining, construction, and transportation—industries desperate for automation as workforces age out. Qasar advocates for staying quiet and focused on customers/product rather than self-promotion, building sustainable businesses through radical pragmatism, and consuming diverse inputs (especially old books and history) to develop taste and judgment as a founder.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models
- **Radical Pragmatism**: Company's meta-value—reduce all decisions to what actually works rather than ideology or ego. Apply situational judgment constantly.
- **The Industrial Revolution Analogy**: View AI as analogous to the Industrial Revolution—focus on net positives over 20+ years rather than fixating on immediate disruptions. Both create suffering and abundance; our job is to steer toward the good.
- **Best Work Done Alone & Quietly**: Minimize public communication/promotion to maximize focus on customers and product. Every minute spent on content is time not spent building.
- **Speed + Safety Balance**: First company value is "move fast, move safe"—hold decisive action and open listening in tension simultaneously.
- **Half the Work is Follow-Up**: Excellence comes from basic maintenance—taking notes, following up, keeping things clean and organized. Not glamorous, but foundational.
- **Read Old Books**: Time filters out noise. Read material that has survived decades/centuries to consume high-signal ideas. Apply 80% old + 20% current as input diet.
- **The Foundation Test**: If things keep going wrong, examine whether the foundation (co-founders, market, life phase) is correct rather than just adjusting the surface.

## Top Insights (Timeless)
1. **Fear comes from misunderstanding**: If you're anxious about AI, spend time learning its limitations. You'll quickly see robots can't understand a cup upside-down yet. The best antidote to fear is knowledge.
2. **Good companies show traction early and sustain it for a decade+**: If you're two years in without clear market signals pointing to a more specific path, consider whether the foundation itself is wrong, not just tactics.
3. **First startup is practice**: Expect your first three years of founding to be learning, not success. You're building the founder muscle. Remove pressure to "make it" immediately.
4. **Physical AI will arrive faster than we imagine, but in unexpected forms**: Just as Instagram was impossible to envision in 2006 (pre-iPhone, pre-mass social networks, pre-ubiquitous cameras), the killer apps for physical AI will emerge from generally-available intelligent hardware in ways we can't predict now.
5. **The real AI revolution is in unsexy industries**: AI's biggest impact in 5–10 years will be farming, mining, construction, trucking—industries with aging workforces (average farmer age: late 50s) where automation is desperately needed, not just productivity nice-to-haves.
6. **Never spent raised capital**: Applied has 1,000+ engineers, nearly 10 years old, raised multiple rounds—and has never spent any of it. They're a functioning business. This discipline connects to culture (cleaning offices themselves, no shoes policy, radical focus).
7. **Emotions hurt rational decisions**: Create systems where the best idea wins regardless of who proposed it or how people feel. Assess managers on values adherence, compensate against it. Make the same decision process yield the same result across different people.
8. **You must be right, not just visionary**: Founders love taking credit for ambition, but the only evidence that matters is building a sustainable standalone business. Taste and judgment matter more than charisma.
9. **China comparisons are wrong**: Don't compare Huawei (quarter of employees are Communist Party members, goal is state power not profit) to Apple. You're comparing companies to governments. Not apples-to-apples.
10. **Self-driving cars will be ubiquitous in 5–7 years**: Both L2++ (Tesla approach: cheap sensors, no maps) and L4 (Waymo approach: expensive sensors, HD maps) will become standard. We'll look back at human-only driving like we view child labor—shocking it was ever acceptable given 30,000+ annual US deaths.

## AI & The Changing PM Role
- **Physical AI is the real frontier**: While software AI (Claude, ChatGPT) gets attention, the biggest impact will be intelligence in physical machines—tractors, mining vehicles, trucks, defense equipment. These industries face existential workforce shortages.
- **Self-driving as baseline expectation**: Just as NAV systems went from premium to CarPlay/Android Auto standard, autonomy will become expected in every vehicle within 5–7 years, dramatically reducing accidents and changing how society thinks about mobility.
- **Robots are already here**: Self-driving cars are robots. Factory automation has been advanced for 25 years. The shift isn't robots appearing, it's intelligence being added to existing physical systems, not humanoids from scratch.
- **Abundance enables access**: Free/cheap autonomous mobility could transform lives for disabled people, those without cars, people in Rwanda two hours from a hospital. AI can democratize access like mobile phones democratized communication.
- **The gap between demo and product is vast**: Nunchuck-wielding humanoid videos are pre-programmed and cost $15M to produce. They look impressive but aren't generalizable intelligence. Don't confuse theater with deployable technology.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
_How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:_
- **Demonstrate breadth of inputs**: Reference diverse reading, cross-industry examples, historical parallels. Shows you think in systems and can draw non-obvious connections (like Industrial Revolution → AI transition).
- **Show pragmatism over ideology**: When discussing past decisions, emphasize outcomes and customer impact over theoretical frameworks. "Radical pragmatism" means adapting to reality, not defending your original plan.
- **Emphasize listening culture**: Describe how you've created environments where junior voices surface critical insights. Give examples of changing course based on frontline feedback.
- **Connect craft to outcomes**: Draw lines between "maintenance" work (follow-up, documentation, hygiene) and business results. Shows you understand unsexy work drives excellence.
- **Use automotive/manufacturing analogies**: Physical world examples (factory quality, car safety) make abstract concepts concrete. Demonstrates understanding of systems thinking and scale.
- **Acknowledge fear, then overcome with knowledge**: When discussing risk/uncertainty, show you investigate deeply rather than reacting emotionally. Frame anxiety as information-gathering opportunity.
- **Be honest about what you don't know**: Qasar repeatedly says "I don't know exactly how/why X leads to Y, but I observe the pattern." Intellectual humility while maintaining conviction is powerful.
