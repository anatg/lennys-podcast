- **Make the Other Mistake (Prompting)**: If Claude tends to be too nice, explicitly ask it to "be brutal" or "roast this strategy." Push against natural tendencies to get more critical, creative output.

- **Context and prompting are specialized skills**: "Knowing what to ask the AI, how to compose the question, how to think about structuring a change between backend and frontend—those are still very difficult and specialized skills" that require engineering expertise.

- **Product development will radically change within a year**: Current process is painful given new capabilities. Expect fundamental shifts in how teams conceive of building and shipping software as AI capabilities continue advancing.

## TL;DR
Mike Krieger shares how over 90% of Anthropic's code is now AI-written, creating new bottlenecks in decision-making and merge queues rather than engineering. He outlines Anthropic's strategy to differentiate from ChatGPT by focusing on builders, developers, and agentic use cases—not chasing consumer mind share—and emphasizes leaning into core strengths and the power of independent, founder-like product development even within a large company.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Three-Part AI Utility Equation**: For an AI product to be truly useful, three parts must converge:  
  1. **Model Intelligence** (the core research/models)  
  2. **Context and Memory** (MCP and integrations)  
  3. **Applications and UI** (discoverability, workflows, UX)  
  Without all three, you won't have a great product.

- **MCP (Model Context Protocol) Philosophy**: Instead of building each integration from scratch, create a repeatable protocol that others can build on. This "commoditizes your complements" (Joel Spolsky reference), allowing third parties to add context for Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, etc. When more integrations exist, everyone benefits.

- **Make the Other Mistake** (Prompting Strategy): If Claude tends to be too nice, push it to be brutal; if it's too vague, ask it to "think hard." By asking it to lean into the opposite of its default tendency, you get more critical, creative output.

- **Product-Research Integration over UX-Only Product**: The highest leverage at Anthropic comes from embedding product people with researchers during model post-training, not just building UX on finished models. Product teams shape model capabilities early, not afterward.

- **Challenger Brand Differentiation**: Rather than compete head-on with a dominant player (like ChatGPT in consumer mind share), "look yourself in the mirror and embrace who you are and what you could be rather than who others are." Focus on unique strengths—builders, developers, agentic coding—and let that define your strategy.

- **Critical Path Bottleneck Theory**: When one part of the system (e.g., engineering) becomes super-efficient via AI, new bottlenecks emerge (decision-making, alignment, merge queue capacity). Continually identify and address the new critical path constraints.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Timelines for AI capability are accelerating faster than many expect.**  
   Mike came in skeptical but has become a believer in predictions like 90% SWE-Bench performance by end of 2025 (already at ~72%). He describes reading Dario Amodei's *Machines of Loving Grace* essay ("AI 2027") and realizing he's "the character in the story"—the future is arriving sooner than it seems.

2. **Over 90% of code at Anthropic is AI-generated, creating entirely new bottlenecks.**  
   Engineering throughput is no longer the constraint. Instead, bottlenecks appear upstream (decision-making, alignment on what to build) and downstream (merge queue capacity, review processes). Traditional product development processes haven't adapted yet.

3. **AI can now provide genuinely independent, creative strategic insights.**  
   With Claude Opus 4, Mike experienced Claude offering novel strategic angles he hadn't considered—no longer "anodyne" feedback. This shift happened within the last month of the interview, showing rapid capability growth.

4. **Curiosity, independent thought, and the scientific process are the enduring human skills.**  
   Mike encourages his kids (and teams) to ask "How would we find out?" before reaching for Claude. Nurturing inquiry, experimentation, and a willingness to challenge AI outputs (his daughter: "You can ask Claude, but I know I'm right") are critical in an AI-augmented world.

5. **"Patient Zero" companies using AI to build AI reveal the future of work.**  
   The Claude Code team uses Claude Code to build Claude Code in a "self-improving" loop. They've moved from line-by-line PR reviews to having another Claude instance review code, with humans doing acceptance testing. Contributions from non-TypeScript engineers now happen in an hour via AI assistance.

6. **Know when to shut down vs. persevere: energy and traction matter more than stubbornness.**  
   Artifact had loyal users but lacked viral growth or the "energy" in the product-market system. Mike describes it as "10 units of input for one unit of output" instead of the reverse. Calling it early freed the team to pursue more impactful work and was met with more respect than criticism.

7. **Metrics for AI products must move beyond traditional engagement.**  
   A good Claude conversation could be 2 messages or 200. Optimizing for engagement or likability risks making Claude sycophantic or prolonging conversations unnecessarily. Better metrics involve real value delivered: "Did it help you get your work done?"—though that's harder to quantify than traditional dashboards.

8. **Differentiated domain knowledge, go-to-market, and form factors remain defensible for startups.**  
   Mike advises AI founders to focus on:  
   - Deep understanding of a specific industry (legal, biotech, lab equipment)  
   - Relationships with the actual decision-maker in that industry  
   - Novel interfaces/form factors that incumbents can't easily replicate  
   - A startup mentality of existential urgency ("you against the world")  
   These aren't areas where foundational model companies will necessarily compete.

9. **Context is as important as model intelligence.**  
   An AI answer using generic web search vs. one with access to your Slack, Google Drive, and specific documents via MCP is "entirely the difference between a good answer and a bad answer." Product work now involves ensuring the right context flows through to the model.

10. **Building at the edge of model capabilities reveals what's next.**  
    The best partners/customers push models to the breaking point, then are "surprised by the next model." This feedback loop—trying hard problems, hitting walls, then seeing new releases solve them—is what drove companies like Cursor, Manus, and Harvey to success.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Embedding PMs with researchers yields the most leverage.** At Anthropic, the highest-value work comes from PMs involved in post-training and fine-tuning conversations, not just building UX on top of finished models.
