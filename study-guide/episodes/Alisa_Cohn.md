## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Observable Facts Framework**: Frame all feedback on observable, specific behaviors — not judgment, not inference about character. Prep by asking: "What's my evidence that this is happening?" Makes feedback easier to give AND easier to hear. Observable facts vs. "I feel like you're..."

- **Three Questions to End Every Meeting**:
  1. What did we decide here? (Go around the room — 6 people give 6 different answers)
  2. Who needs to do what by when?
  3. Who else needs to know?
  These three questions prevent re-meeting the same decision, unclear ownership, and siloed communication cascading failures.

- **Pre-Firing Conversation**: Before firing, have a crystal-clear "last chance" conversation: state specific changes needed, give a concrete deadline (30 days), and name the consequence ("we'll have to part ways"). Most leaders skip this. It makes firing easier if needed (no surprise) and often makes it unnecessary.

- **Leader's Job ≠ Making Employees Happy**: The leader's job is creating conditions to win. Happy-chasing (avoiding hard conversations, bending to avoid upset) leads to unclear expectations, tolerated underperformance, and eventual failure. Culture = how we get work done here (accountability, standards, norms) not perks and socials.

- **Founder Prenup**: Questions every co-founding team should work through before starting:
  1. **Values**: Run a values exercise — pick 20, winnow to 5, find top 3. Compare and discuss conflicts.
  2. **Vision of success**: VC-backed unicorn? Profitable independence? Everyone assumes they're aligned until it's too late.
  3. **Conflict handling style**: Ask someone close to you how you *actually* handle conflict (you may not know).
  4. **Decision-making when you disagree**: Most expertise wins? Person who cares most wins? Alternating?
  5. **Company culture philosophy**: Results-first vs. relationship-first — both can work, but misalignment between co-founders creates two companies.
  (65% of startups fail due to co-founder conflict — Noam Wasserman)

- **Personal Operating Manual**: A written guide to working with you — communication preference, focused block vs. frequent check-ins, best way to reach you urgently, pet peeves, what earns a gold star, delegation style (check in weekly or tell me when done). Saves enormous relationship friction before it starts.

- **Patience vs. Process Diagnostic**: Leaders facing a stalled project must ask: is this a patience situation or a process problem? Sign of a process problem: you can't visualize a clear path to completion and you're not actually in touch with what's happening on the ground.

## Scripts for Difficult Conversations

**Performance feedback (third-party reports):**
> "[Name], I want to chat with you about the way you're interacting with your peers. What I'm hearing from them is [specific observable facts]. We both know that [shared goal] matters. I want to hear your perspective, but I need us to leave this conversation knowing how you're going to [specific change]."

**Performance feedback (first-person observation):**
> "What I've observed is [specific, observable]. I'd love you to look at these examples of what doing it well looks like and model your work on them. The level of your [skill] reflects the level of your thinking."

**When someone gets defensive:**
> "Let's pause. I want you to know I'm telling you this to help you — your career matters to me. I'm noticing the energy between us has changed. Can we continue now or should we pause and come back?"

**Clarity warning before firing:**
> "We've talked multiple times about [X]. After [timeframe], I still see [same issue]. I need you to fix this within the next 30 days. Otherwise, I'm sorry to say, we'll have to part ways. I know you have it in you to change — I value what you bring. But if you don't fix these things, we don't have a future together."

**Not getting a promotion:**
> "I know this is going to be challenging to hear. We're going to look for an external candidate for this role. Here's why: [specific reasoning]. I want you to know it's really important to me that you succeed in your career here. When we bring this person in, I'm committed to finding someone who'll help you develop the skills for the next step."

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The leader who avoids hard conversations creates more problems than the conversation itself would have.** The famous story: eight months in, someone was told they were failing. "Why didn't anyone tell me? I would have changed." Avoidance is selfishness dressed as kindness.

2. **If you feel yourself "hoping it works out," that's the tell.** Crystal clarity vs. hope is the line. If you can't visualize a concrete path forward, you haven't been clear enough with the person.

3. **Positive feedback must meet the same standard as constructive feedback.** Vague "good job" is useless. "The way you kept your peers in the loop — considering you've been here three months — is extraordinary. Keep doing that." Specific, observable, meaningful. Builds the trust reservoir that makes hard feedback land.

4. **Build a relationship before you need to give hard feedback.** When people regularly hear what they're doing well from you, they receive hard feedback through the lens of "this person wants to help me" not "my manager hates me."

5. **Founders always reinvent leadership only to discover the same truths.** OKRs, roles and responsibilities, clear accountability — the "process" founders reject eventually becomes the process they adopt. Speed through the reinvention phase; don't get stuck.

6. **Values misalignment is invisible until it explodes.** Co-founders text cadence fight (one wanted weekends off, one needed responsiveness) — a five-minute conversation could have prevented months of resentment. Talk about it early and often.

7. **Rock bottom is reversible.** Alisa cried on the floor for an hour when she thought her coaching career might not work. The answer: get up, take a nap, make more calls. Action compounds.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"How do you give difficult feedback?"**: Observable facts framework + specific scripts. "What I've observed..." not "you are..." Prep by asking "What's my evidence?" End by agreeing on specific change and next steps.
- **"Tell me about a time you handled a performance issue"**: Use the pre-firing conversation model — crystal clarity with a specific deadline and named consequence. Shows you don't enable underperformance or create surprises.
- **"How do you run effective meetings?"**: Three-question close (what decided, who does what by when, who needs to know). Shows process discipline.
- **"How do you build culture?"**: Leader's job is conditions to win, not happiness. Culture = how we get work done here. Avocado toast isn't culture; accountability norms are.
- **"How do you work with co-founders or peer executives?"**: Values alignment, Founder Prenup process, Personal Operating Manual — shows that relationship structure enables velocity and prevents friction.
