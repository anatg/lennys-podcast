## TL;DR
Daniel Lereya shares how Monday.com transformed from shipping slowly to launching 30 columns in a month by setting impossibly ambitious goals and focusing relentlessly on customer impact rather than features. He reveals how radical transparency—sharing all company metrics with every employee, even before going public—became a strategic advantage by getting "everyone's brains in the challenge, not just one centralized brain and a lot of working hands."

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Deadline Trap**: Set time-boxed goals (not effort-based) to force teams to think differently. Example: "We'll ship in three weeks" forces focus on core value, not "work until perfect." This prevents teams from inventing unnecessary features and ensures they validate with real users quickly.

- **Impact-Driven Product Management**: A great PM is "relentless until they get impact and validate that impact is in place." Every initiative must answer: (1) What will change for customers? (2) How will we measure success? Without these, you risk building "songs for the door"—features never used as intended.

- **The Quarterly Transformation Question**: Every quarter (or relevant time period), ask: "How will the company and product be different and better for customers?" If you can't articulate transformative change—only incremental improvements—you have a focus problem.

- **Speed Through Thinking Differently**: True speed isn't working harder or longer hours ("fake speed"). It's working smarter by: (a) identifying what moves the needle, (b) thinking fundamentally differently about solutions. Ambitious goals force this by making old approaches impossible.

- **Platform Infrastructure Investment**: When building platforms, define what a component "is" (all required capabilities) before scaling. Monday defined what every column type must do (export to Excel, filter, sort, etc.), built shared infrastructure, then individual columns became one-day builds instead of four months.

- **Daily Numbers Living**: Every team maintains a daily numbers update in Slack with metrics they care about. Teams should "live by their numbers," celebrating wins and investigating changes in real-time, not waiting for quarterly reviews.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Competitors as gifts**: When Monday saw competitors launch 30 new column types (vs. their 5, each taking 4 months), they reframed it as proof of possibility. "We received a gift from our competitors. They showed us that it's possible." This eliminated excuses and forced breakthrough thinking—they shipped 30 columns in six weeks.

2. **What got you here won't get you there**: Your greatest strengths at one scale become liabilities at the next. Daniel's "superpower" of mastering every detail became damaging as the company grew. Leaders must constantly evaluate: "What does my current role actually need?" and let go of past superpowers.

3. **The paradox of bold moves**: When successful, companies fear breaking what works. But "not taking bold risks is a risk itself." Monday launched five new products simultaneously (not one safe pilot), which was terrifying but transformed their market position. Some products succeeded (Monday CRM now grows faster than the original product), others were folded back—but they learned fast.

4. **Impact over execution**: You can have "amazing execution" and still fail. Monday shipped constantly but realized they couldn't point to one transformative thing in three months. The shift: measure teams not on features shipped but on validated customer impact. Sometimes the biggest impact isn't building new features—it's making existing value more accessible.

5. **Radical transparency builds partnership**: Sharing all metrics—revenue, churn, signups—even with interviewees, seemed risky. But transparency gives employees "a sense of deep partnership" and gets "everyone's brains in the challenge." As a public company, they created 10b5-1 trading plans so PMs could still access confidential data without insider trading concerns.

6. **The "what can we be proud of" test**: Ask teams: "What are you most proud of from the last three months?" If it takes a long time to answer, you're not focused and not maximizing impact. The answer should be immediate and transformative, not a list of incremental improvements.

7. **Release "premature" products intentionally**: Getting feedback that "this is premature, we need more" is actually success—it means you shipped fast enough to learn. Teams should aim for criticism that the product isn't complete yet, proving they didn't over-build based on assumptions.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Personal AI usage for competitive intelligence**: Daniel used ChatGPT to research competitor pricing strategies in two hours, getting "the full perspective on what everyone else is doing"—work that would traditionally require extensive analyst support.

- **Non-obvious AI applications**: Put MRI results into ChatGPT for immediate analysis rather than waiting days for a doctor appointment. This "opened his mind" about productizing AI—the technology is powerful, but the challenge is making it accessible (like the MRI use case wasn't obvious to most people he shared it with).

- **Monday's AI Blocks**: Built no-code AI integration for workflows since 70% of Monday's customers are non-tech. The discovery moment: realized only a few thousand accounts were using AI features due to ToS requirements, not product limitations. Fixed accessibility in two weeks, opening to 98% of customers—reminder that impact isn't just building features but ensuring reach.

- **Conversational depth advantage**: AI's power isn't just getting information quickly but the ability to "continue and deep dive" indefinitely without human fatigue or annoyance.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate impact orientation**: Don't just describe features you shipped—articulate the customer problem, the metric you moved, and how you validated success. Practice the discipline: "What did this change for users and how did we measure it?"

- **Show you think in transformations, not increments**: When discussing achievements, avoid words like "enhanced," "augmented," "extended." Be specific about transformative changes. Can you point to a quarterly shift that fundamentally altered your product's value proposition?

- **Embrace bold goals as thinking tools**: Share examples where you set seemingly impossible goals (10X, radically compressed timelines) that forced entirely new approaches. Show you understand ambitious goals aren't about working harder but thinking differently.

- **Discuss how you've evolved your strengths**: Prepare stories about skills that made you successful at one level but you had to evolve or abandon as you scaled. This shows self-awareness and adaptability—critical for senior roles.

- **Know your numbers intimately**: Be ready to discuss metrics you track daily/weekly, not just quarterly OKRs. Show you "live your numbers" and can discuss specific spikes, drops, and what you learned from investigating them.

- **Share moments of vulnerability and resilience**: Don't just share successes—discuss moments you questioned if you were the right person for the role, and how you pushed through. This authenticity resonates, especially for high-growth environments.
