## TL;DR
Alex's core insight: most of how we build products and organizations assumes a "builder" mindset — make a plan, execute the plan. But the highest-leverage approach is gardening: find things with compounding potential, plant cheap seeds, water what's growing, stop when signals stop. Combine with a distant North Star and navigate via the "adjacent possible" — take only the next step that's clearly safe, which opens the next set of options.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Gardener vs. Builder Mindset**: Builders create value bounded by their effort. Gardeners find things that can grow on their own and direct/curate them. You are "farming for miracles" — on a systemic basis, some seeds will become oak trees. The art is recognizing compounding potential (network effects, ecosystem dynamics) and planting enough seeds that one almost certainly grows without needing to predict which.

- **Organizational Kayfabe**: From professional wrestling — "a thing everyone knows is fake and yet everyone acts like is real." Small, rational individual decisions (rounding a yellow to green before it rolls up) compound across layers until leadership is many orders of magnitude from ground truth. Late-stage: individuals start to genuinely believe the kayfabe to avoid cognitive dissonance. This is organizational death. To fight it: create psychological safety for disconfirming evidence before it reaches crisis mass.

- **Slime Mold Theory of Organizations**: Organizations (especially autonomy-valuing tech companies) behave like slime molds — emergent, bottom-up, self-organizing. Options: (1) drive like a big rig — intentional pivots, strong program management, accept slower steering; (2) swarm of sports cars — full autonomy but coordination gaps will show. Apple chose coherence; AWS chose swarm. Fighting the underlying reality fails. Embracing it reveals slime molds are remarkably effective at finding solutions to problems they didn't know they were searching for.

- **Adjacent Possible**: The set of actions right in front of you that would almost certainly work if taken. Don't try to jump to an end state. Take the clearly-safe next step → world reconfigures → new adjacent possible opens. Combine with a distant North Star (3-5 year, low-resolution, "I could see how that could work"): in each moment, pick the adjacent action with the steepest gradient toward the North Star. Avoids both aimless incrementalism and castles in the sky.

- **70/30 Exploratory Work Rule**: 70% of your team's effort should go to clearly-legible, clearly-valuable work (prevents "what does this team do anyway?"). The remaining 30% is for planting seeds — supporting junior PMs' interesting hunches, finding emergent opportunities, experimenting with adjacent ideas. The cover fire of the 70% gives the 30% the space it needs.

- **Strategy Salons / Nerd Clubs**: Optional, secret, "yes and"-only environments for half-formed ideas. Key design principles: (1) only people who intrinsically want to be there (not mandatory); (2) no negative "no but" energy — you can ignore ideas you don't like, or respond with "I wonder if..." framing; (3) one facilitator who seeds conversations and protects norms; (4) dribble in new perspectives (1-3/week); (5) send FOMO updates after live sessions. Stochastically generates insights that wouldn't emerge from top-down idea generation.

- **LLMs as Magical Duct Tape**: LLMs are formed from "the distilled intuition of all of society" at a cost structure between human labor and plain computing. They change BOTH sides of the cost equation: cheap to write shitty-but-good-enough software, and more expensive to run than plain computing. Consumer apps can't be ad-supported anymore (inference costs too high). Key question: not "how does LLM solve my problem" but "what can I now build that I couldn't before?"

- **Taste as AI-Era Differentiator**: As LLMs commoditize output, the differentiator is a distinctive perspective that resonates across diverse audiences. Operationally: "differentiate from what the LLM would have written if given the same prompt." Find what you say that resonates with diverse (sales + engineering + design) audiences and lean into that.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Farm for miracles, not plans.** On a systemic basis, if you plant enough cheap seeds in compounding ecosystems, one will grow dramatically. You don't need to know which one in advance. The leverage of a compounding win vastly exceeds the cost of all the failed seeds.

2. **Kayfabe compoundsracially.** Every organization has it. Individual rational action (rounding yellow to green) leads to emergent collective dysfunction. The solution isn't identifying bad actors — there aren't any. The solution is creating channels for disconfirming evidence to reach leadership before it hits crisis mass.

3. **The adjacent possible is smaller than you think — and that's liberating.** You have full agency over the subset actually within reach, and each action reshapes what's next. Stop trying to jump to end states. Take the step that's clearly safe; the path emerges from there.

4. **AI makes individuals better in ways they hide from their organizations.** People use AI to do their job twice as fast and don't tell their managers. This means AI productivity gains are invisible at the org level — but genuinely present at the individual level. The question is how to make these gains collective.

5. **Great metaphors leave one dot unconnected.** This lets readers engage with the argument to close the final connection themselves, making insight feel self-discovered. It also lets you say controversial things safely — the metaphor implies without asserting.

6. **Always rules beat sometimes rules for self-control.** Binary commitments that you can keep indefinitely beat conditional ones you'll negotiate away in moments of weakness. The streak becomes its own motivating force: "Is today the worst day of all 500 days in the streak to break it? No."

7. **The secrets of life sound like Hallmark cards.** Deep insights have been discovered many times before. They sound trite to people who haven't lived enough to need them. Plant them anyway — they germinate when the soil is ready.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- LLMs are a genuinely disruptive technology — they change assumptions baked into every product and business model. We're using end-stage playbooks from the last era in a world that's tilted five degrees on its axis.
- The right question: "What can I now build that I couldn't before with magical duct tape?" Not: "How do I retrofit AI into my existing product?"
- Inference costs make consumer advertising models unviable for AI-heavy products.
- We're in the "community gardening phase, not the factory farming phase" of AI — curiosity and play matter more than execution precision right now.
- AI makes individuals better in ways they hide from organizations. Visible productivity gains at org level may lag reality significantly.
- Risk: we're default-assuming everyone will be locked inside "a super God AI Clippy" — one of a dozen monolithic app boxes. Alex wants to use AI to *escape* the box: to let software and human agency recombine in unexpected ways outside of aggregator control.
- WebSim (type a URL, AI invents what that website is) as the archetype of "alien and weird yet compelling" — the category of AI-native products worth paying attention to.
