# Jeffrey Pfeffer — The Seven Rules of Power

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Jeffrey Pfeffer
- **Role/Company**: Professor of Organizational Behavior at Stanford Graduate School of Business
- **Background**: Jeffrey has taught at Stanford for over 50 years and created one of the most popular and oversubscribed MBA courses: "Paths to Power." He is the author of multiple books on power, including his latest, *Seven Rules of Power*, which synthesizes decades of research and teaching on how to acquire and use power effectively. He approaches power as a set of learnable skills backed by social science research, not innate personality traits.

## TL;DR
Jeffrey Pfeffer teaches that power is a learnable skill set essential for career success, backed by decades of research showing political skill correlates with higher salaries, promotions, and job satisfaction. The seven rules—get out of your own way, break rules, appear powerful, build your brand, network relentlessly, use your power, and understand that success excuses almost everything—are not about personality but about mastering practical behaviors that help you get things done and advance your career, especially if you come from disadvantaged backgrounds.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Seven Rules of Power**: (1) Get out of your own way—overcome imposter syndrome, stop apologizing, don't worry excessively about being liked; (2) Break the rules—stand out by doing things differently, disrupt conventional wisdom, ask for help; (3) Show up in a powerful fashion—master body language, make eye contact, speak louder, don't read from notes, use humor; (4) Build a powerful brand—make yourself visible and known, differentiate yourself, create value that gets noticed; (5) Network relentlessly—build weak ties, become a broker connecting people, practice generosity; (6) Use your power—the more you accomplish, the more resources and opportunities you'll receive; (7) Success excuses almost everything—once you have power, what you did to get there will be forgiven or forgotten.

- **Political Skill Research (Gerald Ferris)**: Empirical research demonstrates that political skill is associated with positive outcomes including higher salary, promotions, career happiness, job satisfaction, and lower stress. This legitimizes power skills as measurable, learnable capabilities.

- **The Knowing-Doing Gap**: Knowledge without action leads to forgetting. To make learning stick, you must practice and implement what you learn through progressive challenges that push you slightly beyond your comfort zone (similar to the 15% rule from Touchy-Feely).

- **Power vs. Autonomy Trade-off**: You can have power or you can have autonomy, but you cannot have both. More power means less control over your time, more scrutiny, loss of privacy, and higher expectations. Understanding this trade-off helps you make intentional career choices.

- **Judgment Suspension**: To build productive relationships with people on your critical path, suspend personal judgment. Whether you like someone is irrelevant—if you need their collaboration to get things done, you must build a positive working relationship.

- **Weak Ties Strength (Mark Granovetter)**: People strongly tied to you (family, close friends, coworkers) know the same things you do. Building ties with people different from you provides non-redundant information and access to new opportunities—the best jobs often come through weak tie referrals.

- **Visibility + Substance Formula**: Visibility without substance means people will know you're useless. Substance without visibility means no one will know what you're capable of. You need both.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Power skills are learnable, not innate personality traits**: Everything Jeffrey teaches—networking, appearing powerful, building your brand—consists of skills that can be mastered through practice and coaching, similar to learning tennis or piano. This reframes power from something you either have or don't have to something you can systematically develop.

2. **The world rewards power, not just competence**: Research shows that political skill leads to higher salaries, faster promotions, greater career satisfaction, and less stress. If you want to advance, understanding and practicing power dynamics is not optional—it's essential, especially for people from underrepresented backgrounds who face structural disadvantages.

3. **Getting out of your own way is the first and most important rule**: Imposter syndrome, excessive apologizing, worrying about being liked, and pre-emptive self-deprecation all undermine your power. If you don't believe you deserve your position, others won't either. Stop apologizing for taking up space and stop using disempowering language about yourself.

4. **Building a personal brand is about visibility for a purpose**: You cannot be promoted or given opportunities if people don't know who you are. Creating a brand isn't about vanity—it's about ensuring decision-makers know your capabilities when opportunities arise. This can mean starting a podcast, writing, leading initiatives, or simply making your contributions visible.

5. **Networking is fundamentally about generosity, not manipulation**: The best networkers focus on "What can I do for you?" and connecting people who benefit from knowing each other. The broader your network, the more knowledge you access and the more value you can create by being a broker between disconnected groups.

6. **Breaking rules and asking for help are underrated strategies**: Conventional wisdom often favors incumbents. To stand out and succeed, you must do things differently—disrupt expectations, ask questions others won't, and request help despite discomfort. The worst outcome of asking is "no," leaving you no worse off than if you hadn't asked.

7. **Use your power to build more power—it's self-reinforcing**: When you deliver results using your power and influence, you gain more resources, more people want to work with you, and you receive bigger opportunities. Power creates a virtuous cycle, but only if you actually use it rather than hoarding it.

8. **Life is self-fulfilling, not homeostatic**: People fear that acquiring power will eventually lead to downfall (the Icarus myth), but research and reality show the opposite. Success builds on success. Once you have power, people overlook your past mistakes and want to be associated with your success. The world doesn't naturally correct for power imbalances—it amplifies them.

9. **Don't confuse the tool with how it's used**: Power is like a hammer or a knife—it can be used for good (curing cancer, building organizations that help people) or for bad (exploitation, self-enrichment). If good people don't acquire power, only bad people will have it. As the opening quote states: "If you want power to be used for good, more good people need to have power."

10. **The "doing power" assignments accelerate learning**: Jeffrey structures his class around progressive practice—networking dinners, creating awards, writing brand statements, acting exercises—because passive learning doesn't stick. Students push 15% beyond their comfort zone repeatedly until new behaviors become natural.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

**How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:**

- **Build visibility before you need it**: Don't wait until you're job searching to build your personal brand. Start a side project, contribute to open source, write about product topics, or speak at meetups now so that when opportunities arise, decision-makers already know who you are.

- **Practice appearing powerful in low-stakes situations**: Record yourself giving presentations, practice making eye contact instead of reading from notes, work on speaking louder and using open body language. These are learnable skills that signal competence and confidence in interviews.

- **Reframe networking as generosity**: Before interviews, reach out to people at target companies not to ask for referrals immediately, but to offer value—share an article, make an introduction, or ask thoughtful questions. This builds genuine relationships that naturally lead to opportunities.

- **Suspend judgment about interviewers and companies**: Even if you don't love everything about a company's culture or an interviewer's style, focus on building positive rapport. Your job in an interview is to demonstrate you can collaborate effectively, not to evaluate whether you like everyone.

- **Ask for what you need without apologizing**: If you need clarification on a question, more time to think, or a follow-up conversation, ask directly. Don't preface requests with "I don't know if this is useful, but..." or "Sorry to bother you." Confidence in asking signals confidence in performing.

- **Prepare stories that show you "use your power"**: Interviewers want to see you can get things done through influence. Prepare examples where you mobilized resources, persuaded stakeholders, or drove results despite not having formal authority. Demonstrate you understand organizational dynamics.

- **Connect your work to outcomes that matter**: When discussing past projects, don't just describe what you built—explain the business impact, the stakeholders you influenced, and the organizational barriers you overcame. Show you understand power dynamics in getting products shipped.

- **Leverage weak ties for referrals**: The best job leads often come from acquaintances, not close friends. Reach out to former colleagues, conference contacts, or people you've met once who work at target companies. They have access to different information and networks than your inner circle.
