## TL;DR
Jessica Lachs shares how she built DoorDash's renowned centralized analytics organization, emphasizing that data teams should have a seat at the table as business impact drivers—not service functions. She explains her contrarian approach to org structure, the importance of simple metrics over composite scores, finding short-term proxies for long-term outcomes, and hiring for curiosity over just technical skills.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Centralized Analytics Model with Embedded Pods**: Structure the data team centrally (all report through one leader) but organize into pods that map to business units (marketing, product, operations). This preserves team culture, consistent talent bar, and knowledge sharing while ensuring data scientists feel embedded with their business partners and share the same goals.

- **Short-Term Metrics as Proxies for Long-Term Outcomes**: Never goal on long-term metrics like retention directly—find short-term inputs you can measure and move quickly that drive the long-term output. Example: Instead of goaling on retention, goal on the behaviors that drive retention (engagement triggers, quality metrics, etc.).

- **Common Currency Translation**: Quantify all business levers (price changes, delivery time improvements, selection increases) in common terms like Gross Order Value (GOV) or volume. This enables cross-functional trade-off decisions—understanding whether a dollar is better spent on marketing, logistics improvements, or supply-side growth.

- **Fail State Metrics**: Beyond averages, explicitly track and goal on edge cases and disaster scenarios (like "Never Delivered" orders at DoorDash). These rare but terrible experiences drive churn and cost far more than their frequency suggests, and often don't show up when looking at averages.

- **Extreme Ownership Culture**: Data scientists should own outcomes, not just their functional box. If solving a problem requires calling customers or doing work outside traditional data science, that's part of the job. The goal is to figure out what's happening and drive business impact.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Analytics as business partner, not service function**: The data team should have a seat at the table alongside product, engineering, and operations—bringing insights, opportunities, and recommendations for "what do we do now," not just answering "why" questions or fulfilling Jira tickets.

2. **Simple metrics beat composite scores**: Avoid creating complex weighted composite metrics that nobody understands. A 0.35 "health score" is meaningless. Instead, use simple, intuitive metrics (even if multiple) that people can understand, internalize, and actually move. The team executed 95% of impact by focusing on the top 3 clear inputs rather than a perfect composite of 6.

3. **Curiosity cannot be taught**: When hiring data scientists, technical skills are table stakes. The differentiator is self-motivated curiosity—people who pull on threads, notice when something seems off, and proactively investigate without being told. Test for this by including inconsistencies in case interviews and observing if candidates notice and dig deeper.

4. **Central org benefits compound**: Centralized analytics organizations enable consistent talent bar, better growth opportunities (cross-team moves, diverse promotion paths), consistent methodologies and metrics across the company, faster scaling by recognizing patterns, and stronger team culture/brand for recruiting and retention.

5. **The average is misleading—look at distributions**: Looking at average metrics obscures critical insights. The referral channel appeared mediocre on average but was actually bimodal: great customers referring great customers (high payback) plus fraud/discount-seekers (terrible payback). Breaking down distributions reveals optimization opportunities that averages hide.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Democratizing data access through AI**: DoorDash built "Ask Data AI" (internal chatbot) to help non-technical employees write and edit SQL queries, reducing bandwidth requirements on the analytics team. This empowers business users to be more self-sufficient while freeing data scientists to focus on higher-impact work.

- **Shifting from reactive to proactive**: AI tools help reduce time spent on routine query requests, allowing more time for exploratory deep dives, hackathons, and self-directed work that finds major opportunities (like the referral fraud investigation that uncovered bimodal distribution).

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate business impact orientation**: In interviews, show you think beyond answering questions to driving outcomes. Frame your past work in terms of business decisions made, not just analyses completed.

- **Tell stories of extreme ownership**: Prepare examples where you went beyond your role boundaries to solve problems—calling customers, working with other functions, building tools yourself when needed.

- **Practice trade-off communication**: Be ready to discuss how you prioritize work, communicate trade-offs to stakeholders, and align on what matters most when capacity is limited.

- **Show curiosity in action**: When presented with data or cases in interviews, actively notice inconsistencies and ask probing questions. Don't just answer what's asked—demonstrate you pull on threads.

- **Explain metrics thoughtfully**: Be prepared to discuss why you chose specific metrics over alternatives, how you simplified complex measurement problems, and how you aligned incentives across teams.

- **Emphasize team building**: If interviewing for leadership roles, discuss how you built culture, maintained quality bar, created growth opportunities, and balanced centralization with embedding.
