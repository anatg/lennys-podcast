## TL;DR
Josh Miller shares how The Browser Company builds Arc by optimizing for feelings over metrics, hiring for "heartfelt intensity," and treating the company itself as the primary product. Their unconventional approach includes no traditional PM org, a "membership team" instead of customer support, radical transparency through public videos, and a vision to build an "internet computer" rather than just a browser.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Optimize for Feelings, Not Metrics**: Rather than asking "how many times per week do users share," ask "do people feel closer to their friends and family?" Use metrics to stay honest, but lead with the human emotion you want to create. Pick the right feeling and it typically tracks with the metric you care about anyway.

- **D5/D7 Metric (L5/L7)**: Track how many people use your product at least 5 days out of 7. Captures retention, engagement, and growth in a single metric that can't be gamed. Focus on week-over-week growth rate rather than absolute numbers.

- **The Company as Product**: View your team and culture as the primary product, not the software. Start by asking "who do we want to work with and how do we want to work?" then find a problem where that team can excel. The Browser Company exists first to build a dream team, second to build Arc.

- **Assume You Don't Know**: Approach every project with beginner's mind, even if you're an expert. Followed by "so we've got to get going"—bias heavily toward action and shipping prototypes to learn. Shed preconceived notions by giving things new names (e.g., "membership team" vs "customer support").

- **Start by Asking "What Could Be?"**: Push for maximum aspiration and ambition. Don't just solve the problem—imagine the most transformative version. This deeply motivates the team working on it.

- **Heartfelt Intensity**: Hire people who show up with fire in their belly and something to prove. Everyone has different craft obsessions, but they all share intrinsic motivation to do career-defining work. This is the #1 hiring criterion.

- **Hire Mutts**: Seek multidisciplinary people who view themselves as makers first, willing to do whatever verb or discipline is needed. Avoid hiring for rigid roles; hire people who pick up tools as needed.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The modern Silicon Valley obsession with graphs and metrics leaves a lot on the table.** When creating products, think about the human on the other end and how you want them to feel—joy, speed, organization, focus. Nike, Disney, and Apple's founders were optimizing for feelings, not OBPS (original broadcasts per session). Numbers are for staying honest, not for driving creation.

2. **Different product categories require different winning strategies.** Browsers are universal, commoditized, and lucrative—so you must win on emotional connection and brand love. That's not romantic idealism; it's practical capitalism. Choose categories where your strengths (craft, experience, storytelling) are how you win.

3. **Public building and radical transparency build trust, which is essential for products handling sensitive data.** Show your meetings, your struggles, your real team—not to become internet celebrities, but because people have lost trust in tech companies. If users know you as humans, they're more likely to trust your intentions.

4. **Celebrate your team publicly, not the CEO.** From the first hire onward, announce people joining and credit individuals who ship features. This creates a reinforcing cycle where talented people see they'll be recognized and celebrated, not subsumed into CEO hero worship.

5. **Values should be discovered, not prescribed.** Interview your entire team to hear what they actually say they love about working there, then codify those patterns. The Browser Company turned theirs into an essay called "Notes on Road Trips" to avoid corporate propaganda feel.

6. **Giving things new names forces first-principles thinking.** Calling browser history something else, or having a "membership team" instead of "customer support," strips away preconceptions and makes people question what they're actually trying to accomplish.

7. **Ship fast by combining heartfelt intensity + beginner's mind + ambitious prompts, then get out of the way.** If you have a team with fire in their belly who assumes they don't know and wants to do career-defining work, empower them fully. The Browser Company has 10%+ week-over-week growth for 8 months with low-to-mid-30s to low-40s D5/D7 retention.

8. **The future of computing is the "internet computer."** Everything is moving to the cloud—your apps, files, photos are already URLs, not local. The next generation will need interfaces to this web-based computing platform that are as powerful as native experiences. That's the real opportunity beyond just "a browser."

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Lead with feelings in your answers**: When discussing a feature you shipped, explain the emotion you wanted users to feel, not just the metric you moved. Show you think about humans first, then validate with data.

- **Demonstrate heartfelt intensity**: Share what you're fired up about in product—whether it's craft details, performance optimization, or user delight. Show you have something to prove and care deeply about your work.

- **Explain your beginner's mind**: Describe times you approached a problem assuming you didn't know the answer, ran experiments, and learned by doing. Show bias to action over planning.

- **Talk about people, not just product**: When discussing past work, credit specific team members by name and what they contributed. Avoid hero narratives centered on yourself.

- **Be authentic in "why this company?" answers**: The Browser Company looks for genuine connection to mission, not polished pitches. Be real about what draws you, even if it's unconventional.

- **Ask revealing questions**: Your questions reveal what you care about—use interview time to understand culture, values, and ways of working deeply, not just to impress.

- **Know when craft matters for the category**: Understand whether the product space requires winning on experience (commoditized consumer) versus other factors (enterprise security), and tailor your approach accordingly.
