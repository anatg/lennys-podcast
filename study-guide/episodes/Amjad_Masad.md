## TL;DR
Amjad's central bet: software creation is a superpower that's been locked behind programming expertise, and AI is the unlock. His frameworks explain why the PM role shifts from building to ideating, why "being generative" becomes the new core skill, and how Javelin's Paradox means cheaper software leads to *more* total software, not less.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **AI Computer Interfaces (ACI) vs. HCI**: LLMs need fundamentally different interfaces than humans do. Human-computer interfaces (GUIs, CLIs) are designed for human perception and attention. LLMs work through text/tokens, have different context windows and latency constraints, and compose differently. Building for LLMs means rethinking from the interface up — not retrofitting human-designed tools. ACI is its own design discipline.

- **Society of Models**: The future of AI development is not one monolithic model but a network of specialized models coordinating on tasks. Different models handle different steps in a workflow — like a company with specialized teams. Product builders should design for model orchestration, not model replacement.

- **Amjad's Law**: The ROI of learning to code doubles approximately every six months as AI coding tools improve. Even as AI handles more implementation, the person who understands code is always ahead — they can direct, debug, evaluate, and compose. Code literacy compounds faster than ever.

- **Being Generative as the Core Skill**: When building is fast and cheap, the bottleneck is no longer execution — it's having ideas worth executing. The highest-leverage skill becomes *generating* new product ideas, identifying underserved problems, and articulating what should be built and why. Traditional PM skills (requirements, scoping, prioritization) become table stakes; generative thinking becomes the differentiator.

- **Javelin's Paradox Applied to Software**: In javelin competitions, using a better throwing technique generates more total distance, increasing demand — the sport doesn't shrink because of improvement. Similarly, as AI makes software cheaper to build, total software demand grows: more ideas become worth building, more small markets become addressable, more workflows get automated. Cheaper building doesn't mean less building.

- **Agile Roadmaps for the AI Era**: Traditional 12-month or even quarterly roadmaps are incompatible with AI capability release cycles. When a major model capability drops (image understanding, reasoning improvement, voice), it can invalidate entire product assumptions overnight. The right posture: maintain a 4-6 week planning horizon and "slaughter the roadmap" when the capability landscape shifts. Fast iteration and willingness to throw out existing plans is a competitive advantage, not a sign of disorganization.

- **Compress Silos via Code as Common Language**: In a world where AI lets designers and PMs write working prototypes, the handoff between designer → PM → engineer collapses. Code becomes the common language that eliminates translation loss. Teams that adopt this produce fewer misunderstandings, faster cycles, and products closer to the original intent.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The zero-employee company is coming.** Within 5 years, someone will run a billion-dollar company with zero employees, using AI agents for every function. This isn't hyperbole — it's an extrapolation of what's already happening with 2-3 person teams shipping top-10 App Store apps.

2. **Cheaper software means more software, not less.** The Javelin's Paradox framing dismantles the "AI will eliminate software jobs" argument. Total demand for software will increase as marginal cost decreases. What changes: *who* builds it, and *what* it's worth building.

3. **The new scarce resource is the idea, not the implementation.** When anyone can build in a weekend what took a team months, the leverage shifts to whoever identifies problems worth solving. Customer insight, vertical expertise, and distribution become the moat.

4. **Don't just teach coding — teach building.** Replit's insight: the goal isn't to make people "write code" — it's to make them capable of building things that work in the world. The right analogy is literacy: reading and writing unlock a world of ideas, but the value is in the ideas, not the letters.

5. **AI requires a new interface paradigm.** Putting AI into existing HCI patterns (forms, menus, notifications) is like putting a car engine in a horse. The interface layer for AI agents needs to be rethought from first principles — ACI.

6. **Constraints created the best work.** Amjad coded on a slow computer with limited internet in Jordan — the constraints forced creative problem-solving and deeper understanding. Frictionless tools can obscure the fundamentals. Intentional constraint sometimes develops a stronger foundation.

7. **Teach yourself out loud.** Amjad taught himself to code by building things and explaining them to others. The act of explaining forces you to find the gaps. Learning compounds when you're building in public.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- The demo Amjad ran live: built a full feature-request tracking web app with admin dashboard in 10 minutes for ~15 cents compute cost. Same project would take a human engineer days and cost thousands in labor.
- PMs who can prototype in Replit (or equivalent) skip the spec-to-design-to-engineer translation chain entirely — the idea goes straight to a testable artifact.
- What matters *more* for PMs in the AI era: generating ideas, customer discovery, articulating problems with precision, evaluating whether something is worth building.
- What matters *less*: traditional tooling knowledge, waterfall-style requirements documentation, lengthy estimation processes.
- Agile-roadmap mindset is mandatory: the team that can throw out the roadmap and rebuild around a new capability in 4 weeks will consistently outrun the team that defends its quarterly plan.
- ACI design is an emerging PM/design discipline — understanding how to structure input/output for LLMs, manage context windows, and design model orchestration workflows.
- Prediction with timeline: a billion-dollar zero-employee company within 5 years (from time of recording).
