## TL;DR
Anneka's core insight: "strategic" isn't a personality trait — it's a specific combination of having a compelling simple *why* AND being the person who champions big, hard-to-execute ideas. Most people have one without the other. Her decision-making frameworks (historian approach, 70% threshold, feedback loops) are among the most practical in the catalog.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **What "Strategic" Actually Means**: Two necessary, insufficient-on-their-own components: (1) A compelling, simple *why* — the ability to explain the story behind decisions so others believe in them; (2) Championing big, hard ideas — identifying what's difficult and important and fighting for it even when there's resistance. Most people who fail to be perceived as "strategic" are missing one. The articulate communicator who only defends incremental ideas isn't strategic. The big-idea person who can't explain why isn't strategic either.

- **Summarization as Strategic Tactic**: The single most underrated communication skill in leadership. In any large meeting, be the person who synthesizes: "Here's what I heard us agreeing on..." and "Here's where we still have tension..." Two reasons this works: (1) It demonstrates you were listening and processing, not just waiting to talk; (2) It shifts the room from debate to convergence without you having to "win." People often treat the summarizer as neutral, authoritative, and collaborative.

- **Historian Approach to Decisions**: When joining a company or team, your first job is not to have opinions — it's to understand the past decisions and why they were made. Read old board decks, talk to the people who made the foundational calls, study what was tried before you arrived. This prevents repeating mistakes, builds credibility with the people who made those calls, and reveals the real organizational constraints (which often aren't in any document).

- **70% Threshold: Making the Decision IS the Decision**: "It's not about making the right decision — it's about making the decision." The value of a crisp decision made at 70% confidence vastly exceeds the value of a perfect decision made at 95% confidence, because execution velocity compounds faster than decision accuracy. The goal: reach alignment fast, iterate once feedback arrives, adjust course. Paralysis in search of certainty is itself a decision (to slow down).

- **Founder Mode in Both Directions**: Anneka explicitly navigates founder mode both as a leader (going deep on key problems, not just delegating) and as a report (treating your manager's goals as your own, not just executing directives). When you run your function the way a founder runs their company — owning outcomes, not just tasks — you scale faster than "the employee who does what they're told."

- **Feedback Framework — Feel It → Curiosity → Direct**: Three-step model for giving and receiving difficult feedback:
  1. **Feel it** — acknowledge the emotional reality of the feedback before rationalizing. Trying to go straight to analysis skips the human part.
  2. **Curiosity** — approach with "tell me more" framing rather than defensiveness. Ask about the specific instance, not the general pattern.
  3. **Direct framing as perception** — give feedback as "my perception" not "the truth." "I perceive X when you do Y" is much harder to argue with than "you always do Y."

- **Breaking into PM from Adjacent Functions**: The most reliable path is not MBA → PM. It's: (1) Join a company in a non-PM role in a domain you care about; (2) Build credibility and demonstrate product thinking in that role; (3) Raise your hand explicitly for PM adjacent work (user research, competitive analysis, spec writing); (4) Make the lateral transfer once you've proven the skill. The domain expertise you bring from the adjacent role is an unfair advantage most PM candidates don't have.

- **Energy Management > Time Management**: Don't try to schedule important work into your worst-energy time slot (Anneka's: 5–6pm). Know your energy rhythm and protect the high-energy windows for the hardest, most creative work. Admin, emails, and meetings can absorb the low-energy slots. This sounds obvious; almost no one actually structures their calendar this way.

- **Journaling for Mindset Management (CBT on Yourself)**: Anneka's personal practice: use journaling to identify cognitive distortions in real time. When anxious or stuck, write out: what am I assuming? What's the evidence against that? What would I tell a friend in this situation? This is cognitive behavioral therapy self-administered — reframing thoughts before they calcify into beliefs.

- **Managing Difficult Personalities**: The first question isn't "how do I get them to behave?" — it's "what do they actually care about?" Every difficult personality is difficult for a reason, usually tied to a fear or unmet need (credit, control, certainty). Diagnosing the motivation shifts the interaction from combat to choreography. The second tactic: gratitude reframe — if you can genuinely appreciate one thing this person contributes, the dynamic almost always improves.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The "strategic" perception problem is fixable.** Most people who are told "you're not strategic enough" are actually articulate with vision but not championing hard changes — or vice versa. The diagnosis tells you exactly what to fix, which makes it actionable in a way that "be more strategic" never is.

2. **Summarization is a leadership hack most people ignore.** It's the lowest-energy, highest-leverage way to demonstrate influence in large-group settings without dominating the conversation.

3. **Speed of decision compounds faster than accuracy of decision.** Operating at 70% and iterating beats waiting for 95% certainty in almost every business context. Shipping the imperfect roadmap beats debating the perfect one.

4. **Longevity at one company can be a career accelerator, not a crutch.** Anneka's 11 years at LiveRamp is a masterclass in growing faster than the company while staying put. The people who job-hop every 18 months often reset their credibility each time. The person who becomes the historian and institutional carrier of a company's decisions grows compound-style.

5. **Breaking into PM is about proving PM skills in a non-PM role.** The barrier isn't credentials — it's demonstrated product thinking. The fastest path: do the work before you have the title.

6. **The hardest part of feedback is the receiving, not the giving.** Most feedback frameworks focus on the giver. The receiver needs a framework too (Feel it → Curiosity → "my perception") or the feedback triggers defensiveness that blocks the content.

7. **Energy management is the neglected axis of productivity.** Calendar optimization for meeting-avoidance is well-understood. Scheduling your hardest work during peak energy windows is not.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"What does strategic mean to you?"**: Two components — compelling why + championing big hard ideas. Show you have both by giving an example where you articulated a clear narrative AND fought through organizational resistance to make something happen.
- **"How do you make decisions under uncertainty?"**: 70% threshold framework. The goal is to reach 70% confidence fast, make the call, then iterate. Paralysis is also a decision.
- **"How do you influence without authority?"**: Summarization tactic. Historian approach (know more about the context than anyone else). Founder mode framing (own the outcome, not just the task).
- **"How do you break into PM / change roles?"**: Adjacent function → build credibility → raise hand for PM work → lateral transfer. The domain expertise is the unfair advantage.
- **"How do you handle giving/receiving difficult feedback?"**: Feel it → Curiosity → "my perception" framing. Make the feedback observable and non-absolute.
