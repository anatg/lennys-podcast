## TL;DR
Brendan Foody built the fastest-growing company in history by recognizing that AI labs' primary bottleneck is creating evals—the rubrics and success criteria that measure and improve model capabilities. Mercor's marketplace connects expert professionals (lawyers, doctors, engineers, bankers) with AI labs to write these evals, growing from $1M to $400M+ revenue in 16 months by focusing maniacally on customer obsession, maintaining extremely high hiring standards, and moving with intensity in a rapidly changing market.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **"If the model is the product, the eval is the PRD"**: Evals are both the product requirements document for researchers building AI capabilities AND the sales collateral proving those capabilities. Labs need systematic ways to measure success across every domain where humans can do something models can't. This framing helps understand why the eval market is potentially the biggest business opportunity in AI.

- **Leading indicators in fast-moving markets**: Don't optimize for legacy market share—focus on leading indicators in new pockets of demand where the wealthiest customers will pay whatever it takes. Look for where the market is headed, not where it's been. Mercor succeeded by identifying the shift from crowdsourced low-skill labor to high-expertise professional labor for AI training.

- **The talent density cascade**: Your first 10 hires shape everything that follows. Be extraordinarily patient and disciplined early (Mercor's second US employee was head of growth at Scale). Once you find clear product-market fit and a market vacuum, shift to optimizing for speed. Half of Mercor's first 10 were former founders or had scaled major products.

- **Elastic demand vs. inelastic demand**: Focus on industries/jobs with elastic demand—where 10x productivity increases create 10-100x more work, not job displacement. Software development, product management, and consulting have nearly unlimited demand. Accounting and similar fields have bounded demand. This framework predicts which roles will thrive with AI.

- **Top 10% power law in expertise**: In any cohort of 100 experts hired, the top 10% drive the majority of model improvement (like companies where top 10% of employees drive majority of impact). Building proprietary advantages in identifying and sourcing that top 10% creates defensible value that's hard to compete against.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **We are entering the era of evals**: The barrier to applying AI agents across the economy isn't compute or model size—it's measuring success. Every capability labs want models to have requires experts to define what "good" looks like. This creates a massive new job category that will last as long as there are things humans can do that AI can't.

2. **You can just do things—initiative is the primary bottleneck to innovation**: Most people have ideas but never act. From selling Safeway donuts in 8th grade (moving his stand 50 feet off campus when shut down) to bootstrapping Mercor to $1M before dropping out, Foody demonstrates that execution beats ideation. With AI making building easier than ever, the constraint is simply starting.

3. **Customer obsession beats sales and marketing in early stages**: Mercor had zero sales/marketing team for 1.5 years while scaling to 9-figures. They focused 100% of resources on building great products and experiences, relying entirely on word-of-mouth. Founders often get caught up in marketing before creating the 6-star experiences that drive organic growth and customer love.

4. **Look for surprisingly easy-to-sell customers, not hard ones**: Don't force product-market fit. Be stubborn about your thesis of how the world will change, but open-minded about the exact form it takes. Real PMF shows up as customers who are surprisingly easy to sell into—that's the signal of a large pain point you can scale with. If every marginal customer is extremely difficult to sell, you won't build a huge business.

5. **The future of work is building RL environments for AI**: The entire economy may become "an oral environment machine, building out all of these worlds and contexts" with rubrics and verifiers. This represents a fundamental shift from job displacement narrative to job creation—a new category of work helping define what we want AI to achieve across every domain.

6. **Set ridiculously ambitious goals and trajectory forms around them**: When Mercor was at $1.5M run rate, Foody told Benchmark they'd hit $50M by year-end. They thought he was insane, but he hit it within two weeks of target. Setting 10-50x goals (not 2x) forces the organization to think differently and often becomes self-fulfilling through team ambition and focus.

7. **High standards + can-do attitude + intensity = exceptional outcomes**: Mercor's three core values aren't about specific processes but mindset: believing ambitious goals are achievable, refusing to compromise on talent quality, and recognizing that building legendary businesses requires extraordinary effort. This isn't about mandated hours but finding people with deep ownership who naturally push boundaries.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **PMs are in an "elastic demand" category**: When AI makes PMs 10x more productive, we'll build 10-100x more software, not eliminate PM roles. PMs who can leverage AI to do vastly more will be extremely well-positioned in the future economy.

- **The new assessment paradigm—use whatever tools available**: Traditional assessment fights against AI use. Modern assessment says "use ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor—whatever tools are available—and show me what product you can build in an hour." This mirrors calculator adoption: don't test arithmetic, test what people can do WITH the tool.

- **From pre-training to post-training focus**: The shift in AI development is from feeding raw data (pre-training gets knowledge IN) to post-training and reinforcement learning (teaches models what to prioritize and how to reason). PMs need to understand this: models know a lot but need expert-defined success criteria to learn which knowledge to apply when.

- **Models are only as good as their evals**: Greg Brockman tweeted "evals are all you need" and it's proving true—labs can rapidly saturate benchmarks once they have good evals (Olympiad Math, SWE-bench). For PMs, this means the ability to define clear success criteria and measurement systems becomes paramount for leveraging AI in products.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate initiative over credentials**: Foody's donut business in 8th grade matters more than his college dropout status. In interviews, show concrete examples of seeing opportunities and acting on them, even small-scale—bias toward action impresses more than perfect planning.

- **Focus on leading indicators when discussing market analysis**: Don't just describe current state—identify what's changing and where demand is heading. Show you can spot inflection points (like Mercor seeing the shift from crowdsourced to expert labor) before they're obvious.

- **Frame problems around customer obsession**: When discussing product decisions, emphasize how you deeply understood customer needs first, built great experiences, and let word-of-mouth drive growth. Show you prioritize customer value over go-to-market tactics in early stages.

- **Articulate your learning from failure or pivots**: Mercor started as a general hiring platform and pivoted to AI training data. Show how you stayed open-minded about form while stubborn about vision—be able to describe what signals told you to pivot.

- **Use concrete examples of high standards**: Don't just say "I have high standards"—describe specific hiring decisions where you waited months for the right person, or quality bars you refused to compromise on. Show the patience/speed trade-off understanding.

- **Show how you think about AI's impact on your domain**: For PM roles especially, demonstrate clear thinking about elastic vs. inelastic demand, how AI changes workflows, and how you'd assess people's ability to leverage AI tools rather than fight them.
