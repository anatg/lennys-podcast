## TL;DR
Shweta Shrivastava shares lessons from leading PM teams at Waymo, where they're building fully autonomous vehicles (Level 4) already operating commercially in Phoenix and San Francisco. She explains how Waymo designs human-like driving behavior using deep learning while prioritizing safety, tracks both commercial metrics and system behavior performance, and how the PM role differs when building hardware-software integrated products with extremely high safety bars that change the traditional "MVP" mindset.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Working Backwards from the Customer Problem (Amazon's PR/FAQ)**: Before building any product, write a press release as if the product has already launched, describing what you're telling users about it. This forces clarity on value proposition and customer problem being solved. Shweta notes this is a core tenant across all product contexts—whether startup or large company, software or hardware.

- **Rule of Seven (Email Escalation)**: If an email thread reaches seven exchanges (or ten in larger organizations) without resolution, immediately escalate to a live conversation—call or huddle in person. Prevents wasteful async cycles and forces direct problem-solving.

- **Disrupt Yourself Before Someone Else Does**: Large companies with market leadership can become incremental in product strategy. Product leaders must constantly challenge complacency and build transformational capabilities that might cannibalize existing products, rather than wait for upstarts to do it. Example: Shweta led Amazon Honey Code, AWS's first no-code platform, venturing into entirely new territory.

- **MVP with Safety-First Bar**: In autonomous vehicles, "minimum viable product" has a fundamentally different meaning than SaaS products. The MVP bar for safety is extremely high from day one—you can't cut corners on safety to ship faster. The iteration philosophy still applies, but the baseline threshold is non-negotiable.

- **Dual Metrics Framework (Commercial + System Behavior)**: Track both business/operational metrics (trips per week, daily/weekly active users, funnel metrics, operational cost) and technical performance metrics (safety vs. human driving benchmarks, compliance to road rules, adequate progress without undue stops/strands, impact on traffic flow). Both categories must show progress.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Design for trust through subtle cues, not explicit features**: Trust in autonomous systems comes from naturalness and transparency. Waymo uses deep learning models trained on human driving data (filtered for good behaviors) to create familiar, human-like driving patterns—like slowing down on San Francisco hills even when not technically required, because that's what riders expect. Small design choices (visibility into what the car sees, rider support calling if seatbelt not worn, subtle body language like inching forward to merge) compound into trust.

2. **Focus on momentum and showing success to maintain stakeholder buy-in**: Whether you're working on a multi-billion dollar autonomous vehicle program or a smaller initiative, the way to maintain investment is to show meaningful progress—both technological and commercial. Don't do unnatural things for short-term "brownie points" with investors; focus on creating customer value and building the right business. Results speak for themselves.

3. **Know what you're NOT building**: A product that tries to be all things to all people goes nowhere. Prioritization and focus are critical whether you're at a startup or large company. It's easy to get swayed by customer X wanting this and customer Y wanting that, but clarity on scope and strategic "no's" is essential for impact.

4. **The way to get promoted is to not want it too badly**: Focus on impact and doing what's right for the business rather than optimizing for your own promotion. Make your ambitions known to your manager so you're considered for challenging projects, but don't maneuver too much—it becomes visible and is a negative signal. Improve your skillset, work on high-visibility projects that stretch your abilities, and dedicate yourself to business impact.

5. **Listening and empathy are easier said than done**: These are core PM skills for influencing without authority, but require genuine practice across many contexts. The key is proactively challenging your own assumptions. If you're a seasoned PM or leader and there's no conflict or contention, something is missing—you might not be listening well or picking up on cues. Adopt a beginner's mindset and take time to truly understand customer, market, and team constraints.

6. **Level 4 autonomy solves the problem without needing Level 5**: Full autonomy without a human driver (L4) achieves the transformational vision for most use cases. Level 5 (fully unstructured off-road environments, no maps, no priors) may remain niche. Waymo's commercial L4 service in Phoenix and San Francisco already realizes the dream of autonomous driving without requiring the ultimate theoretical capability.

7. **Deep technical depth required for hardware+software PMs**: PMs in complex hardware-software systems like autonomous vehicles must go deeper technically than traditional software PMs. They need to be comfortable with uncertainty and ambiguity, have tenacity to play the long game, and be driven by mission (making roads safer, addressing 1.35 million annual traffic deaths globally). Self-selection for mission-driven work is critical.

8. **Human driving data trains AI, but social norms matter too**: Waymo uses deep learning models trained on human driving data to mimic natural behavior patterns, but also must learn social norms that vary by city, intersection, and context (like pedestrians crossing in certain San Francisco neighborhoods even without walk signals). Driving is more sophisticated, interactive, and social than people realize.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Deep learning models for human-like behavior**: Waymo uses AI to understand other road users' intent (pedestrian body orientation, where they're looking, hand gestures, signs). Models are trained on human driving data, filtering out bad behaviors while preserving natural-feeling driving patterns.

- **Simulation for validation at scale**: AI-powered simulation tools validate autonomous vehicle performance before real-world deployment, enabling safer iteration and testing of edge cases without physical risk.

- **Social norm learning**: AI systems must learn context-dependent social norms (when pedestrians cross, how aggressive to be in merging) that vary by city and aren't captured in explicit rules. This requires adaptive learning beyond simple rule-following.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate listening and empathy in behavioral questions**: When discussing cross-functional collaboration or conflict resolution, show how you actively challenged your own assumptions, sought to understand others' constraints, and adjusted your approach. Be specific about what you learned from different perspectives.

- **Prepare a genuine failure story**: Senior-level interviewers (like Shweta) explicitly look for candidates who can share authentic failures with learnings. Avoid disguising successes as failures. Show vulnerability and growth mindset.

- **Articulate customer-backward thinking**: Be ready to explain how you've worked backwards from customer problems in past projects. Can you write a "press release" version of your product vision? Can you clearly state what you chose NOT to build and why?

- **Show how you've maintained stakeholder buy-in**: Prepare examples of long-term projects where you demonstrated progress and maintained investment through showing both technical and business results. Emphasize momentum and tangible outcomes.

- **For hardware/robotics/AI roles, demonstrate technical depth**: Be prepared to go deeper into technical details than typical software PM roles. Show comfort with uncertainty, ambiguity, and long time horizons. Express mission-driven motivation.

- **Discuss metrics holistically**: When discussing KPIs, show you think about both business/operational metrics AND user/system performance metrics. How do you balance competing objectives (e.g., safety vs. progress)?
