## TL;DR
Jeanne Grosser shares how AI is revolutionizing go-to-market through GTM engineers who build agents that automate prospecting and sales workflows—enabling one SDR to do the work of ten. She emphasizes treating go-to-market as a product with thoughtful customer journey design, rigorous segmentation frameworks, and sales teams so product-savvy that "it should take engineers 10 minutes to figure out you aren't a product manager."

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Go-to-Market as a Product**: Map the entire customer journey from awareness to five-year retention as distinct experiences. Design each touchpoint to feel collaborative and valuable rather than transactional. Apply product thinking: iterate on workflows, treat sales process bugs like engineering bugs, run weekly sprints to fix objection handling gaps.

- **Segmentation Framework (2-3 Axis Model)**: Start with company size (SMB/mid-market/enterprise) on X-axis, then add 2-3 differentiating attributes based on your product. Examples: growth potential (consumption models), traffic volume (performance products), business model (B2B vs B2C vs marketplace), workload type. Use regression analysis on existing customers to identify attributes correlated with revenue and win rates.

- **The 80/20 Buying Principle**: Remember that 80% of customers buy to avoid pain or reduce risk, only 20% to increase upside. Focus sales messaging on competitive disadvantage, career risk, and missed revenue targets rather than visionary "art of the possible" (which resonates mainly with other founders).

- **Project Rosland (Company Universe Approach)**: Build a comprehensive database where every row is a company and every column is an attribute relevant to your sale. Use AI to auto-generate personalized outreach based on industry, business model, tech stack, etc. This turns prospecting into a "Mad Libs" where 80% is dynamically filled based on company attributes.

- **Human-in-the-Loop Agent Development**: Shadow your highest-performing team members, document their workflow (what tabs they open, what research they do), encode that into an agent, then keep humans reviewing outputs initially. Track the same KPIs you'd hold humans accountable to (conversion rates, touches to convert) before removing human review.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The rise of the GTM Engineer is transforming sales efficiency**: One go-to-market engineer working 25-30% time for six weeks reduced Vercel's inbound SDR team from 10 people to 1, maintaining the same lead-to-opportunity conversion rate while reducing touches needed. The agent costs ~$1,000/year to run versus over $1 million in salary costs. This role requires GTM experience (often former sales engineers) plus coding ability.

2. **Sales should spend 70% of time with humans, not 30%**: For 20 years, sellers have spent only 30-40% of their time actually talking to customers. AI agents can now handle research, follow-up, and rote tasks, flipping that ratio so salespeople focus on high-value human interaction and consultative work.

3. **The best sales orgs are "equal parts revenue driving and R&D"**: A 20-person sales team talks to vastly more customers per week than any UXR or PM team. If they're trained to discern signal from noise and understand when objections reveal product opportunities versus need coaching, they become an extension of product management.

4. **Consumption-based pricing demands consultative selling**: When the initial land is just the beginning and represents a small fraction of eventual revenue, go-to-market must shift from transactional to deeply understanding customer goals and helping them think through solutions—almost like consultants or forward-deployed engineers.

5. **Discovery is a skill, not just "talking to customers"**: Excellent salespeople talk well under half the time in conversations. When customers ask questions, respond with a question about the question before answering. Use the "five whys" to go deep rather than immediately jumping to problem-solving mode.

6. **Build versus buy calculus has changed for GTM tools**: Because agents can be built quickly (the lost-opportunity review agent took two days) and cheaply (~$1K/year to run), companies should experiment with building internal agents for esoteric workflows rather than defaulting to buying 20 specialized tools. The value is in encoding your unique context and content.

7. **Segmentation is a company-wide discipline, not just GTM**: Deliver segmentation frameworks to all new hires (including product and engineering) so when building features, they ask "Is this targeted at enterprise or startup?" This alignment prevents product and GTM from pursuing overlapping but imperfect Venn diagram strategies.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI enables 10x GTM efficiency gains**: Real example of 10:1 reduction in SDR headcount with AI agents maintaining quality, allowing humans to move up the value chain to more sophisticated work like outbound prospecting.

- **The "deal-bot" as product insight engine**: Vercel runs AI agents against all Gong transcripts to diagnose why deals were really lost (often different from AE reports—"lost on price" might actually be "never spoke to economic buyer"). Also provides real-time coaching in Slack channels during active deals.

- **Rapid iteration becomes possible**: With iteration velocity at all-time highs (launching features "every other day"), AI agents can analyze calls/interactions to identify where enablement failed, turning sales process bugs into weekly sprints—mirroring engineering workflows.

- **Forward-deployed engineering replaces traditional professional services**: Getting embedded in customer environments with AI to help them build, learning what's generalizable to build into product vs. what remains services.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate GTM-as-product thinking**: When discussing go-to-market strategy, map out the customer journey with specific touchpoints and explain how you'd measure and iterate on each "experience" like a product feature.

- **Show segmentation rigor**: Be prepared to articulate a 2-3 dimensional segmentation framework with clear attributes that drive differentiated buying behavior, not just size-based segments.

- **Understand the pain-avoidance principle**: Frame product value in terms of risk reduction, competitive disadvantage avoidance, and protecting revenue/careers—not just upside opportunity.

- **Blend product and sales thinking**: If interviewing for product roles at sales-led companies, show you understand discovery as a discipline, can translate customer objections into product insights, and view sales as R&D partners.

- **Speak to AI's practical application**: Have specific examples of where AI could automate workflows in your domain (not vague aspirations). Demonstrate understanding of when human-in-the-loop is needed versus full automation.

- **Treat pricing as strategic product work**: Be ready to discuss how pricing structure aligns value capture with value delivery, when to unbundle features, and how pricing affects PLG-to-sales-led transitions.
