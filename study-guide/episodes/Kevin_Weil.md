## TL;DR
Kevin Weil shares how OpenAI operates at the cutting edge of AI development, emphasizing iterative deployment, model maximalism, and bottoms-up empowerment. He reveals that the key to building AI products is understanding that technology changes every two months, requiring product teams to be incredibly agile, embrace ambiguity, and think about AI models the way you'd think about teams of humans working together.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Model Maximalism**: Don't build extensive scaffolding around current model limitations—in two months there will be a better model that eliminates those constraints. If your product is right at the edge of model capabilities, keep building because the models will catch up and make it sing.

- **Iterative Deployment Philosophy**: Ship early and often, iterate in public. Co-evolve with society as everyone learns about AI capabilities together. Don't wait for perfection—launch when it's directionally right and improve continuously.

- **The Human Analogy for AI Design**: When designing AI experiences or debugging why something works, reason about it like you would a human. If you needed 20 seconds to think, you wouldn't go silent—you'd say "that's a good question, let me think." Apply this to UI/UX decisions for AI products.

- **Ensemble of Models Approach**: Break problems into specific tasks and use specialized models (different sizes, fine-tuned versions, various prompts) for each, similar to how a company is an ensemble of humans with different expertise working together.

- **"AI is whatever hasn't been done yet"**: Once something works, it becomes machine learning. Once it's ubiquitous, it's just an algorithm. The frontier constantly moves forward.

- **Good Work Consistently Over Time**: Excellence isn't about silver bullets—it's about showing up daily, doing good work, getting incrementally better. Gains compound over months and years even when weekly progress isn't obvious.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The worst AI you'll ever use is today's**: Every model you interact with is the worst you'll ever use for the rest of your life. This exponential improvement curve (10x gains yearly, not just Moore's Law) fundamentally reshapes what products are possible and how to think about building for the future.

2. **Evals are the new core PM skill**: Writing evaluations (tests/quizzes for AI models) is becoming essential because you need to know if your model is 60% accurate (build different product), 95% accurate (build different product), or 99.5% accurate (build yet another product). Evals enable continuous fine-tuning and hill-climbing toward better outcomes.

3. **Fine-tuning will be everywhere**: Most teams don't yet fine-tune models for specific use cases, but the future means quasi-researcher/ML engineers on every product team, customizing models with company-specific data and processes—just like new employees need onboarding to learn internal knowledge.

4. **Chat is actually the perfect interface**: Despite criticism, chat is incredibly versatile because it's how humans communicate across all intelligence levels and topics. It's the lowest-common-denominator, zero-restrictions way of interacting with AI that can handle anything from simple queries to complex reasoning.

5. **High agency + comfort with ambiguity wins**: In fast-moving AI environments, you can't wait for clear direction. PMs need to see problems and solve them independently, be decisive when needed, and lead through influence across research, engineering, and design functions.

6. **There are more smart people outside your walls**: No matter how ambitious or capable your company, 3+ million developers using your API will build more use cases, solve more industry-specific problems, and create more value than you ever could internally. Build great platforms and empower others.

7. **Plans are useless, planning is helpful**: Quarterly roadmaps will be wrong in AI because technology changes underneath you constantly. The value is in the planning process—checking dependencies, aligning direction, learning what worked—not in adherence to the plan.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Researchers join every product team**: Future product teams will have ML/research functions built in because fine-tuning models for specific use cases becomes standard workflow, not just for foundation model companies.

- **Vibe coding changes prototyping**: PMs should be generating working prototypes through AI-assisted coding rather than static Figma mocks. OpenAI's CPO admits they should be doing this more—it's totally possible today.

- **ChatGPT as the ultimate reskilling tool**: While AI may displace some roles, it's also the best possible tool for learning new skills and reskilling, making transitions more graceful.

- **Less prompt engineering required over time**: The goal is that users won't need to be "prompt engineers"—models will just understand intent. But today, providing examples in prompts (poor man's fine-tuning) and framing context ("you are the world's best X") still significantly improves outputs.

- **Product teams become more bottoms-up**: With technology changing every two months, centralized roadmapping becomes less viable. Empowered teams closest to the problems need to make decisions and move fast.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate high agency in your stories**: Show examples where you saw a problem and solved it without waiting for permission. AI companies especially value self-directed problem-solvers who don't need detailed roadmaps.

- **Get comfortable with "I don't know yet"**: In fast-moving environments, admitting uncertainty while showing how you'd learn and adapt is more valuable than false confidence. Kevin's team doesn't know what they'll ship in 3-6 months—that's normal.

- **Practice talking about learning from failure**: Kevin openly discusses Libra as his biggest career disappointment and what he'd do differently. This vulnerability plus learning mindset is what great interviewers look for.

- **Show how you'd break down ambiguous problems**: When discussing product challenges, demonstrate how you'd decompose them into testable components with clear metrics (similar to building evals for AI).

- **Emphasize cross-functional influence skills**: With researchers, engineers, designers, and no direct reports, talk about how you've led through influence, built rapport across functions, and brought teams along on decisions.

- **Ask about the role of new technology in their products**: Kevin's insight that models change every 2 months could apply to any fast-moving tech. Show you understand how to build when the foundation is shifting.
