## TL;DR
Arielle's three-part brand framework (Purpose + Positioning + Personality) is the most complete and actionable brand strategy curriculum in the catalog for early-stage startups. Her naming process, the "bar test" for human-language positioning, and the Aker 5-dimension personality framework are immediately applicable. The key insight: brand is not logo/colors — it's how people think about you, and you can deliberately shape that.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Brand = Purpose + Positioning + Personality**: The three components of a complete brand strategy (not logo, font, and colors — those are outputs, not brand):
  1. **Purpose**: Why you do what you do. "We exist to [X]." One sentence. 10-year timeframe. Makes people want to root for you.
  2. **Positioning**: The space you occupy in your target customer's mind. What do you want them to think when they think of your product? How do they describe it to others?
  3. **Personality**: If your brand was a person, who would it be? How does it show up in the world?
  All three together feed into visual design and tone of voice. Without them, a style guide is just "here are our colors."

- **Purpose Development Process**:
  1. List all the cultural tensions relevant to your business (zeitgeist trends, subconscious audience concerns, things they may not even articulate)
  2. List all the ways you'd describe your brand's best self (what does it deliver when everything works perfectly?)
  3. Pick the best one from each column
  4. With that context: complete the sentence "The world would be a better place if..." then "We exist to..."
  The result should be speakable as an introduction ("Hi, I'm Arielle, I run X, and we exist to...") and work as the header of an About page.
  Great examples: Google ("organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful"), Stripe ("increase the GDP of the internet"), Nike ("bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world; if you have a body, you are an athlete"), LogicLoop ("make operations data work harder than operations people").

- **Positioning Definition and Process**:
  Positioning = the space you occupy in your target customer's mind and everything you can do to influence how they describe your product.
  
  Signs of a positioning problem: (1) ask 10 employees/customers what the company does and get 10 different answers, (2) can't explain what you do in a sentence.
  
  Arielle's positioning process (concentric circles model):
  1. **Who is this for?** — TAM (biggest circle) → target audience (who you'll actively try to acquire for 18 months) → model persona (a specific named person with demographics, job, priorities, feelings)
  2. **What problem do they have?** — may be one they don't even articulate
  3. **How do they address it today?** — not just competitors; could be a manual workaround, old-school tools, or nothing
  4. **What do you make?** — concise description
  5. **How does it work?**
  6. **What would a user tell someone else?** — the ideal word-of-mouth sentence you want spreading
  
  Output: The classic four-part positioning statement: *"For [target audience] who [need/problem], our [product] is a [category] that [benefit/differentiator], unlike [alternative], which [contrast]."*

- **The Bar Test**: The most practical positioning tool in the episode. Once you have a draft positioning statement, test it by literally saying it out loud in human language, as if you were telling someone at a bar:
  *"Hey, I just started using [product]. It's this really great [category] that [benefit]. You know how [pain with current approach]? Well, [product] [differentiator]."*
  If you couldn't say it naturally to a friend — if it sounds like a press release or uses words like "leverages" and "empowers" — your positioning is broken. Forcing it into spoken bar conversation instantly reveals corporate jargon and abstraction.
  Example that works: "Turn your iPad into a point of sale" (Square Stand). This is both the homepage headline AND what a small business owner would say to another at drinks.

- **The Four Types of Names** (spectrum from most to least descriptive):
  1. **Descriptive** (e.g., Internet Explorer): tells you exactly what it does; no ambiguity; easier to understand, harder to extend
  2. **Suggestive** (e.g., Chrome): hints at meaning without spelling it out; Arielle's personal preference
  3. **Evocative** (e.g., Seesaw for ed-tech that connects students/teachers/parents; Maven for expert knowledge platform): when you hear the name and learn what the company does, the name makes sense; has emotional resonance
  4. **Empty vessel / fanciful** (e.g., Yahoo, Apple, Eero): no inherent meaning; requires more marketing work to build association; can be very memorable but you're starting from nothing
  Key insight: **a bad name won't kill a good company** (Disney, Volvo, Nike were all "bad" names on paper). But a good name does some of your marketing work for you without extra spend.

- **7 Naming Criteria (Always Apply)**:
  1. **Trademark**: can you use it; do you need to protect it proactively
  2. **Domain**: .com preferred but not required (SquareUp.com worked for Square for years)
  3. **Distinctiveness**: doesn't sound like competitors; memorable
  4. **Timelessness**: avoid naming trends (names ending in "-ly" for one era, removing vowels for another) that date the company
  5. **Messaging fit**: does it reflect or suggest what the company does or the emotion it's trying to convey
  6. **Sound and pronunciation**: especially critical for B2B sales (will someone say this confidently on a phone call?); Lattice example — dropped because "lettuce?" confusion
  7. **Appearance**: how does it look in a logo? Letter height symmetry matters to designers

- **Naming Process**:
  1. Do positioning first — the positioning statement provides the source words for the brainstorm
  2. Write a naming brief: what are you naming? what to communicate? what to avoid? competitive names? additional constraints?
  3. Brainstorm in a group of 5-7 people (including writers, linguists, or "interested but disinterested" people): Part 1 = run synonyms, antonyms, free associations, translations on the key words from positioning; Part 2 = thematic brainstorm (think Jeopardy categories related to the product domain)
  4. Filter down to 10-25 concepts using red/yellow/green on each criterion
  5. Select 3-5 top candidates (never 1 — trademark may kill your favorite)
  6. Run trademark and domain processes
  Practical tip: use a ridiculous code name when incorporating early so you don't get attached to something that isn't the right long-term name.

- **Sub-Brand vs. Master Brand Decision**: When a company has strong brand equity, products should ride the master brand (boring names) rather than create new sub-brands that split equity. Google Maps, Square Register, Square Stand — boring, descriptive, effective. Google's equity does the work. Create a distinct product name only when you deliberately want to diverge from the master brand (Google → Android, Square → Cash App).

- **Brand Personality — Aker's 5 Dimensions**: Academic framework by Jennifer Aker (Stanford) that analyzed the world's top brands and found they cluster around 5 dimensions:
  1. **Sincerity** — down to earth, honest
  2. **Excitement** — spirited, daring, Mountain Dew
  3. **Competence** — reliable, intelligent
  4. **Sophistication** — charming, upper class, Rolex
  5. **Ruggedness** — outdoorsy, tough
  Strong brands spike in exactly 2 of these 5. Most tech companies end up at Sincerity + Competence (Amazon, Google). Apple adds Sophistication. Mountain Dew is Ruggedness + Excitement.

- **"We Are X, But Not Y" Attribute Statements**: After picking 2 personality dimensions, define 5 brand attributes as tension statements. Brand personality needs tension to be interesting — "we are helpful, nice, approachable, competent, and reliable" says almost nothing (three words for one thing, two for another). The right format: **"We are [X], but not [Y]"** where Y takes X too far. Examples: Google → "We are playful, but not silly." Mountain Dew → "We are daring, but not stupid." These 5 statements then directly inform copy tone, photography style, illustration direction, and ad creative.

- **PR for Early-Stage Startups**:
  - Get story straight first (positioning must be clear before pitching anyone)
  - Exclusive model has replaced embargo model for seed-stage companies — give one outlet the story first
  - Don't do a funding announcement alone — use it as a hook for a larger story (product launch, customer milestone, partnership)
  - Don't target NYT or WSJ for seed stage — they don't cover it; target outlets that cover your space at your stage
  - Local press is underused — tech PR firms aren't expert in local press, but local outlets are hungry for business stories with a local angle
  - Make the story interesting to readers, not just to you: tie to a larger cultural trend; make your customers the heroes

- **When to Hire a Marketer**: Roughly ~10 people for marketing-driven businesses (when freelancers and agencies become too hard to manage alone). For sales-driven businesses: wait until you have a repeatable sales motion. Hire a T-shaped marketer (deep in one function, dangerous across all). Decide which function is most critical first: product marketing, performance marketing, comms, or creative.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Brand is not your logo — it's what people think you are.** You can shape what people think through deliberate choices about Purpose, Positioning, and Personality. Most companies leave this to chance.

2. **Positioning work pays for itself in saved time everywhere else.** Once positioning is clear, the website almost writes itself. Every campaign decision has a test ("does this reinforce our positioning?"). Internal alignment improves. The hour of positioning work saves weeks of copy rewrites.

3. **The model persona should be a specific person, not a demographic.** Not "tech-savvy dads" — a specific VP of Sales at a tech-adjacent company who has teenage gamers, works from home on Fridays, lives in suburban St. Louis. That specificity tells you exactly what to say and how to say it.

4. **Go focused first, then expand.** Early-stage companies that try to appeal to everyone appeal to no one. Find the audience that's large enough (if you won a significant share, it'd be a big business) and target them exclusively for 18 months. Non-target customers will come anyway.

5. **Brand personality needs tension.** Five synonymous positive attributes communicate nothing. "We are [X] but not [Y]" — where Y takes X to an unwanted extreme — is the format that actually differentiates and constrains creative work.

6. **Volvo didn't earn "safety" through logo design.** They gave away the three-point seatbelt patent for free in 1959 because it made all cars safer. Company decisions are what build brand meaning. The visual expression follows from who you are, not the other way around.

7. **Use a ridiculous code name when incorporating early.** The #1 naming mistake is getting attached to a placeholder. Make the code name so absurd you'd never launch with it, so you stay motivated to find the real name.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- The purpose, positioning, and personality framework applies directly to AI products, which face a particularly acute positioning problem: "AI" is both a feature and a claimed category, and claiming "AI" as differentiation is increasingly meaningless (everyone is "AI-powered")
- The bar test is especially useful for AI products whose capabilities are hard to explain: if you can't say what your AI product does to someone at a bar in one sentence, you have a positioning problem before you have a product problem
- The empty-vessel naming pitfall hits AI companies especially hard: names like "Nexus AI" or "Vertex AI" have become their own naming trend and will date companies
- Arielle's advice that the brand purpose should be on a 10-year frame while positioning is on an 18-month frame is particularly useful for AI companies whose capabilities are evolving fast — separate what you're always trying to be from what problem you're solving right now

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"How do you approach brand and marketing strategy?"**: Brand = Purpose + Positioning + Personality. Do positioning first (who is this for, what's their problem, how do they solve it today, what do you do differently). Then purpose (why you exist). Then personality (5 "we are X but not Y" attributes). Feed all three into visual design and copy.
- **"How do you think about naming a product or company?"**: 4 name types from descriptive to empty vessel; personal preference is suggestive/evocative. 7 criteria (trademark, domain, distinctiveness, timelessness, messaging fit, sound/pronunciation, appearance). Process: positioning → brief → group brainstorm → filter → 3-5 candidates → trademark/domain.
- **"How do you write positioning for a product?"**: Start with who it's for (model persona), what problem they have, how they solve it today. Output a one-sentence positioning statement. Run it through the bar test — say it out loud to your target audience at a bar.
- **"When should a startup invest in marketing?"**: As early as possible on positioning (saves all downstream work). First full-time marketer at ~10 people. Use clear frameworks to know what kind of marketer you need. Brand and positioning you can do yourself in weeks.
- **"How do you get press coverage?"**: Exclusives, not embargoes. Get story straight first. Use funding as a hook, not the story. Target publications that actually cover your stage. Don't sleep on local press.
