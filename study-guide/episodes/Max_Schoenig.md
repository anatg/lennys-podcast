## TL;DR
Max Schoening argues that agency—not skills—is the key differentiator as AI transforms product building, and that the first 10% of every project is now essentially free through AI prototyping. He advocates for malleable software that serves users' interests over corporate control, emphasizes finding the "tiny core superpower" in great products rather than adding features, and pushes his team to think in code not for production utility but to master the material they're designing with.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Tiny Core Principle**: All great products have "something tiny that is a superpower"—one exceptionally good core feature that everything else flows from. Examples: iPhone's multi-touch, GitHub's pull request, Notion's blocks and slash commands, Figma's seamless real-time collaboration. Adding features hoping the product becomes "finally great" never works—focus on making the core exceptional.

- **Agency Over Skills**: Before AI, people could blame lack of skills for not building things. Now with AI providing skills on-demand, agency (the belief that you can change things) is the true differentiator. Agency is cultivated through making/tinkering, which teaches you "the world is made up by people no smarter than you."

- **The First 10% is Free Framework**: AI has made the first 10% of every project essentially costless—you can now build janky first versions and demos instead of writing PRDs. However, the last 10% is still 90% of the work (quality, polish, scale, maintenance). This enables massive exploration but doesn't eliminate craft.

- **Malleable Software Philosophy**: Software should work closer to users' interests than the corporation's interests. Like rearranging your living room vs. being forced into Apple's layout. True malleability requires platforms that encourage customization while maintaining the "as a service" maintenance most people actually want.

- **Demos Not Memos**: Give people something to react to rather than documents to read. With AI, it's now easier to create multiple working prototypes to explore directions than to write specifications. This builds iteration into product development much earlier.

- **Obviously Good Standard**: The quality bar should be "obviously good"—something nobody would argue about (like the first iPhone or ChatGPT). But achieve this through "incremental correctness"—rapid iteration rather than isolation. You know it when you see it; don't make people squint.

- **Taste as Virtual Machine**: Taste means "running a virtual machine in your head where given an idea, you can predict for a certain in-group whether they're going to like it or not." Build taste through reps with feedback loops—it's literally training a model through backpropagation on human reactions.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Engineering principles matter more than ever in the AI era**: As AI makes prototyping trivial, real engineering becomes about "optimizing the factory for 100 million people" and ensuring reliability at scale. The current obsession with token spend and feature velocity misses this—we're losing specialists who understand how to build durable, high-quality software that actually works.

2. **Direct manipulation in code replaces design tool manipulation**: The shift isn't about designers shipping to production—it's about "thinking and designing in the medium that will actually be the real thing." Understanding how agent loops work matters infinitely more than knowing how to tweak CSS. Mastering the material (code) is the point, not becoming a delivery mechanism.

3. **The "shots on goal" mindset requires reconciliation**: Increasing experimentation velocity is valuable, but you must do the hard work of consolidating evolutionary branches back into the "naked robotic core." Otherwise you end up with six automation primitives that confuse users. Someone has to sit down and figure out what should outlive the other branches.

4. **Preciousness kills innovation in successful products**: Companies with product-market fit often develop a harmful preciousness—"What will users think if we break something?" Users are more upset by lack of innovation than by occasional bugs. The same team that created the first act can create the second, but they have to try. Reminding people "you can just do stuff" is often the most important leadership work.

5. **Exclusivity enables quality**: "Inclusivity isn't always all that great." Building for everyone means upsetting your top users. The world is run by "group chats of eight people or fewer." Being willing to build exceptional products for the top of the class (at something) often means intentionally excluding others—and that's okay for non-essential services.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

**The Prototyping Revolution**: PMs at Notion now spend thousands of dollars monthly on AI tokens, primarily using developer tools (terminal, Claude Code, Codex, Cursor) rather than traditional PM tools. The role shift isn't about shipping to production—it's about being able to prototype and think in the actual medium (code) rather than abstractions (PRDs, wireframes).

**Terminal over GUI**: Designers and PMs are encouraged to use text-based interfaces (TUIs) rather than GUIs because "over time they're going to be curious and pull at other threads"—eventually understanding more of how computers work. This builds the foundation for deeper contributions.

**The Agency Bottleneck**: The biggest challenge isn't tools or capabilities—it's "how much prodding you need to do to get people out of the way they're used to working." Even with powerful AI at their fingertips, people need encouragement to "identify the outer loop of their work and enlist an agent" to automate it. Creating leaderboards or incentives becomes necessary at scale.

**Quality Still Requires Humans**: While vibe coding accelerates output, "the quality of software has [not] increased all that much in the last 12 months." The amount of software has, but reliability is harder to find. Manual intervention in code should feel "a little bit like a bug"—but human review and taste remain essential.

**Future Uncertainty**: Max is conflicted about whether we'll continue chatting back-and-forth with AI or return to direct manipulation as inference becomes instant. "Is direct manipulation going away because the agent is doing the direct manipulation?" The answer depends on whether intelligence saturates (like retina displays—past a point, you don't need more) or continues improving.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

**Demonstrate Agency, Not Just Skills**: In interviews, don't just talk about technical capabilities—show examples where you changed things because you believed you could, not because someone asked. The best answer to "Why did you do that?" is "Because I realized I could" rather than "Because it was my job."

**Tell the Story of Your Tiny Core**: When discussing products you've built, identify and articulate the one small superpower that made it work. Avoid the trap of listing all the features—great PMs know which 5% of the product created 95% of the value.

**Show You Think in Multiple Mediums**: Modern product leaders prototype across design tools, code, and direct user interaction. In interviews, discuss how you've explored ideas through different mediums—not to show off skills, but to show you interrogate the material you're working with.

**Embrace "Demos Not Memos"**: When asked about planning or strategy, demonstrate that you prefer showing over telling. Talk about times you built quick prototypes to align teams rather than writing lengthy documents. This shows you value reaction and iteration over perfection.

**Practice Taste Articulation**: Be ready to explain *why* something is good or bad, not just that it is. The best interviews reveal your ability to predict user reactions and articulate design principles. Reference specific examples where your taste was validated (or challenged) by real user behavior.

**Discuss Quality vs. Velocity Trade-offs**: Be prepared to talk about how you balance shipping fast with maintaining quality. Strong candidates acknowledge both sides—the need for shots on goal AND the importance of consolidating back to a simple core.
