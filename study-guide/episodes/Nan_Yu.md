## TL;DR
Nan Yu reveals Linear's secrets to building beloved B2B software: ship working prototypes in the first 10% of allocated time, prioritize IC experience over middle manager reporting needs, and understand the emotional pain customers feel (not just their stated goals). He demonstrates that speed and quality aren't trade-offs—competence enables both—and that building extreme versions of features reveals non-obvious solutions.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **10% Rule for Speed**: By the time 10% of your timeline has passed (e.g., week one of a month-long project), you should have something workable that tests key hypotheses internally. This enables rapid learning without waiting until you're 80% done to validate assumptions.

- **IC vs. Middle Manager Filter**: Automatically say no to customization features requested by middle managers for reporting that make IC workflows worse. These requests are the primary cause of software bloat that makes users hate their tools. The data will be wrong anyway because ICs won't maintain it.

- **Emotion-First Discovery**: Don't just ask "what's your goal?" Ask questions until you understand the specific emotional pain—the moment they felt bad. Example: "I marked a ship date as Dec 30th, my marketing team lost their minds, now I never want to set dates again." This reveals the real problem to solve.

- **Extreme Variation Testing**: Build the most extreme version of a feature along specific dimensions (fastest/safest, most luxurious/most minimal) to expand your search space. The best solution isn't obvious from the middle—you discover it by feeling the extremes. Test these with real users, not just internally.

- **Double Triangle Collaboration**: PM sits at the intersection of two triangles: (1) Building side: Engineering, Design, Product; (2) Selling side: Sales, Marketing, Product. PMs must translate between builders and sellers, originating messaging that reflects deep customer understanding.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Speed indicates competence, not sloppiness**: At the pinnacle of any craft (chess, cooking, programming), speed and quality correlate positively. Fast practitioners get more iterations, which produces better outcomes. The "speed vs. quality trade-off" is a myth perpetuated by the slow.

2. **B2B software teaches users how to work**: When companies adopt tools, they're not just solving problems—they're importing best practices and work methodologies. Every tool encodes a process that becomes "the worst we can do." This is why being opinionated about workflows (based on high-performing teams) creates value.

3. **Real people, not personas**: Tie feature decisions all the way back to specific individuals—"Bob from Company X has this workflow." Not hypothetical Alice and Bob. This forces you to match reality's messiness rather than building elegant solutions that don't fit actual use cases.

4. **Deadlines are P0 or don't exist**: Don't have many deadlines, but when you do, treat them as P0 incidents. Engineers work on nothing else. PMs ruthlessly cut scope. The goal is to reach the decision point with a shippable product (even if minimal), not to be 50% done with something broken. Marketing has limited annual opportunities—missing them costs momentum you can't recover.

5. **Discovery is about feeling bad the same way**: Empathy isn't abstract—it's asking questions from enough angles that you viscerally feel the customer's pain. When they say "marketing and I never align," dig until you understand the 11pm moment they realized their December 30th deadline meant everyone's on vacation. Then build to prevent that feeling.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Identify the hiring manager's burning problem**: Don't just present yourself as generically great at everything. Through discovery questions, figure out what specific problem this PM hire solves for the team. Position yourself as the solution to that problem versus being compared on generic dimensions against all candidates.

- **Act like you already have the job**: Ask interviewers about their OKRs this quarter, how you could help them achieve goals, what's blocking their team. This specificity makes you memorable and demonstrates how you'd collaborate if hired. Request to talk with engineers or other team members to deepen understanding.

- **Demonstrate product thinking through extremes**: When discussing product decisions, show you can think beyond obvious choices by exploring extreme variations ("What's the fastest possible version? The safest?"). This reveals your ability to expand search spaces and find non-obvious solutions.

- **Show go-to-market fluency**: For B2B PM roles especially, demonstrate that you understand the double triangle—that PMs must bridge building and selling. Discuss how you'd work with sales/marketing to craft messaging, not just how you'd collaborate with engineering/design.

- **Connect to emotional outcomes**: When discussing past work, don't just describe features shipped. Explain the specific emotional moment that users avoided (frustration, embarrassment, stress) and how your solution prevented that bad feeling. This shows deep empathy and customer understanding.
