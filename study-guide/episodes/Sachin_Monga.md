## TL;DR
Sachin Monga shares how Substack built its legendary recommendations feature—now driving millions of subscriptions by putting writers in control rather than using algorithms. He explains the transition from tool to network, how to be a head of product with a strong founder, and why the internet is entering a "golden era" for writers who can now make a living from small, dedicated audiences rather than chasing millions of eyeballs.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Build with Writers, Build with Readers**: Before shipping major features, bring users along through pilot programs and product labs. Get feedback from 10-100 writers in an invite-only group, iterate based on real usage, then roll out broadly. This builds trust and ensures features serve actual needs rather than theoretical ones.

- **Control as Product Principle**: When prioritizing or designing features, default to giving users (writers and readers) maximum control over their experience and destiny. If a feature could be built multiple ways, choose the one that provides more agency even if it seems harder to execute. This principle led to writer-curated recommendations rather than algorithmic suggestions.

- **Customer-Oriented Teams Over Surface-Area Teams**: Structure product teams around timeless customer problems (writers, readers, growth) rather than product surfaces (app team, dashboard team, podcast team). This prevents constant reorganizations as the product evolves, since you'll never be "done" serving these customer segments.

- **Rate of Change as Job Definition**: At high-growth startups, the PM role fundamentally requires comfort with constant uncertainty. As soon as you perfect a process, growth makes it obsolete. Success means you won't know what you're doing—you just need to get better every week/month/year. This isn't for everyone; it's a personality fit question.

- **Facilitator Before Decision-Maker**: When joining as head of product with a visionary founder, prioritize facilitating communication between the founder and teams over making decisions. Ensure the founder knows what teams are doing and teams understand the founder's vision. Trust and autonomy build from this foundation of transparent communication (weekly alignment meetings help).

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The Recommendations Feature Success Formula**: Substack's recommendations work because they maximized writer control—writers manually curate who they recommend, creating a goodwill viral loop. When someone receives recommendations, they get an email showing who's sending them readers, creating organic reciprocity. This "simple" feature now drives 1 in 3 new subscriptions and has generated millions of subscriptions across tens of thousands of writers. The key insight: maximal user control can work better than sophisticated algorithms if you design the incentives right.

2. **1,000 True Fans Is Real**: Kevin Kelly's thesis is validated—creators can make ~$100K/year with just 1,000 paid subscribers at $10/month. The market is often much larger than you think; what seems like a ceiling is often just a milestone. You don't need millions of viewers or to play attention games; high-quality work valued by a relatively small dedicated audience is now economically viable for the first time in internet history.

3. **Comparison: Facebook vs. Startup PM Work**: At large companies like Facebook, PM work centers on managing trade-offs where doing A makes B permanently harder (not just later). At startups, prioritization is more about sequencing and time, with more reversible decisions but critical ordering that unlocks future possibilities. The stakes and second-order effects are fundamentally different types of problems.

4. **It's Never Too Late**: People consistently feel they've missed the boat (Lenny felt this 3 years ago starting his newsletter; Marc Andreessen felt it in the '80s joining tech). We're actually incredibly early for how the internet will evolve. The rewiring of internet economics around direct subscriptions rather than advertising is just beginning, enabling entirely new business models and creator opportunities.

5. **Writers Underestimate Reader Support**: Creators worry excessively about audience perception—is three emails/week too much? Can I charge? Can I take vacation? Subscribers, especially paid ones, are remarkably forgiving and supportive. The "subscribe" action is a heavyweight signal granting "write access to their brain," meaning they're genuinely invested in your success and want you to succeed sustainably.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

Sachin mentions exploring AI-assisted writing tools like Jasper and Copy.ai that can generate paragraphs from titles and provide smart autocomplete. He calls this "an interesting can of worms" but acknowledges potential value—especially for features like DALL-E generated publication logos. The team is clearly aware of AI capabilities but proceeding thoughtfully, balancing innovation with Substack's principles of writer control and authenticity.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Show principle-driven thinking**: When discussing product decisions, demonstrate how you start from core principles (user control, long-term value) rather than just metrics. Explain how principles guide trade-offs when multiple solutions exist.

- **Demonstrate comfort with ambiguity**: Emphasize your ability to thrive when processes are constantly changing. Share examples of building systems that quickly became obsolete due to growth, and how you adapted. High-growth companies want PMs who embrace rather than fear uncertainty.

- **Facilitate, then lead**: For roles with strong founders or senior stakeholders, emphasize your skills in facilitating communication and building trust before asserting decision-making authority. Show you understand the gradual trust-building process.

- **Talk about network effects thoughtfully**: Discuss marketplace or platform dynamics with nuance—how you think about supply/demand balance, viral loops, and sustainable growth mechanisms that respect user agency rather than just optimizing for metrics.

- **Tailor interview questions to context**: When asked about your interview approach, show you adapt questions based on candidate background (startup vs. big company) to get consistent signal (grit, drive) rather than using one-size-fits-all questions.
