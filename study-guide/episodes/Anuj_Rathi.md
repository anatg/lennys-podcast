## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Lazy, Vain, and Selfish User Model** (from Scott Belsky, Adobe): Every new-user onboarding problem is easier to solve once you internalize that users are:
  - **Lazy**: "I don't have time for this. Blow my mind or I'm not paying attention."
  - **Vain**: "I already have a habit solving this problem. You're asking me to change? Really?"
  - **Selfish**: "Show me what's in it for me."
  Most products are designed for loyal users who care. The biggest growth delta is building for the marginal user who doesn't yet care and won't work hard to understand you.

- **Three Press Releases (Working Backwards Extended)**: The Amazon PR FAQ process extended into three *divergent* alternatives before committing to one direction:
  1. Write three fully thought-through, concrete PR FAQs pointing in genuinely different directions (e.g., two-tier vs. three-tier membership; membership vs. tiered pricing points)
  2. Present all three to leadership — they can compare and contrast, not just react to one idea
  3. Recommend one with your reasoning, but let leadership see what was explored and rejected
  Benefits: demonstrates comprehensive thinking, incorporates stakeholder viewpoints even when rejected, gives leadership a richer decision context. The FAQ section is also where compliance requirements, legal sign-offs, and cross-marketplace implications get explicitly surfaced (especially critical in fintech and multi-sided marketplaces).

- **Show Don't Tell (User Journey Wall)**: A product review methodology where product managers build a literal physical or virtual "wall" showing:
  - The exact trigger moment that brings a new user to the product (marketing message, promotion, word of mouth)
  - The complete emotional journey from first awareness through to loyalty
  - All the scenarios in real-time marketplaces (e.g., in food delivery: what the user feels while waiting 30 minutes, the bike breakdown scenario, the restaurant delay scenario)
  - Current state vs. future state comparison side by side
  This eliminates the assumption that users understand the product the way internal teams do. 50% of Anuj's product reviews focus on this journey, pixel by pixel, copy word by copy word.

- **Four BB Framework (Product Strategy Allocation)**: Four buckets that cover all product investment decisions — the key is making the allocation an explicit leadership conversation, not a bottom-up PM call:
  1. **Brilliant Basics**: Tech debt, platform investments, infrastructure — rebranded from "tech debt" to signal importance (these are the brilliant foundations the product is built on)
  2. **Bread and Butter**: The product backlog — feature enhancements, bug fixes, A/B experiments, version improvements across existing product lines
  3. **Big Bets**: Large, cross-team initiatives requiring CEO/leadership alignment; worked-backwards PR FAQs are essential here; can change the game if they succeed
  4. **Breaking Bad**: Company transformation or pivot — e.g., moving from food delivery to convenience company, adding grocery delivery as a whole new business line
  The value: leadership can decide "If I gave you 100 focus points, how many go to each bucket?" — and the three alternative strategies show what happens if you shift allocations dramatically. This surfaces trade-offs that would otherwise be invisible.

- **Can't Do / Won't Do / Not Set Up To Do**: Three exhaustive reasons things don't happen as a leader:
  1. **Can't do** — capability issue: coach, mentor, or move to a role better suited to their skills
  2. **Won't do** — motivation or alignment issue: dig into why; is it disagreement with vision? Lack of time? Misalignment on incentives?
  3. **Not set up to do** — setup issue: org design, OKRs, ways of working — **70-80% of failures fall here**, and it's the leader's problem, not the individual's
  Reference: *Team Topologies* and Conway's Law — org design creates product design; you can read the org structure from the product architecture.

- **Full-Stack PM as the Standard**: Product managers must own outcomes, not just features. That means being as good as the marketer at understanding the marginal user, as good as the sales team at understanding the buyer, and as good as the business leader at understanding trade-offs. Anuj explicitly reframes PMs as "full-stack influencers" — you're in the business of influence: influencing users to change behavior, engineers to build things, and leadership to sign off on direction.

- **PM Core Skills (Rule of Three)**: Three necessary attributes for PM success:
  1. **Raw smarts**: Problem identification, problem solving, higher-order thinking — 80% achievable through domain knowledge and deliberate development
  2. **Drive/Grit**: Curiosity, learnability, never giving up, consumer-backward thinking — hardest to coach from scratch
  3. **Influence**: The non-negotiable; if it scares you that you have to be influential to succeed, PM is not the right field

- **Speed vs. Excellence (Contrarian Take)**: When forced to choose, choose excellence. Most experiments should be *thought experiments* first, run in your head rather than wasted engineering time. "If you had smart people who could do meta-thinking, a lot of experiments would just not even be tried." Speed and excellence are separate axes; the goal is both, but if you must choose, excellence compounds better long-term.

- **Marketplace-Specific Frameworks**:
  - OKRs frequently fail in three-sided marketplaces because all goals are structurally in tension (raise delivery fee → hurts consumers + strains restaurant commissions + squeezes delivery partner earnings). Big Bets work better than individual OKRs in this context.
  - A/B experiments are unreliable in two-sided or three-sided marketplaces because of intra-group network effects.
  - Prioritization principle: Establish stable marketplace first (no side should be at risk of collapse), then identify which side the company is in ultimate service of (Swiggy: the end consumer; Taobao: the seller; Amazon: the buyer).

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The biggest growth unlock is designing for the user who doesn't yet care.** Most products are optimized for existing users. The lazy, vain, and selfish framework forces you to design for skeptics who have no existing motivation to try you.

2. **Marketing and onboarding must be a single continuous story.** Whatever promise the marketing message makes, the first screen of the app must continue. Breaks in this thread cost enormous conversion.

3. **Three alternatives before one commitment.** Always explore three divergent directions before recommending one. This prevents premature lock-in, demonstrates thoroughness, and lets leadership make a better decision.

4. **70-80% of execution failures are setup failures.** When something doesn't happen, default assumption should be that the system wasn't set up to make it happen — not that the person couldn't or wouldn't. Org design, OKR structure, and ways of working are leadership problems.

5. **India at scale means building for 1.4B users in 22 official languages, with per capita income 30x lower than the US.** Choosing the right paying customer within a massive audience is the craft. Every Western PM framework requires re-evaluation against this context.

6. **Product management is the business of influence.** If you're not already influencing every direction (users, engineers, leadership), you're just managing a backlog. PM = full-stack influencer.

7. **Marketplaces need a north star side.** If all sides are in tension (they always are), the company needs an explicit value statement about which side it ultimately serves. That resolves prioritization conflicts that OKRs can't.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- Anuj's take: AI needs HI (Human Intelligence). From Swiggy CEO Sriharsha: "AI is as important as human intelligence; if you're not humanly intelligent, AI won't help you much." The combination of great UX, behavioral science, and AI is what delivers consumer outcomes.
- The most common mistake: treating AI as a solution looking for a problem inside the company. Better framing: "How do I get AI to work with HI to deliver better outcomes for the user I was already trying to serve?"
- The Show Don't Tell methodology applies directly to AI products: if you build the full user journey wall for an AI-powered experience, you immediately surface where the AI adds genuine value vs. where it creates confusing breaks in the journey.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"How do you prioritize what to build?"**: Four BB framework — make the bucket allocation explicit as a leadership conversation. Then the product backlog becomes tactical execution within agreed strategic direction. Three alternatives before committing.
- **"How do you think about product strategy?"**: Working Backwards with three press releases. Show Don't Tell for the user journey. Full-stack thinking about adoption, marketing, and outcomes, not just features.
- **"How do you build for emerging markets?"**: India context: Jio revolution unlocked a different internet user cohort; UPI changed payment behavior; India Stack enables new product layers. The lazy/vain/selfish framework is especially critical when per-capita income is low and switching costs are emotional.
- **"What makes a great PM?"**: Raw smarts + drive + influence. All three must be present or the person should reconsider the field.
- **"How do you manage a marketplace?"**: Stable marketplace first. OKRs don't work across three sides. Big Bets work better. Identify the north star side that the company ultimately serves.
