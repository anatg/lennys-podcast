# Varun Mohan — CEO & Co-founder of Codeium/Windsurf

## TL;DR
Varun Mohan shares how Codeium pivoted twice—from GPU infrastructure to AI coding tools to building their own IDE (Windsurf)—and why they bet the company each time based on changing assumptions. He reveals counterintuitive lessons about staying lean (hire only when "underwater"), building for 6-12 month cannibalization cycles rather than incremental features, and why 90% AI-written code actually means hiring *more* engineers, not fewer.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Dehydrated Organization**: Varun's hiring philosophy—"I want the company to almost be like this dehydrated entity. Every hire is like a little bit of water, and we only go back and hire someone when we're back to being dehydrated." Only hire when teams are truly underwater and raising their hands for help. This forces ruthless prioritization and prevents manufacturing work.

- **Six-to-Twelve Month Cannibalization Cycles**: "We should be cannibalizing the existing state of our product every six to 12 months. Every six to 12 months, it should make our existing product look silly. It should almost make the form factor of existing product look dumb." Balance incremental iteration with bold longer-term bets that disrupt your own product.

- **Kill Your Hypotheses, Not Your Company**: Accept that most initial hypotheses will be wrong. Build an organization that's truth-seeking rather than idea-defending. When core assumptions change (like "model architectures will be heterogeneous"), pivot overnight rather than slowly—focus is critical for startups.

- **The A+ in One Class Philosophy**: "You don't win by doing 10 things well. You win by doing one thing really well and maybe you fail nine things... I just need to get an A+ on the one class that matters. And then I can get an F in all the other classes." Optimize for excellence in the critical path, not total GPA.

- **Amdahl's Law for Productivity**: Even if AI writes 90% of code, engineers do more than write code (review, test, debug, design, deploy, navigate). Making one task instant doesn't remove the entire critical path. A 90% improvement in writing might only be 27-40% total productivity gain—which actually increases ROI of hiring more engineers.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Value accrues where you can differentiate continuously**: Infrastructure commoditized when everyone ran the same generative models. Application layer (where UX and workflow improvements are infinite) became the differentiation point. Differentiation on proprietary training data (incomplete code, user preference signals) compounds over time.

2. **Enterprise sales is a feature, not a compromise**: Varun hired a VP of Sales over a year ago; go-to-market is now 80+ people. For Fortune 500 customers, credit card swipe isn't enough. Enterprise requirements (FedRAMP, hybrid deployment, deep code base understanding) became competitive moats, not distractions.

3. **Fork when APIs limit your ceiling**: Windsurf forked VSCode because API limitations capped their AI capabilities. Same ML models, custom UI for reviews and inline refactors—acceptance rates tripled. When the platform constrains your vision, own the platform (but still support JetBrains where it's extensible enough).

4. **Agency > Technical Skills in AI Era**: "Agency is what matters." When tools can build 90% of software, the constraint becomes who has ideas and will to execute them. Codeium's sales/go-to-market teams built internal tools that saved $500K+ in SaaS subscriptions—domain specialists can now build their own verticalized tools.

5. **Hire for the problem you're underwater on, not the one you anticipate**: Teams must be raising their hands saying "I'm dying" before you hire. This prevents politics where people manufacture importance for unnecessary roles. Most valuable employees do ambitious projects with fewest people, not those who grow large teams.

6. **The "secret roadmap" is essential**: Users guide incremental improvements (table stakes), but 6-12 month disruptive bets aren't user-informed—they come from internal conviction about where technology is heading. You need both roadmaps running in parallel.

7. **Engineers should optimize for problem-solving, not just coding syntax**: CS degrees teach distributed systems principles, parallel computing concepts, memory/computation trade-offs—these mental models matter more than language syntax. As AI handles syntax, understanding "how computers work" becomes the durable skill.

8. **Vertical SaaS with kitchen-sink features is vulnerable**: Companies previously bought software with 100 features to use 10%, because they couldn't maintain custom code. Now domain specialists can build exactly the 10% they need in hours, with native integrations. Companies that differentiated on "we have every feature" face existential risk.

9. **Small teams prevent armchair quarterbacking**: Two-pizza teams keep leaders in the weeds of technology. In fast-moving spaces, leaders who don't understand tech deeply make dangerous decisions. Flat structure with flexible team assignment (no one "owns" people) lets you reallocate to highest-priority work.

10. **Be okay with being wrong faster**: Varun wishes he'd pivoted months earlier each time. The tension: you need irrational optimism to start, but uncompromising realism to kill ideas. Most startup ideas are bad; the non-obvious good ones only reveal themselves through aggressive hypothesis testing.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **PMs can now code**: Product managers at large companies (JPMorgan Chase's 50K developer org) can use Windsurf to make direct code changes, create PRs, ship features themselves. This changes PM/eng dynamics—PMs who build credibility and move faster.

- **Domain experts bypass engineering bottlenecks**: Codeium's sales team built partner portals and internal tools without engineering help, saving $500K+ in SaaS costs. The constraint shifts from "can we build it?" to "what should we build?"—agency and taste become premium skills.

- **Engineers shift from "solving it" to "what should I solve"**: AI handles implementation ("solving it") and increasingly "how should I solve it" (via code base understanding). Engineers' value moves to prioritizing business problems, making technical decisions, maintaining taste and judgment.

- **Review flows replace writing flows**: Developers spend more time reviewing AI-generated code than writing it. IDEs need custom review UIs (like Windsurf's inline refactor acceptance). The interface paradigm shifts from text editor to review/approval tool.

- **Preference data becomes moat**: Companies with user interaction data (acceptance rates on incomplete code, retrieval quality signals, evolutionary code history) can train better models than frontier labs. The application layer captures differentiated training signal.

- **Engineering hiring paradox resolved**: 90% AI-written code doesn't mean 10X fewer engineers—it means ROI of building technology increases, so you invest more. Companies with high technology ceilings (like JPMorgan Chase's $17B software budget) hire *more* engineers as productivity rises.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate agency over credentials**: In AI-era interviews, show examples where you didn't wait for permission—you identified problems and solved them yourself. Varun values people who "want to build something" over those who execute perfect problem sets.

- **Articulate your hypothesis-testing velocity**: Be ready to discuss times you killed your own ideas quickly when assumptions changed. Show you're truth-seeking, not ego-defending. Example: "I believed X, but when Y signal emerged, I pivoted in 2 weeks because..."

- **Show deep understanding of problem context, not just solutions**: When discussing past work, emphasize "why this problem mattered" and "what we chose NOT to do" over implementation details. The A+ in one class philosophy—show ruthless prioritization.

- **Discuss your tolerance for being underwater**: Strong candidates can articulate working in high-pressure, under-resourced environments where prioritization was forced. Weakness: candidates who need perfect resources or get defensive about constraints.

- **For AI product roles, show hands-on tool usage**: Varun's advice: "Get your hands dirty with all of these products." In interviews, reference specific AI tools you've used to solve real problems. PMs who've actually shipped code with AI tools stand out.

- **Embrace "working smart AND hard" framing**: Don't deflect hard work questions with "I work smart." Strong answer: "I'm excited to work both smart and hard because this space moves fast and competitors won't wait."

- **Prepare 6-12 month vision examples**: Discuss times you pushed for longer-term disruptive work alongside incremental improvements. Show you can balance user feedback (table stakes) with contrarian bets (competitive moats).

- **Ask about pivot history**: Companies that pivoted successfully have strong truth-seeking cultures. Ask: "When did leadership kill a major initiative and why?" Quality of answer reveals organizational health.
