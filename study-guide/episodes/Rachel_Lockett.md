## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **GROW Coaching Model**: A structured approach to coaching conversations with four steps: Goal (what does success look like?), Reality (where are you stuck? what have you tried?), Options (what paths could you take?), Way forward (what will you do next?). This framework helps leaders guide their teams to solutions rather than providing all the answers.

- **Three Levels of Listening**: Level 1 is internal (thinking about implications on yourself, completely distracted with your own inner dialogue). Level 2 is focused (you can repeat back what the person is saying). Level 3 is global listening (hearing what's beneath the words, body language, tone of voice, context—understanding everything they're communicating). Leaders should aim for Level 3 to truly connect.

- **Discovering Your Gifts Exercise**: For two weeks, every night reflect on what are the five things today that gave me the most energy? And what are the five things that depleted my energy the most? Look at patterns. Additionally, ask five to ten people in your life: When I walk in the room, what shows up? What are my strengths? What are the gifts?

- **Nonviolent Communication (Marshall Rosenberg)**: Four steps for difficult conversations: 1) Observations (note what is happening factually), 2) Feelings (express feelings without blame), 3) Needs (universal human needs related to this topic), 4) Request (olive branch to help the other person meet you). The request should be small and achievable to help the other person feel successful in connecting.

- **Stay on Your Side of the Net**: From Stanford Business School's "Touchy Feely" course (Carol Robbins)—when discussing feelings, stay on your side of the net; crossing the net to blame or assume triggers defensive responses. This keeps conversations productive.

- **One-Page Plan Structure**: Column 1 = vision and values, Column 2 = strategic intentions and KPIs, Column 3 = annual goals, Column 4 = quarterly goals. This simplifies alignment by putting everything leaders need in one place rather than scattered across multiple documents.

- **Operating Rhythm**: Schedule regular times to step onto the balcony (work on the business) versus staying on the dance floor (in the business). Leadership teams need spacious time to tinker, reflect, create, and come back energized.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Coaching unlocks more than advising**: Most leaders, especially technical leaders, assume they have to have all the answers. People have climbed the ladder because they've been dependable, reliable, the smartest person in the room. But great leaders know that when you try to advise and have the answer all the time, you're not actually equipping your team to go solve the hard problems. You're training your team to come to you with all of the hard problems. Leaders over-rotate, assuming the people that they've hired who are experts in their domain need them to solve the problem.

2. **Active listening creates connection in under a minute**: There's some emotional connection when you listen actively, and that took less than a minute. Active listening doesn't mean you're setting up an hour coaching session with every person on your team. Asking powerful questions means I'm curious about what's really going on, and there's not one right answer. A powerful question helps you gain insight and it takes you to a new solution set you didn't have before.

3. **Living in your gifts is your responsibility**: When people are in their gifts and their strengths, they have more energy. We all have more energy when we're operating from the things we naturally are good at. It's no one else's job to help you live in your gifts. What I notice in big companies is people are often annoyed or frustrated with their management for not making their job more interesting. No, your manager's job is to help you perform in the job you are hired to do. It's your job to navigate your career. It takes risk taking, it takes intention to design your life around your gifts. My litmus test is 80%, that's the goal. You're always going to have 20% of things you don't love doing.

4. **Co-founder conflict kills startups**: 65% of startups fail because of co-founder conflict. What you need in a healthy relationship is, one, self-awareness. What do I bring into this dynamic, and how am I being experienced by the other person? CTOs tend to be skeptics. They love facts, they seek knowledge. The CEO is the person who had to sell the vision. This creates an inherent tension between blind optimism and skepticism. Co-founders need time and space to connect with each other to come together and say, How's this working for you? Are we still aligned on our vision and our strategy? How are we working together?

5. **Difficult conversations aim for mutual understanding**: The goal of any conflict is to create mutual understanding. There's a misguided view that the goal is to convince the other person that what they're doing is wrong. There is always something we're doing to contribute to conflict, even when it feels like the other person is at fault—enter with humility and curiosity. Difficult conversations arise because something important is at stake—reframe them as learning opportunities rather than things to avoid.

6. **Professionals have feelings**: Professionals have feelings. We operate in tech. We're supposed to give all of ourselves, all of our time, all of our energy to this endeavor, and it's purely logical. That's not at all true. It's completely emotional. Professionals have feelings that will bubble up unconsciously if ignored—acknowledging emotions is critical in tech environments that pretend to be purely logical.

7. **Everything besides PMF is human**: Everything in business besides product-market fit and timing is human: talent and the environment you create for that talent to thrive. The quality of our relationships determines the quality of our life; healthy relationships require the ability to have conflict. The world is getting more lonely despite technology—leaders must actively overcome the default state of blind grind and loneliness.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

Rachel is actively experimenting with AI in her coaching practice:

- **Using ChatGPT for creative retreat planning** by inputting objectives, audience, and past activities to generate new ideas for leadership offsites and team experiences.

- **Experimenting with AI support between coaching sessions**: bot trained on client context, development plans, frameworks, and session notes for tactical support—personal coaching remains critical for vision and behavior change, but AI can provide helpful tactical support between sessions.

- **Using Loom to record trainings** for scaled holding company clients in a human, connected way that maintains personal connection at scale.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

_How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:_

- **Practice Level 3 listening in interviews**: Show you can hear what's beneath the words, not just the literal question. This demonstrates executive presence and emotional intelligence that interviewers value in senior candidates.

- **Use the GROW model when discussing past challenges**: Structure your stories to show how you coached teams to solutions rather than always being the hero with the answer. This signals leadership maturity and ability to scale through others.

- **Demonstrate self-awareness about your gifts**: Be ready to articulate what energizes you and what depletes you. Show you've done the work to understand your strengths and have intentionally designed your career around them—this signals career intentionality.

- **Ask powerful questions during your interview**: Don't just answer questions—ask curious, open-ended questions that show you're thinking deeply about the role and the company's challenges. This demonstrates coaching mindset and strategic thinking.

- **Acknowledge the human/emotional side**: When discussing team or cross-functional challenges, show you understand that professionals have feelings and that emotional intelligence matters. Tech companies increasingly value leaders who can navigate the human side of building products.
