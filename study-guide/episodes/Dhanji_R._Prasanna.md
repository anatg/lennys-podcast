## TL;DR
Block's CTO shares how they transformed from a GM-based structure to a functional org focused on technology-first principles, achieving 20-25% manual hours saved across the company through AI tools like their open-source agent Goose. The key to AI adoption isn't just the tools—it's organizational structure, leadership usage, and focusing on small experiments that solve real problems rather than boiling the ocean.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Conway's Law in Practice**: "You ship your org structure"—the structure of relationships between people in an organization directly determines outcomes. Block moved from a GM structure (separate silos for Square, Cash App, Afterpay, TIDAL) to a functional structure (all engineers report to one head, all designers to one head) to enable deeper technical focus and AI adoption.

- **Start Small Philosophy**: "If you try to boil the ocean to make a cup of tea, you'll never get there." Apply this to everything—Goose started as one engineer's side project, Cash App started as a hack week idea, Bitcoin on Cash App was a three-person hackathon team (including Jack Dorsey). Don't try to solve everything at once.

- **Question Base Assumptions**: Before optimizing or automating a process, ask "do we even need to do this at all?" Dhanji's example: InfoSec teams trying to secure something that the building team could just not build, eliminating the security surface area entirely.

- **Technology Serves Purpose, Not Vice Versa**: Code quality and product success have "nothing to do with each other." YouTube succeeded with videos stored as blobs in MySQL and a Python stack, while Google Video had superior architecture but failed. Focus on solving real problems for real people, not technical perfection.

- **Controlled Chaos**: Create a foundation that prevents major liabilities (security, financial risk), then give engineers freedom to experiment and iterate. Don't harness brilliant people into strict blinkered areas—allow some wheel-spinning if they're also delivering amazing things.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Functional org structure unlocks AI adoption at scale**: The shift from GM structure (where teams operated as independent companies) to functional structure (where all engineers report to one leader) was the "key to making the transformation into being more of an AI-native company." It enabled singular focus, shared tools and policies, common technical strategy, and cross-team movement.

2. **AI productivity gains are real but variable by context**: Teams report 8-10 hours saved per week (self-reported), with overall company trending toward 20-25% manual hours saved. But this varies dramatically—greenfield codebases and new platforms see aggressive gains, while complex legacy codebases see much less. "This is the worst it will ever be"—these are baseline numbers that will only improve.

3. **Non-technical teams building software is the biggest surprise**: "The non-technical people using AI agents and programming tools to build things" showed the most impact. Enterprise risk management teams building self-service systems in hours instead of waiting quarters for internal apps teams. This compresses weeks of work into hours and eliminates dependency bottlenecks.

4. **Leadership must use the tools themselves**: "Jack uses Goose, I use Goose, our executive team all have used Goose and use it regularly and we do it every single day." You learn more about workflow changes by feeling and using products yourself than reading think pieces. This is how they drive adoption—lead by example.

5. **The future is autonomous, multi-hour AI work**: Current AI is "highly limiting" ping-pong (you prompt, wait 3-4 minutes, nudge, massage). The future is LLMs working overnight and weekends when humans aren't there, trying multiple experimental approaches simultaneously, and building in anticipation of what humans want. Median Goose session is 5 minutes; they're pushing toward hours-long autonomous work.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Engineers will rewrite from scratch regularly**: Instead of refactoring apps to evolve UI, teams will "rewrite that app from scratch" each release. The question becomes: "What would our world look like if every single release, RM minus RF deleted the entire app and rebuilt it from scratch?" This challenges the traditional "never rewrite" wisdom in software engineering.

- **Agents watch and anticipate work**: One engineer has Goose watching his screen and listening to voice all the time. It anticipates needs—reading Slack conversations about features and opening PRs before he asks, rescheduling meetings when conflicts arise, organizing receipts into Apple Notes without prompting. This shows where AI assistance is heading.

- **Lines blur between technical and non-technical roles**: "The lines are going to be blurred between whether you're in legal or in risk or in engineering and design." The people who "embrace it to optimize for their particular workday and their particular set of tasks" show the most impact, regardless of technical background.

- **Senior and junior engineers adopt most eagerly**: Senior people "understand in great depth how everything works" and are "relieved that this tool exists that can go and do all these things that they've done a million times before." Junior people are "blitzing through" like "my niece and nephew on a BlackBerry... on iPhones now." The middle struggles more with adoption.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate adaptability and learning mindset**: Block looks for people "embracing these tools and that are eager to try and learn"—not necessarily expert AI practitioners from day one. Show you're willing to experiment with new tools and evolve your workflow.

- **Question everything, including the question**: When given a problem to solve, practice asking "do we even need to solve this?" or "is there a simpler approach?" Dhanji's example of InfoSec teams securing things that could just not be built shows systems-level thinking.

- **Show you've built small things that grew**: The best products at Block started small—Goose (one engineer's side project), Cash App (hack week), Bitcoin on Cash App (three-person hackathon). In interviews, emphasize starting with minimal viable experiments rather than comprehensive solutions.

- **Separate code quality from product success**: Be prepared to discuss tradeoffs between technical excellence and shipping value to users. YouTube's success despite "terrible" architecture versus Google Video's failure despite superior tech is a powerful mental model.

- **Focus on human judgment, not just execution**: AI can't yet handle "portfolio judgment or judgment across a global sense of what's important and what matters." Show you understand taste, design anchoring, and strategic prioritization—areas where humans remain essential.
