## TL;DR
Sanchan Saxena shares lessons from building products at Instagram, Airbnb, and Coinbase, emphasizing that successful PMs must master both the "art and science" of product management—with intentionality and vision mattering more than rigid A/B testing. He details how Airbnb survived COVID by moving to two-week planning cycles, cutting costs while maintaining mission belief, and how founder-driven cultures like Brian Chesky's prioritize building ideal experiences first (even "15 out of 10") then iterating, rather than designing by committee or optimizing incrementally.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Little Bets**: Take small, rapid experiments and pivot quickly based on learnings. Career and product development should be about trying things, building rapid recovery mechanisms, and iterating—not five-year plans. Success comes from making many small bets and learning fast.

- **Content vs. Process**: "Content" = knowing what to build and when (the roadmap, the strategy, the actual decisions). "Process" = how you execute (scrum, ceremonies, meetings). Hire for content knowledge first, teach process second. Process people without content knowledge can't scale innovation—they manage meetings instead of building product.

- **Intentionality-First Product Development**: Start with the ideal end-state experience you want to create for customers (Brian Chesky's "15 out of 10 experience"), design that unconstrained vision first, then figure out how to scale it. Don't start with constraints. Example: Build one perfect Airbnb lounge with the best coffee, seats, wifi—then scale the parts that worked. The opposite is building an MVP lounge and A/B testing each feature addition.

- **Art and Science of PM**: Art = intuition, vision, understanding where trends are going by being in the community. Science = data, experimentation, metrics. Both are necessary. Over-reliance on science makes you miss opportunities that aren't yet measurable. "Gut and intuition is data—it's just not statistically significant yet."

- **Rapid Recovery Over Perfect Planning**: Real genius isn't in avoiding all mistakes upfront—it's in how quickly you recover when things go wrong. Build a culture where failure is celebrated as learning. Speed of pivoting matters more than perfection of initial plan.

- **DRI (Directly Responsible Individual)**: Coinbase's decision-making framework borrowed from Amazon. One person (could be from any function—ops, legal, engineering, product) is designated DRI for each project. They collect written input from all cross-functional partners, synthesize it, and make the final call. Others must "disagree and champion" the decision (not just "disagree and commit"). This eliminates passive-aggressive politics and speeds decisions by removing decision-by-committee.

- **Two-Week Planning Cycles in Crisis**: When COVID hit Airbnb, they shifted from annual/quarterly planning to two-week sprints. Every two weeks leadership decided: What's most urgent for *the company* (not individual teams)? Teams and resources flowed to highest priority. Dissolved sub-team boundaries—became one team rowing together.

- **Stories as Vision > Stories as Metrics**: Kevin Systrom's intentionality with Instagram Stories—he wanted "the world's largest real-time TV channel," so he initially prevented users from uploading old photos (against data/user research requests). The intentionality defined the product, data came second. Once vision is proven, you can iterate.

- **Impatience to Start, Patience to Persist**: Brian Chesky's principle: Be impatient about getting started (don't overthink), but have patience to see ideas through. Great ideas need time—don't kill them too early because of one bad A/B test.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Hire leaders who can create content, not just manage process**: The most important thing for startup success isn't which process framework you use—it's whether people know what to build and when. Hire executives who could roll up their sleeves and do the work their team does if needed. If an exec's entire team disappeared, they should still be able to tell you exactly what to build, when, and how (even if at lower capacity). Process becomes the product at big companies—avoid this trap.

2. **Test intentionality, don't substitute A/B tests for vision**: Don't go to your GPS and ask "where should I go?"—you tell it the destination (intentionality), then use it to find the fastest route (A/B tests). Start with conviction about the customer problem and ideal experience, then test paths to get there. A/B testing as the starting point leads to local maxima and incremental thinking. Kevin Systrom didn't A/B test whether to add Stories to Instagram—he shipped it with conviction because it aligned with his vision.

3. **Crisis management requires belief-based thinking**: When COVID crushed Airbnb revenue to single-digit percentages of the prior year, data couldn't predict recovery. Leadership had to shift employees from "feature obsession" and "revenue obsession" to "belief obsession"—rallying around first principles like "humans are genetically wired to travel" and "coronavirus will end." Stories and vulnerability about uncertainty mattered more than false precision. Rapid recovery (two-week cycles) beat long-term planning.

4. **Simplicity is a superpower**: Kevin Systrom's greatest skill was listening to all the noise (users, data, competitors, internal teams) and simplifying strategy into clear, actionable beliefs that the team could execute on. Example: Instagram Stories must be real-time, not old photos—this simplified product direction despite data suggesting otherwise.

5. **The path from Web 2.0 to Web 3.0 goes through Web 2.5**: Don't expect perfect decentralization immediately in crypto/Web3. The journey requires intermediate steps where things are partially centralized. Successful Web3 builders are those who understand the idealistic end state but are pragmatic about building through messy middle stages. Look past today's constraints to see tomorrow's potential (like looking past 1980s internet with $200 dial-up to see Zoom calls).

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate content knowledge over process knowledge**: In interviews, focus on *what* you built and *why*, not just the process you followed. Show you can create strategy, roadmaps, and make decisions—not just facilitate meetings. When asked about past work, emphasize your specific role and decisions you made, not just what your team accomplished.

- **Show intentionality in product thinking**: When given a product case or challenge, start with the ideal customer experience you want to create (unconstrained), then work backward to constraints. Don't immediately jump to "here's my MVP" or "I need more data first." Show vision combined with pragmatic iteration.

- **Prepare for work challenges over presentation formats**: Airbnb changed interviews to give real work problems ("We need millions of Airbnb Plus homes, how would you do it?"). Practice solving open-ended strategic problems on the spot. Depth of thinking matters more than having the "right" answer with perfect data.

- **Tell your career story with 'why' not just 'what'**: Sanchan's favorite interview question: "Tell me the story of your career and focus on why you did what you did." Practice explaining your pivots, choices, and learnings—not just accomplishments. Shows self-awareness and decision-making ability.

- **Demonstrate rapid recovery examples**: Share stories where things went wrong and you pivoted quickly. The ability to recover from failure is more important than avoiding all failures. Show you've built cultures or processes that celebrate learning from failure.

- **Balance data with conviction**: Show you can use data to inform decisions, but don't hide behind data to avoid taking a stance. Demonstrate situations where you had conviction despite incomplete data, and explain your reasoning.
