## TL;DR
Tanguy Crusson shares hard-won lessons from a decade at Atlassian, including the painful shutdown of HipChat/Stride and the eventual success of Jira Product Discovery—emphasizing that large companies must artificially create scarcity and autonomy for new products, validate assumptions early (especially about customer segments), ignore competitive myopia, and protect "ugly baby" products through clear stage gates and relentless internal communication that demonstrates momentum.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Incubate, Iterate, Integrate**: Build detached from the main product, iterate until you get it right, then integrate back. Used by Noah Weiss at Instagram to evolve from chronological feed to algorithm-based feed. Allows teams to break rules and move fast without dragging the entire company into early bets.

- **Point A Innovation Program (Four Stages)**: Atlassian's internal incubator with clear vocabulary for each stage—Wonder (prove there's a problem and market, articulate why Atlassian and why now), Explore (validate solutions with customers), Make (build in phases: alpha, beta, GA), and Impact (product becomes a real business). Teams write a six-pager and present to stakeholders and founders.

- **Safety Funnel**: Put a hard stop to limit the number of people who have bad experiences until the product is proven, rather than maximizing user acquisition early. Protects both users and the product's reputation.

- **Lighthouse Users Program**: Formalized stages with 10 users to prove problem-solution fit, 100 users to test variations and scenarios, 1,000 users to achieve self-service, then graduate. Emphasizes named customers and direct relationships over anonymous metrics.

- **Creating Artificial Scarcity**: When launching new products internally, remind everyone things are going to fail (frame it as "70% chance whatever you're working on won't exist in six months"). This prevents over-investment and emulates startup constraints in a large company environment.

- **70% Code Reuse Principle**: Even failed products can generate value—70% of code written for HipChat is probably still in the Atlassian platform today as the Confluence editor. Great work doesn't disappear, it just needs the right trigger for "why now."

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Large companies must artificially create scarcity**: Startups have the benefit of starving—big companies can throw resources at problems, but this actually slows things down. Creating constraints and reminding teams that failure is the most likely outcome helps emulate startup speed and prevents the company from over-investing in unproven bets.

2. **"What took us here won't take us there"**: Teams see success and believe it's because of X, but X is often not validated. Atlassian's playbook was bottom-up adoption in tech/IT teams, but they assumed it would work for HipChat without validating early enough. Atlassian was going after tech teams while Slack was going after business teams—never underestimate the challenge of convincing a new segment, even if they seem similar.

3. **Competitive myopia is dangerous**: Teams react to whatever competitors are doing and lose what made them successful. You only see the tip of the iceberg—features shipped now are based on research from a year ago. Ignore competition except for watching every three months for big announcements, and disconnect creative and research processes from what competitors do.

4. **Integrations are mostly about people, not technology**: When acquired, startup teams face culture shock—they had full control of their destiny, then lose decision-making power. Big companies look much further into the future (3-year plans vs. 3-6 months), and top-down messages say "keep doing what you're doing" while teams on the ground require processes that interrupt work. Expect a big slowdown after acquisition before things accelerate again.

5. **Success metrics should match product stage**: For a long time at Atlassian, they treated all products equally with the same success metrics like monthly active users. In big companies, a $100 million business is a good start; in most companies, that's a home run. Early-stage products need qualitative metrics (user video snippets, problem-solution stories) rather than quantitative metrics, and you need clear vocabulary for each stage to facilitate conversations and protect teams from chiming in too early.

6. **Never do a rewrite**: Atlassian made the decision to rewrite HipChat which became Stride. There's good literature around this and good reasons not to—failure really sucks, and no one tells you this even though everyone says you learn from failure. Don't eat your own bullshit; be open about things as they are and don't hide the truth.

7. **Internal communication is everything**: Be very clear about what you're testing and use data and personal customer stories to give people a sense of velocity and speed—no one wants to interfere with a high-speed train. Weekly updates showing momentum, demos, and customer snippets build support and protect the "ugly baby." Hiding behind research and formal CSAT surveys distances teams from customers.

8. **Build super siloed teams for new bets**: Super silo teams disconnected from core business are key to success. Hire principal-level engineers with internal credits who can commit to any team's repo without questions. Being in Europe/away from mothership helped because people didn't care enough to stay up late to disagree and stop progress. Teams need to break established rules without breaking trust—rules were created to support the business at the stage where it was successful, but might not work for new bets.

9. **Validate demand before writing code**: Before writing a single line of code, validate demand. Atlassian put an ad in the Jira newsletter and got 3,000 signups in two weeks, proving the problem space was worth pursuing.

10. **Product managers are biased toward action**: Recognize when you're idle waiting for confirmation and step back. Make it clear why an opportunity is perishable—why do we have to do this now—to give people motivation and create urgency around the bet.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Product engineers emerge with user context**: When engineers are exposed to the right user context—knowing 10 customers by name, context, and problems—it builds empathy that drives them to advocate for product changes. This creates "product engineers" who think beyond features and understand customer impact.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Frame failure honestly**: When discussing past product failures in interviews, be radically candid like Tanguy—"failure really sucks" and "my track record has been 50/50 at best." Don't eat your own bullshit. Interviewers respect authenticity over spinning narratives.

- **Validate assumptions about customer segments early**: Be prepared to discuss how you validated (or failed to validate) that a playbook or strategy would work for a new customer segment. Show you understand that segments that seem similar can be fundamentally different.

- **Demonstrate how you protect early-stage work**: Explain your communication strategy for building internal support—how you used customer stories, demos, and velocity metrics to create a "high-speed train" that people didn't want to stop.

- **Show understanding of stage-appropriate metrics**: Articulate why qualitative metrics (named customers, video snippets) matter more than vanity metrics in early stages, and how you transition to quantitative measures as products mature.

- **Use Scott Farquhar's reference check question**: "When I call this person, what do you think they're going to say about that?" This breaks scripted responses and reveals authenticity—consider how you'd answer this about your own references.

- **Treat culture fit seriously**: Don't settle for cynical work environments—you are not impermeable to your surroundings. The environment will change you as a person over time. Show interviewers you're thoughtful about organizational culture and values alignment.
