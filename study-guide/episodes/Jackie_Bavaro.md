## TL;DR
Jackie Bavaro shares her journey from Microsoft intern to Head of Product at Asana, explaining that great strategy connects three components: vision, strategic framework, and roadmap. The key to PM career growth is doing solid execution early on, asking your manager "what should I work on now to be ready when the opportunity comes up," and understanding that being a great IC can be more fulfilling and lucrative than climbing the management ladder.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Three Components of Strategy**: (1) **Vision** — inspiring picture of the future you want to build; (2) **Strategic Framework** — your market, definition of success, and big bets on what it takes to win (your "pillars"); (3) **Roadmap** — working backwards from vision to check feasibility and identify what must start now. The roadmap isn't a commitment; it's a reality check showing if your vision fits your timeframe.

- **PEARL Framework for Behavioral Interviews**: **P**roblem (was it big enough?), **E**piphany (what insight did you have that others didn't?), **A**ction (what did you do and what made it hard?), **R**esult (what happened?), **L**earning (what did you take away, especially from failures?). More nuanced than STAR because it emphasizes the unique insight.

- **Strategy as Connecting the Dots**: Good strategy connects high-level business goals ("increase revenue 50%") down to specific features through intermediate steps. Each "dot" answers "why this" and "what's next." Missing dots appear as confusion when you communicate your strategy.

- **Consistency vs. Comprehensiveness**: Strategic framework borrowed from Google Shopping by Shishir Mehrotra at YouTube. Does your product win by having consistent results every time, or by being comprehensive even if experience varies? Helps frame product decisions about linking to external content.

- **Repeated Disagreements Signal Missing Strategy**: When you and teammates keep fighting over individual features or decisions, it's actually a disagreement about strategy or principles. Write down the strategic framework to resolve at that level rather than battling feature-by-feature.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Early PMs should execute, not strategize**: For your first six months on a product, don't worry about strategy. Focus on customer research, learning the existing strategy, and delivering solid work. This builds trust and earns you the right to shape strategy later. Many early-career PMs fail by trying to reinvent everything immediately.

2. **Saying "no" defensively is anti-collaboration**: Jackie's biggest mistake at Asana was seeing her role as "say no to everybody as efficiently as possible." After her boss called her out, a coach challenged her to "say yes to everything for two weeks"—not saying yes to shipping, but yes to "that's a real problem" or "we could test that." This opened real collaboration.

3. **IC paths can be more lucrative than management**: At senior PM level at top companies, you can make doctor/lawyer money (check levels.fyi). You don't need to become a manager to have impact or income. Management is lonelier, more painful, and requires keeping secrets. Consider it a "two-way door" you can exit.

4. **Ask "what should I work on NOW to be ready LATER"**: Frame career conversations with your manager as future-focused ("I'd love to become X someday—what should I work on now?"). This brings them onto your side as a coach, targets your feedback on what actually matters for promotion, and isn't threatening since it's about the future.

5. **Strategy improves through accumulated experience**: You get better at strategy by (a) doing it repeatedly and seeing results over time (ideally 8+ years on a product to iterate), (b) being in collaborative conversations with experienced leaders, and (c) cross-applying frameworks from other products/domains (like Shishir taking "consistency vs. comprehensiveness" from Shopping to YouTube).

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **For behavioral questions**: Use the PEARL framework—emphasize your unique epiphany/insight, not just actions. Interviewers look for whether you noticed something others didn't.
- **Structure matters more than substance**: Jackie noticed as a Google interviewer that candidates who said "I'll tell you three things: one, two, three" sounded good even when their ideas weren't actually better. Clear structure signals competence.
- **"Design a bathroom" questions aren't about your preferences**: They're testing if you can identify the best customer segment and design for their needs, not what *you* want in a bathroom. Understand what the trick questions are really asking.
- **Recent examples at the right level**: When asked about a proud project, choose something recent that demonstrates the level you're applying for (APM vs. Director answers should be dramatically different in scope/impact).
- **Ask your interviewer-friends for real prep**: Jackie made a point to share her Google interview prep publicly because only telling "friends of friends" from Stanford would increase privilege gaps. Seek out people willing to give honest feedback on your practice answers.
- **Level the playing field**: If someone's willing to help you prep privately, that same information should be available publicly. Look for resources (like Jackie's books) that democratize this knowledge.
