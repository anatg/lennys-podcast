# Melanie Perkins — CEO & Co-founder of Canva

## Guest Profile
- **Name**: Melanie Perkins
- **Role/Company**: CEO & Co-founder of Canva
- **Background**: Started as a university student teaching design software, co-founded Fusion Books (yearbook platform in Australia), then pivoted to create Canva. Built Canva into a $42B+ company with $3.3B+ in annual revenue, profitable for 8 consecutive years, serving 240M+ monthly active users. ## TL;DR
Melanie Perkins shares how Canva succeeded through "Column B thinking"—building from a dream future rather than current constraints—and maintaining crazy big goals tied to clear mission pillars. Despite 100+ investor rejections and a 2-year product freeze during a technical rewrite, Canva's relentless focus on customer feedback (1M+ requests/year, 200+ "loops closed"), authentic execution, and the two-step plan (build a valuable company AND do maximum good) created one of the most successful tech companies in history.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Column A vs Column B Thinking**: Column A = building with the bricks around you (incremental, constraint-based). Column B = imagining the perfect future first, then working backward to create it. Start with "What future do I want to exist?" rather than "What can I do with what I have?" Most planning is Column A; transformative companies require Column B.

- **Chaos to Clarity Process**: Every idea starts in chaos (amorphous, in your head). Progress means adding clarity at each step: write it down → create pitch deck → refine → design → prototype → ship. The first step is "embarrassing" because you lack mastery, but it's essential. Visual communication accelerates this journey.

- **Crazy Big Goals Framework**: Goals should be so ambitious you feel "completely inadequate before them," making you want to work extremely hard. They must be important enough that you'll persist through obstacles. Break down into: Mission → Mission Pillars → Annual Goals → Celebrations. Timing may slip (6 months becomes 2 years), but the compound effect over a decade is transformative.

- **Two-Step Plan**: Step 1: Build one of the world's most valuable companies. Step 2: Do the most good you can do. Originally sequential, now simultaneous—each step fuels the other. Operationalized through: 1% Pledge (time, money, equity, profit), Canva Foundation (30% of founder equity), free products for education (100M users) and nonprofits ($1.5B/year in product value).

- **Closing the Loop**: Systematically collect 1M+ customer requests annually, tally and categorize them, deliver to product teams, ship features, close loops (200+ annually). Each closed loop represents many more silent users with the same need. Complements vision-driven roadmap with user-driven priorities.

- **Wild Success / Terrible Failure Exercise**: For any initiative, articulate both extremes 10 years out. Clarifies what you truly want and don't want. Creates alignment and surfaces assumptions. Applied to company-level, area-level, and project-level planning.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Rejection as Iteration Fuel**: Melanie pitched 100+ investors who said no. Rather than taking it personally, she used each rejection as feedback: "market too small" → added market size slide; "same as competitor X" → added competitive landscape slide. The rejection loop strengthened the pitch and clarified the vision. Her 2012 pitch deck remains valid today because it was battle-tested through extensive feedback.

2. **Celebrate What You Focus On**: Couple crazy big goals with memorable celebrations (smashing plates, releasing doves, La Tomatina festivals). This creates moments of recognition, prevents burnout from "always climbing the mountain," and motivates teams to push hard toward milestones. What you celebrate signals what matters.

3. **The First Step Can Be Embarrassingly Small**: The gap between a crazy big vision and the first tiny step feels embarrassing—you have "none of the answers." But if that microscopic step works toward the future you want, it's the right move. Progress compounds over decades. Better to solve a small number of people's problem really well than many people's problem poorly.

4. **Systems Break at Every Doubling**: When the company doubles, all systems fail—meetings that worked, communication patterns, goal-setting processes. Don't copy another company's "bricks" (systems); design what's authentic to your culture at the new scale. Canva evolved from daily standups → weekly → monthly → season openers → new formats as they grew to avoid 6-hour meetings.

5. **Problem-First, Not Solution-First**: Melanie initially led with "here's the cool solution" and investors didn't get it. She learned to articulate the problem first—if people don't understand the pain point, they can't care about your solution. Map problems to larger markets. Look for gaps where people have real pain that hasn't been solved, not solutions seeking problems.

6. **Balance Column B Vision with Column A Steps**: Have a "ladder to the moon" with clear rungs. Vision boards (like Melanie's 2050 wall) are powerful, but you need incremental milestones. The mission pillars (empower everyone to design anything, with every ingredient, in every language, on every device) broke Canva's abstract mission into concrete expansion vectors.

7. **Protect Headspace to Think**: Melanie has no email on her phone—when laptop closes, she's "all out." Uses walks with voice notes (dictated into Apple Notes/Canva Docs) to process thoughts away from Slack/email noise. Healthy boundaries enable stepping back to see "forest from the trees" and focus on what truly matters versus just working harder on the wrong thing.

8. **User Testing Reveals Universal Truths**: Watch 10 random people use your product—their hesitations and confusions represent thousands more. Melanie personally ran hundreds/thousands of user tests. Use tools like UserTesting.com for frank, unfiltered feedback. Remote testing gets more honesty than in-person observation.

9. **The Two-Year Tunnel Was Worth It**: Canva spent 2 years rewriting their front-end (expected: 6 months), unable to ship new features. Existentially hard for a product company. But it enabled right-to-left languages, 5 → thousands of simultaneous collaborators, cross-platform consistency, and scaling to 2,500+ engineers. Sometimes the tunnel is as long as it needs to be.

10. **Purpose Drives Persistence**: Canva's two-step plan gives work deeper meaning than "get rich." Donated $50M to GiveDirectly (direct cash to extreme poverty in Malawi), committing another $100M over 4 years. Vision: everyone's basic human needs met by 2050. Purpose fuels persistence through 100+ rejections and 2-year product freezes. Loneliness and lack of purpose are societal crises—working toward something bigger than yourself is the answer.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI as Enabler of Core Mission**: Canva's mission is empowering everyone to design by minimizing friction between idea and design. AI naturally fits this: generate presentations, videos, emails, websites in the design tab (used 170M times/month); generate elements (photos, videos, code) in elements tab (used 900M times/month); collaborate via @canva comments that understand design context.

- **AI Before AI Was Cool**: Looking at old 2012 Canva decks, they were "trying to do AI before AI was actually a thing" because it was critical to their vision. The technology finally caught up to the original ambition.

- **Integration Over Innovation Theater**: Don't add AI because "investors like AI"—integrate where it genuinely helps customers achieve goals. Listen to community feedback on what's working. AI should fit your product's existing workflow, not be bolted on.

- **Personal AI Use Cases**: Melanie uses AI for: (1) Exploring initial ideas—first place to go when brainstorming; (2) "AI walks"—voice dictation during walks to brain dump, then summarize in Canva Docs for clarity and action items; (3) Native Canva features like @canva for in-context collaboration.

- **Advice for Founders**: Focus on where AI genuinely reduces friction for your users' core workflow. If it doesn't help them achieve their goals faster/better, it's not worth front-and-centering regardless of hype cycle.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **When Pitching Vision**: Lead with the problem, not the solution. Investors/interviewers need to understand the pain point before they can appreciate your approach. Practice articulating: (1) the problem/gap, (2) why current solutions fail, (3) why your approach uniquely solves it.

- **Handling Rejection in Interviews**: Frame rejection as iteration fuel. If you don't get an offer, ask for specific feedback and incorporate it into your next story/pitch. Show growth mindset: "I took that feedback and here's what I learned/changed."

- **Demonstrate Column B Thinking**: Show you can think from desired future state, not just today's constraints. Example: "Here's where I want the product to be in 5 years, and here's the first small step we could take to get there." Interviewers want to see both big vision and pragmatic execution.

- **Storytelling Structure—Chaos to Clarity**: When describing a project, use this arc: started in chaos (ambiguous problem/idea) → added clarity iteratively (research, prototypes, tests) → shipped clear solution. Shows process thinking and comfort with ambiguity.

- **Closing the Loop on Feedback**: Describe a system where you collected user feedback at scale, categorized it, acted on it, and measured impact. Quantify: X requests → Y features shipped → Z satisfaction increase. Shows you value community input and can operationalize it.

- **Crazy Big Goals with Realistic Milestones**: Articulate both the audacious 10-year vision AND the next 3-month milestone. Show you can balance ambition with pragmatism. Example: "Long-term we want to empower everyone to design. Near-term, we're focusing on social media creators because..."

- **Culture Fit with Values**: For companies with strong mission/values like Canva's "Crazy Big Goals," show how your personal values align. Reference specific initiatives (e.g., two-step plan, 1% pledge) and how that resonates with your own approach to impact.

- **Resilience Stories**: Have a "two-year tunnel" equivalent—a time you persisted through a long, hard period with unclear end date. Emphasize: (1) what kept you going, (2) how you maintained team morale, (3) why it was worth it in retrospect.
