## TL;DR
Megan Cook shares how Atlassian maintains Jira's dominance through customer obsession, intentional remote work practices, and a culture of psychological safety built through rituals like "Fight Club" meetings and peer feedback groups. She reveals Atlassian's stage-gated process (wonder, explore, make, impact, scale) for launching 15+ product lines and emphasizes getting early buy-in by partnering with stakeholders throughout development rather than presenting polished proposals.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Fight Club Meetings**: 30-minute weekly standing meeting with engineering and design leaders specifically for having difficult conversations and resolving conflicts early. Creates psychological safety by normalizing disagreement in a structured setting before issues escalate.

- **Peer Feedback Groups**: Divide larger PM teams (15+) into smaller groups that meet every two weeks. Members bring rough drafts for review, building comfort with vulnerability and creating multiple feedback loops before formal reviews.

- **The $10 Priority Game**: Allocate $10 across all priorities to visualize time allocation. Reveals misalignment ("10 cents this week = 20 minutes") and forces honest conversation about what truly moves the business forward.

- **Wonder, Explore, Make, Impact, Scale**: Atlassian's stage-gated process for new products. Start tiny (wonder = one person with idea), add small teams at each gate (explore = 3 people, make = 12 people), validate before scaling. Each stage requires proof before additional investment.

- **Buy-in as Journey, Not Event**: Reframe buy-in from "one perfect presentation" to ongoing partnership. Loop in stakeholders early when there's clarity but not full polish, incorporate their concerns, create advocates before the final decision meeting.

- **Show Don't Tell**: Per head of design Charlie Sutton—use visuals, customer videos, and prototypes to make problems and solutions visceral. Emotional connection drives buy-in more than abstract arguments.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The opposite of play is fear, not work**: When teams show overly polished work, avoid speaking up, or become incremental in ideas, it signals fear is blocking the flow state needed for innovation. Psychological safety enables the play mindset that drives breakthrough thinking.

2. **Remote connection boosts last months, not days**: Atlassian research shows that intentional in-person gatherings boost connection and productivity by ~30%, but the effect lasts months. Meeting 3-4 times per year is sufficient; they gather PM teams every 6 months and full product teams (eng, design, PM) every other 6 months.

3. **Block deep work synchronously across leadership**: Megan's leadership team syncs calendars to create 3-4 hour deep work blocks twice weekly at the same time. This protects creative time while ensuring flex capacity when unexpected high-priority work emerges.

4. **Status updates are not meetings**: In remote environments, meeting time becomes precious. If it's just a status update, make it asynchronous (they use Atlas). Reserve synchronous time exclusively for problem-solving and decision-making.

5. **Start small to prove big bets**: For the CSAT improvement initiative, starting with ~40 people and shipping small wins created momentum and excitement. Small teams move faster and prove hypotheses before requesting major investment—whether launching new products or fixing core issues.

6. **Expose your hypotheses, not just your facts**: Distinguish clearly between what you know (facts/data) and what you believe will happen (hypotheses). This builds credibility and invites stakeholders to contribute knowledge that strengthens your thinking rather than feeling like you're forcing a predetermined solution.

7. **Low-cost shepherds enable cross-team work**: When dependent teams can't prioritize your work, ask them to assign a "shepherd"—someone who reviews your team's work in their codebase without bearing full development cost. This unlocked the CSAT improvements that required changes across multiple platform teams.

8. **Flexibility follows productivity principles, not location**: Atlassian's "anywhere" policy focuses on *how* to be productive remotely (intentional connection, deep work time, documentation) rather than *where* people work. This makes flexibility human-centered rather than a perk.

9. **Innovation needs different standards**: Seed products can't be held to the same bar as mature products (resilience, scale). Protect small teams from quarterly planning and business review processes; give them freedom to move fast and solve problems differently.

10. **Ask "Where could this go in 5 years?"**: Megan's biggest failure was shipping automation that worked well but thinking too small—years later Atlassian acquired a company doing advanced automation across all products. Always push ideas further: Can this 10X? Apply more broadly? Serve more users?

## AI & The Changing PM Role

Atlassian carved off a small dedicated team to explore generative AI opportunities following ChatGPT's emergence, treating it as foundational technology requiring focused exploration. The company maintains a long-standing central AI team but recognized the "huge leap forward" required fresh investigation into applications across products.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Show vulnerability strategically**: Megan's example of distinguishing facts from hypotheses demonstrates strength, not weakness. In interviews, acknowledge what you don't know while showing clear thinking about how you'd learn or validate assumptions.

- **Focus on the problem, not your solution**: When discussing past work, emphasize how you stayed anchored to customer value rather than falling in love with a specific approach. Show you can pivot when new information emerges.

- **Demonstrate partnership in buy-in stories**: Don't describe stakeholder management as "convincing" people. Frame it as "How did you create partners who became advocates?" Show you loop people in early and incorporate diverse perspectives.

- **Quantify remote work effectiveness**: If you've worked remotely, be specific about rituals that made you effective (deep work blocks, async documentation, intentional connection). Remote isn't just "worked from home"—it's mastering new productivity principles.

- **Tell failure stories that show growth**: Follow Megan's structure: what happened, what you missed/learned, how it changed your approach going forward. The automation story shows self-awareness and strategic thinking evolution.

- **Use "show don't tell" in case studies**: When discussing projects, mention if you used prototypes, customer videos, or data visualizations to build buy-in. This shows sophistication in influence tactics.

- **Size psychological safety initiatives**: Megan's examples (peer feedback groups at 15+ people, Fight Club for leadership trio) show awareness of when team dynamics need intervention and how to scale solutions.
