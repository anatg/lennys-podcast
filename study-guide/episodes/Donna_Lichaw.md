## TL;DR
Donna Lichaw shares her story-driven leadership approach, explaining how the stories we tell ourselves shape our effectiveness as leaders. She provides practical frameworks for identifying your superpowers and kryptonite, reframing imposter syndrome as functional, and using product thinking (experiments, data-driven approaches) to unlock personal growth and career advancement.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Story-Driven Leadership**: Leadership isn't about telling stories to others—it's about understanding the stories you tell yourself. These internal narratives (true or not) drive your actions and identity. Use data (talk to colleagues, get feedback) to validate or debunk limiting stories like "I'm too nice" or "people don't listen to me."

- **Concentric Circles of Leadership (Ken Blanchard model)**: Start with you at the center, then expand outward: lead yourself → lead one-on-one relationships → lead teams → lead the business. You can't effectively lead others without first leading yourself from the inside out.

- **Head, Heart, Hands Filter**: When running experiments or trying new behaviors, evaluate through three lenses: (1) Head—what are your thoughts? (2) Heart—what emotions arise? (3) Hands/Body—what physical sensations do you notice? Your body stores experiences and tells you what action to take next.

- **Superpowers from Peak Experiences**: Extract your strengths by examining 3+ peak experiences from your life (childhood through present). Look for patterns—what capabilities showed up repeatedly when you were at your best and most energized? These reveal your core superpowers.

- **Kryptonite as Functional**: What you think harms you often serves a purpose. Ask "How does this serve me?" before trying to fix it. Example: Imposter syndrome can drive you to learn and grow (functional), but overdone it leads to burnout (detrimental). Find the right dosage on the spectrum.

- **Envision the Ending First**: To discover hidden goals, start by imagining your desired future in vivid sensory detail (what you see, hear, feel, smell). Then work backward to create the journey. This creates fuel for action and helps clarify purpose.

- **Small Experiments → Iterate**: Apply product thinking to personal growth. Run tiny experiments first (even 30-second role plays), gather data on how they feel, then scale up gradually. Don't force big changes all at once.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **The most powerful stories are the ones we tell ourselves**: Your brain doesn't distinguish between stories that are true and those that aren't. Stories like "I'm an imposter" or "no one listens to me" become your reality. Use research (talk to your team, gather feedback) to test whether your internal narrative matches external reality.

2. **Imposter syndrome is functional when used correctly**: Instead of denying it, ask "How does this serve me?" Often it triggers learning, growth, and hard work. The problem is over-indexing—doing 10x the work, taking on others' emotional labor, or burning out. Find the balance between functional discomfort and destructive overdrive.

3. **Superpowers come from your stories, not from tests**: While StrengthsFinder-type assessments can help, the most powerful approach is mining your own peak experiences. Look at 3+ moments when you were at your best (including childhood), then identify the patterns—these recurring capabilities are your superpowers.

4. **You can achieve the same goals through different strengths**: There's no single template for great leadership. You can be an amazing CEO as a quiet listener or as a loud visionary. The key is identifying your natural strengths and building your path around them, not forcing yourself into someone else's model.

5. **Energy management reveals what works**: Pay attention to what gives you energy versus what drains it. Activities that align with your superpowers energize you; those that don't, deplete you. If most of your day is energy-draining and you can't change it through small adjustments, you may be in the wrong role or context.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Use product frameworks for self-awareness**: Just as you'd research users and run experiments in product work, apply the same rigor to understanding yourself. Talk to colleagues (your "customers"), gather data on how you're perceived, run small behavioral experiments, and iterate based on what you learn.

- **Prepare stories of peak experiences**: In interviews, have 2-3 stories ready from different life stages where you were at your best. These demonstrate your core strengths better than generic claims like "I'm strategic" or "I'm a problem solver." Show the pattern across your life.

- **Reframe perceived weaknesses**: If asked about weaknesses, use the kryptonite framework. Acknowledge the trait, explain how it's served you in the right doses, and describe how you manage it to avoid the extremes. This shows self-awareness and maturity.

- **Communicate your work style proactively**: Like the executive who was "too quiet," clarify your processing style to interviewers. "I'm a deep listener who needs time to process before responding" is much better than appearing checked out. A personal README or OS can prevent misunderstandings.

- **Show you can lead yourself first**: Demonstrate that you understand your own drivers, energy sources, and blindspots. Leaders who can't lead themselves can't effectively lead others. Share examples of how you've adapted your approach based on self-knowledge.

- **Ask about the ending**: In interviews, try asking "If we're having coffee three years from now celebrating success, what would we be celebrating?" This reveals culture, expectations, and whether the role aligns with your vision—and shows you think strategically about outcomes.
