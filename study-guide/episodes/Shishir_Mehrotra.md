## TL;DR
Shishir Mehrotra shares two powerful frameworks that shaped YouTube's success and now guide Coda: Black Loops and Blue Loops (growth through sharing vs. publishing), and Eigenquestions (finding the one question that answers all subsequent questions, like "consistency vs. comprehensiveness"). He's also writing a book on Rituals of Great Teams and reveals his PSHE framework for evaluating product talent, emphasizing that reference checks trump all other interview signals.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Black Loops vs. Blue Loops**: Growth ecosystems have two fundamental patterns. Black Loop = viral sharing within teams (share → create → share). Blue Loop = publishing to the world for discovery (create → publish → discover → copy). Black Loop is the Microsoft model (documents spread through sharing). Blue Loop is the YouTube model (content spreads through publishing). Understanding which loop drives your product determines pricing, features, and go-to-market strategy.

- **Eigenquestions**: The question that when answered also answers the most subsequent questions. Named after eigenvectors in linear algebra. Don't rank decisions by importance or order—rank by leverage. Example: YouTube's "consistency vs. comprehensiveness" eigenquestion answered whether to link out to competitors, own the iPhone app, and dozens of other strategic choices. Practice in low-stakes scenarios (like the teleportation device interview question) to build the skill.

- **PSHE Framework (Problem-Solution-How-Execution)**: Evaluates talent on a spectrum from junior (given problem, solution, how → just execute) to senior (identifies problems themselves). The "trough of disillusionment" happens mid-career when evaluation shifts from scope to PSHE depth. Works across all roles, not just product. Best assessed through reference checks, not interviews.

- **Bing's Three Rules of Golden Rituals**: Great companies have very few golden rituals, and they must be: (1) Named, (2) Known by every employee by their first Friday, and (3) Templated. Rituals are mirrors of culture—they reveal what a company truly values.

- **Switch Framework (Direct the Rider, Motivate the Elephant, Shape the Path)**: From Chip and Dan Heath's book. To change behavior: direct the rider (tell people what to do), motivate the elephant (create emotional buy-in, branding), shape the path (make it easy through templates and structure). Maps perfectly to implementing organizational rituals.

- **Maker Billing**: Coda only charges for people who create documents, not editors or viewers. Removes friction from the share edge (Black Loop). Pricing model should align with growth loops—never put "dollar signs in the share dial."

- **Home Court, Away Court, Neutral Court Interviewing**: Balance interview questions across three types. Home court (your company/product) = risky, candidate can't know as much as you. Away court (their experience) = hard to verify. Neutral court (hypothetical scenarios) = most fair and revealing. Most interviews should be neutral court.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Draw your business as loops, not funnels**: Almost all successful products grow through loops (where output feeds back as input), not linear funnels. Understanding your loops changes everything from pricing to team structure to product decisions. The Blue Loop (publishing) required invention at Coda; the Black Loop (sharing) existed in all competitors.

2. **Reference checks are your most important signal**: Stack rank interview signals: reference checks first, real work exercises second, then all other interviews. People who worked with a candidate for years know infinitely more than you'll learn in 30 minutes of artificial scenarios. Do them early if possible to avoid wasting everyone's time.

3. **The best reference check questions avoid asking for judgments directly**: Don't ask "Is this person good?" Ask comparative questions: "When you think of your team, who regularly identifies which problems to focus on?" Give people outs with contrast options. Ask "What questions should I have asked that I didn't?" Get them talking—the details reveal truth.

4. **Most people confuse execution excellence with strategic leadership**: Someone who "runs great meetings" (H-level) isn't necessarily someone who "identifies the right problems" (P-level) or "invents creative solutions" (S-level). Both are valuable, but they're different skills at different career stages. PSHE helps distinguish them.

5. **Practice high-stakes skills in low-stakes environments**: Eigenquestions are hard to learn in real strategic decisions. Practice with frivolous scenarios (teleportation device, gas station placement, family games). Kids are naturally good at finding eigenquestions—adults train ourselves out of it.

6. **Culture is the product you build for employees**: Rituals are how culture manifests. When people describe a company's culture, they actually describe rituals. Collecting rituals from 1,000+ people across companies reveals that (a) everyone loves sharing their rituals, (b) the tiny details matter enormously, and (c) rituals can spread if properly named, taught, and templated.

7. **The highest leverage decisions eliminate other decisions**: Don't just prioritize by importance—prioritize by which decision makes other decisions easy or unnecessary. YouTube's consistency-over-comprehensiveness decision instantly resolved dozens of contentious debates.

8. **Platform thinking requires accepting you don't control the narrative**: Unlike Apple's locked-down approach, platforms (YouTube, Coda, Airbnb) assume your connection to end users is through someone else. Your community/creators/hosts control the message. This fundamentally changes product, pricing, and growth strategy.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Master the art of reference checking**: Ask comparative questions ("Who on the team is best at X?") rather than direct judgments. Create safe outs for the reference to be honest. Push for specifics: "What questions should I have asked?" Reference checks reveal PSHE level better than any interview.

- **Practice eigenquestion thinking in daily life**: Train yourself with low-stakes scenarios—restaurant choices, family decisions, random products. Ask your kids eigenquestions (they're naturally good at this). Build the muscle before high-stakes strategic decisions.

- **Balance your interview questions across home/away/neutral court**: Don't ask candidates to solve your actual problems (home court)—they'll never know enough. Don't just ask about their past (away court)—hard to verify. Use neutral scenarios (teleportation device, dashboard design for unfamiliar product) to see problem-solving in real-time.

- **In interviews, demonstrate PSHE at your level**: Junior roles: show execution excellence. Mid-career: show how you determine "how" (milestones, org, rituals). Senior: show you identify problems, not just solve given ones. Be explicit about which level you operated at in past roles.

- **Give yourself the "brag session"**: When interviewing, use presentation time to upper-bound yourself. Don't leave saying "I wish they'd learned X about me." Control your narrative—most interview questions are about lower-bounding (how bad could you be), so create space to show how good you are.

- **Explain your loops, not just your metrics**: When discussing past products, describe growth loops, not funnels. Show you understand second-order effects: this feature drives this behavior which creates this loop. Platform thinkers recognize they don't fully control their user experience.
