## TL;DR
Paige Costello shares how Asana develops product using the Double Diamond framework, plans in rolling 12-month cycles every six months, and organizes around pillars, areas, and teams with nested metrics. She emphasizes building trust through deep customer insight ("know thy customer, market, competitors, numbers, product"), leading by example through exposure and repetition, and staying curious rather than needing to have all the answers—especially as a younger leader among more experienced peers.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Double Diamond Process**: Asana's product development follows a pattern of going broad (diverge) then narrow (converge) twice: first on *who* to solve for (customer selection), then on *what* problem to solve, then broad on *how* (solution concepts), then narrow to final spec. Each phase maps to specific review artifacts (kickoff → customer/direction selection → design concept review → product spec → full experience review → launch). Forces teams to be systematic and curious rather than opinion-driven.

- **Three Es of Growth (Experience, Exposure, Education)**: Career growth comes from direct experience doing the work, exposure to observing others (being in the room even when not driving), and formal education/training. Most early-career people over-index on education; exposure is underrated—being present and analytical in meetings you're not leading accelerates learning dramatically.

- **Trust Equation (Anne Raimondi)**: Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Authenticity) / Perception of Self-Interest. Credibility comes from bringing insight; reliability from say-do ratio; authenticity from vulnerability; minimize perception that you're serving yourself rather than the mission.

- **Above/Below the Line (Conscious Leadership)**: Self-awareness tool from *The 15 Commitments of a Conscious Leader*. Above the line = committed to learning, open, curious, playful. Below the line = committed to being right/winning, black-and-white thinking. Recognizing when you or others are "below the line" helps navigate difficult conversations and reject false trade-offs.

- **"How Might the Opposite Be True?"**: Mental model to escape scarcity mindset and false binaries. When feeling overwhelmed by conflicting priorities, asking this question creates space to see alternative paths (e.g., "I can't do both X and Y" → "How might I actually do both?").

- **Think Big, Ship Small**: Strategic principle to avoid incremental thinking while maintaining iterative execution. Don't let the need to ship small chunks cause you to *think* small—keep the ambitious vision while breaking delivery into manageable pieces.

- **Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI) Feedback**: Intuit's structured feedback framework. State the specific situation (when/where), describe the observable behavior (what you saw/heard), share the impact (how it affected you or outcomes). Frames feedback as subjective observation rather than objective judgment, making it safer to deliver and receive.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Bring the insight to build trust**: When you're younger or less experienced than others in the room, establish credibility by being the person who deeply knows the customer, market, competitors, numbers, and product. Watch customers use your product firsthand. This creates trust faster than pretending to have experience you don't have.

2. **Planning altitude and time horizons matter at scale**: Asana evolved from annual planning around features/locations to six-month rolling 12-month plans focused on problems and target customers, organized in nested layers (R&D → pillars → areas → teams, each with clear metrics). This structure provides high confidence in the immediate half, lower confidence in the next half, and enables faster pivots while reducing churn.

3. **Lead by teaching through repetition, not just instruction**: People learn through repetition and observation. Run your meetings the way you want others to run meetings. Model the behavior (starting with clear agendas, managing conversation courteously, being present) rather than just telling people how to do it. Learning happens through exposure when people are analytically present in rooms where decisions are made.

4. **"Always answer the question they should have asked"**: High-stakes leadership advice Paige received at Intuit. Don't just respond one-to-one to questions asked. Think at a higher altitude about the more important point, the bigger picture, or the alternative the questioner might not have considered. This shifts conversations from performance to strategy.

5. **Stay curious, not confident**: Early-career PMs often feel pressure to be all-knowing and confident, which pushes them toward advocacy instead of inquiry. This leads to building in dark rooms and presenting finished work defensively. Real strength comes from collaborative curiosity—bringing people along, asking questions, being open to feedback while distinguishing between "must do," "should do," and "should consider."

6. **Don't self-select out of opportunities**: Many people, especially early in their careers, don't apply for roles or opportunities because they think they lack some qualification. Don't eliminate yourself—let others make that decision. This applies to stretch projects, promotions, and career moves.

7. **Evaluate roles by learning curve, environment, and problem—not titles**: Instead of planning career by specific roles or companies, assess whether: (1) your learning curve is steep enough, (2) your environment (staffing, tools, people, advocates) sets you up for success, and (3) the problem is interesting to you. Passions are made, not found—try things and see what becomes engaging.

8. **Limit reviews and approvals to increase agency**: Asana reduced bureaucracy by capping: max 3 reviewers per piece of work (only 1 blocking approval), and max 10 people per meeting (or explicitly kick people out and write better decision notes). This dramatically increased pace and quality by giving working teams more agency.

9. **AI exploration needs dedicated fast-cycle teams**: When LLMs emerged, Asana staffed a team to prototype quickly *outside* the normal Double Diamond process—skipping straight to testing hypotheses. This rapid learning phase identified what worked surprisingly well vs. what sounded good in theory, then handed proven concepts to domain expert teams to scale.

10. **Be real about what's incomplete or wrong**: Optimistic leaders can accidentally lose trust if teams think they're not acknowledging problems. Paige learned to explicitly state when work is "70% done" and name the 30% that's missing or uncertain. Being authentic about gaps builds more trust than forced positivity.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Rapid prototyping over traditional discovery**: For AI/LLM features, Asana created dedicated teams that skipped the typical Double Diamond process to prototype immediately. This "try it before you theorize" approach revealed capabilities that would never have been prioritized under normal planning because teams underestimated what was possible.

- **Domain expertise still owns delivery**: After the AI exploration team validated concepts through dogfooding, they handed them to teams with deep customer problem expertise (e.g., status reporting team) to refine and scale. The pattern: centralized rapid experimentation → decentralized domain-specific implementation.

- **AI as work reduction, not feature addition**: Asana's ML team has long focused on reducing work (test prioritization, notification prioritization) rather than adding features. LLMs accelerate this—the focus is on making the product require less effort from users to get value.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate customer insight, not just experience**: In interviews, show you've watched customers use products, understand their workarounds, and can articulate problems from their perspective. This builds credibility even when you lack specific domain experience.

- **Use structured frameworks to show thinking**: Reference frameworks like Double Diamond, SBI feedback, or the trust equation to demonstrate systematic product thinking. Shows you can scale your approach beyond opinion-based decisions.

- **Tell failure stories that reveal learning**: When asked "tell me about a time something went wrong," focus on your mindset, how you evaluated the situation, and what you changed—not just what happened. Paige asks this question because it reveals how candidates think when things aren't working.

- **Show collaborative curiosity over defensive advocacy**: In case studies or product discussions, ask clarifying questions, distinguish between "must do / should do / should consider" feedback, and demonstrate openness to alternative solutions. This signals you'll work well cross-functionally.

- **Have specific examples of building trust**: Prepare stories about building credibility with more senior stakeholders, especially through bringing data/insight they didn't have. Younger PMs should expect questions about gaining trust from experienced colleagues.
