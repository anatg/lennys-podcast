## TL;DR
Barbra is the canonical voice on B2B SaaS category creation — when to pursue it, when to abandon it, and the mechanics of executing it. Her Miro experience (creating "visual collaboration") and Greenhouse experience (failed attempt to create "recruiting optimization platform") give rare before-and-after insight into what makes category creation work or fail. Her advice on rebranding and opinionated software rounds out a comprehensive GTM playbook for startup marketers.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **What is a Category**: A set of products solving a specific pain point (or set of pain points) that buyers have budget allocated for. Categories appear on G2 Crowd, Software Advice, Gartner — they're the frame through which buyers organize purchasing decisions. A category can be broad (all-in-one) or narrow (best-in-class). The key signal: customers use the category language to describe their pain even before they've seen your product.

- **When to Create a New Category**: Category creation makes sense when:
  1. **Existing category is too small**: Miro wanted to be more than "online whiteboard" — a category used by product/engineering teams. "Visual collaboration" expanded the TAM to every team in every organization.
  2. **Existing category carries negative connotations**: Greenhouse didn't want to be ATS (applicant tracking system) because ATS was synonymous with bad UX and transactional, non-strategic software.
  3. **Tech inflection points create new needs**: COVID and distributed work created "employment platforms" (Deel, Oyster) and new HR tech categories. Category creation follows macro shifts, not company decisions.
  4. **Product is genuinely disruptive**: When customers describe the product in wildly different ways (mind mapping, diagramming, whiteboard, project management), that's a signal the product is creating something new that needs naming.

- **When NOT to Create a New Category**:
  1. **Customers keep using existing language**: Greenhouse tried "recruiting optimization platform." Press picked it up. But every customer, even after seeing the differentiation, still called Greenhouse an ATS. The budget line was "ATS." The words they searched were ATS. They abandoned the new category and instead *elevated the value of the ATS category* — becoming the premium, strategic ATS rather than a new thing.
  2. **Already established**: Category creation doesn't generate new business at scale for a mature company; the marketing investment doesn't flow back to growth meaningfully.
  3. **Not sufficiently differentiated**: If you really are similar to existing players, claiming a new category just confuses buyers.

- **The Middle Ground Strategy**: You don't have to choose between "we're in this existing category" OR "we're creating a new category." The practical approach:
  - Sell into the existing category (that's where budget exists and where buyers search)
  - Simultaneously build the new category narrative through thought leadership, analyst relations, and content
  - Let the category narrative mature as the market catches up
  Greenhouse sold as an ATS while building thought leadership around structured recruiting. Miro sold as an online whiteboard while positioning toward visual collaboration.

- **Category Creation Process (Mechanics)**:
  1. **Deep customer listening**: The best positioning and category names come directly from how customers describe their own pain and how they describe what your product does
  2. **PR and thought leadership**: Need media coverage to establish the category narrative; if journalists aren't writing about it, the category doesn't exist in buyers' minds
  3. **Analyst and directory relationships**: G2, Gartner, Forrester, Software Advice — they shape buyer thinking in B2B. Need to actively work with them to create the category listing, explain differentiation from adjacent categories, and get companies listed in the right bucket
  4. **Content marketing**: When creating a new category, you're also educating buyers that a budget line should exist for this. Heavy content investment required.
  5. **Validate by watching competitors arrive**: You haven't created a category until multiple companies are claiming to be in it. Competitive entries validate, not threaten, the category.

- **Rebranding — When and How**:
  - RealtimeBoard → Miro: Name was too literal and too small for the product's potential. "RealtimeBoard" implied one specific feature. The product was an ever-expanding universe of use cases. Better to rebrand before too much brand equity is built in the old name (before scale, after product-market fit).
  - Barbra almost didn't join Miro because of the name — her joining criterion was that leadership would be open to a rebrand.
  - **Process**: Rebrand as a product development process, not a marketing exercise. Sprint teams, agile, owners for each component (naming, logo, website, product UI, sales decks, event collateral, domain), all reporting back. Gather customer feedback throughout — not to slow down, but to bring early advocates along.
  - **Continuity nod**: Kept the yellow color from RealtimeBoard's Post-it logo → yellow M in the new Miro logo. Early customers recognized the continuity.
  - **Domain logistics**: Miro.com was secured 48 hours before the public launch at South by Southwest. All physical booth materials had already been printed with the new URL. High stress, but some blind faith required.
  - **Company-wide involvement**: At 100 people, the entire company had feelings about the rebrand. Critical to be transparent, involve everyone, and make them feel part of the outcome — not just announce the result.

- **Brand as a System**: Brand is not just logo + colors. It's a system:
  - Logo, colors, fonts
  - Photography vs. illustration decisions
  - Brand voice (how the brand sounds in writing, marketing, customer conversations)
  - Visual language that encodes values (Miro: each letter M-I-R-O had a different shape representing a different company value — the "I" was wiggly for Agility)
  - All should compose into a coherent system that scales across every touchpoint without requiring constant top-down enforcement

- **Brand = Interchangeable with Company Values**: The strongest brands are inseparable from the people who work there and the values they embody. Values should be:
  - Simple enough to remember (not 8 long principles)
  - Specific enough to be non-generic ("agility" is better than "we work hard")
  - Embodied in behavior and product decisions, not just wall posters
  Values process: leadership brain dump → group into themes → vote on short list → one skilled writer drafts definitions → group feedback.

- **Love Mark vs. Trademark**: Great brands create emotional connection, not just recognition. Miro's goal was to be a "love mark" — a brand that users feel genuinely committed to and invested in, not just one they recognize. Authenticity is the prerequisite: the brand has to reflect who the company actually is, not who it aspires to brand itself as.

- **Opinionated Software**: Software that builds best practices or rules directly into the product experience, rather than offering unlimited flexibility. Two paths:
  1. **Encode an emerging best practice**: Atlassian built for agile workflows that were already gaining traction but lacked software support. Greenhouse introduced "structured recruiting" — defined interview kits, pipeline stages, scorecards — as the enforced way to recruit.
  2. **Teach a better way**: When there's a demonstrably better method and most companies don't know it (or don't practice it), opinionated software can serve as the teacher. Pando: current performance review processes are full of bias and systemic problems; Pando enforces transparency (documented levels, competencies, evaluation criteria) rather than leaving it to discretion.
  Trade-off: opinionated software is harder to sell because buyers need to believe in the approach, not just the features. But once they believe, the product creates deeper behavioral change and retention.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **You know category creation has failed when customers keep using the old words.** Greenhouse spent significant marketing resources on "recruiting optimization platform." Journalists mentioned it. But every buyer, every time, called it an ATS. The budget line said ATS. That was the signal to stop fighting and instead own the category more powerfully.

2. **You're not building a category until competitors arrive.** Category creation isn't successful when you declare it — it's successful when other companies start claiming to be in the same category. Competitor entries validate the category; they don't threaten you if you're first.

3. **Category creation is a long tail strategy, not a launch event.** Build the new category narrative in parallel with selling into the existing one. Don't force buyers to choose between a budget line they have and a new concept they don't yet have budget for.

4. **Rebrand before too much equity accumulates — but after product-market fit.** Too early: you don't know what you're branding. Too late: you've already trained thousands of customers on the old name. The window is after you know you have something real, before scale.

5. **Brand is a system, not a collection of assets.** Logo, color, voice, photography direction, and visual values encoding all need to compose coherently. If you can't explain how a brand decision maps back to your values, it's probably noise.

6. **Opinionated software is both harder to sell and stickier once sold.** You're selling a belief, not just a tool. Buyers need to buy into the philosophy (structured recruiting, agile workflows, transparent progression) before they'll buy the product. But those buyers retain far longer than buyers who just wanted generic flexibility.

7. **Talk to your customers continuously; category names come from them, not from internal brainstorms.** Every positioning shift that worked — visual collaboration, structured recruiting, employee experience — came from listening to how customers described what they were doing and what they needed.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- Category creation dynamics will accelerate with AI: new tech inflection points (AI agents, autonomous workflows) are already creating new categories; the mechanics Barbra describes (analyst relations, content marketing, competitor validation) apply directly
- Opinionated software becomes more important in AI: when AI can customize almost anything, the products that have a strong point of view on *how* work should be done (not just enabling it) will create more durable value
- The "love mark" aspiration is even harder to achieve for AI products, where trust is fragile — brand authenticity and values-in-product-behavior (not just marketing) becomes critical
- Category naming pressure is intense for AI: "AI-powered X" is already a generic category; the companies that name the specific outcome or workflow (not the technology) will own clearer mental real estate

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"Should we try to create a new category?"**: Depends on whether (1) existing category is too small or carries negative connotations, (2) customers describe their pain in ways that don't map to existing categories, and (3) there's a tech inflection point creating new needs. Test it: if customers keep using old category language despite your repositioning, abandon it and elevate the existing category instead.
- **"How do you approach category creation?"**: Customer listening first. Then PR/thought leadership to establish the narrative. Then analyst/directory relationships (G2, Gartner). Then sustained content marketing to educate buyers that a budget line should exist. Sell into existing category while building new category narrative in parallel.
- **"How do you build a lasting brand?"**: Brand = logo/colors + voice + photography/illustration decisions + values encoding. Build it as a system, not a collection of assets. Keep values simple enough to remember and specific enough to not be generic. Make brand inseparable from company behavior.
- **"When should we rebrand?"**: After product-market fit, before scale. Rebrand as a product development process (sprint teams, cross-functional owners, customer feedback loops). Budget for domain, all collateral, product UI, and sales materials changing simultaneously. Nod to the original brand to maintain continuity for early advocates.
- **"What is opinionated software and should we build it?"**: Encode best practices or a better way of working into the product experience rather than offering unlimited flexibility. Makes sales harder (buyers need to buy the philosophy) but drives deeper behavior change and retention. Especially powerful when there's a demonstrably better method most companies aren't practicing.
