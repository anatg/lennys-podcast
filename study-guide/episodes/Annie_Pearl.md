## TL;DR
Annie's core frameworks are unusually operational — not abstract philosophy but the actual templates, meetings, and horizon planning she uses at Calendly. Her "Playing to Win" strategy cascade, discovery/solutioning/build lifecycle, and OPA peer review meeting are among the most concrete and immediately replicable process frameworks in the catalog.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Strategy = Integrated Set of Choices (Playing to Win)**: A good product strategy must answer: (1) What's your winning aspiration? (2) Where are you going to play? — which markets, segments, and personas; (3) How are you going to win with that target audience? The "where to play" question is the most important and hardest. It forces you to *say no* to some markets/users — which unlocks the focus to be genuinely excellent for those you serve. Annie uses Roger Martin's *Playing to Win* as the source framework.

- **Horizon Planning with Resource Allocation Percentages**: At Calendly, annual strategy is broken into three horizons (H1 = core platform, H2 = teams/departments, H3 = future). Resource allocation shifts over time as the company matures:
  - Year 1: 70% H1, 30% H2, 0% H3
  - Year 2: 50% H1, 50% H2, 0% H3
  - Year 3: 30% H1, 60% H2, 10% H3
  This is a concrete, trackable way to manage the tension between sustaining the core and investing in the future.

- **Discovery → Solutioning → Build → Launch/Measure/Iterate (Development Lifecycle)**: Annie separates product development into explicit phases and only commits dates on work that's already been de-risked:
  - **Discovery**: understand the problem space. Commit to a timeline for the research effort, not for the solution.
  - **Solutioning**: explore and test solutions. Commit to when you'll have user testing done.
  - **Build**: once you know what you're building, do estimation and commit to a delivery date.
  - **Launch/Measure/Iterate**: post-ship phase.
  This prevents false date promises on work where the problem and solution aren't yet understood.

- **OPA (Opportunity/Problem Assessment) Meeting**: A recurring PM-only forum where product managers debate and spar around: (a) opportunities worth investigating (problem space), and (b) research findings and whether to proceed to solutioning. Critical detail: the CPO does *not* attend. This is intentional — Annie wants PMs to be fully open and honest with each other without worrying about judgment from above. This is one of the most candid examples in the podcast catalog of a CPO designing their own absence into the system.

- **Competitive War Gaming**: Each quarter, teams are assigned ownership of a competitor. Their job: immerse in the competitor's product, do a SWOT, understand their trajectory, and present findings. At the end of the quarter: a competitive war gaming day where all teams present, with prizes. Keeps competitive intelligence decentralized and comprehensive without requiring every PM to track every competitor.

- **OKR Maturity Model**: Three stages she's seen at companies:
  1. No OKRs / ad-hoc work without clear measurement (low coordination, unclear success)
  2. Department-level OKRs (each function has their own, but siloed)
  3. Company OKRs with tight cross-functional integration and dependency mapping (the goal state)
  The biggest unlock was moving from stage 2 to stage 3 — connecting company objectives all the way through to the teams doing the work.

- **"Focus Wisely" as Cultural Operating System**: Calendly's core principle "focus wisely" is embedded into templates, planning docs, product reviews, and onboarding. Every PRD template asks: who is the target customer? What are their needs? How do we solve it better than any alternative? The principle operationalized means saying no to real requests. Annie's example: Venmo integration is clearly deprioritized because the target ICP is enterprise sales/CS/recruiting teams, not solopreneurs — even though many users would use it.

- **PLG + SLG Transition Playbook**:
  - At Calendly: 70% of signups come through viral loop (someone shares a Calendly link → recipient signs up)
  - Team expansion: solo users invite teammates → team starts using → PQL signal triggers outreach to team lead → enterprise conversation begins
  - First sales reps should match the motion (inbound/PLG → not heavy hunters) and the buyer (department heads, not CIOs — that comes later)
  - As PLG slows at scale, the team/department expansion motion becomes the next growth curve

- **Breaking Into PM — Four Paths**:
  1. APM programs (Google, Meta, Box — search Glassdoor for "Associate Product Manager" to find more)
  2. Internal job board: apply for junior PM role when you're in a product-adjacent function
  3. Partner with a PM, volunteer to take on work, prove the skill before the title (most effective in Annie's experience)
  4. Join an early-stage startup where everyone does everything

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **"Where to play" is the hardest and most important strategic decision.** Most companies claim to have ICP clarity but can't articulate it to individual PMs or designers. The test: can a PM on your team instantly say which work fits the strategy and which doesn't?

2. **The date you commit to should only cover work you've already de-risked.** Don't estimate shipping dates for features you haven't discovered or designed yet. Commit to the discovery timeline, not the build timeline.

3. **Giving PMs a room without the boss isn't a nice-to-have — it changes how openly they engage.** The OPA design (no CPO) is a deliberate structural choice that changes the quality of peer debate. Leaders who attend every meeting don't see what they're missing.

4. **Focus wisely means making trade-off decisions that feel painful in the moment.** The companies that stay focused do so because focus is embedded in how every decision is framed, not because focus was announced once and people remember it.

5. **The PLG viral loop slows — plan for the next curve before you need it.** Calendly's team/department business was pulled by customers before it was pushed by strategy. Watching usage data early is how you find the next curve in time to invest.

6. **Box lesson: understand the *why* behind what customers ask for, then build the most scalable version.** Building exactly what customer A, B, and C each want separately destroys the product. Building what addresses the underlying problem for all of them compounds.

7. **Glassdoor lesson: consumer product discipline (funnel optimization, A/B testing, growth teams) transfers directly to PLG products.** A CPO who has done both consumer and enterprise work is unusually well-positioned for PLG + SLG companies.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- This episode predates the current AI product wave — focused on B2B scheduling and PLG mechanics
- Annie's development lifecycle framework (Discovery → Solutioning → Build) maps cleanly to AI product work: AI products especially require not committing to ship dates before discovery because capabilities keep changing
- The "focus wisely" principle is especially critical for AI product teams: the temptation to ship every new model capability is strong; the discipline to ask "is this for our ICP?" is what separates focused AI products from feature-spray ones

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **"How do you think about product strategy?"**: Playing to Win cascade — winning aspiration, where to play, how to win. The "where to play" question is the hardest and most important. Horizon planning with explicit resource allocation percentages.
- **"How do you manage PLG and enterprise sales together?"**: PLG viral loop as acquisition engine; PQL signal triggers sales conversation; first sales reps should match the motion (inbound) and the buyer (department heads, not CIOs). Teams business grows faster than solo business at scale.
- **"How do you run planning and OKRs?"**: Company OKRs → quarterly milestones → product roadmap. Commit to dates only on work you've already de-risked. OKR maturity model: siloed → integrated.
- **"What is your product development process?"**: Discovery → Solutioning → Build → Launch/Measure/Iterate. Only commit delivery dates after Solutioning is complete.
- **"How do you hire into product or build a strong PM culture?"**: Four paths to PM entry. OPA peer review meeting (without CPO). Competitive war gaming to keep competitive knowledge fresh.
