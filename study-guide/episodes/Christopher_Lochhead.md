## TL;DR
Category design is the idea that legendary companies don't compete in existing markets — they create entirely new ones by reframing the problem. In most tech categories, one company earns ~76% of total market value; the only path to that position is to design a new market category rather than fight for scraps of existing demand.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Category Design vs. Category Competition**: Instead of competing for existing demand ("capture demand"), design a new category that creates demand. The category makes the brand, not the other way around. One company earns ~76% of total market value in any given category.
- **The "Better" Trap**: Launching a better version of an existing solution in an existing category almost never works. The market must first accept a new problem framing before it will accept a new solution. Examples: Threads, Amazon Fire Phone, Red Bull Cola, Microsoft Stores — all better products, all failed because they didn't reframe the problem.
- **Frame, Name, Claim**: The three-step process for category creation. Frame a new or different problem, name it with distinctive language, and claim your position as the category leader.
- **Languaging**: Strategic use of language to change thinking. New language creates new mental scaffolding. Examples: Starbucks inventing "grande/venti/latte," OpenAI coining "large language model" and "training data," Otis calling the elevator a "vertical railway."
- **Backcasting vs. Forecasting**: Instead of forecasting from the present into the future, stand in the future and look back. Reject all current premises, envision the fully realized future state, then work backward to identify what must happen to get there. This liberates thinking from the constraints of the present.
- **Magic Triangle**: Legendary companies must get three things right simultaneously — Product, Company, and Category. All three are equally important. Most founders obsess over product and neglect category.
- **Dam the Demand**: Compete category-to-category, not product-to-product. Redirect existing demand flows rather than fighting for share within them. Example: Spinning damned cycling demand; Peloton then damned spinning demand.
- **Lightning Strike vs. Peanut Butter**: Concentrate marketing energy into rare, intense moments rather than spreading budget evenly across the year. Aim to be undeniable for one week rather than forgettable all year. Borrowed from how Hollywood launches movies.
- **Super Consumers**: ~8-10% of buyers drive the majority of category profits and are the zeitgeist leaders others look to. Identify and educate them first — they drive word-of-mouth at scale.
- **Point of View (POV)**: A structured articulation of: here's the problem, here's why the old solution is inadequate, here's the new way (the "Froto" — From-To). The POV is not about your product; it's about the customer's world.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Categories make products, not the other way around.** The reason most products fail isn't quality — it's that they tried to fit into an existing problem frame rather than redefining the problem entirely.
2. **The #1 unquestioned decision most entrepreneurs make is to compete.** They never consciously chose to fight for the 24%; they just defaulted into it. Awareness of this choice is the first step toward category design.
3. **Product market fit may be backwards.** You don't want your product to fit a market — you want to design a market that fits your product. Threads had the fastest "product market fit" ever achieved and still failed.
4. **Problems create categories, solutions don't.** Unless you reframe the problem, even a dramatically better solution will fail. Customers buy new insight about a problem, not a better feature set.
5. **Reflexive vs. reflective thinking is the root issue.** Most entrepreneurs unconsciously assume the future is a continuation of the past. Legendary entrepreneurs design different futures. They start from the future and backcast.
6. **Word of mouth is the most powerful marketing — and is almost never in a marketing plan.** WOM plus a compelling category POV directed at super consumers is the ultimate growth engine, and it can be launched with very little money.
7. **Disruption is the wrong mental model.** "Disrupting" something means your reference point is the past. The electric guitar didn't disrupt the acoustic guitar — it expanded the total guitar market. Design net-new demand.

## Interview Prep Takeaways
- **Framework to reference**: Category design as a lens distinct from product strategy. Use the "magic triangle" (product + company + category) when asked about go-to-market or product strategy.
- **Stories to draw on**: When asked about competitive positioning, challenge the premise — ask whether you're competing in an existing category or defining a new one. Reference Lomi, Peloton, Starbucks, or Otis as illustrations.
- **Mindset to demonstrate**: Don't default to "we'll build a better product" — show that you think about demand creation, not just demand capture. Show you understand that customers buy new problem framings, not feature lists.
- **Contrarian take to use**: When asked about product market fit, note the tension — fitting a product to a market vs. designing the market. Shows depth of thinking.
