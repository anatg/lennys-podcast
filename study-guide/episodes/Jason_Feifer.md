## TL;DR
Jason Feifer shares tactical advice on getting press coverage, explaining that journalists don't care about you—they care about serving their audience. Success requires understanding each publication's mission, finding the right writer (often freelancers are hungrier), and crafting personalized pitches that demonstrate how your story serves their readers. Press should be treated like fundraising: know what you need it for, customize your approach, and remember it's entirely outside your control.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Opportunity Set A vs. B**: Set A is everything asked of you (your job responsibilities). Set B is what's available to you that nobody's asking you to do (side projects, new skills, hobbies). Growth and career transformation happen in Set B—it's where you become qualified for things beyond your current role.

- **Press as Investor Pitch**: Approach media coverage like fundraising. Just as you wouldn't pitch an investor without understanding their thesis, don't pitch a journalist without understanding their publication's mission and audience. Customize every approach based on what that specific writer/outlet needs.

- **Three-Step Press Strategy**: (1) **Prep** - Define your goal for press, understand what's interesting about your story, know which publications reach your target audience. (2) **Target** - Find the right writer/editor, understand their beat, consider freelancers over staff. (3) **Pitch** - Send short, customized emails (3 paragraphs max) that demonstrate you understand their work and audience.

- **Context Creation Strategy**: You don't always need to be the story—you can create or present context in which you happen to exist. Examples: Barbara Corcoran creating the "Corcoran Report" on Manhattan real estate, Zapier publishing "fastest growing apps" based on their data, or positioning yourself as an expert source for trend stories.

- **The Airport Research Method**: When you can't afford traditional market research, find creative alternatives. Example: The butter dish founder who did market research at airports instead of paying $10,000 to a firm, because airports are full of people with nothing better to do than answer questions.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Journalists serve their audience, not you**: The fundamental mistake founders make is treating media as a service provider. Writers and editors don't care about you—they care about their readers. Your job is to help them serve their audience by offering genuinely useful information or insights. Approach them understanding what they're trying to do for their readers, not what you want them to do for you.

2. **Freelancers are often your best target**: Staff writers get 30-50+ pitches per day and have salaried positions. Freelancers "have to hustle for their food"—they need to find stories to pitch to editors to get paid. They're hungrier, get fewer pitches, and are more likely to read and respond to relevant outreach. Find who's writing about your space and check if they're freelancers (look for "contributor" vs. "staff" in bios).

3. **Publication mission matters more than category**: Don't pitch based on surface-level fit (e.g., "I'm an entrepreneur, so Entrepreneur Magazine should cover me"). Understand each publication's actual editorial mission. Entrepreneur wants counterintuitive problem-solving stories from inside a business. Fast Company (under previous leadership) wanted stories representing the evolution of business. Cosmo might want your product in a seasonal roundup. Study what they actually publish.

4. **The story you pitch should match what they'll actually write**: The butter dish company couldn't get coverage by pitching the product itself—that's not what business publications write about. But the story of doing market research at airports instead of paying $10,000? That's a fascinating entrepreneurial problem-solving story. Think about what the actual article would be, not just what you want covered.

5. **Press outcomes are unpredictable—plan accordingly**: One Entrepreneur Magazine story might drive more app downloads than all paid marketing, or it might drive nothing directly but serve other purposes (investor credibility, "as seen in" social proof, partnership conversations). Never bank on press as your primary growth strategy. It's a great add-on, but entirely outside your control. Have realistic expectations about what success looks like.

6. **Be vulnerable and human throughout**: Press releases don't work because they're not human. People on corporate talking points aren't interesting to interview. Success stories alone are boring—journalists want to hear about challenges you faced and overcame. The more human and open you are (while being professional), the better the story will be. Trying to control everything makes reporters suspicious.

7. **Social proof and validation may be more valuable than reach**: Even if only 5,000-10,000 people read your story (common for major publications publishing constantly), the credibility of being "featured in" that outlet can be used in emails to investors, partners, and customers indefinitely. Sometimes the trophy you walk around with is more valuable than the immediate audience reach.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

*How to apply lessons from this episode to your own PM interviews:*

- **Understand your audience before pitching**: Just as you need to understand a publication's mission before pitching a story, understand what the company/interviewer actually needs before interviewing. Research the team, the product challenges, the company stage—then frame your experience in terms of how you solve their problems, not just listing your achievements.

- **Tell problem-solving stories, not success stories**: Jason emphasized he hates pure success stories—he wants to hear about challenges and how you overcame them. In PM interviews, don't just say "I launched this feature and it was successful." Explain the messy problem, the constraints, the difficult decisions, and how you navigated to a solution. Vulnerability and honesty about challenges makes better stories.

- **Be specific and human**: Generic corporate-speak kills press coverage, and it kills interviews too. Use specific examples, real numbers, actual challenges. Show personality. The "theory" interview technique Jason described (listening carefully then proposing a connection between things someone said) works because it's deeply human and shows real engagement.

- **Create context where you belong**: If you don't have perfect experience for a role, create the context where your experience makes sense—like founders creating surveys or reports to generate press. Position your background as solving similar problems in different contexts, or show how your unique combination of skills addresses their specific needs.

- **Prepare multiple versions of your story**: Just as you'd pitch different angles to different publications, prepare to tell your PM story different ways depending on what the interviewer cares about (technical depth, user empathy, business impact, team leadership). Read the room and emphasize what's most relevant.
