## TL;DR
Katie Dill shares how design excellence directly drives business growth—including a 10.5% revenue increase from Stripe's checkout improvements—and explains practical systems for operationalizing quality, including the "essential journeys" program where leaders regularly walk through user experiences as customers would. She emphasizes that quality isn't opposed to growth but is growth itself, and introduces the performance formula: Performance = Potential - Interference.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Performance = Potential - Interference**: A formula for improving team effectiveness. Increase potential through hiring well and developing talent. Decrease interference by removing obstacles like poor processes, unclear communication, or misaligned org structure. Both levers must be pulled continuously as the company grows.

- **Essential Journeys Program**: Identify 15 critical user journeys. Assign engineering, product, and design leaders to own each journey. Leaders "walk the store" quarterly—experiencing the product as users do from Google search through product usage. They friction log issues, score quality on a rubric, and calibrate scores together in product quality reviews. Creates accountability and surfaces systemic issues.

- **Levels of Quality Framework**:
  - Level 1: Does it work? (baseline)
  - Level 2: Error-free and well-rounded?
  - Level 3+: Exceeds expectations, does things users didn't ask for
  - Quality bar should be based on user expectations, not arbitrary standards

- **Reach for the Stars, Land on the Moon**: Do comprehensive vision work showing the ideal 2-year outcome (the "11-star experience"), then work backward to determine phased execution. Prevents incremental thinking that never reaches greatness. Vision work isn't wasted if it informs execution.

- **Beauty Begets Beauty**: When you demonstrate painstaking craft and care, users trust you more (they assume you care about invisible details too), find products easier to use, and are inspired to bring that same care to their own work. Beauty enhances functionality rather than opposing it.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Quality is growth, not opposed to it**: Stripe's Growth team focuses maniacally on building better experiences because quality improvements directly drive business metrics. Small checkout flow improvements led to 10.5% revenue increases for businesses. Making products easier to understand increases activation. Quality reduces support burden.

2. **Trust must be earned before change**: When Katie started at Airbnb, she came in "swinging" with changes. Five designers staged an intervention after one month, reading prepared feedback about what wasn't working. The lesson: Even right changes fail without trust. She shifted to listening first, understanding what individuals cared about, then bringing the team along with changes rather than inflicting change on them.

3. **Design is intention for anyone**: At its core, design is simply bringing intentionality to decisions—thinking about who something is for and how they'll use it. Anyone in any function can practice this, from designing a doorknob to designing org structure. While creative expertise matters, everyone can bring more care and intentionality to improve outcomes.

4. **Multidisciplinary proximity drives quality**: Teams should wear two T-shirts—their functional discipline (Design) and their cross-functional team (Marketplace). Designers need time together to build community and align on craft standards, but must sit with engineers and PMs for daily work to prevent misalignment and wasted effort. The website team at Stripe has design and engineering report into the same org.

5. **Quality regresses without systems**: Products can ship at high quality then degrade over time as new features make old ones look worse, or as teams focus narrowly on their area and lose sight of the journey. Organizations need regular "walk the store" rituals, clear ownership of journeys, and calibration moments to maintain standards as they scale.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate humility about learning**: Katie's candid story about the intervention at Airbnb shows that great leaders admit mistakes and learn from hard feedback. In interviews, don't just share successes—share what you learned from failures and how you changed.

- **Connect quality to business outcomes**: Don't position design/quality as opposed to metrics. Show specific examples where improving user experience moved business metrics (conversion, retention, support reduction). Katie cites the 10.5% revenue lift and reduced support tickets.

- **Show systems thinking for scale**: As companies grow, informal quality standards break down. Describe systems you've built to operationalize quality—regular reviews, clear ownership, calibration processes, shared visibility (like the design deck every two weeks).

- **Lead with trust-building**: Before proposing changes or improvements, show how you'd listen and understand stakeholder motivations. The formula "performance = potential - interference" gives you language to discuss both hiring/development and removing obstacles.

- **Bring multidisciplinary thinking**: Even as a PM, discuss how you'd structure teams to work closely across functions, share information widely, and create shared understanding of quality standards. Don't silo functions.
