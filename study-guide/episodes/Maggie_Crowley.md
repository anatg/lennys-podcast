## TL;DR
Maggie Crowley shares three defining traits of great PMs: simplifying complex problems down to the one thing that matters, consistently following up on results after shipping, and doing unglamorous work without saying "that's not my job." She provides a tactical template for writing product strategy (from mission through landscape to opportunity) and warns against over-relying on data at the expense of qualitative insights and good judgment.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Three Traits of Great PMs**: (1) Simplifying—breaking down complexity to find the one thing to work on and staying focused long enough to finish it; (2) Following up on results—remembering to check metrics weeks/months after launch and sharing what happened; (3) Carrying the water—doing unglamorous work (support, sales, QA, implementation) because your job is delivering outcomes, not just writing specs.

- **Product Strategy Template**: Start with mission/goals → Landscape (market, competitors, SWOT, risks) → Current state (honest accounting of product, tech debt, bottlenecks) → Opportunity (where to play/win based on unique advantage) → Challenges (what must be true) → Solution (3 bullets max) → Plan. Write it all in a long Google doc (can be 20+ pages) with a summary at the top using Minto Principle.

- **The Minto Principle**: Put your conclusion/headline first, then supporting arguments second. Don't build up to a point—just state it upfront. Applies to emails, docs, and presentations.

- **"When in doubt, it's your job"**: As a PM, you're responsible for business results, not just specs. If something needs doing (project management, QA, customer support, sales calls), and no one else is doing it, you do it. Your job is to fill all gaps to deliver outcomes.

- **Data-Driven as a Red Flag**: Being overly "data-driven" often signals over-emphasis on quantitative metrics at the expense of qualitative insights and judgment. Talking to 10 users often yields better insights than any dashboard. You need data, but it won't tell you *why* things happen.

- **Side Quests vs. Main Quest**: Distinguish between the core mission (main quest) and distractions (side quests). The discipline to stay on the main quest long enough to complete it separates successful PMs from those who fragment their focus.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Simplification is a superpower**: The best PMs can identify the one thing that truly matters among hundreds of priorities and have the resilience to stick with it until it's done. Delete the first two paragraphs of anything you write. Read your work out loud. Apply the "rule of three" (never more than three priorities/bullets).

2. **Following up on results is rare and valuable**: Set calendar reminders for 2 weeks, 1 month, and 6 months after launch to check your metrics and share results. Leaders almost never have to ask the best PMs for this—they proactively share what happened. This separates great from good PMs.

3. **Shipping volume drives expertise**: It takes 4-5 years and many reps to become confident as a PM. You learn more by staying somewhere long enough to see 2-3 cycles of the same product and experience the consequences of your decisions than by job-hopping every year.

4. **Strategy is homework, not theater**: Write strategy docs primarily for yourself to do the thinking work and gain confidence in your decisions. Share them widely, but don't expect everyone to read 20 pages. The exercise forces you to understand the logic chain from company mission to individual priority.

5. **"Obviously better" beats analysis paralysis**: If something is logically and obviously better for users, stop over-analyzing with frameworks and dashboards. Use good judgment and just do it. The best PMs know when to throw out all frameworks and trust their intuition built from experience.

6. **Creating content accelerates learning**: Writing and speaking about your work forces you to process what you've learned in ways you wouldn't otherwise. The most basic things you find easy are often most valuable to share—someone will find it transformative.

7. **Authenticity beats personal brand**: Don't create content to build a following. Share what genuinely interests you and what you're actively working on. People can tell when you're performing versus when you're sharing real questions and real processes from your job.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate simplification skills**: In case interviews, show you can distill complex problems to the one critical thing. Practice explaining your work clearly and concisely. If asked about a project, lead with the conclusion (Minto Principle), then supporting details.

- **Share shipping examples with follow-up**: Prepare stories that include "here's what we built AND here's what happened after launch"—metrics that moved, lessons learned, iterations made. The follow-through is what separates candidates.

- **Embrace unglamorous work**: When discussing past projects, highlight times you did QA, supported customers, wrote copy, helped with sales. Show you're willing to "carry the water" and don't see anything as beneath you.

- **Answer "worst product you shipped"**: Prepare this story. Laugh about it. Show humility, self-awareness, and what you learned. The best answer shows you've shipped enough to have failures and learned from them.

- **Demonstrate judgment over frameworks**: Don't just recite frameworks. Show you know when to apply them and when to trust intuition. Share examples where you made a call based on "this is obviously better" rather than running endless analyses.

- **Show depth over breadth**: When discussing your PM experience, emphasize what you learned from seeing products through multiple cycles, not just shipping and moving on. Depth of learning often beats variety of experience.
