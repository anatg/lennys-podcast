## TL;DR
Wes Kao shares powerful frameworks for effective communication including the "super specific who," state change method for engaging meetings, and managing up strategies. She emphasizes that great writing focuses on the "how" not the "what/why," that variety (state changes) keeps audiences engaged, and that managing your boss proactively—not waiting for them to manage you—accelerates your career through increased trust and opportunity.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **The Super Specific Who**: Most writers spend too much time on "what" and "why" instead of "how." Audiences already agree with your premise—they want tactical application. Cut the backstory and get to implementation details, nuances, and specific examples. Start right before you get eaten by the bear (skip all the preamble about buying gear and booking campsites).

- **Content Hierarchy of BS**: A pyramid showing how much room different formats allow for unsubstantiated claims. Bottom (most BS room): Twitter, podcasts, keynotes. Middle: long-form articles, books. Top (least BS room): interactive courses where students can challenge ideas in real-time. Higher standards emerge when your audience can engage and question you directly.

- **State Change Method**: Punctuate monologues with variety every 3-5 minutes or slides. State changes include: switching between gallery view and screen share, using breakout rooms, polls, chat participation, having others speak, asking people to guess before revealing information. This combats Zoom fatigue and maintains engagement without relying solely on monologues.

- **Eyes Light Up**: Watch for visceral reactions when presenting—when someone's face changes, they lean forward, or their voice gets excited. These moments reveal what truly resonates. Don't be delusional about polite nods; faces can't lie. Use this data to refine pitches, content, and communication with managers and teams.

- **Trade-offs Framework for Saying No**: Instead of directly saying no, explain what you'll deprioritize: "Yes, I can design that PDF. That means the website redesign will wait until later this week. Does that sound good, or should I stick with the original priority?" Shifts conversation from yes/no to smart prioritization, protecting bandwidth without seeming uncooperative.

- **Prioritize and Communicate**: When given new work, both prioritize it among existing tasks AND communicate where it sits: "This will be third on my priority list after X and Y. Does that seem reasonable?" Prevents misalignment and manages expectations proactively.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Don't reject yourself before you get rejected**: Wes applied to work with Seth Godin thinking thousands would apply and did her video in one take rather than overthinking it. That "good enough" application changed her career trajectory. Put your foot forward even if it's not perfect.

2. **High standards don't require trading speed for quality**: Working with Seth Godin, Wes discovered you can ship fast, high-quality work economically—all three sides of the triangle. Most people assume quality and speed are mutually exclusive, but rigorous processes and high expectations can deliver both.

3. **Managing up is for everyone, especially senior people**: The most senior people are best at managing up—it's why they got promoted. They understand what keeps their boss up at night and proactively take those concerns off their plate. It's not about being junior; it's about being strategic and creating trust.

4. **Over-communication in remote work feels like the right amount**: What you think is over-communicating is usually just enough for remote teams. Even after discussing something three times, you might not be as aligned as you think. Err on the side of more communication, not less.

5. **Structure your communication for scanners and deep readers**: Put the most important point at the top (TLDR/decision), then add "Context:" with multiple paragraphs below. Readers who agree can stop; those who need rationale can continue. Never mix action items randomly throughout context—separate them clearly.

6. **Surprises are bad in work contexts**: Unless you're bringing someone a snack, don't surprise your manager or team. Proactive communication that prevents surprises builds trust and keeps stakeholders informed without creating extra work for them.

7. **Pros/cons lists imply objectivity**: When you present pros and cons, the structure signals equal weight and objectivity. If you're biased, readers lose trust. Either be genuinely objective or state your recommendation upfront, then share risks/downsides transparently.

8. **Learn writing craft, not just engagement tactics**: Study sentence structure, paragraph logic, and technical writing mechanics (books: *It Was the Best of Sentences, It Was the Worst of Sentences*; *Better Business Writing* by HBR). Too many people learn writing by mimicking social media rather than mastering the fundamentals of precise communication.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate managing up skills**: Share specific examples of how you've kept managers informed proactively, avoided surprises, and helped them prioritize by presenting trade-offs rather than just asking for direction.

- **Show communication precision**: In your interview answers, structure responses with conclusion first, then context. Practice the "bottom line up front" approach to demonstrate clear thinking and respect for the interviewer's time.

- **Highlight state change thinking**: When discussing how you run meetings or present to stakeholders, mention specific techniques you use to maintain engagement—polls, breakouts, switching formats, involving others. This shows sophistication in facilitation.

- **Use the "eyes light up" skill**: During the interview, watch for what genuinely interests your interviewer. When you notice energy shift, lean into those topics. This demonstrates emotional intelligence and adaptability.

- **Practice saying no with trade-offs**: If asked how you handle competing priorities, use the trade-offs framework to show you don't just push back but help stakeholders make informed decisions about what matters most.

- **Get specific on the "how"**: When answering behavioral questions, don't just explain what you did and why—go deep on the specific steps, nuances you navigated, and tactical decisions. Interviewers want super specific who, not general principles.
