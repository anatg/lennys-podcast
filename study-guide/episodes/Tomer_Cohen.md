## TL;DR
Tomer Cohen shares how LinkedIn transformed its feed from a promotional "activity tracker" into a daily-use knowledge-sharing platform through AI-first thinking, starting years before generative AI became popular. His core philosophy—"We might be wrong, but we're not confused"—emphasizes clarity of thought and execution over being right, and he demonstrates how carving out 2 million users for experimentation helped prove a controversial product vision without disrupting the broader business.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **"We might be wrong, but we're not confused"**: Prioritize clarity and alignment over being right. Better to move forward unified in one direction than hedge across multiple approaches. Requires two components: (1) clarity of thought (deep problem understanding, first-principles thinking, strong opinions with tradeoffs), and (2) clarity of execution (ensuring priorities actually manifest in resource allocation and team focus).

- **Minus One to One Products (Turnarounds)**: Transforming underperforming products is harder than zero-to-one because you must change entrenched processes, metrics, and team mindsets. Solution: Carve out a subset of users (LinkedIn used 2M members) to build the new experience without disrupting company-wide metrics, prove the concept, then scale.

- **AI as Ultimate Matchmaker**: In marketplace products, AI's primary role is value exchange—matching creators with the right audience and consumers with relevant content. Focus AI strategy on optimizing this matchmaking, not just adding AI features superficially.

- **Starting from the Peak**: Set ambitious goals by visualizing the mountain peak (end state) and base camp (starting point), even if the middle is unclear. Work backwards from market potential, not from current metrics. Example: "Millions will come to LinkedIn feed daily" when metrics showed otherwise.

- **Becoming vs. Being**: Continuous growth mindset—the moment you think you've mastered something is when you become obsolete. Always focus on becoming rather than achieving a fixed state.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Disagree vs. Misunderstand**: When someone says "I'm not clear on this," push back—are they disagreeing or misunderstanding? If misunderstanding, clarify until each person can articulate the other's view. If disagreeing, stop explaining and start debating the actual disagreement. This cuts through corporate politeness to reach real clarity.

2. **Product Leaders Must Own AI Strategy**: AI is not a black box to delegate to engineers. Product leaders should define algorithm objectives, specify features to train on, drive data collection strategy, and even influence infrastructure choices. The "river rafting" analogy: everyone rows, but the guide (PM) holds the two steering paddles—and those paddles are AI.

3. **AI First Means Letting Go of Control**: Traditional PMs dictate every detail of the user experience. AI-first PMs provide ingredients and guidelines, then let AI personalize the experience. This requires a fundamental mindset shift from deterministic product design to probabilistic outcomes with guardrails.

4. **Index to Conviction Over Challenge**: Career acceleration comes from pursuing what you're genuinely excited about and have conviction in, not just what's most prestigious or challenging. Ask: "What do I care about? Where can I make a dent?" not "What's most in demand?"

5. **Principles Must Have Teeth**: Saying "we should build a simple product" is useless—everyone wants that. Strong principles require tradeoffs: "I'm willing to sacrifice X to achieve Y." The willingness to trade off reveals true strategic thinking.

6. **Purpose Transformation Requires Team Unity**: When LinkedIn feed shifted from "traffic jumpstart for other products" to "people that matter, talking about things I care about professionally," it required bringing the AI team (previously centralized and misaligned) directly into the product team with unified objectives. The engine (AI) must align with the direction.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI First Academy**: LinkedIn required every PM to go through AI training (starting 2020), similar to how they made the company mobile-first in 2014. Product leaders must understand objectives, features, data collection, and fine-tuning—not delegate these to engineering.

- **Fall 2022 Transformation**: When LLMs emerged, LinkedIn leadership asked every team to "let go of your roadmap" and return to core problems with fresh thinking. Process: (1) Focus on objectives, not technology ("What are you trying to solve?"), (2) Allow exploration period for teams to experiment broadly, (3) Converge top-down on 4-5 key bets with weekly reviews.

- **From Background to Foreground**: AI historically worked behind the scenes as a matchmaker (jobs, feed content). New wave of AI brings it to the front as direct user experience (coaching, content creation). This requires rethinking entire product surfaces.

- **Infrastructure as Product Lever**: Changing AI infrastructure can create bigger product impact than adding new surface-level features. Product leaders must think about infrastructure, not just UX.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate clarity of thinking**: Practice articulating not just what you want to build, but specifically what problem you're solving, for which audience, with what unique criteria. Interviewers value precision over vague goals.

- **Show conviction with tradeoffs**: When discussing product decisions, explicitly state what you're willing to sacrifice. "We prioritized X over Y because..." is stronger than "We built a great experience."

- **Lead with beliefs, prove with evidence**: Strong product leaders have hypotheses about where products should go, then gather data to validate. Don't wait for perfect data before forming a point of view.

- **Own the full stack in marketplace products**: If discussing marketplace or feed products, show you understand both sides (creation and consumption), the matching algorithm, and how to measure healthy marketplace dynamics beyond vanity metrics.

- **Prove you can drive organizational change**: Transformation stories (like the feed turnaround) demonstrate ability to navigate politics, change entrenched processes, and deliver results despite resistance—valuable for senior roles.
