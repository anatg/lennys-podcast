## TL;DR
Christina Wodtke, author of *Radical Focus*, explains that OKRs are a "vitamin, not medicine"—they supercharge healthy companies but reveal dysfunction in struggling ones. The atomic unit of effective OKRs is answering weekly: "What am I doing this week to get closer to our goals?" with the key benefits being focus, alignment, cadence, and learning cycles rather than rigid spreadsheets of tasks.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **OKRs as Focus Tool, Not Fix-All**: OKRs work best for companies that already have strategy, empowered teams, and psychological safety. They're vitamins that enhance performance, not medicine that cures organizational dysfunction.

- **The Weekly Question Framework**: "What am I doing this week to get closer to our outcomes?" This question, asked every Monday, forms the atomic unit of effective goal-setting and prevents the "tomorrow problem" where important work gets perpetually postponed.

- **Temporal Landmarks for Cadence**: Leverage natural time markers (quarters, Mondays, Fridays) to create rhythm. Monday commitments and Friday celebrations create bookends that drive progress and learning.

- **How Do We Know? (Key Results Discovery)**: After setting an objective, ask "How will we know we succeeded?" three times to triangulate meaningful metrics—typically one hardcore number, one quality measure, and one financial indicator.

- **Strategy as Half-Built Framework**: Outline quarterly themes for the year but only detail Q1 OKRs fully. This provides enough structure to align while maintaining flexibility to adapt based on learning.

- **Three-Peer Approval Process**: Instead of hierarchical approval chains, have three relevant teams review OKRs with 24-hour turnaround. This speeds up planning from weeks to days while maintaining quality.

- **Estimation as Learning Practice**: View OKR grading (aiming for 70-80% achievement) not as performance measurement but as practice in estimation—a learnable business skill that improves with repetition.

- **Make New Mistakes Philosophy**: OKRs create learning cycles that prevent repeating the same errors. Each quarter's retrospective feeds into better decision-making, building organizational intelligence over time.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Speed Kills OKR Success**: The biggest mistake is rushing implementation without understanding. Companies read one book, get excited, roll out OKRs company-wide immediately, then abandon them when they fail. Instead, pilot with your best team for a quarter, learn what works in your culture, then expand slowly.

2. **Celebrations Are Underestimated**: Friday celebrations asking "What was the most awesome thing that happened this week?" can transform company culture even before implementing full OKRs. One CEO reported seeing improvement just from starting weekly celebrations mid-quarter.

3. **Tasks vs. Outcomes Confusion**: The most common OKR mistake is writing tasks as key results. Real key results answer "How will we know?" with measurable outcomes that allow flexibility in execution methods. If it feels like a task, ask whether it truly represents an outcome.

4. **Boring Meetings Signal Broken Process**: If OKR review meetings drag on for hours reviewing spreadsheets of hundreds of items, you're operating at the wrong level. Reviews should take 10 minutes after initial setup, focusing only on what's interesting or stuck.

5. **Scale Is a Tactic, Not a Virtue**: The healthiest product cultures often exist in companies around 250 people working on meaningful missions. These organizations maintain alignment and shared purpose more easily than massive companies where scale becomes an end in itself.

6. **Product Managers Must Serve the Business**: While user empathy matters, PMs fundamentally serve business needs. Understanding business models, target markets, revenue strategies, and economic trends is more critical than intuition or "product sense"—which is just compressed experience young PMs don't yet have.

7. **Storytelling Activates Ancient Brain Structures**: Stories create attention, comprehension, and retention (the "teacher's Holy Trinity") by engaging evolutionary mechanisms. Facts embedded in narratives stick while isolated facts get filtered by existing mental models.

8. **Psychological Safety Precedes Process**: If teams don't feel safe saying "this isn't working" without being told how to fix it, no OKR system will help. Leaders must examine whether they're creating fear that prevents autonomous problem-solving.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate Learning Cycles**: When discussing past projects, emphasize not just what you shipped but what you learned quarter-over-quarter and how that informed subsequent decisions. Show you build knowledge systematically.

- **Connect Weekly Actions to Strategic Goals**: Practice articulating how specific initiatives ladder up to business outcomes. Interviewers value PMs who can draw clear lines from tactical work to strategic impact.

- **Know Your Business Models**: Don't just focus on user empathy and product sense. Be prepared to discuss why subscription vs. one-time purchase makes sense, how pricing impacts retention, and how your decisions affected revenue—not just engagement.

- **Show Comfort with Ambiguity in Measurement**: Acknowledge that not everything is perfectly measurable, but demonstrate you try to quantify qualitative goals and iterate on measurement approaches based on what you learn.

- **Use Storytelling Structure**: When answering behavioral questions, use beginning (hook/problem), middle (approach/message), and end (success/learning) structure to make your examples more memorable and compelling.

- **Ask "How Will We Know?"**: When presented with hypothetical product challenges, show you think in outcomes by asking how success would be measured before jumping to solutions.
