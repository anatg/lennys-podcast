## TL;DR
Ebi shares the most tactical framework for developing and communicating product vision: empathize (create a "top 10 problems" doc), create (use "once upon a time" storytelling, mock headlines, or sketches), and evangelize (team → stakeholders → leadership). Product management is fundamentally about clarity (understanding problems deeply) and conviction (having a clear point of view on solutions), not spreadsheets and roadmaps.

## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Vision vs. Mission**: Mission is the *why* you exist (purpose, guiding principles). Vision is *what it looks like* when you get there—a vivid picture of the future state, not confused with strategy or goals.

- **Four Elements of a Good Vision**: (1) Lofty—feels exciting, almost scary; (2) Realistic/attainable—within reach; (3) Devoid of today's limitations—time-travel to the future without current tech constraints; (4) Grounded in a potent user problem.

- **Three Ways to Communicate Vision**:
  1. **"Once Upon a Time" Mad Libs**: "Once upon a time [problem], and then [problem worsened], and one day [what happened—your solution], and as a result [how the world changed]."
  2. **Working Backwards Article**: Write the TechCrunch headline and subtitle (or full article) announcing your shipped product. Forces clarity on impact.
  3. **Visualize It**: Sketch mocks, app store screenshots, or storyboards. Even low-fidelity post-its work—get a designer to polish later.

- **Empathize → Create → Evangelize** (Vision Development):
  - *Empathize*: "Understand work"—use your product (dog food), use competitors (cat food), create a living "Top 10 Things You Should Know" doc of biggest problems (qualitative, quantitative, tech debt).
  - *Create*: Convert conviction into a consumable vision using one of the three storytelling formats.
  - *Evangelize*: Three concentric circles—(1) Core team first, (2) Stakeholders second (open for comments, get buy-in), (3) Leadership last. Go as big as possible, let them pull you back.

- **Clarity and Conviction** (Core PM Craft): Product management = bringing *clarity* (transparent understanding of problems, removing noise) and *conviction* (a feeling/point of view on the right solution, not certainty). If you have conviction but it's unclear, you don't have conviction.

- **Narrative Structure (Insights → Strategy → Big Rocks)**: Ebi's strategic planning framework:
  - *Insights*: Day 1 of strategy session—understand work, review top problems, teardowns, ask experts (stakeholders share their "10 things").
  - *Strategy/Approach*: Day 2—which problems to focus on, in what order?
  - *Big Rocks*: Day 3—the 3-5 most important things to deliver (not a laundry list). Ice in the cocktail first, then the drink—everything else is sand around the rocks.

- **Top 10 Things You Should Know**: Living Google Doc per PM area with stack-ranked problems (qualitative, quantitative, tech debt). Update quarterly. Use as foundation for strategy sessions. Farm for problems continuously.

- **Informed Captain**: There must be one person accountable for a decision (like RAPID's "decider"). Not consensus by committee—empower that person to gather context and decide.

- **Product Sense**: Not logic—it's a *feeling* of what's right, refined over 10,000 hours of exposure to great products. Curiosity and immersion build this sense.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Vision is evergreen, not annual**: A good vision lasts 3-5 years. You don't rewrite it every year—you add fidelity (more detailed mocks, sizzle reels). For shorter-term work, use "micro-visions" (mocks, tweets, app store screenshots).

2. **Start with problems, not solutions**: Ebi's tactical mantra: If you can't clearly articulate the top problems keeping you up at night, you're not ready to build. Create a "Top 10 Things You Should Know" doc and update it quarterly—problems are your North Star.

3. **User research updates your mental model**: Research doesn't tell you what to build; it refines your understanding of customer problems. PMs must develop product sense through immersion (dog food your product, cat food competitors) and trust their gut informed by data.

4. **Write to force clarity**: Fancy decks don't create clarity—writing does. Ebi requires two-page documents (Insights, Strategy, Big Rocks) because writing forces you to synthesize and prioritize. If you can't write it clearly, you don't understand it.

5. **Evangelizing is 50% of vision work**: Having a great vision no one knows about is useless (the Seinfeld car reservation). Evangelize in concentric circles: team first (multiple sessions, open doc for comments), stakeholders second (incorporate feedback), leadership last (go big, let them pull back).

6. **Platform PMs need even stronger vision**: If you're building infrastructure, you must force partner teams to articulate their future vision so you can build the right foundations today. Use the same vision frameworks to pressure-test their direction.

7. **Culture is norms and beliefs—it always evolves**: Whether you shape it intentionally or not, culture changes. Uber's "toe-stepping/principled confrontation" enabled a junior PM to challenge the CEO and launch Uber Cash. Netflix's "informed captain" created liberating accountability. Google's microcultures allow YouTube, Cloud, and Search to have different operating styles.

8. **Love over likability**: Ebi doesn't optimize for being liked—she optimizes for being loved (defined as "the choice to extend yourself for someone's growth"). Love sometimes means hard, raw feedback when you've shown you care about the human behind the role.

9. **Vulnerability is strength**: Great teams know the human behind the role. Ebi asks engineering partners: Do you know your EM's birthday? Their work anniversary? Their career goals? Spend time bonding because you spend half your waking life together.

10. **Agency over fate**: From the poem *Invictus*—"I am the master of my fate, I am the captain of my soul." Even if you're not responsible for what happened, you're responsible for what you do about it. This mindset is core to being a great PM.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **AI as a creative partner for creators**: YouTube launched AI-powered inspiration tools to help creators research video ideas based on what their audience is watching. The goal: remove pre-work friction so creators can focus on storytelling.
- **Thumbnail Test & Compare**: YouTube rolled out AB testing for thumbnails—a long-awaited feature creators were hacking together with external tools. Enables data-driven iteration.
- **AI can't replace PM judgment**: If you can put all your research into ChatGPT and it spits out a PRD, you haven't done your job. PMs must curate problems, apply product sense, and make judgment calls informed by—but not dictated by—data.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Practice articulating problems clearly**: In interviews, if asked about a product area, immediately articulate the top 3-5 user problems before jumping to solutions. Use the "once upon a time" framework to tell a story.
- **Demonstrate vision-setting**: When asked "What would you build?", don't just list features. Paint a picture of the future state—write a headline, sketch a mock, or describe the world when the problem is solved.
- **Show clarity and conviction**: Structure your answers with clarity (here's the problem/data) and conviction (here's my informed point of view). If you waffle between options without a recommendation, you appear indecisive.
- **Ask about culture and norms**: Use Ebi's framing—"What are the good behaviors you reward and bad behaviors you condone?" This shows you understand culture drives product outcomes.
- **For leadership interviews, articulate your philosophy**: If interviewing for a people manager role, have a crisp answer to "What is your leadership philosophy?" Ebi's: Freedom + responsibility, informed captain, vulnerability as strength, love over likability.
- **Demonstrate "understand work"**: Talk about how you've used your product, competitors, done teardowns, synthesized research. Show you dog food and cat food. Interviewers want to see you immerse yourself.
- **Show you influence through storytelling**: Reference how you've evangelized a vision or strategy—team first, stakeholders second, leadership last. PMs influence without authority via narrative.
