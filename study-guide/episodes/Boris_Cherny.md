## Core Frameworks & Mental Models

- **Latent Demand**: Look for how users are "abusing" or misusing your product to accomplish tasks it wasn't designed for—this reveals where to build next. Example: 40% of Facebook group posts were buying/selling, leading to Marketplace. Claude Code users were growing tomatoes, analyzing genomes, and recovering wedding photos—leading to Cowork.

- **The Bitter Lesson**: Always bet on the more general model over the more specific one. Don't over-engineer workflows or fine-tune when you can use a more capable general model. Scaffolding might improve performance 10-20%, but gains get wiped out with the next model release.

- **Build for the Model Six Months Out**: Don't optimize for current model capabilities—build for where the model will be in 6 months. Accept uncomfortable product-market fit initially, but you'll "hit the ground running" when the better model arrives.

- **Don't Box the Model In**: Give the model tools and a goal, then let it figure out the approach. Don't layer strict workflows or over-curate context. Modern models perform better with autonomy than with heavy orchestration.

- **Under-Resource Intentionally**: Constraining team size forces people to leverage AI more creatively. One engineer on a project will naturally "Claude-ify" their work to ship faster. This principle accelerates AI adoption organically.

- **Bring Product to Where People Are**: Meet users in their existing workflows rather than forcing them to adopt new tools. Claude Code started in the terminal because it was easiest to build, but succeeded because it integrated into developers' existing environments.

## Top Insights (Timeless)

1. **Coding is "virtually solved"**: As of November 2024, Boris writes 0% of his code by hand—Claude Code writes 100%. He ships 10-30 pull requests daily. This isn't a future prediction; it's already happening for advanced engineers. The profession has fundamentally transformed in under a year.

2. **The printing press analogy**: AI coding tools are like the printing press—democratizing a skill previously held by <1% (scribes/programmers). In 50 years after Gutenberg, more material was printed than in the prior 1,000 years. Literacy took 200 years to reach 70% globally. Similarly, "everyone will be able to program" soon, unlocking unimaginable coordination and creation.

3. **Agents are what matters, not chatbots**: The key distinction is that agents can *use tools* and *act in the world*—not just converse. Claude Code was the first widely-adopted agent, and Cowork brings this to non-technical work. The transition from conversational AI to agentic AI is the next frontier across all roles.

4. **Speed as the only advantage**: For Claude Code, "our only advantage was speed" initially. Respond to feedback within minutes, ship daily, maintain fast feedback loops. This creates psychological safety for users to contribute more feedback, creating a virtuous cycle that compounds advantage.

5. **Multi-Claude-ing as the new paradigm**: Run 5+ Claude agents in parallel constantly. Each works autonomously while you context-switch. This is how work gets done now—not sitting and coding, but orchestrating multiple agents on different tasks simultaneously.

6. **Product managers, designers, finance—everyone codes now**: On the Claude Code team, the PM, designer, finance person, and data scientist all code. Roles are blurring—there's 50% overlap already. By end of year, expect "software engineer" title to evolve into "builder" or for "everyone to be a product manager who codes."

7. **Maximum tokens, maximum capability**: Don't cost-optimize early. Give engineers unlimited tokens and the most capable model (currently Opus 4.6). Cheaper models often use *more* tokens through correction and iteration. Optimize only after proving the idea works.

## AI & The Changing PM Role

- **Everyone becomes a product manager**: Boris predicts that by end of 2025, traditional software engineer roles will dissolve. "Everyone is going to be a product manager, and everyone codes." The distinction between PM/engineer/designer is becoming "murkier" with 50% overlap already.

- **PMs need to code now**: All non-engineering functions at Anthropic are "fairly technical" and screened for technical ability. The PM on Claude Code codes regularly. This isn't optional anymore—it's table stakes.

- **Generalists will win**: The most effective people cross disciplines—product engineers with design sense, engineers who love talking to users, PMs who understand infrastructure. Being "AI native" isn't enough; you need curiosity across multiple domains.

- **PMs are sweating, but shouldn't be**: While 70% of PMs report enjoying their jobs *more* with AI (per Lenny's informal survey), the real shift is that AI is starting to come up with product ideas by analyzing feedback, bug reports, and telemetry. This was previously the PM's domain.

- **Adjacent roles are next**: After engineering, the next roles most impacted will be "adjacent to engineering"—product management, design, data science. "Any kind of job where you use computer tools" will be transformed as agents become the norm.

## Interview Prep Takeaways

- **Demonstrate AI-first thinking**: Show you understand agents vs. chatbots, and can articulate how AI changes workflows—not just assists them. Reference concrete examples like multi-agent orchestration.

- **Talk about under-resourcing strategically**: Explain how constraints force innovation and AI adoption. Boris's team built Cowork in 10 days *because* they were under-resourced, forcing them to leverage Claude Code fully.

- **Show cross-functional versatility**: Emphasize where you've crossed traditional role boundaries—PMs who code, engineers with design sense, etc. The future rewards generalists who use AI to operate across disciplines.

- **Use the latent demand framework**: When discussing product decisions, reference examples of observing how users "misuse" features to identify new opportunities. This shows sophisticated product thinking.

- **Articulate exponential thinking**: Boris's prediction that engineers wouldn't need IDEs "made the room audibly gasp" in May 2025, but happened by November. Show you can trace exponential curves and make bold but grounded predictions.

- **Emphasize speed and iteration**: Boris's competitive advantage was fixing bugs "within minutes" of user reports. Discuss your approach to rapid feedback loops and shipping velocity.

- **Connect work to mission**: Boris returned to Anthropic because "whatever the work might be...it's just not really a substitute" for mission-driven culture. Show you understand why mission matters beyond building cool products.
