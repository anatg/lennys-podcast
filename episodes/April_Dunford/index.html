    April Dunford — Author (Obviously Awesome) & Positioning Consultant¶

    Guest Profile¶

    • Name: April Dunford
    • Role/Company: Founder of Ambient Strategy; author of Obviously Awesome: How to Nail Product Positioning So Customers Get It, Buy It, Love It; positioning consultant and speaker; formerly VP Marketing at multiple B2B tech companies (Sybase, IBM, Nortel, Janna Systems, Pivotal, and others)
    • Background: 25+ years of B2B tech marketing and positioning experience. Has repositioned roughly 16 companies over her career, often taking products that were struggling to close deals and helping them dramatically improve pipeline conversion by clarifying how and why they win. Author of the definitive practical book on B2B tech positioning.

    Tags¶

    Positioning Marketing B2B Frameworks Sales Go-to-Market Messaging Product Strategy Startup Category Design

    TL;DR¶

    April's five-step positioning process is the most practical, actionable framework for product positioning in the podcast catalog. The key insight is that positioning isn't a tagline or a mission statement — it's a deliberate choice about which competitive context makes your product the obvious best option. Her Help Scout walkthrough and tuna fishing net analogy are immediately usable for any B2B product.

    Core Frameworks & Mental Models¶

    • Positioning Definition: How your product is the best in the world at delivering a specific kind of value that a well-defined set of customers care a lot about. The four components are: (1) a competitive alternative, (2) differentiated capabilities relative to that alternative, (3) value those capabilities create, (4) target customers who find that value compelling. "Best in the world" is relative to the competitive context you choose to compete in.

    • Five-Step Positioning Process:

    • Competitive alternatives: What would customers do if your product didn't exist? Not just direct competitors — could be "hire someone," "use Excel," "do nothing." This determines the baseline you're actually beating.
    • Differentiated capabilities: What do you do demonstrably better than the competitive alternatives? Features, capabilities, properties that are genuinely different (not just "we're better").
    • Value themes: What is the value those differentiated capabilities deliver? For each capability, ask: "So what? What does that mean for the customer?" Keep asking until you get to a business outcome.
    • Best-fit customers: Which customers value those value themes the most? Who are the customers for whom your differentiators matter the most?

    • Market category: What market context makes your differentiated value obvious? The category is the frame of reference — choose it deliberately to make your positioning land without explanation.

    • Competitive Context as the Positioning Lever: The same product can be positioned in multiple ways — and the right answer depends on which competitive frame makes your strengths most obvious and your weaknesses least relevant. Example: a database with strong graph capabilities could be positioned as: (a) a graph database (specialty niche), (b) a relational database with graph features (positioned against Oracle/Postgres), or (c) a multi-model database. Each framing changes who buys it, at what price, and how fast they close.

    • Help Scout Walkthrough (Canonical Example): Help Scout had a feature ("conversations" not "tickets") that customers loved — they described it as feeling like email, human, not robotic. The competitive alternative wasn't Zendesk — it was Gmail, shared inboxes, and email chains. When positioned against email, Help Scout's features looked amazing (assignments, collision detection, templates). When positioned against Zendesk, they looked like a toy. Repositioning them against "chaotic shared email inboxes" transformed their market and growth.

    • Tuna Fishing Net for Early-Stage Positioning: Early-stage companies should deliberately cast a wide net — don't tighten positioning prematurely. Explore broadly which customers are responding, why, and what they value. Loose positioning in early stage is appropriate because you're still learning which customers are best-fit. The right time to tighten is when you have enough signal (multiple similar customers, consistent reasons for winning) to recognize a pattern. Over-constraining positioning too early cuts off discovery.

    • Bowling Pin Strategy: Once you have a clear target, dominate a narrow segment first, then expand concentrically. The bowling pin insight: win the kingpin (the segment you can dominate) before expanding to adjacent pins. This is the opposite of "total addressable market" thinking — which tempts you to keep the net wide to look bigger, at the cost of never owning any segment decisively.

    • Segmentation vs. Personas in B2B: Personas matter for UX. For positioning and sales, the unit that matters is the champion persona — the person inside the buying organization who will champion your product internally. Different from the economic buyer (who writes the check), the end user (who uses it daily), and the evaluator (who runs the RFP). Positioning must resonate with the champion, because the champion has to re-sell your product internally without your help.

    • Signs of Weak Positioning: You have a positioning problem when:

    • Customers describe your product in ways that don't match how you describe it
    • Prospects say "I could build that in a spreadsheet" or compare you to a manual alternative you weren't targeting
    • Sales cycles are long and exhausting, with lots of education needed before prospects understand what you do
    • You win deals but don't understand clearly why; you lose deals and don't understand why

    • Different salespeople pitch the product differently

    • Positioning vs. Messaging vs. Branding: Positioning is the foundation — the strategic choices about competitive context, differentiation, and best-fit customers. Messaging is how you communicate the positioning (language, copy, narratives). Branding is the visual and emotional identity. The hierarchy matters: weak positioning can't be fixed with better messaging or a rebrand. Many companies confuse all three and try to solve a positioning problem with copywriting.

    • Sales Narrative as Applied Positioning: The sales narrative is the story salespeople tell — and it should be built directly on the positioning. The flow: (1) here's the world as it exists now, (2) here's the problem with the current world, (3) here's a better world that's possible, (4) here's what makes it possible (your product), (5) here's why we're the best at delivering it. The narrative makes the positioning emotionally resonant for buyers.

    • When to Bring In Outside Help vs. DIY: The positioning process in Obviously Awesome is self-serve — a company can run it internally. The main reason to bring in an outside consultant: companies are often too close to their own product and too invested in current positioning to challenge it honestly. Founders especially struggle to question positioning they've been using for years. An outside eye helps surface assumptions that have been invisible.

    Top Insights (Timeless)¶

    1. The customer sees the product; you see the product AND the technology behind it. Founders and engineers know every capability and how it was built. Customers only see what the product does for them. The positioning work is translating capabilities into customer value — and that translation is rarely obvious from the inside.

    2. You are always positioned — the question is whether you chose the position or let the market choose for you. If you haven't done explicit positioning work, your customers are positioning you based on whatever frame of reference your product reminds them of. That frame may not be the one that makes you look best.

    3. The competitive alternative is the most underrated input in positioning. Most companies list direct competitors. The real competitive alternative is "what would the customer do if your product didn't exist?" That's often Excel, a manual process, or nothing — and those alternatives change everything about how you frame your value.

    4. Market category is a frame, not a fact. There is no "true" market category for your product. You choose the frame. The right frame is the one that makes your strengths obvious without requiring explanation.

    5. Positioning is a team sport. The best positioning process involves sales, marketing, product, and ideally CS — because each function has different customer conversations and different data. A positioning workshop that only involves marketing produces positioning that doesn't survive contact with the sales conversation.

    6. Early-stage: fish with a net, not a spear. The biggest early-stage positioning mistake is premature specificity. Until you have pattern recognition from actual customers about who buys and why they win, tightening positioning kills discovery. Let the market teach you first.

    7. Positioning has a shelf life. As the market changes (new competitors enter, technology shifts, customer sophistication grows), positioning that worked two years ago may actively hurt you today. Revisit it every 12–18 months or after any major competitive move.

    AI & The Changing PM Role¶

    • This episode predates the current AI product wave but the framework is directly applicable: AI products face the hardest positioning challenge of any product category because "AI" is both a feature description and a market category — and using it as a category name places you against every other AI product
    • The biggest AI positioning trap: competing in a "we use AI" category where every product makes the same claim. The positioning fix: choose a more specific category where your AI capabilities make you obviously best (e.g., "AI for clinical documentation" vs. "medical AI")
    • The differentiated capabilities step is especially important for AI products where the underlying model may be similar across competitors — the real differentiation is often in workflow integration, data access, reliability, or domain-specific training
    • The Help Scout insight applies to AI products: ask what the customer would do if your AI product didn't exist (often: hire a human, use a manual process, or do without) and position against that, not against other AI tools

    Interview Prep Takeaways¶

    • "How do you define and communicate your product's positioning?": Positioning = competitive context + differentiated capabilities + value delivered + best-fit customers + market category. Not a tagline. Built from the ground up by understanding what alternatives the customer would use if you didn't exist.
    • "How do you approach go-to-market strategy for a new product?": Start with the positioning process. Who wins? Why? Against what alternatives? Use that to define the category frame, the narrative, and the ICP. Sales motion follows positioning.
    • "How do you handle competitive differentiation?": List capabilities that are genuinely different (not better in the same way). For each, ask "so what?" until you get to a business outcome. That's your value theme.
    • "When is the right time to niche down vs. stay broad?": Early stage: broad (tuna net). Once you have pattern recognition from multiple similar wins — tighten. Use bowling pin to dominate the kingpin segment first, then expand.
    • "What makes a great marketing or positioning strategy?": Makes the product obviously the best option for the right customer, without requiring a 30-minute explanation. If your rep has to educate every prospect from scratch, positioning is broken.
