    Matt MacInnis — Chief Product Officer at Rippling¶

    Guest Profile¶

    • Name: Matt MacInnis
    • Role/Company: Chief Product Officer (CPO) at Rippling; formerly longtime Chief Operating Officer (COO)
    • Background: 45 years old; spent 7 years at Apple under Steve Jobs; founded Inkling (9-year journey, 2009-2018); early investor in Notion, Rippling, Clever, Zenefits, Decagon, and ~70 other companies; Canadian; former radio personality. Transitioned from COO to CPO at Rippling in early 2025 to address R&D leadership gaps. Known for bringing order to chaos across multiple functions.

    Tags¶

    Leadership Product Strategy Execution Intensity Culture Hiring Org Design Frameworks AI/ML SaaS Enterprise B2B Startup Vision Communication Career Process

    TL;DR¶

    Matt MacInnis shares brutal truths about building extraordinary businesses: extraordinary outcomes demand extraordinary efforts, and if your team is ever comfortable, you're making a mistake. He explains how to fight entropy through relentless intensity, why product market fit is unmistakable when real, when founders should quit (year 4-5 without clear traction), and how Rippling's success stems from bundling business software on the "people primitive" with comprehensive first-party data—positioning them uniquely for the AI era.

    Core Frameworks & Mental Models¶

    • Alpha vs. Beta Framework (from finance): Alpha = outperformance/upside; Beta = volatility/unpredictability. High alpha people are valuable but volatile (like Dennis Rodman—every team can have one). Low beta people/processes provide stability. Apply by: deliberately choosing high alpha for 0-to-1 products where you need creativity; low beta for mature products (like payroll) where reliability matters. Processes exist solely to lower beta—but they suppress alpha, so use them judiciously.

    • Power Law + Entropy + Compounding: Power law distributions mean 99th percentile effort doesn't yield 10% more reward—it yields 10-100X more. Entropy (second law of thermodynamics) means all systems decay toward disorder without constant energy injection. Teams naturally optimize for local comfort over company outcomes. Your job as a leader: inject energy relentlessly to fight entropy at every level, every day. This is why intensity cannot drop.

    • Oversteer vs. Understeer: When you can't get a decision exactly right (staffing, budget, deadlines), decide whether it's better to oversteer or understeer. For staffing: deliberately understaffing is less evil than overstaffing. Overstaffing creates politics, work on low-priority items, cruft, and waste. Understaffing forces focus on highest priorities. The wisdom is knowing not to under-under-staff.

    • Binding Receptors (Drug Discovery Analogy): Markets are immutable—no amount of marketing/tweeting changes whether binding receptors exist for your product. View your startup as running an experiment: fate has already decided if the market wants it. Focus on finding the right "drug" (product), not convincing the "body" (market) to develop receptors. Launches don't matter much; bottom-up traction does. This mindset is liberating—stop trying to market your way through lack of product-market fit.

    • SPOTAC Hiring Framework: People should be Smart, Passionate, Optimistic, Tenacious, Adaptable, and Kind. Not a top-down checklist, but useful for decoding your intuition post-interview: "Why didn't this candidate click?" Go through SPOTAC to identify what was missing (e.g., "They talked shit about their last manager—not optimistic").

    • The PQL (Product Quality List): A lightweight, iterative checklist of standards products must meet before shipping. It's deliberately branded with an acronym/meme ("pickle") to create a vessel for meaning that sticks in the culture. Evolves as you learn (e.g., adding "one feature flag maximum" after a blank-screen bug). Balances lowering beta (quality/reliability) without crushing alpha (creativity). Made public so all PMs see how it applies.

    • Narrative Violations (Jeff Lewis concept): Every massively successful company violates common wisdom in some important way. Don't try to replicate Notion's "never give up" story or Rippling's "move fast" story—each company succeeds on the idiosyncrasies of its founder. Great investors spot that spikiness.

    Top Insights (Timeless)¶

    1. Extraordinary results require extraordinary efforts—and it's supposed to be exhausting: If you want 99th percentile outcomes, you need extreme intensity. If your team ever finds themselves in the comfort zone, they're making a mistake. This isn't sustainable long-term grinding individuals down, but the team collectively must stay "on the ball" relentlessly. Good teams get tired; that's when great teams kick their asses. The key: this only works when you're winning. If growth is 30-40% or outcomes aren't great, it's nearly impossible to extract extraordinary effort because people don't believe it will yield much.

    2. You learn far more from success than failure: "You don't really learn from your mistakes, you learn from your successes." The valley romanticizes learning from failure, but it's mostly feel-good VC propaganda. If you had to pick a maintenance technician for your plane—one who's seen it done right 100 times vs. one who's seen it done wrong 100 times but "learned from mistakes"—the choice is obvious. Join winning teams at exciting growth moments to learn what right looks like. Matt learned more in 6-7 years at Rippling than in 9 years at Inkling.

    3. Product-market fit is unmistakable—if you're unsure, you don't have it: That VC line is true: "You absolutely know it when you see it; if you don't absolutely know it, you don't have it." Matt thought Inkling had it multiple times over 9 years—in hindsight with Rippling's obvious PMF, it was clear they never did. Most companies should quit by year 4-5 after a couple pivots if it's not screaming growth. The "never quit" narrative is pro-VC (they can't get money back, so they want you to keep trying), not pro-entrepreneur. Seed investors assume zero return; don't sacrifice your limited years on earth for a cap table.

    4. Withholding feedback is fundamentally selfish: The most selfish thing you can do is think something that would help someone improve and not say it because it makes you uncomfortable—you're optimizing for your own comfort. Demand feedback and give it. Escalations from customers are gifts, not inconveniences—they help you find root causes and iterate on the system that builds the system. Never let people avoid escalating to you. Matt personally triages escalations and minor issues from customers to bring them directly to product teams.

    5. As a leader, preserve the founder's intensity—don't buffer it: The founder CEO is the purest source of energy/ambition. Every concentric circle of management beyond the founder risks an order-of-magnitude drop in intensity—this is dangerous. Your job isn't to buffer people from the CEO's intensity; it's to mirror it exactly. Let buffering happen elsewhere. Model intensity publicly: jump into Slack huddles immediately, upside-down feedback in public channels, personally review every product flow, demand the 99th percentile of energy when people are in the "bored and tired zone." People don't want a "chill boss"—that's the most pejorative label. Be respectful and intense, not chill.

    6. Product is the highest-order bit: As COO, Matt optimized everything around the product as it was. As CPO, he realized: if you get product right and it fits the market, everything else gets easier—finance, sales, marketing, recruiting, all of it. This is why great product managers are polymaths who must be good at communication, project management, systems thinking, and working with the smartest people in the company. It's why point SaaS solutions are in trouble in the AI era—without comprehensive first-party data, you can't build great AI, and you're stuck integrating through "straws" (APIs/flat files).

    7. Bottom-up leadership: Lead from what you observe, in order: When Matt jumped into CPO, he wanted to measure adoption metrics immediately. But the team lacked basics: test coverage standards, product quality checklists, factory inspection processes. You can't skip steps. Everything must be done in its time and order, moving fast but building foundational layers first. Asking for adoption metrics before establishing quality baselines is "absolute insanity." Think first principles because you have no choice when you lack domain playbooks—this forces rigor.

    AI & The Changing PM Role¶

    • Point solutions are in trouble in the AI era: Without enough first-party data, point SaaS tools can't provide AI with the context/joins/correlations needed to be useful. They're forced to integrate to third-party systems and "drink data through a straw." Even the biggest HCM + payroll integrations still use flat files via SFTP—they won't build viable AI.

    • Shovel sellers and mine owners win; the middle gets crushed: If you sell the shovels (OpenAI, Google/Gemini) or own the mine (Rippling with comprehensive business data), you can make money. If you're in between—building AI software that rents shovels and borrows ore—you face unit economics death because both parties will demand value capture. 80-90% of standalone AI businesses lack a durable insight for viable unit economics.

    • This is a bundling era, not unbundling: "There's two ways to make money in software: bundling and unbundling. You just have to get the timing right." We're in a bundling cycle because AI needs lots of context across systems. Rippling's competitive advantage: all business data on a common graph with a semantic layer, enabling AI "back flips" that will set the standard in 2025+. They've "shipped nothing yet" compared to what's coming.

    • Matt uses AI as a non-judgmental thought partner for language: He writes the essay/idea himself, then asks ChatGPT/Gemini for pithy ways to articulate concepts. 80% is trash, but 20% yields new words or angles. AI isn't useful for generating ideas themselves, but refining how to communicate them—critical since language is the most important tool for bringing brains along on a journey. (Note: Claude is better at writing/language, per Lenny.)

    Interview Prep Takeaways¶

    • Model the intensity you want to see: In PM interviews and on the job, demonstrate relentless follow-through. If a bug comes up in your take-home or during onsite conversation, don't brush past it—dig into root cause. Show you'll escalate issues and give direct feedback. Interviewers want to see you won't optimize for local comfort.

    • Decode your intuition with frameworks: When evaluating a product decision or candidate (in case interviews), use structured models (alpha/beta, SPOTAC, oversteer/understeer) to articulate why something feels right or wrong. This shows you can think from first principles and communicate reasoning clearly—critical PM skills.

    • Ask for relevant experience, not advice: When given a behavioral or situational question, dig into the interviewer's actual experience with similar problems. If they lack it, discount generic advice. And when you answer, provide concrete specifics from your own relevant experience, not abstract principles. Matt's investment list example (naming failures, not just wins) shows intellectual honesty.

    • Demonstrate you've learned from successes, not just failures: When discussing past roles, emphasize what you learned from being on winning teams or successful projects—how they operated, what made them great—rather than only "what I learned from my mistakes" stories. Shows you know what good looks like.

    • Show you understand compound, power law, entropy dynamics: When discussing growth strategy or product roadmap, reference how small advantages compound, how being in the top 10% yields 10X outcomes (not 10% more), and how systems decay without energy. Demonstrates sophisticated systems thinking. Use examples like deliberately understaffing to force prioritization.

    • Talk about synthesis and communication as core skills: Matt emphasizes that his job is synthesizing ideas and communicating them clearly. In interviews, show how you've created frameworks, named concepts (like "PQL"), and brought teams along on a change. Use crisp language. If asked about stakeholder management, discuss how you distill complex ideas into memorable vessels.

    • Be brutally honest about wins and losses: Don't just list your "portfolio" of shipped products. Talk about what failed, what you'd do differently, when you should have quit sooner. Matt's candor about Inkling and his 70 investments (most went to zero) builds trust. Interviewers respect self-awareness over resume polishing.
